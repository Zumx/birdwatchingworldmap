The United States has world-class wetland and woodland refuges, and the best of
them maintain photography blinds and observation hides that turn a hopeful
morning into a guaranteed encounter. These ten are the ones serious birders
plan trips around.

## 1. Bosque del Apache NWR, New Mexico

Winter dawn here is a spectacle: tens of thousands of snow geese and sandhill
cranes lifting off at once, watched from boardwalk hides along the flight deck.

## 2. High Island, Texas

The Gulf Coast migrant trap. Spring "fallouts" pack the sanctuary boardwalks
and rookery hides with warblers, tanagers, and nesting spoonbills.

## 3. Anhinga Trail, Everglades, Florida

A short boardwalk that functions as one long open hide — alligators below,
anhingas and herons at arm's length.

## 4. Magee Marsh, Ohio

"The Biggest Week" boardwalk on Lake Erie, where exhausted spring warblers feed
metres from a continuous low hide.

## 5. Cape May, New Jersey

Autumn raptor and songbird migration funnel, with platform hides over ponds
and the famous hawk-watch deck.

## 6. Sacramento NWR, California

Auto-tour wetlands with photo blinds delivering close waterfowl in the Pacific
Flyway's winter heart.

## 7. Bear River MBR, Utah

A vast Great Salt Lake wetland with an auto loop and viewing structures over
clouds of phalaropes and ibis.

## 8. Bombay Hook NWR, Delaware

Atlantic Flyway tidal flats with observation towers built for shorebird
spectacles at high tide.

## 9. Brazos Bend State Park, Texas

Lakeside hides and towers among Spanish moss, reliable for wading birds and the
park's celebrated alligators.

## 10. Ridgefield NWR, Washington

A Pacific Northwest auto-route refuge with a dedicated blind overlooking
wintering swans, sandhill cranes, and raptors.

## How American hides work

Most sit inside National Wildlife Refuges run by the US Fish and Wildlife
Service. Many refuges charge a small entry fee or accept the federal Duck Stamp
and recreation passes. Some photo blinds are reservable for a fee; others are
first-come. Auto-tour loops double as mobile hides — your car is the best blind
many refuges offer.

## Timing is everything

Plan around the flyways. The interior wetlands peak with wintering waterfowl
(November–February); the Gulf and Great Lakes peak with spring migration
(April–May); coastal sites peak with autumn raptor passage (September–October).
Arrive at first light — refuge activity collapses by mid-morning.

## Etiquette in the hide

Move slowly, talk quietly, and never bang the shutters or doors. Do not bait or
play loud calls near nesting areas. Give other watchers space and a clear
sightline. Keep dogs out. The whole system depends on birds not learning that
hides mean disturbance.

The bee-eaters — family Meropidae — represent one of the most visually arresting groups of birds on Earth. Slender-bodied, long-tailed, and clothed in combinations of chestnut, turquoise, green, yellow, and scarlet that seem almost too intense for any real creature to carry, they hunt in open country and over forest canopy with an aerial mastery that belies the apparent fragility of their build. The family contains 26 species distributed across Africa, southern Europe, Asia, and Australia, with the greatest diversity concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa, where 17 species occur.

Bee-eaters catch stinging insects — not only bees but also wasps, hornets, dragonflies, and other aerial invertebrates — in flight, returning to a perch to rub the prey against a branch to discharge the venom before swallowing. The characteristic thwacking sound of a bee-eater devenoming its catch is as distinctive as the birds' calls, a liquid, rolling note that carries across open landscapes and announces the family's presence before the bird itself is seen.

## Family Overview and Structure

The 26 species of Meropidae are grouped into three genera. Merops contains 24 species and accounts for most of the familiar bee-eaters. Nyctyornis contains two species — the Blue-bearded Bee-eater of South and Southeast Asia and the Red-bearded Bee-eater of the Malay Peninsula and Greater Sundas — which are larger, more forest-dependent, and lack the elongated central tail feathers that characterize many Merops species. Meropogon contains a single species, the Purple-bearded Bee-eater of Sulawesi and the Sula Islands.

Most bee-eaters are gregarious, nesting colonially in earthen burrows excavated in sandy banks, flat ground, or even road cuttings. Colony sizes range from a handful of pairs to several thousand in the large African species. Many species have helpers at the nest — non-breeding birds that assist with incubation and chick-feeding — a form of cooperative breeding that has made the family a focus of avian behavioral research.

## The European Bee-Eater

Merops apiaster is the species most familiar to European birders and the one most likely to appear on a European list as a rarity in northern countries. Breeding across southern Europe from Portugal east through the Balkans, Turkey, and Central Asia to Kazakhstan, the European Bee-eater winters in sub-Saharan Africa, undertaking a round trip of thousands of kilometres. The combination of chestnut crown and back, golden-yellow throat, turquoise-blue underparts, and elongated central tail streamers makes it one of the most striking birds in the Western Palearctic.

In Spain and Portugal, European Bee-eaters nest abundantly in sandy river banks and road cuttings. The Extremadura region of western Spain is particularly productive, with colonies often visible from roadsides. In recent decades, the species has been expanding its range northward into France, Germany, the Netherlands, and Switzerland, likely in response to warming temperatures.

## The Southern Carmine Bee-Eater

Merops nubicoides is arguably the most spectacular bee-eater by any measure. Breeding colonially in the Zambezi Valley of Zimbabwe and Zambia and along several other major southern African rivers, the Southern Carmine produces nesting colonies of tens of thousands of pairs in sandy river banks — spectacles comparable to the great flamingo or quelea concentrations of the continent. The plumage is predominantly deep carmine-red with a turquoise-blue crown, rump, and undertail, and a black mask.

Mana Pools National Park in Zimbabwe, along the Zambezi floodplain, is one of the premier sites to observe this species in its breeding season from August to November. Birds nest in the banks, hunt from the backs of large mammals including Cape Buffalo and African Elephant, and catch insects flushed by the grazing animals — a behavior that makes them uniquely approachable for observers on game drives.

## African Forest Bee-Eaters

Several African bee-eaters are birds of forest edges and interior canopy rather than open country. The Black Bee-eater (Merops gularis) of West and Central African forest is a small, deep-black species with scarlet throat and turquoise rump, found singly or in pairs along forest streams. The Blue-headed Bee-eater (Merops muelleri) is another forest specialist, restricted to the Congo Basin. The Rosy Bee-eater (Merops malimbicus) nests in enormous colonies in sandy beaches along major Central African rivers — colonies of this species on the Congo and its tributaries can number many thousands of pairs.

## Asian Species

Asia hosts a diverse bee-eater fauna, particularly in South and Southeast Asia. The Chestnut-headed Bee-eater (Merops leschenaulti) ranges from India across mainland Southeast Asia to Java, nesting in riverbanks and forest clearings. The Blue-tailed Bee-eater (Merops philippinus) is a widespread species from India through the Philippines and Indonesia, often nesting in paddy field embankments where it is conspicuous on telephone wires and bare branches.

The Rainbow Bee-eater (Merops ornatus) is the sole Australian representative of the family, a migratory species that breeds across mainland Australia and winters in New Guinea and eastern Indonesia. Its combination of green and orange plumage with a long, narrow tail extension makes it unmistakable in the Australian landscape.

## Identification Features

Identifying bee-eaters in the field draws on plumage pattern, bill shape, tail length, and call. Most species have a prominent dark eye-stripe (mask), which varies in width and extent. The presence or absence of elongated central tail feathers, and their length relative to body size, is often diagnostic at the species level. Call quality is species-specific: the European Bee-eater's liquid rolling prrt-prrt is distinct from the harsher calls of the African species, and the deep, resonant call of the Blue-bearded Bee-eater in Asian forests is immediately recognizable.

Habitat is a strong first filter: a bee-eater seen in open African savanna is unlikely to be a forest specialist, and range narrows the possibilities considerably in most regions. Confusion is most likely between closely related species in overlap zones — the Northern Carmine and Southern Carmine Bee-eaters meet in the Zambezi Valley, and several Asian species have partially overlapping ranges in South and Southeast Asia.

## Watching and Photographing Bee-Eaters

Bee-eaters are among the most rewarding subjects for bird photography, combining vivid color with predictable behavior — returning repeatedly to favored perches after each hunting sortie. Morning light in open country with a colony visible in a nearby bank is a reliable setup; the birds are active from first light and most vocal and conspicuous in the first two hours after sunrise.

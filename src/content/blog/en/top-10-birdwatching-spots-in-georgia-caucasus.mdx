For a country smaller than Ireland, Georgia offers an outrageous concentration of birding riches. The Black Sea coast at Batumi hosts one of the largest raptor migrations on the planet, the high Greater Caucasus holds endemics found nowhere else on Earth, and the semi-deserts of the east add a Central Asian flavour to the list. In a fortnight you can stand under a river of a million migrating honey buzzards, scan alpine ridges for Caucasian Snowcock, and watch Black Francolin in the eastern steppe. The country's roughly 350 recorded species reflect its position at the meeting point of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East, all compressed between the Greater and Lesser Caucasus ranges.

## 1. Batumi Raptor Count, Adjara

The hills above Batumi form the most famous bottleneck in the country and one of the great raptor spectacles on Earth. Each autumn, from late August to mid-October, well over a million raptors funnel along the narrow coastal strip between the Black Sea and the mountains. European Honey Buzzard (Pernis apivorus), Steppe Buzzard, Black Kite, and Montagu's Harrier pass in vast streams, with rarer Crested Honey Buzzard and Pallid Harrier sprinkled through. Two counting stations above the villages of Sakhalvasho and Shuamta give organised counts and visitor viewing.

## 2. Chorokhi Delta, Batumi

The mouth of the Chorokhi River just south of Batumi is the coastal counterpoint to the raptor watch, a migration hotspot for waders, gulls, terns, and passerines. Black-winged Pratincole, Caspian Tern, and Paddyfield Warbler turn up, and the riverine scrub holds migrant warblers and flycatchers. It is the best wader site on the Georgian coast and an easy add-on between raptor sessions.

## 3. Kazbegi (Stepantsminda) and the Greater Caucasus

The area around the village of Stepantsminda, beneath the 5,047-metre volcano of Mount Kazbek, is the classic base for high-mountain endemics. Caucasian Snowcock (Tetraogallus caucasicus) and Caucasian Grouse (Lyrurus mlokosiewiczi, Near Threatened) — both restricted to the Caucasus — display on the alpine slopes, while Great Rosefinch, Güldenstädt's Redstart, Wallcreeper, and Lammergeier work the cliffs and meadows above the Georgian Military Highway.

## 4. Lagodekhi Protected Areas, Kakheti

Georgia's oldest reserve, in the far north-east where the country meets Azerbaijan and Dagestan, protects pristine forest rising into the high Caucasus. The lower beech and hornbeam woods hold Green Warbler, Semi-collared Flycatcher, and Krüper's-type tits, while the alpine zone again offers the Caucasian gamebirds. Its waterfalls and old-growth forest make it one of the most scenic walking destinations in the country.

## 5. Javakheti Plateau and its Lakes

The volcanic plateau in the south, around 2,000 metres, holds a string of shallow lakes — Madatapa, Khanchali, Kartsakhi, and Bughdasheni — that form the most important breeding wetlands in Georgia. Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened), Velvet Scoter, White-headed Duck (Oxyura leucocephala, Endangered), and Armenian Gull breed or stage here, and Demoiselle Crane passes on migration. The surrounding steppe holds larks, pipits, and harriers.

## 6. Vashlovani Protected Areas, Kakheti

In the arid far south-east near the Azerbaijani border, Vashlovani is Georgia's only true semi-desert, a badland of clay ravines, juniper savanna, and gallery forest along the Alazani River. This is the place for Black Francolin (Francolinus francolinus), Eastern Rock Nuthatch, Finsch's Wheatear, Chukar, and breeding Egyptian Vulture (Neophron percnopterus, Endangered). The landscape feels more Central Asian than European.

## 7. Tbilisi Sea and Surroundings

The large reservoir on the edge of the capital, known as the Tbilisi Sea, is a convenient migration and wintering site for grebes, ducks, gulls, and waders. The surrounding scrub-covered hills hold Rock Nuthatch, See-see-type partridges, and breeding shrikes, making it an easy half-day for visitors based in the city.

## 8. Borjomi-Kharagauli National Park

One of Europe's largest national parks protects vast spruce and fir forest in the central mountains. The conifer zone holds Three-toed Woodpecker (Picoides tridactylus), Red-fronted Serin, Nutcracker, and the full montane forest assemblage, while the higher meadows add accentors and Twite. Marked trails and huts make multi-day forest birding straightforward.

## 9. Kolkheti National Park, Poti

The Kolkheti wetlands on the central Black Sea coast, including Lake Paliastomi and the Rioni delta, protect Ramsar-listed marshes and flooded alder forest. Pygmy Cormorant (Microcarbo pygmeus), Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened), and large numbers of migrant and wintering waterbirds use the area, and seawatching off Poti can produce shearwaters and skuas in passage.

## 10. Davit Gareja, Kakheti

The semi-desert ridges around the Davit Gareja cave monastery complex, on the Azerbaijani border, combine remarkable medieval architecture with arid-country birding. Eastern Rock Nuthatch, Finsch's Wheatear, Isabelline Wheatear, and Bimaculated Lark occur on the bare slopes, and raptors including Long-legged Buzzard and Egyptian Vulture patrol the ridgelines.

## When to visit

Georgia's headline event is the autumn raptor migration at Batumi, peaking from late August through September and tailing into mid-October. Mid-September is the classic window, when honey buzzard numbers are at their height and a single good day can produce six figures of birds overhead. The Batumi Raptor Count, run by international volunteers, welcomes visitors at its hillside stations and posts daily totals through the season.

Spring, from April to June, is the time for breeding endemics and territory song. The Kazbegi and Lagodekhi high country becomes accessible as snow retreats, and Caucasian Snowcock and Caucasian Grouse display on the alpine slopes in May and June. The Javakheti lakes fill with breeding waterbirds, and the eastern semi-deserts of Vashlovani and Davit Gareja are at their best before the summer heat, with Black Francolin calling at dawn.

Winter is quieter but rewarding on the coast and the lowland reservoirs, where wildfowl, divers, and gulls gather. The high mountains are largely snowbound, and the Javakheti plateau freezes, so the season concentrates birds at lower elevations around Batumi, Kolkheti, and the Tbilisi Sea.

## Practical notes

Georgia is easy and affordable to travel. Tbilisi and Kutaisi both have international airports, and Batumi is well served in season. Distances are short by Caucasian standards: Batumi to Tbilisi is around five to six hours by road or a short domestic flight, and the eastern reserves of Kakheti are within two to three hours of the capital. A hired car gives the most flexibility, though local guides and birding operators are widely available and inexpensive.

The Batumi Raptor Count organisation provides the best coordination for the autumn migration and can arrange accommodation in the host villages of Sakhalvasho and Shuamta. Local birding services — some trading under names such as Bashnya Birdwatching — handle high-mountain logistics for Kazbegi and permits for the eastern protected areas. The high Caucasus roads can be rough and snow-bound into late spring, so an early-summer visit to Kazbegi is safest. Carry a scope for the Javakheti lakes and the coast, and warm layers for the mountains in any season.

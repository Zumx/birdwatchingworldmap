Brazil lists approximately 1,900 bird species, more than any other country, spread across habitats that include the world's largest wetland, its largest tropical forest, and a substantial block of Atlantic Forest that is one of the most threatened and most endemic-rich biomes on Earth. Planning a Brazilian birding trip means choosing between these distinct ecosystems; this list covers all the major habitats. Every site is marked on the [map](/map).

## 1. Pantanal — Pixaim and Porto Jofre, Mato Grosso

The Transpantaneira road running south from Poconé to Porto Jofre provides access to the most densely wildlife-rich wetland landscape in the Americas. Hyacinth Macaws nest in the palms along the road; Jabiru storks stalk the shallow bays. The Pixaim region holds the best concentration of pousadas (lodges) with dedicated birding guides. Porto Jofre, at the southern end, is the base for jaguar boat trips, and the river banks here hold Giant Kingfisher, Sunbittern, Agami Heron, and Sungrebe. The dry season (May to September) concentrates waterbirds dramatically at permanent pools; the wet season (October to April) disperses birds across a vast inundated plain.

## 2. Cristalino Jungle Lodge, Mato Grosso

Cristalino sits on the southern edge of the Amazon Basin at the Cristalino River, on the border between Amazon and cerrado vegetation. The lodge's private reserve gives access to 620-plus species, including several specialties of the southern Amazon transition zone — Pavonine Quetzal, Bare-necked Fruitcrow, and Black-faced Cotinga. The lodge operates dedicated birding towers that rise above the canopy into the flight paths of macaws, toucans, and raptors. Accommodation is premium; advance booking is required months ahead.

## 3. Itatiaia National Park, Rio de Janeiro state

Brazil's oldest national park, established in 1937, protects the highest massif in southern Brazil. The park divides into a lower subtropical forest zone, excellent for Atlantic Forest specialties, and a high plateau above 2,000 metres with distinctive campos rupestres birdlife. The lower zone holds Mantled Hawk, Blue-winged Macaw, and a full complement of tanager and cotinga species accessible from the Agulhas Negras road. The Itatiaia high zone, reached by the paved road to the Abrigo Rebouças shelter, gives access to Diademed Tanager, Gray-backed Tachuri, and Itatiaia Thistletail.

## 4. Intervales State Park, São Paulo

Intervales is the most accessible large block of Atlantic Forest remaining in São Paulo state. The park's forests hold most of the Atlantic Forest bird families: Mantled Hawk, Giant Antshrike, White-bearded Antshrike, and several cotingas and manakins found nowhere else. The park lodge and trail infrastructure are well-maintained; guided walks start before dawn to target forest-floor species including Spotted Bamboowren and Rufous-tailed Antthrush. The best birding months are August to November.

## 5. Serra dos Tucanos Lodge, Rio de Janeiro state

Serra dos Tucanos near Cachoeiras de Macacu is the most well-organised Atlantic Forest birding operation in Brazil. Guide Ricardo Gagliardi pioneered the site's birding programme decades ago and built a lodge and trail network targeting the region's cotingas, manakins, and tanagers. Specialties include the critically endangered Black-headed Berryeater, Buff-throated Purpletuft, and the Serra Antwren. Itatiaia is easily combined in the same trip; the two sites together cover the Atlantic Forest bird list comprehensively.

## 6. REGUA — Reserva Ecológica de Guapiaçu, Rio de Janeiro state

REGUA is a private reserve in the Guapiaçu valley on the lower slopes of the Serra dos Órgãos. The reserve has replanted thousands of hectares of degraded land with native Atlantic Forest species, and the bird list has responded: 480-plus species, including a reliable population of Solitary Tinamou and Rusty-margined Guan. Wetland restoration has attracted Rufescent Tiger Heron and Least Bittern. REGUA is explicitly organised for international birders and operates affordable accommodation with trails that are safe and well-maintained.

## 7. Caraça Sanctuary, Minas Gerais

Caraça is a 19th-century seminary complex converted into a conservation reserve, set in Atlantic Forest at 1,200 metres. The monks have attracted Maned Wolves — not a bird, but transformative — to the steps of the church at dusk for decades by leaving food. The surrounding forest is excellent for Atlantic Forest specialties, but Caraça is primarily on this list because of what happens after dark: Giant Anteaters move through the grounds, Pauraque and Ocellated Poorwill call from the church steps, and Swallow-tailed Nightjar displays over the headlights of arriving guests.

## 8. Chapada dos Veadeiros, Goiás

The cerrado plateau of Chapada dos Veadeiros holds a bird community almost entirely different from the Atlantic Forest sites. Cock-tailed Tyrant displays over the open grassland; Curl-crested Jay is common in cerrado scrub; and the park is one of the better sites for Blue-winged and Red-shouldered Macaw. The annual dry-season burns (July to September) flush Helmeted Manakin and White-banded Tanager into open areas. The town of Alto Paraíso de Goiás is a well-organised base with a range of birding-oriented guides.

## 9. Folha Seca Lodge, São Paulo

Folha Seca in the Paraíba Valley between São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro is a small private lodge that has been managed for Atlantic Forest bird recovery. The proprietors have thinned bamboo to encourage forest understorey regeneration, and the results are visible in the species list: Cinnamon-vented Piha, Spot-backed Antbird, and Rufous-backed Antvireo all occur reliably. The lodge is small, informal, and genuinely birder-focused — a useful contrast to the larger operations at Serra dos Tucanos.

## 10. Itamonte — Itatiaia High Zone, Minas Gerais side

The Minas Gerais approach to Itatiaia's high plateau, accessed from the town of Itamonte, provides a quieter alternative to the busier Rio de Janeiro side. The road through the Mantiqueira range passes through Atlantic Forest transitional habitat before climbing to the high-altitude campos. Black-and-gold Cotinga occurs on the forest edge below the high zone; the plateau itself adds the range's endemic Itatiaia Spinetail and fields of Long-tailed Cinclodes.

## Planning a Brazilian birding trip

Brazil's birding circuits divide by habitat. The Pantanal circuit (Poconé–Porto Jofre) is distinct from the Atlantic Forest circuit (Serra dos Tucanos, Intervales, REGUA, Itatiaia) and the Amazon circuit (Cristalino, Manaus area, Alta Floresta). Most international birders combine the Atlantic Forest — which is most accessible from Rio de Janeiro and São Paulo airports — with either the Pantanal or the Amazon in a two-week trip.

August to November suits both the Atlantic Forest and the Pantanal: the dry season concentrates Pantanal wildlife, and Atlantic Forest birds are active and visible in the lead-up to the wet season. The critically endangered Black-faced Lion Tamarin — found only in Atlantic Forest on São Paulo's coast — inhabits the same forest as the birds at Intervales and Superagui National Park; a dedicated ferry trip from Guaraqueçaba allows combining the primate with Atlantic Forest birds along the Paraná coast. The Pantanal wet season (December to March) floods access roads and disperses waterbirds but produces spectacular raptor diversity as fish concentrate in shrinking pools.

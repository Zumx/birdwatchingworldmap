Senegal is the premier birdwatching destination in West Africa, combining exceptional species diversity with political stability, a developed tourist infrastructure, and relatively easy access from Europe. The country's position on the coast of the Atlantic just south of the Sahara places it along one of the world's most important bird migration routes — the East Atlantic Flyway — and enormous numbers of Palearctic-breeding waterbirds winter in the Senegalese wetlands and estuaries. Over 650 species have been recorded, and the country's diverse habitats range from the Sahel scrub of the north through the Senegambian savannah to the Guinea forest mosaic of the Casamance in the south.

The Gambia — politically a thin strip within Senegal — shares most of the same bird community and is frequently combined with Senegalese birding in a single tour. The region around Dakar, the Langue de Barbarie near St. Louis, the Sine-Saloum Delta, and the Casamance form the four principal birding zones, each with distinct species. Senegal's avifauna is dominated by Afrotropical savannah species in the dry season and swelled by Palearctic migrants during the November to March winter period.

## 1. Djoudj National Bird Sanctuary, St. Louis Region

Djoudj is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most important bird sanctuaries in the world, located in the Senegal River delta north of St. Louis near the Mauritanian border. The site encompasses lakes, ponds, streams, and backwater channels that provide critical wintering habitat for millions of waterbirds migrating from Europe and Siberia through the Saharan crossing. The Great White Pelican colony at Djoudj is spectacular — tens of thousands of birds breed and gather at the site. African Spoonbill, Great Egret, Black-crowned Night Heron, and Garganey winter in large numbers. White Stork gathers in enormous post-migration flocks before returning north. The boat tours through the sanctuary are one of West Africa's most celebrated wildlife experiences.

## 2. Langue de Barbarie National Park, St. Louis Region

The Langue de Barbarie is a narrow sand spit separating the Atlantic from the Senegal River estuary, and the sheltered waters behind the spit provide wintering habitat for enormous concentrations of shorebirds and terns. Royal Tern and Caspian Tern are extremely common. Mediterranean Gull winters in significant numbers. Kentish Plover and Sanderling inhabit the Atlantic beach. In the backwater lagoon, Pink-backed Pelican and White Pelican are regular. The site is close to St. Louis town, making it accessible for half-day visits. The colonial architecture of St. Louis island, combined with the bird-rich estuary, makes this one of Senegal's most atmospheric birding locations.

## 3. Sine-Saloum Delta, Fatick Region

The Sine-Saloum Delta south of Dakar is a vast mangrove and tidal creek system designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The delta's hypersaline bolon channels attract large numbers of Western Reef Heron and Goliath Heron fishing at the creek edges. Pink-backed Pelican is resident year-round. Eleonora's Falcon, which breeds in the Mediterranean, winters in the Sine-Saloum woodlands. The baobab-dotted islands within the delta hold breeding Spur-winged Lapwing and Grey Kestrel. Carmine Bee-eater is conspicuous during the summer months, perching on exposed branches. The Abéné and Missirah areas within the delta are particularly productive for forest and woodland birds.

## 4. Bandia Reserve, Thiès Region

Bandia is a private fenced reserve south of Dakar that has been managed for wildlife including reintroduced species such as White Rhinoceros and African Buffalo. The open acacia and Combretum woodland holds a good sample of Senegalese savannah birds including Beautiful Sunbird, Lavender Waxbill, and Violet-backed Starling. Western Plantain-eater is conspicuous in the fruiting trees. Striped Kingfisher inhabits the dry woodland. The reserve is particularly valuable as an accessible day trip from Dakar, allowing visitors to sample the savannah bird community without travelling to remote areas. Village Weaver builds its pendulous nests in the thorn trees at the reserve margins.

## 5. Casamance River Valley, Ziguinchor Region

The Casamance in southern Senegal holds the most diverse bird community in the country, combining Guinea savannah birds at their northernmost range limit with species shared with Guinea-Bissau and Guinea. The riverine forest along the Casamance holds White-spotted Flufftail — a secretive ground-dwelling rail heard more easily than seen — and African Piculet, the smallest woodpecker in Africa. The seasonal wetlands along the river attract long-tailed cormorants and various herons. Black-rumped Waxbill and Orange-cheeked Waxbill inhabit the grassland and crop edges. Casamance Indigobird, a brood parasite of African Firefinch, is endemic to this zone.

## 6. Saloum Delta Islands (Betenti Reserve), Fatick Region

The offshore islands and tidal channels of the Betenti section of the Saloum Delta hold breeding Caspian Tern, Royal Tern, and African Skimmer on the sandy island beaches. The Skimmer colony is particularly significant, as this globally vulnerable species has declined across much of its range. The mangrove channels hold Hamerkop and Giant Kingfisher. Woolly-necked Stork inhabits the inundated woodland during the wet season. The traditional pirogues used by local guides to navigate the delta channels provide an excellent low-disturbance method for accessing bird colonies. The combination of Saloum's cultural heritage — traditional fishing villages and ancient shell middens — with the exceptional birding makes this a compelling multi-day destination.

## 7. Somone Lagoon and Reserve, Thiès Region

The Somone Lagoon near Saly on the Petite Côte is a small but productive coastal reserve within day-trip distance of Dakar. Greater Flamingo visits the lagoon regularly, and Ringed Plover and Dunlin winter on the muddy shores. Pygmy Sunbird inhabits the acacia scrub around the lagoon margin — the male in breeding plumage is iridescent green with an extremely long tail. The nearby woodland holds Northern White-faced Owl, active at dusk around the baobab trees. The mangroves at the lagoon entrance hold Mangrove Kingfisher. The site is easily combined with the Bandia reserve for a full Thiès Region day.

## 8. Falémé River Valley, Tambacounda Region

The Falémé River valley on the Mali-Guinea border in eastern Senegal holds the most diverse savannah woodland bird community in the country. The Guinea savannah and gallery forest along the river holds Yellow-mantled Widowbird in breeding plumage during the summer, the male with its extraordinary flowing black tail. African Grey Hornbill and Abyssinian Ground Hornbill both occur. Long-crested Eagle perches on exposed dead trees at the forest edge. African Crake calls from the riparian grass during the rains. The Niokolo-Koba National Park nearby holds the last viable Chimpanzee population in Senegal and shares many of the Falémé Valley bird species.

## 9. Niokolo-Koba National Park, Tambacounda Region

Niokolo-Koba is Senegal's largest national park and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, protecting Guinea savannah, gallery forest, and the extensive floodplain of the Gambia River headwaters. The park holds the most intact large mammal community in Senegal, including Derby's Giant Eland and Lion. The bird community is correspondingly rich — over 300 species recorded — with Beaudouin's Snake Eagle and Red-throated Bee-eater among the highlights. Denham's Bustard inhabits the park's open grassy areas. Pel's Fishing Owl is resident along the larger rivers, where it hunts fish by night. The park is most productive during the dry season when animals and birds concentrate at water sources.

## 10. Cap-Vert Peninsula and Dakar Urban Areas

The Dakar peninsula itself is underrated as a birding location, with the rocky cliffs of the Cap-Vert holding breeding Eleonora's Falcon in summer and the rocky shores attracting shorebirds including Turnstone and Purple Sandpiper in winter. The Medina district gardens hold Common Bulbul and Laughing Dove in abundance. The N'Gor Island boat trip passes seabird colonies including Brown Booby. The Dakar fish market at Soumbédioune provides excellent close views of Brown Pelican, Royal Tern, and Kelp Gull. The IFAN Natural History Museum has a reference collection and useful regional field guide stocks. Dakar's urban birding serves as a warm-up exercise before travelling to the major sites north and south of the capital.

---

Senegal is one of the most beginner-friendly birding destinations in Africa, combining accessible infrastructure, reliable guide networks, and a spectacular winter concentration of Palearctic migrants with a year-round resident Afrotropical community. The optimal visiting season is November through February when both resident and visiting species are present.

Argentina is one of South America's most biogeographically diverse countries, encompassing subtropical Mesopotamia in the northeast, the Andean altiplano in the northwest, the pampas grasslands of the center, and the Patagonian steppe and Fuegian forest in the south. Over 1,000 bird species have been recorded in Argentina, including many restricted to single biomes and several IUCN-listed species. The sites below cover the country's major habitats and ornithological highlights, drawing on localities represented in the map database.

## 1. Iberá Wetlands, Corrientes

Iberá is a vast shallow-water wetland system of esteros, lagoons, and gallery forest in the province of Corrientes. The wetland complex supports Jabiru Stork (Jabiru mycteria), Comb Duck, Black-and-white Monjita, and the full suite of Neotropical waterbirds. The Taiamá area of neighboring Paraguay blends ecologically with Iberá; the combined wetland is one of the most productive in South America. Access is from the town of Mercedes and the Esteros del Iberá National Park visitors' center. The Rincón de las Aves birding site near Corrientes (documented in the map database) covers edge habitat around the esteros.

## 2. Iguazú National Park, Misiones

Iguazú combines the drama of the world's widest waterfall with subtropical Atlantic Forest in exceptional condition. Purple-winged Ground Dove (Claravis geoffroyi, Critically Endangered), Saffron Toucanet (Pteroglossus bailloni), and Spot-backed Antbird are among the Atlantic Forest specialties. The Argentine side of the park provides better forest access than the Brazilian side; the Macuco Trail and Sendero Verde are particularly productive before 8 am.

## 3. Quebrada del Condorito National Park, Córdoba

This reserve in the Córdoba sierras protects the largest known breeding colony of Andean Condor (Vultur gryphus, Vulnerable) in the country. The quebrada's open grassland and cliffs allow condors to be seen at close range from designated viewing points. Burrowing Parrot (Myiopsitta monachus), Chilean Swallow, and White-throated Hawk also occur.

## 4. Peninsula Valdés, Chubut

Peninsula Valdés is a UNESCO World Heritage Site best known for breeding Southern Right Whale (October), Orca (March-April), and Magellanic Penguin (Spheniscus magellanicus) colonies. The penguin colony at Punta Tombo (nearby on the mainland) is the largest on the South American continent. Southern Giant Petrel (Macronectes giganteus), Kelp Gull (Larus dominicanus), and Patagonian Yellow Finch are the characteristic breeding birds of the peninsula cliffs.

## 5. Los Glaciares National Park, Santa Cruz

Los Glaciares encompasses the Perito Moreno glacier and the Fitz Roy massif in the southern Patagonian Andes. The bird community reflects the Andean-Patagonian zone: Torrent Duck (Merganetta armata), Diademed Sandpiper-Plover (Phegornis mitchellii), and White-tufted Grebe (Rollandia rolland) in the lakes. The beech forest on the lower slopes holds the Magellanic Woodpecker (Campephilus magellanicus), the largest woodpecker in South America.

## 6. Tierra del Fuego National Park, Tierra del Fuego

Argentina's southernmost national park encompasses lenga beech forest, peat bogs, and coastal mudflats at the foot of the Andes. Steamer Ducks, Magellanic Oystercatcher (Haematopus leucopodus), and Imperial Cormorant (Leucocarbo atriceps) breed on the coast. The park provides the most accessible sightings of the Thorn-tailed Rayadito (Aphrastura spinicauda) and Des Murs' Wiretail (Sylviorthorhynchus desmurii) in Fuegian beech forest.

## 7. Reserva Los Tres Cerros, Entre Ríos

This private nature reserve on the Paraná River floodplain (documented in the map database) covers gallery forest and savanna habitats with the bird community of the Mesopotamian Argentina lowlands. Black-capped Warbling Finch, Strange-tailed Tyrant (Alectrurus risora, Vulnerable), and Saffron-cowled Blackbird (Xanthopsar flavus, Vulnerable) are among the grassland specialties of the Entre Ríos riverine zone.

## 8. Laguna de los Pozuelos, Jujuy

This Ramsar site at 3,600 metres on the Bolivian border is the most important Andean flamingo breeding site in Argentina. James's Flamingo (Phoenicoparrus jamesi, Near Threatened), Chilean Flamingo (Phoenicopterus chilensis, Near Threatened), and Andean Flamingo (Phoenicoparrus andinus, Vulnerable) all breed here. Puna Plover, Diademed Sandpiper-Plover, and Andean Avocet share the shallow salt lake.

## 9. Costanera Sur Ecological Reserve, Buenos Aires

An urban reserve on the Río de la Plata shoreline in the capital, Costanera Sur holds over 340 species and is accessible by public transport. Monk Parakeet (Myiopsitta monachus), the introduced urban parrot, nests in electricity pylons throughout Buenos Aires; the reserve's wetland and grass areas add marsh species. The eBird hotspot is one of the most actively used in Argentina, with daily checklists year-round.

## 10. Isla de los Pájaros, Chubut

A small island in Golfo San José within the Valdés Peninsula buffer zone (documented in the map database), Isla de los Pájaros holds colonial nesting seabirds and shorebirds in a reserve where access is restricted to protect breeding birds. Observation is by telescope from the mainland shore; the visible species include breeding Southern Giant Petrel and Imperial Cormorant.

## Planning an Argentina birding trip

Argentina's vast size — the eighth-largest country in the world — means that a productive birding trip requires a clear regional focus. The three most distinct birding regions are the subtropical northwest (Jujuy and Salta, accessible from Salta city), the Pampas and Chaco lowlands (Buenos Aires province and Formosa, accessible from Buenos Aires), and Patagonia (from Bahía Blanca south to Tierra del Fuego).

Buenos Aires province provides the most accessible birding from the international gateway. The Río Paraná delta north of Buenos Aires city holds the Paraná forest community and excellent marsh birding for Chestnut Seedeater, Strange-tailed Tyrant (Alectrurus risora, Vulnerable), and the endemic Greater Rhea-associated grassland community of the Pampas. Costanera Sur ecological reserve in Buenos Aires city centre is documented as one of the most species-rich urban reserves in South America, with over 350 species recorded within the city limits.

The northwest — the Yungas cloud forests of Jujuy and Salta — holds the most concentrated Argentine endemic community. The Red-faced Guan (Penelope dabbenei, Vulnerable), Tucuman Amazon (Amazona tucumana, Endangered), and the full Andean foothills bird community are accessible from Salta city. The Calilegua National Park and the Cachi highlands are both within three hours of Salta.

Patagonian birding focuses on the Atlantic coast penguins (Punta Tombo, Valdés Peninsula), the Andean steppe raptors, and the subantarctic forest community of Tierra del Fuego. The Magellanic Penguin colony at Punta Tombo holds over 400,000 pairs and is accessible from Trelew airport. Ushuaia at the southern tip of Tierra del Fuego provides access to the Beagle Channel seabirds, Kelp Goose (Chloephaga hybrida), and Rock Shag (Leucocarbo magellanicus).

Buenos Aires Ezeiza airport serves direct flights from most major international hubs. Domestic flights from Buenos Aires to Salta, Trelew, and Ushuaia connect to the three main birding regions.

## Patagonian birding logistics

The Punta Tombo Penguin Reserve in Chubut province is the most visited natural site in Argentine Patagonia. The reserve is open from September to March (the Magellanic Penguin breeding season); a two-hour drive from Trelew airport provides straightforward access. During the peak breeding season (October-November), over 400,000 penguin pairs are present, and visitors walk among the nesting burrows on marked pathways. The nearby Valdés Peninsula (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) adds Southern Right Whale, Orca, and a diverse shoreline bird community to the Chubut province circuit.

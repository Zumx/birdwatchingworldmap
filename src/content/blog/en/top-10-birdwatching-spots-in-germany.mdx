Germany holds over 500 bird species and a network of federal and state nature reserves that provide excellent public access to every major habitat type. The country sits at the junction of the East Atlantic and Central European migration routes, making it one of the most productive migration-watching countries in Europe during spring and autumn. The Rhine delta system, the North Sea Wadden coast, and the lower Oder Valley wetlands are internationally designated sites holding species that are rare or absent elsewhere in Central Europe. The map database includes numerous observation infrastructure points — birdwatching hides and towers — in the Rhine floodplain area near Alsace.

## 1. Nationalpark Wattenmeer, Schleswig-Holstein and Hamburg

Germany's section of the Wadden Sea National Park covers 4,430 square kilometres of the most ecologically productive intertidal zone in Europe. Bar-tailed Godwit, Dunlin, Knot, and Eurasian Curlew use the tidal flats in their millions during migration; Avocet and Common Tern breed on the saltmarsh islands. The Hallig islands — small elevated marsh islands in the Wadden — hold breeding populations of waders and terns inaccessible from the mainland. Regular ferry services from Husum and List reach the outer islands.

## 2. Vorpommern Lagoon Landscape and Rügen, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern

The Baltic coast around Rügen and the Greifswalder Bodden lagoon system holds the largest Crane (Grus grus) roost in Western Europe each October. Up to 70,000 cranes gather in the Günzer See area and the Rügen preening grounds before continuing to Spain and France. White-tailed Eagle (Haliaeetus albicilla, Least Concern in recovery) breeds in the mature beech forests of Rügen, which are designated as UNESCO World Heritage old-growth beech forest.

## 3. Bayerischer Wald (Bavarian Forest National Park), Bavaria

Germany's first national park, established in 1970, protects the largest contiguous tract of old-growth forest in Central Europe. Three-toed Woodpecker (Picoides tridactylus), Tengmalm's Owl (Aegolius funereus), Pygmy Owl (Glaucidium passerinum), Nutcracker (Nucifraga caryocatactes), and Hazel Grouse (Tetrastes bonasia) represent the boreal forest community at its most intact in Germany. The park is managed as a wilderness reserve; fallen trees are left, and the forest structure supports species that cannot tolerate managed woodland.

## 4. Berchtesgaden National Park, Bavaria

Germany's only Alpine national park abuts the Austrian border and provides access to the full mountain bird community: Golden Eagle (Aquila chrysaetos), Alpine Chough (Pyrrhocorax graculus), Wallcreeper (Tichodroma muraria), Citril Finch (Carduelis citrinella), and Water Pipit (Anthus spinoletta). The Watzmann massif and Königssee valley hold some of the finest mountain scenery in Germany alongside consistently productive birding from valley floor to alpine meadow.

## 5. Rhine Floodplain, Baden-Württemberg and Rhineland-Palatinate

The Rhine floodplain oxbow lakes and floodplain forests between Freiburg and Mainz provide habitat for Middle Spotted Woodpecker (Leiopicus medius), White Stork (Ciconia ciconia), and Eurasian Penduline Tit (Remiz pendulinus). The Bischwald observation area near Strasbourg (documented in the map database, including several named observation posts) provides hide access to the Rhine floodplain fauna on the French-German border.

## 6. Müritz National Park, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern

Müritz, centered on Germany's largest inland lake, holds White-tailed Eagle, Greater Spotted Eagle (Clanga clanga, Vulnerable), and Common Crane breeding in the surrounding mire forest. The lake complex provides wintering habitat for Smew (Mergellus albellus), Goldeneye (Bucephala clangula), and Goosander (Mergus merganser). The national park's trail network accesses both lake shore and bog habitats.

## 7. Diepholzer Moorniederung, Lower Saxony

This peatland complex in Lower Saxony holds one of Germany's most important Black Grouse (Lyrurus tetrix) populations and significant breeding Snipe (Gallinago gallinago), Curlew (Numenius arquata), and Lapwing (Vanellus vanellus) — all declining farmland waders in Western Europe. The site is managed by NABU with extensive raised viewing platforms to minimize disturbance.

## 8. Neusiedler See (German side), Burgenland

The German corner of Lake Neusiedl, though primarily Austrian, extends across the border and the Bavarian section of the lake's Hungarian steppe habitat holds Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable) on the Hanság grassland. The Seewinkel area provides the most important remaining Great Bustard population in central Europe.

## 9. Elbe-Auen, Saxony-Anhalt

The Elbe floodplain near Dessau preserves some of the most intact river meadow habitats in Germany. White Stork, Black Stork (Ciconia nigra), Corncrake (Crex crex), and Aquatic Warbler (Acrocephalus paludicola, Vulnerable) breed in the flood meadows and riverside forest. The UNESCO Biosphere Reserve Flusslandschaft Elbe covers the key area.

## 10. Nationalpark Kellerwald-Edersee, Hesse

Kellerwald protects an extensive area of old-growth beech forest in central Germany, part of the UNESCO Heritage ancient beech forest network. Lesser Spotted Eagle (Clanga pomarina), Middle Spotted Woodpecker, and Bechstein's Bat share the old-growth habitat. The forest's combination of mature trees and extensive woodland floor makes it one of Germany's best sites for spring woodland birding.

## Planning a Germany birding trip

Germany's central position in Europe and its well-developed rail and road network make it an ideal base for combining German birding sites with adjacent countries. Several of the most productive German sites — the Wadden Sea, the Upper Rhine floodplain, the Saxon Elbe Sandstone mountains — are shared ecosystems with the Netherlands, France, and the Czech Republic, and combining cross-border sites in a single trip maximizes diversity.

The Wadden Sea National Park (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) stretches along the entire German North Sea coast from the Dutch border to the Danish border, protecting the most productive intertidal zone in Europe. The tidal-flat viewpoints at Neuharlingersiel, the Hallig islands, and the Sylt Wadden are all accessible by car from Hamburg or from the Nordfriesland ferry network. The North Frisian island of Helgoland — a remarkable offshore rock in the middle of the North Sea — provides one of the best autumn migration traps in Central Europe, with tens of thousands of passerines concentrated on the island during October easterly winds.

The Rhine floodplains between Karlsruhe and Basel, protected in the Naturschutzgebiet Taubergießen and the Kaiserstuhl hills, hold the most complete Central European lowland floodplain community. Black Stork, White Stork, Middle Spotted Woodpecker, and Kingfisher breed in the Rhine gallery forest; the Kaiserstuhl's warm slopes hold Serin (Serinus serinus) and Ortolan Bunting (Emberiza hortulana, Near Threatened).

Munich provides the most efficient base for accessing both the Bavarian Alpine foothills (Berchtesgaden for Wallcreeper, Nutcracker, and Alpine Chough) and the Danube marshes east of the city. The Ismaninger Teich reservoir north of Munich holds one of the highest concentrations of breeding diving ducks in Germany — Ferruginous Duck, Tufted Duck, Pochard — in a suburban location accessible by public transport.

Frankfurt and Munich airports serve as the main entry hubs, with the German rail network connecting to all major birding regions within three hours.

## The Rhine Valley and central Germany

The Rhine Valley's role as a continental migration corridor produces excellent autumn raptor watching between Frankfurt and Cologne, where Red Kite (Milvus milvus) winters in large numbers in the Rhineland agricultural landscape. The Nationalpark Eifel in Nordrhein-Westfalen — west of Cologne — holds Black Stork, Peregrine, and middle-spotted woodpecker in a relatively intact mid-altitude forest landscape. The Federsee moor in Baden-Württemberg (two hours south of Stuttgart) is one of the most important Black-necked Grebe (Podiceps nigricollis) breeding sites in Germany and a productive spring migration staging area for marsh birds.

There are few places in Britain where the act of migration feels as physically present as it does at Spurn. A slender finger of sand and shingle reaching three and a half miles into the mouth of the Humber Estuary on the East Yorkshire coast, Spurn is barely a few dozen metres wide in places, with the grey North Sea on one flank and the brown tidal estuary on the other. On the right October morning, with a brisk easterly wind and low cloud, the bushes fairly drip with newly arrived migrants and the sky overhead streams with thrushes pouring in off the sea. For British birders, Spurn is shorthand for the drama of autumn migration.

## Why the geography concentrates birds

Spurn works because of its shape and position. Migrating birds moving down the North Sea coast in autumn are reluctant to cross open water, so they funnel along the land. The narrowing peninsula gathers them into an ever-tighter corridor, and the tip forces a decision: cross the Humber mouth or turn back. The result is concentration. Visible migration, or vismig, can be spectacular at the narrow neck, where streams of Meadow Pipits, finches, larks, and hirundines pass low overhead in their thousands on a good morning. The same geography that funnels common migrants also delivers the rarities that make Spurn famous.

## The magic of the fall

The word every Spurn regular waits to use is fall. When migrants crossing the North Sea at night meet poor visibility, headwinds, or rain, they make landfall en masse at first light, and Spurn's limited cover — the sea buckthorn, the bramble, the gardens of the few houses — fills with grounded birds. In the classic conditions of an easterly airflow with rain over the southern North Sea in late September or October, the bushes can hold Redwings, Song Thrushes, Robins, Goldcrests, and Bramblings in extraordinary numbers, with scarcer migrants like Yellow-browed Warblers and Red-breasted Flycatchers among them. A big fall is one of the genuine wonders of British birding.

## Drift migrants and rarities

Spurn's reputation rests above all on rarity. Easterly winds that displace migrants from Scandinavia and beyond — so-called drift migration — carry vagrants far off course, and the spit's position and observer coverage mean these birds get found. Spurn's records read like a roll-call of birding legend: Pallas's Warblers, Olive-backed Pipits, and the occasional true Siberian extreme such as a Pied Wheatear or a Siberian Stonechat. Autumn after autumn, scarce drift migrants from the near Continent and genuine eastern vagrants turn up, and the constant presence of skilled observers ensures the bushes are checked thoroughly.

## The seawatching dimension

Spurn is not only about landbirds. The tip and the seawatching hides give one of the finest vantage points on the English east coast for watching seabird passage. In strong onshore north or northeasterly winds, especially in autumn, a seawatch can produce streaming Gannets, skuas of several species harrying terns, shearwaters, Little Auks late in the season, and the chance of a Sabine's Gull or a Leach's Storm Petrel driven inshore by a gale. Wrapped against the wind in the hide, watching birds battle past just offshore, is a different but equally addictive face of Spurn birding.

## The observatory and a century of records

Spurn Bird Observatory was founded in 1945 and has maintained systematic recording ever since, making it one of the cornerstones of British migration study. Daily logs, ringing, and visible migration counts have built a dataset that documents long-term changes in timing and abundance across decades. The observatory provides accommodation for visiting birders and remains the social and scientific heart of the place. The Yorkshire Wildlife Trust manages much of the peninsula as a nature reserve, and the Spurn Discovery Centre near the base offers an introduction for newcomers. Continuous coverage is what turns good geography into a great list.

## A changing, dynamic landscape

Spurn is restless. In December 2013 a powerful tidal surge breached the peninsula, washing away the road and turning the point into a tidal island cut off at high water. The spit is a naturally mobile feature, constantly reshaped by longshore drift and storms, and the breach has changed how birders access the tip — much of the walk is now done on foot, with safe-crossing times dictated by the tide. This dynamism is part of Spurn's character; it is a living, shifting landform, and the experience of birding it is bound up with reading the sea and the weather as much as the birds.

## Working the peninsula on foot

Birding Spurn well means knowing how to work its length. The Canal Zone and the Crown and Anchor area near the base hold cover and gardens that grounded migrants favour, and many of the best finds come from patiently checking these bushes rather than walking straight to the point. The Warren, the Triangle, and the Kilnsea wetlands each pull in different birds — waders and wildfowl on the pools, pipits and chats on the open ground, warblers and flycatchers in the scrub. Local birders move methodically from one patch to the next, listening for calls overhead and checking every bush, because at Spurn a scarce migrant can be in any patch of cover at any time. The walk to the point and back is long and exposed, so most plan their route around the tide and the wind rather than simply marching to the tip.

## Visiting and timing

The prime season is autumn, from late August through November, with the peak for falls and rarities falling in late September and October. Spring brings its own passage, lighter but rewarding, and even winter holds wildfowl, waders, and the chance of a Shorelark or Snow Bunting on the shore. Come prepared for exposure: there is little shelter, the wind is relentless, and the walk to the point and back is long. Check tide times before walking out, dress for a North Sea gale whatever the forecast, and give yourself a full day. On the right morning, you will understand why generations of birders make the pilgrimage.

Jordan is one of the smallest countries in the Middle East, but its position at the junction of three major biogeographical zones — the Mediterranean, the Irano-Turanian desert, and the Afrotropical region — gives it a bird list disproportionate to its size. Over 430 species have been recorded. More significantly, Jordan lies along the Great Rift Valley migration corridor, one of the most heavily trafficked bird migration routes in the world, with hundreds of millions of raptors, storks, pelicans, and passerines funnelling through the region each spring and autumn. The diversity of habitats within a compact country — Mediterranean woodland in the north, arid steppe in the central plateau, sandstone desert in the south, the Dead Sea depression, and the Red Sea coast at Aqaba — provides exceptional variety.

Royal Society for the Conservation of Nature (RSCN) manages several protected areas specifically open to wildlife tourism, with hides, trails, and accommodation that make independent birding straightforward. The country is politically stable and accessible, making it an easy first birding destination in the Middle East for visitors unfamiliar with the region.

## 1. Azraq Wetland Reserve, Eastern Jordan

Azraq is the only permanent freshwater body in the entire eastern desert of Jordan, making it one of the most important oases for migratory waterbirds on the Eastern Mediterranean flyway. The wetland was nearly destroyed by water extraction in the 1980s but has been partially restored through RSCN management. In spring migration — March through May — the reedbeds, open pools, and surrounding scrub hold extraordinary concentrations of migrants. Whiskered Tern, Shoveler, Garganey, and Marsh Harrier are among the species regularly present. Breeding species include Marbled Duck — globally vulnerable — and Little Crake. The reserve hides give excellent views over the open water, and the visitor facilities are among the best in Jordan.

## 2. Wadi Rum Protected Area, Aqaba Governorate

The spectacular sandstone desert of Wadi Rum holds a desert bird community centred on the rocky wadis and open plains. Hume's Wheatear — a pale wheatear of rocky desert habitat with a specific preference for sandstone cliffs — is resident and one of the site's target species. Hooded Wheatear inhabits the cliff bases. Lichtenstein's Sandgrouse drinks at desert water points at dawn, and flocks can be conspicuous in early morning. Trumpeter Finch is common in the gravelly desert plains, and small parties feed on fallen seeds around the wadi floors. Eagle Owl nests on the sandstone towers. Night visits with a local guide produce a desert atmosphere unlike any other birding experience, with Pharaoh Eagle-Owl audible after dark.

## 3. Dana Biosphere Reserve, Tafilah Governorate

Dana is Jordan's largest protected area, covering a dramatic landscape of cliff and canyon descending from the highland plateau to the Wadi Araba below. The altitudinal range — over 1500 metres — creates multiple distinct habitats within one site. The Mediterranean-influenced woodland at the top holds Syrian Woodpecker and Sombre Tit. The cliff faces hold breeding Bonelli's Eagle and Long-legged Buzzard. The desert floor of the wadi produces Scrub Warbler and Blackstart. Sin-Sin desert plateau near the reserve boundary is one of the most reliable sites in Jordan for Houbara Bustard. Griffon Vulture can be seen soaring above the cliffs on thermals. The RSCN guesthouse at Dana village provides accommodation within the reserve.

## 4. Aqaba Area and Gulf of Aqaba Coast

Aqaba at the northern tip of the Red Sea is Jordan's only coastal city and sits at the head of a narrow arm of the Red Sea shared with Saudi Arabia, Israel, and Egypt. The position makes it exceptional for seabirds, shorebirds, and migrants arriving from Africa across the sea. White-eyed Gull is a Red Sea speciality, with a restricted distribution, and is regularly seen from the waterfront. Osprey is resident year-round on the rocky promontories. Brown Booby roosts on the buoys in the bay. The Aqaba Bird Observatory south of the town was established specifically to study migration through the site, and its autumn ringing records document enormous raptor passage — Honey Buzzard and Levant Sparrowhawk are among the most numerous species.

## 5. Dibeen Forest Reserve, Jerash Governorate

Dibeen protects one of the last remaining patches of Aleppo Pine forest in Jordan. The woodland habitat, in the northern highlands, is the most reliable site in the country for Persian Squirrel — a mammal curiosity — and ornithologically it holds Syrian Woodpecker, Eurasian Sparrowhawk, and Short-toed Treecreeper. In spring, migrant passerines pass through the forest, including Pied Flycatcher, Common Redstart, and various warblers. The understory scrub is occupied by Cetti's Warbler and Blackcap year-round. The reserve is a pleasant half-day trip from Amman. Honey Buzzard and Booted Eagle are recorded as migrants in May.

## 6. Mujib Biosphere Reserve, Madaba Governorate

The Mujib gorge cuts through the central Jordanian plateau and descends steeply into the Dead Sea. The gorge itself, with permanent flowing water and dense riparian vegetation, holds a microhabitat entirely different from the surrounding steppe. Little Green Bee-eater is conspicuous along the lower canyon walls. White-throated Robin passes through on spring migration, and the canyon scrub holds Rufous-tailed Rock Thrush. Verreaux's Eagle — more characteristic of Africa and southwestern Arabia — has been recorded on the higher crags above the reserve. Tristram's Starling is common on the rocky slopes. The RSCN manages boat trips and guided wading excursions through the lower gorge.

## 7. Jordan Valley and Balqa Highlands

The northern Jordan Valley, where the Jordan River flows into the Dead Sea, is a key raptor migration corridor and an important wintering area for raptors and larks. In winter, large numbers of Greater Spotted Eagle and Imperial Eagle hunt over the valley floor. Long-eared Owl roosts communally in the citrus groves. The agricultural land attracts enormous flocks of Common Starling and mixed finch flocks. The hillsides above the valley on both sides hold breeding Palestine Sunbird — the characteristic small nectarivore of the eastern Mediterranean scrub. Dead Sea salt flats near the water's edge attract Kentish Plover and Little Stint during migration.

## 8. Yarmouk Nature Reserve, Northern Jordan

The Yarmouk River forms the northern border of Jordan with Syria, and the reserve along its lower gorge holds the most Mediterranean-influenced flora and fauna in the country. This is the most accessible location in Jordan for Eurasian Roller — nesting in old orchard trees in the valley — and for both species of Syrian Woodpecker and Middle Spotted Woodpecker. The dense valley scrub produces good numbers of Nightingale on spring migration, and the open garrigue on the plateau above the gorge holds Crested Lark and Corn Bunting. Access from Irbid is straightforward.

## 9. Burqu Castle Area, Eastern Desert

The basalt desert around the ancient Roman-era castle of Burqu in the far northeast of Jordan holds a steppe bird community of the Irano-Turanian zone. The site is remote but rewarding for birders interested in desert specialities. Cream-coloured Courser and Macqueen's Bustard are recorded from the flat gravel plains. The seasonal Qa Burqu' pool, when filled with winter rainfall, attracts waterfowl and wading birds in numbers remarkable for such an arid location. Bar-tailed Desert Lark and Desert Lark are resident year-round. The basalt rock fields hold Red-tailed Wheatear during winter. Permits and logistics require advance coordination.

## 10. Shaumari Wildlife Reserve, Eastern Jordan

Adjacent to Azraq, Shaumari is primarily managed for reintroduced large mammals — Arabian Oryx, Onager, and ostrich — but the open grassland and scattered acacia scrub provide habitat for Cream-coloured Courser, Desert Finch, and sandgrouse. Arabian Warbler inhabits the acacia scrub, at or near its northern range boundary in Jordan. In winter, Short-toed Lark and Calandra Lark flock on the open plains. Pallid Harrier hunts over the reserve in winter, quartering the grassland in the characteristic low buoyant flight. The reserve is managed for ecotourism, and entrance fees support the RSCN mammal reintroduction programme.

---

Jordan's spring migration window — mid-March to mid-May — is exceptional, particularly for raptors, pelicans, and storks riding thermals northward through the Rift Valley. Autumn passage is also dramatic, while winter brings Central Asian raptors and larks to the eastern steppe.

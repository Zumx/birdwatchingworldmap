Ecuador's position on the equator, combined with the Andes dividing the country into Pacific slope, inter-Andean valleys, and eastern slope leading to the Amazon basin, creates a density of habitat transitions unmatched anywhere else. The result is a species-per-square-kilometre ratio that makes Ecuador the most efficient birding destination on Earth. A two-week itinerary in western and eastern Ecuador can produce 500 to 700 species if planned well. All sites are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Mindo Cloudforest, Pichincha

Mindo is the most accessible cloud forest destination from Quito: a two-hour drive on the old road over the Nono-Mindo pass. The combination of private reserves, bed-and-breakfast operations with feeders, and the Río Nambillo drainage produces over 400 species within a ten-kilometre radius of the village. Cock-of-the-Rock (the Andean Cock-of-the-Rock lek on the Mindo-Nambillo road), Torrent Duck on the river, and an extraordinary hummingbird diversity at feeder stations make Mindo the first site most visitors to Ecuador stay at. Bird Mindo Lodge and Yellow House are the established base options.

## 2. Tandayapa Lodge, Pichincha

Tandayapa sits above Mindo at 1,700 metres on the western Andean slope, targeting the species that require slightly higher elevation. Plate-billed Mountain Toucan, Golden-headed Quetzal, and Club-winged Manakin are the signature species; the feeder hummingbirds include Velvet-purple Coronet and Purple-bibbed Whitetip at close range. The lodge's feeder terrace is one of the most photographed spots in Neotropical bird photography, justifiably. The club-winged manakin's mechanical wing-song display is visible from the forest trails adjacent to the lodge.

## 3. Sani Lodge, Napo (Amazon)

Sani is a community-owned ecolodge on a lake in the Napo drainage, a three-hour motorcanoe journey from the town of Coca. The lodge operates inside Yasuni Biosphere Reserve, the most biodiverse area in the Western Hemisphere by most measures. The Amazon bird community here is at peak diversity: over 500 species have been recorded on lodge-area checklists. Harpy Eagle is occasional; the clay lick (collpa) at Sani attracts macaws and parakeets in spectacular morning numbers. The community ownership model means local guides are thoroughly integrated with the operation.

## 4. Mashpi Lodge, Pichincha

Mashpi is a high-end reserve and lodge in the Chocó Megadiversity Hotspot at 900 metres. The Chocó — the wet Pacific slope from Panama through Colombia into northwestern Ecuador — holds extraordinarily high plant and bird endemism due to a combination of geological stability and extreme rainfall. Mashpi's concession covers 3,000 hectares of largely intact Chocó forest; specialties include Long-wattled Umbrellabird, Banded Ground Cuckoo (one of the rarest birds in the world), and dozens of Chocó endemics not found elsewhere in Ecuador.

## 5. Yanacocha Reserve, Pichincha

Yanacocha, at 3,400 to 4,000 metres above Quito on the western Andes slope, holds the combination of Andean Cock-of-the-Rock leks in the forest edge below the páramo, and the high Andean specialties of the páramo itself. Rainbow-bearded Thornbill, Black-breasted Puffleg, and Viridian Metaltail hummingbirds attend the flowering plants at the reserve entrance. The Golden-breasted Puffleg, one of Ecuador's most endangered endemics, has been recorded here. Jocotoco Conservation Foundation manages Yanacocha; a feeder station makes the high-altitude hummingbirds exceptionally accessible.

## 6. Antisana Ecological Reserve, Napo / Pichincha

Antisana at 3,000 to 4,800 metres gives access to the Andean Condor (Vultur gryphus) on a reliable basis. Condors roost on the cliffs above the Mica-Antisana road and are regularly visible from the road at dawn. The páramo around the volcano is home to Carunculated Caracara, Ecuadorian Hillstar, and the Andean Lapwing; Andean Condors soaring over a 5,700-metre snow-capped volcano is one of the most dramatic birding experiences in South America.

## 7. Cabañas San Isidro, Napo

San Isidro at 2,000 metres on the eastern Andean slope has become famous for one species: the Giant Antpitta (Grallaria gigantea), which a local guide trained to attend an earthworm feeding station in the cloud forest below the lodge. The lodge sits in a rich transitional zone where highland and subtropical species overlap; Lanceolated Monklet, Colombian Screech Owl, and Chestnut-bellied Cotinga are recorded alongside highland tanagers. The combination of extraordinary amphibian diversity and strong bird list makes this a destination in its own right beyond the antpitta.

## 8. Wildsumaco Lodge, Napo

Wildsumaco occupies the 1,200 to 1,800 metre transitional zone below the San Isidro area, focusing on subtropical eastern Andean species. The lodge is run by dedicated birders and offers feeder areas, night walks, and guided forest trails. Key species include Royal Sunangel, Napo Sabrewing, Orange-throated Tanager, and the range-restricted Rufous-headed Woodpecker. The position between subtropical forest and premontane forest gives access to a species complement that neither lower Amazon lodges nor higher highland sites can replicate.

## 9. Bellavista Lodge, Pichincha

Bellavista is a cloud forest lodge at 2,200 metres on the old Calacalí-Mindo road — one of the oldest birding lodges on the western slope and still among the most productive. Plate-billed Mountain Toucan breeds in the forest above the lodge; Toucan Barbets attend the feeders at dawn. The primary forest within the Bellavista 700-hectare private reserve gives access to species that forest-edge sites miss: Scaled Antpitta, White-faced Nunbird, and Barred Hawk. Three nights here combined with Mindo and Tandayapa covers the western slope cloud forest comprehensively.

## 10. Maquipucuna Reserve, Pichincha

Maquipucuna at 1,200 to 2,700 metres near Nanegalito is a community-managed reserve whose altitude range compresses several elevational zones into a single property. The reserve has documented Spectacled Bear activity on its trail cameras and holds a bird list including the Spectacled Bristle-Tyrant, Brown-banded Antpitta, and Sickle-winged Guan. The feeder area at the research station targets hummingbirds; the forest trails below give access to the frugivore community that the higher elevation reserves miss.

## Planning an Ecuador birding trip

Ecuador divides into three major birding circuits that can be combined in a two-week trip: the western slope and Chocó (Mindo, Tandayapa, Mashpi, Bellavista, Maquipucuna), the highlands and inter-Andean valley (Yanacocha, Antisana, Papallacta Pass), and the eastern slope and Amazon (San Isidro, Wildsumaco, Napo-area lodges including Sani). A single week on the western slope alone typically produces 300 to 400 species.

January to April is generally the best time for the western slope; the eastern slope is productive year-round, though October through December has the best mix of breeding activity and migrant passage. The Napo Wildlife Center — a community-owned lodge deeper in the Yasuni Biosphere Reserve than Sani Lodge — operates its own clay lick and adds Wire-tailed Manakin and Plum-throated Cotinga to the species available in the Napo drainage; for maximum Amazon species, combining both lodges in one itinerary is the recommended approach. The high páramo is best in the dry months of June and July when the ground is firm and the raptors are active.

Ecuador's guide network centred on Mindo and Quito is excellent. Numerous specialist birding lodges provide packages with local guide expertise included. The [map](/map) shows all ten sites in geographic relationship — planning a route from west to east takes maximum advantage of the altitudinal gradients that create the species diversity.

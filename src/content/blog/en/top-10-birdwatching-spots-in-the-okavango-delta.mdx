The Okavango Delta is a hydrological anomaly: an inland delta that drains not to the sea but into the Kalahari sands, where the water evaporates and is absorbed by vegetation. The Okavango River rises in the Angolan highlands and flows southeast into Botswana, spreading across a fan-shaped inland basin to create a seasonal wetland of roughly 20,000 square kilometres at peak flood. The flooding is counterintuitive — it peaks in June and July, the dry season, because rains fall in the Angolan headwaters months before the water arrives downstream. The result is a wetland that expands precisely when surrounding savanna is driest, making it a magnet for wildlife across Botswana, Namibia, and Zimbabwe.

For birders, the delta supports over 550 species including many waterbirds, raptors, and woodland species found throughout southern Africa. The African Fish Eagle is ubiquitous, its call the defining sound of the Okavango. But the delta also holds the globally threatened Slaty Egret, African Skimmer, and Wattled Crane, along with a wealth of kingfishers, bee-eaters, and rollers. Most access is through safari camps operating traditional mokoro (dugout canoe) and boat excursions. The following ten locations and areas represent the most consistently productive birding in and around the delta.

## 1. Moremi Game Reserve, Khwai Area

The Khwai River at the eastern boundary of Moremi Game Reserve flows through permanent floodplain woodland and produces exceptional birding year-round. The roadside trees along the Khwai community campground area regularly hold nesting Marico Flycatcher, Brown-crowned Tchagre, and Racket-tailed Roller. The river itself supports African Skimmer from July through October, skimming low over the water surface during dawn and dusk feeding flights. Collared Palm Thrush is found in the wild date palms along the bank. The combination of floodplain birds and dry woodland species accessible on a single game drive makes Khwai one of the highest-productivity birding areas in the delta.

## 2. Chief's Island and the Moremi Interior

Chief's Island is the largest island in the delta and forms the core of Moremi Game Reserve. The interior woodland of mopane and mixed acacia supports Bradfield's Hornbill, which reaches the western edge of its range here. Arnot's Chat inhabits the dense mopane scrub. Verreaux's Eagle-Owl roosts in large trees during the day and is one of the most sought-after nocturnal species. The floodplain edges of the island hold Wattled Crane in pairs or small groups. Access is primarily by light aircraft or from mobile camps, and the low visitor density makes encounters more intimate than in vehicle-accessible areas.

## 3. Jao and Tubu Area, Northwestern Delta

The Jao flats in the northwestern delta are among the most permanently flooded sections of the Okavango and support large concentrations of waterbirds during and after the annual flood. The Slaty Egret, a Botswana near-endemic of significant conservation concern, occurs on these shallow floodplains, most reliably seen wading in water less than 30 centimetres deep where it probes for small fish and invertebrates. African Pygmy Goose, perhaps the most elegantly patterned duck on the continent, is common in the lily-covered backwaters. The camps in this sector operate predominantly by boat and mokoro, giving views of Malachite Kingfisher, Pygmy Kingfisher, and African Jacana from very close range.

## 4. Gomoti Channel and Private Concessions

The Gomoti River system in the eastern delta flows through a mix of open floodplain and riparian woodland that is exceptional for kingfishers and bee-eaters. All eight southern African kingfisher species have been recorded along this system, including the Giant Kingfisher and the Pied Kingfisher in conspicuous numbers and the smaller Malachite and Half-collared Kingfishers in the densely shaded backwaters. Carmine Bee-eater nests colonially in sandy river banks from October through December, and the active colonies of hundreds of birds are among the most vivid spectacles in African ornithology. Southern Carmine Bee-eater begins nesting at Gomoti and similar sites in late October, with the colonies remaining active through early January.

## 5. Xakanaxa Lagoon, Moremi Game Reserve

Xakanaxa is a permanent lagoon within Moremi connected to the main channel system, and the trees around the campsite support a large mixed heronry. African Darter, Reed Cormorant, Grey Heron, Black-headed Heron, and Rufous-bellied Heron all nest in the leadwood trees around the lagoon. Pel's Fishing Owl — one of the most sought-after and elusive birds in Africa — roosts in the large trees along the Xakanaxa channel and is occasionally found during afternoon boat trips or guided walks near likely roost trees. White-backed Night Heron is also present in the denser vegetation. The combination of the open lagoon and dense riverside trees makes the area attractive to a wide range of species.

## 6. Maun Town and Thamalakane River

Maun is the gateway town for the Okavango and the Thamalakane River, which skirts its southern edge, provides productive riparian birding without entering the delta proper. Long-tailed Shrike, Black-winged Pratincole, and Rock Pratincole are all present along the river, and the surrounding Kalahari scrub adds species like the Shaft-tailed Whydah and Black-cheeked Waxbill. The Maun area is also a staging point for birders en route to the delta, and several lodges along the river provide a useful introduction to southern African birds before the main trip. The Thamalakane Weir concentrates waterbirds in the dry season.

## 7. Nxabega Concession, Central Delta

The Nxabega area in the central delta occupies a zone between permanent and seasonal flooding, creating habitat diversity that supports a wide variety of species. The mopane woodland behind the floodplain edge holds Arnot's Chat and African Barred Owlet. The floodplain itself is good for Wattled Crane, Saddlebilled Stork, and the rare Shoebill in very occasional records from unusual flooding years. The camp area here is positioned to give views across open water toward papyrus beds where the Chirping Cisticola, a papyrus specialist, can be heard and occasionally glimpsed. African Grass Owl has been recorded in the longer grassland at the floodplain edge.

## 8. Duba Plains

Duba in the northern delta receives among the highest seasonal flooding in the entire Okavango system and has become famous for its large lion prides. For birders, the high flood level supports unusual concentrations of herons and egrets on the edges of advancing water, with Goliath Heron, Black-headed Heron, and Intermediate Egret all foraging in the same areas. Painted Snipe occurs in the soft muddy margins as the flood recedes. The papyrus beds in the deeper channels hold the White-backed Duck and the small endemic Okavango Cisticola at the edge of its global range. African Fish Eagle occupies every elevated perch along the permanent channels.

## 9. Kwando and Linyanti Rivers

The Linyanti and Kwando rivers form the northern boundary of the Okavango-Chobe system and are technically distinct from the Okavango Delta but are ecologically continuous with it. The area produces some of the best elephant birding in Africa — large herds wade across the rivers and the disturbance creates feeding opportunities that attract dozens of species simultaneously. Southern Ground Hornbill is regularly encountered in family groups on game drives. Racket-tailed Roller, Lilac-breasted Roller, and European Roller are all present. The river borders Namibia's Caprivi Strip and receives fewer visitors than the main Okavango concessions, making it feel less crowded.

## 10. Chobe National Park, Savuti Marsh

Savuti is a semi-arid area within Chobe that contains the Savuti Marsh, a shallow seasonal marsh that floods unpredictably depending on water levels in the Savuti Channel. When flooded, it attracts enormous concentrations of waterbirds including Painted Snipe, Black-winged Stilt, and large flocks of Abdim's Stork and White Stork during spring migration. In dry conditions, the shrinking marsh edge concentrates eagles, storks, and herons. Bateleur Eagle is conspicuous, and Martial Eagle soars over the woodland savanna beyond the marsh. The combination of dry and wet habitats around Savuti gives it exceptional species diversity regardless of the season.

New Guinea is the world's second largest island and one of the most important centres of bird evolution on Earth. The island is divided politically between Papua New Guinea in the east and the Indonesian provinces of Papua and West Papua in the west, but ornithologically it forms a unified whole. Over 700 bird species are found here, of which more than 400 are endemic to the island and its offshore archipelagos. The 39 species of birds-of-paradise — arguably the most spectacular group of birds anywhere in the world — evolved their extraordinary plumages and displays here in relative isolation from placental mammal predators.

The island's central mountain spine rises to over 5,000 metres, creating an extreme altitudinal gradient from coastal mangrove and lowland rainforest through cloud forest to alpine grassland and glaciated peaks. Each zone holds its own distinct and largely endemic community. New Guinea's ornithological diversity is still being documented — new species continue to be described from remote mountain ranges, and the avifauna of the western highlands in particular remains incompletely known.

## 1. Varirata National Park, Central Province, Papua New Guinea

Varirata, easily accessible from Port Moresby, is the classic introduction to Papua New Guinea birding. The park protects lowland and foothill forest on the Sogeri Plateau, and the roadside birding along the main park trail is among the most productive in the Pacific. Raggiana Bird-of-Paradise — the national bird of Papua New Guinea — displays in large communal leks in the forest canopy and is guaranteed here on any morning visit. Manus Friarbird and Papuan Babbler are found in the forest. The view from the plateau overlooks the Coral Sea, and Pacific Swallows hawk over the edge. The proximity to Port Moresby and the quality of the birding make this an essential first stop for any Papua New Guinea birding trip.

## 2. Kumul Lodge Area, Western Highlands Province, Papua New Guinea

Kumul Lodge above Enga Province sits at cloud forest altitude on the Tomba Plateau and provides the most accessible highland birding in Papua New Guinea. The lodge maintains feeding stations for Ribbon-tailed Astrapia — a bird-of-paradise with an extraordinarily long white tail that hangs several times the length of its body — and the birds visit regularly. Superb Bird-of-Paradise is found in the forest canopy. MacGregor's Bird-of-Paradise inhabits the higher forest. Blue-backed Robin is found in the scrub near the lodge. The area is also one of the best sites for Papuan Lorikeet and Mountain Peltops. The lodge infrastructure makes this the most comfortable highland birding base in Papua New Guinea.

## 3. Tari Valley and Ambua Lodge, Southern Highlands Province, Papua New Guinea

The Tari area is the centre of the Huli people's territory and holds some of the most accessible and productive highlands birding in Papua New Guinea. Ambua Lodge sits at 2,100 metres in cloud forest that holds Stephanie's Astrapia — a bird-of-paradise found only in the Southern Highlands. Crested Satinbird occupies the mid-storey of the forest with its orange and black plumage. Blue-capped Ifrit — a small, alert insectivore with colourful plumage — is found here, carrying toxic skin compounds similar to those of poison-dart frogs. Papuan Treecreeper works the bark of old Podocarpus trees. The lek of the Superb Bird-of-Paradise provides close views from the trail behind the lodge.

## 4. Baiem Valley, Baliem Valley, West Papua, Indonesia

The Baliem Valley in the central highlands of the Indonesian side of New Guinea sits at approximately 1600 metres and is surrounded by high mountain forest. The valley itself holds Long-tailed Paradigalla and Vogelkop Bowerbird — the latter constructing elaborate stick bower structures decorated with colourful objects on the forest floor. Cinnamon Ground Dove inhabits the valley floor gardens. The forested ridges above the valley hold several species shared only with Papua New Guinea's highland avifauna. The regional capital of Wamena is accessible by air from Jayapura and serves as the base for exploration of the surrounding mountains.

## 5. Arfak Mountains, West Papua, Indonesia

The Arfak Mountains near Manokwari in the Bird's Head Peninsula of western New Guinea hold an endemic avifauna distinct from the main island's central range. The Vogelkop Lophorina — split from the Superb Bird-of-Paradise in recent taxonomic revisions — displays in the cloud forest and was the subject of a recent study showing its display dance differs fundamentally from that of its eastern relative. Arfak Astrapia inhabits the higher forest. Western Parotia performs its extraordinary cape-spreading court display on a cleared forest floor display ground. The accessible trails above Angguruk village provide one of the better entry points into the mountain's interior forest.

## 6. Nimbokrang Lowlands, West Papua, Indonesia

The lowland forests north of Jayapura around Nimbokrang are among the most productive and accessible lowland birding sites in the Indonesian half of New Guinea. The area holds Western Bowerbird building its avenue-of-sticks bower in the lowland forest. Twelve-wired Bird-of-Paradise displays from dead tree perches with its twelve long wire-like tail feathers spread sideways. Red Bird-of-Paradise — among the most spectacular of all — inhabits the forest canopy. Shovel-billed Kookaburra lives along the forested streams. Palm Cockatoo — the world's largest black cockatoo — is heard before being seen, crashing through the canopy in small groups.

## 7. Lake Murray and Fly River Plains, Western Province, Papua New Guinea

The vast, seasonally flooded plains of the Fly River and Lake Murray in Western Province hold one of the largest inland wetland bird communities in the Pacific. Black-necked Stork is common on the floodplain. Brolga — the large grey crane characteristic of Australian grasslands — reaches its New Guinea range limit here. Little Kingfisher inhabits the forested creek margins. New Guinea Flightless Rail inhabits the dense marsh vegetation but is extremely secretive. Magpie Goose is present in enormous flocks on the seasonal grasslands. The Fly River plain is difficult to access, typically requiring chartered boat travel, but represents a genuinely wild frontier landscape.

## 8. Crater Mountain Wildlife Management Area, Eastern Highlands Province, Papua New Guinea

Crater Mountain is one of the most important conservation areas in Papua New Guinea, protecting a large tract of mid-montane forest managed in partnership with local communities. The forest holds Crinkle-collared Manucode — a manucode bird-of-paradise with glossy black plumage — as well as Vogelkop Bowerbird at its eastern distribution limit. The area is also significant for several montane endemic species and serves as a research base for ornithological fieldwork. Access requires coordination with the Wildlife Conservation Society programme operating here and involves charter flight to a remote airstrip.

## 9. Karawari Lodge, East Sepik Province, Papua New Guinea

Karawari Lodge sits on a tributary of the Sepik River in the lowland forests of East Sepik Province. The lodge provides boat-based birding along the slow, tea-coloured river channels through intact lowland forest. Gurney's Eagle — one of the rarest eagles in the world, with a total population of perhaps several hundred — inhabits the lowland forest in this region and is occasionally observed soaring above the riverine trees. Shovel-billed Kookaburra is regular. The forest along the Karawari River is extremely species-rich and little-studied. Yellow-faced Myna, King Bird-of-Paradise, and Papuan Dwarf Kingfisher are regular species.

## 10. Fergusson Island and Milne Bay Region, Papua New Guinea

The islands of Milne Bay Province at the eastern tip of Papua New Guinea hold their own endemic birds distinct from the mainland. Fergusson Island holds the Fergusson Island Dwarf Kingfisher. The Milne Bay region overall is famous for its marine biodiversity, but the forest birding is equally rewarding. Streak-headed Honeyeater, Mottled Berryhunter, and Milne Bay Honeyeater are island-restricted species. Boat travel between islands provides opportunities for seabirds including Jouanin's Petrel and Swinhoe's Storm Petrel in the open Coral Sea waters. The combination of forest and marine birding makes Milne Bay a distinctive addition to a Papua New Guinea tour.

---

New Guinea's birding requires genuine expedition spirit — many of the best sites involve forest camps, charter flights to remote airstrips, and tolerance for challenging tropical conditions. The rewards are unparalleled in the Pacific, with birds-of-paradise and hundreds of forest endemics found nowhere else on Earth.

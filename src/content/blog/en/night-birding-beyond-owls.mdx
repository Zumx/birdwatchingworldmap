Most birders operate during daylight hours, and for good reason — the majority of bird species are diurnally active and most field identification is easier in natural light. But restricting birding to daylight means missing a substantial component of avian diversity and activity. A rich community of birds is active exclusively or primarily at night: nightjars hunting moths over heathland, Corncrakes calling from meadow vegetation, Water Rails squealing in reedbeds, secretive rails crossing open fields under cover of darkness, and the continuous overhead passage of millions of migrating birds that most daytime observers never observe.

Night birding requires different techniques, different preparedness, and an adjustment of expectations. It is not simply daytime birding in the dark. The sensory emphasis shifts from vision to hearing; the birds encountered are often heard rather than seen; and the experience of being in a familiar habitat at night — the difference in the soundscape, the quality of the darkness — is intrinsically rewarding in a way that cannot be replicated at any other time.

## Nightjars

Nightjars (family Caprimulgidae) are among the most distinctive nocturnal birds of temperate and tropical habitats. Their cryptic plumage renders them virtually invisible by day against bark and leaf litter, but at dusk and through the night they hunt aerial insects with a silent, buoyant flight over open woodland, heathland, and savannah.

The European Nightjar (Caprimulgus europaeus) is a summer visitor to European heathlands and pine forest edges, and its churring song — a sustained, dry trill delivered from a low perch at dusk and through the night — is among the most atmospheric sounds of the European summer. Dartmoor in Devon, the Breckland of East Anglia, and heathlands in the Netherlands are reliable sites. Males display by clapping their wings and gliding over their territories with white wing spots visible.

North America's Whip-poor-will (Antrostomus vociferus) is named for its insistent three-note call, repeated hundreds of times on summer nights in eastern woodland edges. The closely related Chuck-will's-widow occupies similar habitat in the southeast. Standard Winged Nightjar and Pennant-winged Nightjar of sub-Saharan Africa are among the most spectacular, the breeding males of both species carrying extraordinarily elongated primary feathers visible in display flights.

## Rails and Crakes

Rails and crakes (family Rallidae) have evolved a largely nocturnal or crepuscular activity pattern as a response to predation pressure. Many species are nearly impossible to observe by day, spending daylight hours deep in reedbeds, rank grass, or marsh vegetation. At night, particularly in spring when calling males are establishing territories, they become conspicuous through their vocalisations even if rarely seen.

The Corncrake (Crex crex), once widespread across European meadows, is now a conservation priority species. Males produce a harsh, rasping call, described accurately by the onomatopoeic Latin name Crex, through short summer nights from their arrival in May through July. Surviving strongholds in Ireland, Scotland's machair landscape, and in parts of Eastern Europe still provide opportunities to hear calling males. RSPB reserves at Balranald on North Uist and Oronsay in the Inner Hebrides offer the best regular access in Britain.

Spotted Crake (Porzana porzana) calls primarily at night from wetland reedbeds — a whistling weet repeated at intervals throughout the short northern summer nights. Virginia Rail in North America is similarly nocturnal in its calling behaviour. Recording equipment and a still night near a productive reedbed produces extraordinary audio even when no birds are seen.

## Nocturnal Migration

The scale of nocturnal bird migration is difficult to comprehend. On clear nights in autumn and spring, radar studies have documented millions of birds crossing any given transect of European or North American airspace overnight. The vast majority pass entirely undetected by observers on the ground. The development of nocturnal flight call recording — using permanently mounted microphones connected to recording software that captures and logs the brief calls of species as they migrate overhead — has opened a window onto this invisible movement.

Programs like the BirdCast initiative in North America and the NocMig (nocturnal migration) community in Europe coordinate amateur recorders who deploy microphones from their gardens overnight and submit recordings for species identification. A single recording session in appropriate conditions during peak migration can reveal dozens of species passing overhead: thrushes, warblers, pipits, buntings, waders, and occasional rarities calling briefly as they cross in the darkness.

The calls used by migrants in flight are often distinct from the territorial or alarm calls given by the same species in daylight, and building familiarity with nocturnal flight calls requires a specific learning effort. Resources including the Xeno-Canto database and dedicated nocmig identification guides help with this learning process.

## Owls as Context

Owls are the most familiar nocturnal birds and are the entry point for most birders exploring night birding. But even with owls, there is substantial diversity beyond the species commonly encountered. Pygmy Owls in European coniferous forests call at dusk and dawn; Long-eared Owls winter in communal roosts in farmland; Tawny Owls call from deep woodland throughout autumn and winter. Tropical forests hold Fishing Owls, which hunt fish from overhanging branches above still water, and the Spectacled Owl of Central America, which calls with a series of resonant hoots that carry surprisingly far in the dense forest understorey.

## Safety and Preparation

Night birding requires basic preparation. A headtorch with a red-light mode — which preserves night vision better than white light — is essential. Familiarity with the terrain in daylight before walking it at night prevents stumbles and allows confident movement to productive listening spots. In regions with venomous snakes or other hazards, appropriate footwear and awareness are necessary. Birding in pairs or groups is safer and more enjoyable than solo night birding in unfamiliar places.

Paraguay is the great sleeper of South American birding. Landlocked between Brazil, Argentina and Bolivia, it sits at the seam where four major biomes collide: the thorny Chaco in the west, the disappearing Atlantic forest in the east, the Cerrado savannas of the north, and the flooded grasslands feeding into the Pantanal. More than 700 bird species have been recorded, yet the country sees only a fraction of the visitors that pour into neighbouring Brazil. The waterfront viewpoints at the Mirador de Aves Bahia de Asuncion and the nearby Mirador de Aves II let a traveller start watching shorebirds within sight of the capital before heading into the wilder interior.

## 1. Bahía de Asunción and the city ecological reserve

The Bahía de Asunción, a broad bend of the Río Paraguay right beside the capital, is the easiest serious birding in the country. The Reserva Ecológica Banco San Miguel and the Mirador de Aves Bahia de Asuncion overlook mudflats and lagoons that swell with migrant shorebirds from September to March. Wilson's Phalarope, Hudsonian Godwit and Stilt Sandpiper feed alongside resident Southern Screamers and the Mirador de Aves II adds a second vantage on the wetland. After heavy rains the bay can hold thousands of waterbirds within a taxi ride of downtown.

## 2. Defensores del Chaco National Park

The largest protected area in the country, Defensores del Chaco covers more than 7,200 square kilometres of dry thorn forest and Cerro León's low limestone hills in the remote northwest. This is core dry-Chaco habitat: Black-legged Seriema, Crested Gallito, Chaco Chachalaca and the Quebracho Crested-Tinamou occur here, along with the spectacular and range-restricted Black-bodied Woodpecker. Access requires a high-clearance vehicle, fuel reserves and permits, but the reward is some of the most undisturbed Chaco birding anywhere.

## 3. Estancia La Golondrina and the Mbaracayú Forest

The Reserva Natural del Bosque Mbaracayú in Canindeyú department protects one of the best remaining blocks of Atlantic forest in Paraguay. Managed by the Fundación Moisés Bertoni, it shelters Atlantic forest specialties that are vanishing across their range: Black-fronted Piping-Guan, Bare-throated Bellbird, Helmeted Woodpecker and the diminutive Russet-winged Spadebill. The transition zone where forest meets Cerrado adds open-country species, making bird lists here unusually long for a single reserve.

## 4. San Rafael (Reserva para Parque Nacional)

San Rafael, straddling Itapúa and Caazapá departments, holds the most extensive surviving Atlantic forest in the country. It is the Paraguayan stronghold for some of South America's most threatened birds, including the Vinaceous-breasted Amazon and the Helmeted Woodpecker. Several private ranches and the Pro Cosara conservation initiative provide access and lodging. Mixed-species flocks, antbirds and tanagers move through the understorey, and dawn brings a wall of sound utterly unlike the dry west.

## 5. Estero Patiño and the Humid Chaco

Between the dry western Chaco and the river lies the Humid Chaco, a mosaic of palm savanna, marsh and seasonal lagoons. The Estero Patiño is the largest wetland in the region, drawing Jabiru, Maguari Stork, Southern Screamer and large numbers of whistling-ducks. Caraguatay palm stands hold roosting parakeets, and the open grassland fringe is good for Greater Rhea and the long-legged Red-legged Seriema.

## 6. Tinfunqué National Park

Tinfunqué protects a vast tract of seasonally flooded Humid Chaco grassland and saline lagoon in Presidente Hayes department. When the floods recede, the drying lagoons concentrate waterbirds in remarkable numbers: Roseate Spoonbill, Wood Stork, Plumbeous Ibis and an array of herons. The surrounding palm savanna supports Strange-tailed Tyrant, a striking and declining grassland bird that is one of the region's most wanted species.

## 7. Lago Ypacaraí and Areguá

Closer to Asunción, Lago Ypacaraí and the wetlands around the towns of Areguá and San Bernardino offer accessible day-trip birding. Reedbeds and lake margins hold Wattled Jacana, Many-colored Rush-Tyrant, Yellow-winged Blackbird and several rail species. The cultural towns make a comfortable base, and the surrounding low hills add forest-edge birds within easy reach of the capital.

## 8. Cerro Corá National Park

In the northeast near the Brazilian border, Cerro Corá protects Cerrado, gallery forest and dramatic sandstone hills in Amambay department. This is the place for Cerrado specialists rarely seen elsewhere in Paraguay: White-banded Tanager, White-rumped Tanager, Collared Crescentchest and Rufous-winged Antshrike. The historic site marking the end of the Triple Alliance War adds context to a visit, and the cliffs hold nesting raptors.

## 9. Ñacunday and the eastern rivers

Ñacunday National Park and the rivers of Alto Paraná department in the far east preserve patches of riverine and Atlantic forest near the Paraná. The Salto Ñacunday waterfall is a scenic anchor, and the surrounding forest holds toucans, trogons and the Surucua Trogon. Although heavily fragmented by soy agriculture, the remaining forest islands still produce strong lists for those who explore the riverbanks at dawn.

## 10. Pantanal margin near Bahía Negra

In the extreme north, the Río Paraguay opens into the southern fringe of the Pantanal near Bahía Negra and the Río Negro. Boat-based birding here reaches Hyacinth Macaw, Jabiru, Sunbittern and Agami Heron in flooded forest and marsh. This is the most logistically demanding region in the country — reached by riverboat over several days — but it delivers a genuine Pantanal experience without the crowds of the Brazilian side.

## Planning a Paraguay birding trip

Paraguay's two great seasons split the country neatly. The cooler dry months from May to September are most comfortable for the western Chaco, where summer heat can exceed 45°C and roads turn to dust or mud depending on the year. The austral spring and summer, roughly October to March, bring breeding activity, returning migrants and the swollen wetlands of the Humid Chaco and Bahía de Asunción.

Asunción is the natural hub. Silvio Pettirossi International Airport receives regional flights, and the city's own Bahía de Asunción provides immediate birding. From there the paved Ruta Transchaco runs northwest toward the dry Chaco, while routes east lead to the Atlantic forest reserves of San Rafael and Mbaracayú. A 4WD vehicle, generous fuel margins and Spanish are close to essential for the interior, and Guyra Paraguay, the national BirdLife partner, is the best source of current site access information and conservation news.

Portugal occupies the southwestern corner of Europe, where the Atlantic meets the Mediterranean-climate Iberian Peninsula. Its position gives it both the Atlantic seabird communities of its offshore archipelagos and the Mediterranean steppe and scrub species of the Alentejo and Algarve. The Tagus Estuary is one of the most important waterbird sites in Europe; the Castro Verde plains hold the highest densities of Great Bustard remaining in Portugal; Sagres is the premier autumn seawatch point on the Atlantic coast of Southern Europe. The Azores and Madeira add endemic landbirds and breeding petrels of global significance.

## 1. Tagus Estuary (Estuário do Tejo), Setubal and Lisbon

The Tagus Estuary is the largest in Western Europe and one of the most important waterbird sites in the Palearctic. Up to 150,000 waders, 10,000 flamingos, and large wintering duck populations use the estuary's 35,000 hectares of tidal mudflat. Dunlin, Avocet, Black-tailed Godwit (Limosa limosa islandica, which winters here in internationally important numbers), and Greater Flamingo are the dominant species. The Ponta da Erva viewpoint and the Alcochete saltpans provide the most accessible observation points.

## 2. Castro Verde Special Protection Area, Baixo Alentejo

The Castro Verde plains hold the largest Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable) population in Portugal and one of the most important in western Europe. The Liga para a Protecção da Natureza (LPN) manages the Castro Verde reserve specifically for the bustard and steppe-grassland bird community: Little Bustard (Tetrax tetrax, Near Threatened), Montagu's Harrier (Circus pygargus), and Black-bellied Sandgrouse (Pterocles orientalis). The plains are best visited October through April when the grass is short and birds are visible at distance.

## 3. Sagres Headland, Algarve

Sagres, the southwestern corner of Europe at Cabo de São Vicente, is the most productive seawatch point on the Southern European Atlantic coast. In autumn (October to November), Pomarine Skua (Stercorarius pomarinus), Long-tailed Skua (Stercorarius longicaudus), and Sabine's Gull (Xema sabini) pass offshore. Cory's Shearwater (Calonectris borealis), European Shearwater (Puffinus puffinus), and Wilson's Storm Petrel (Oceanites oceanicus) are regular. The headland also concentrates migrants: Bonelli's Eagle (Aquila fasciata), Merlin, and Peregrine.

## 4. Ria Formosa, Algarve

The Ria Formosa lagoon, protected as a national park, is the most important waterbird habitat in the Algarve. The tidal flat and saltpan complex supports Greater Flamingo, Little Tern (Sternula albifrons), Slender-billed Gull, and Audouin's Gull. The Purple Swamphen (Porphyrio porphyrio) is abundant in the reed beds — this is one of the sites where the species has its most visible population in Western Europe.

## 5. Azores: Corvo Island

Corvo, the smallest island in the Azores, is Europe's premier vagrant serendipity location. Every autumn (October-November), Nearctic passerine vagrants arriving on Corvo — Yellow-billed Cuckoo (Coccyzus americanus), thrushes, warblers, and sparrows from North America — are worked by birders who fly to the island specifically for the chance encounters. Corvo's geography and the prevailing westerlies make it the most reliable European location for American vagrant landbirds.

## 6. Paul de Arzila, Beira Litoral

This Ramsar wetland near Coimbra holds breeding Purple Heron, Little Egret, and Night Heron alongside the central Portugal reed-bed community. The site is one of the most accessible wetland birding sites in northern Portugal.

## 7. Castro Marim Salt Pans and Castro Marim NNR, Algarve

The Castro Marim salt works at the mouth of the Guadiana River hold one of the largest breeding Flamingo colonies in Portugal and a productive wader-staging area. Slender-billed Gull, Black-winged Stilt, and Avocet breed; Spoonbill (Platalea leucorodia) and Black Stork are regular migrants.

## 8. Madeira and its Laurisilva Forest

Madeira's laurel forest holds two endemic species: Trocaz Pigeon (Columba trocaz, Near Threatened) and Madeira Firecrest (Regulus madeirensis). The Zino's Petrel (Pterodroma madeira, Endangered) breeds only on Madeira's highest mountain, with a total population of around 170 pairs; night-time colony visits organized by Freira Conservation Project provide the only legal viewing opportunity.

## 9. Sado Estuary, Setubal

The Sado Estuary south of Lisbon holds Greater Flamingo, Avocet, and a substantial resident population of Common Bottlenose Dolphin (Tursiops truncatus). The saltpans around Alcácer do Sal provide access to breeding White Stork and a range of wetland waders.

## 10. Peneda-Gerês National Park, Minho

Portugal's only national park, in the green northwest, holds breeding Golden Eagle (Aquila chrysaetos), Eagle Owl (Bubo bubo), and the small resident Red Deer population. The park's bog and heathland community includes breeding Merlin, Golden Plover, and Red Grouse (Lagopus lagopus scotica) — a species at its southernmost range limit here.

## Planning a Portugal birding trip

Portugal occupies an unusually productive position for European birding: it is the westernmost country on the Iberian Peninsula and therefore traps vagrant birds from North America and sub-Saharan Africa before they can move further into Europe. The autumn vagrant season (September to November) at the Sagres point and Cape St. Vincent draws European and North American birders for the combination of western Palearctic endemics and Atlantic rarities.

The Alentejo region — the rolling cork oak and holm oak dehesa (montado) landscape of central Portugal — is the most productive area for the Spanish steppe endemic community that extends into Portugal: Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable), Little Bustard, Black-bellied Sandgrouse (Pterocles orientalis), and Pin-tailed Sandgrouse (Pterocles alchata) occur in the cereal plains. Évora is the logical base for the Alentejo montado, with the Castro Verde Special Protection Area representing the most important Great Bustard population in western Europe.

The Tagus Estuary in greater Lisbon provides one of the largest wintering wader and wildfowl concentrations in the western Palearctic. Greater Flamingo winters in the estuary's salt pans; Spoonbill, Avocet, and Marsh Harrier are common. The Tagus estuary's observation hides and the Sapal de Castro Marim (in the eastern Algarve) provide the best tidal-flat birding.

The Algarve's role as a winter sun destination makes it the most visited birding region for northern European visitors. Lagos Bay and Ria Formosa are productive for wintering sea ducks — Black-throated Diver (Gavia arctica) and Common Scoter are regular offshore — and the Ria Formosa network of salt pans and lagoons holds the largest regular Flamingo flock in Portugal. Cape St. Vincent and Sagres are 30 minutes west of Lagos.

Lisbon serves as the entry point for most visitors. Car rental from Lisbon allows a circuit through the Tagus Estuary, Alentejo montado, and Algarve in a seven-day trip. The Castro Verde plains are three hours southeast of Lisbon; the Serra da Malcata Lynx reserve (excellent for Bonelli's Eagle and Middle Spotted Woodpecker) is four hours northeast.

## Southwest Portugal's vagrant trap

The combination of Cape St. Vincent and the Alentejo coast forms the most significant vagrant bird trap in continental Europe. The cape's geographic position at the southwestern corner of the Iberian Peninsula — and of Europe — concentrates birds that have drifted off course from migration routes between Africa and northern Europe. In October, this concentration of displaced migrants, combined with the regular passage of Balearic Shearwater (Puffinus mauretanicus, Critically Endangered) and Cory's Shearwater offshore, makes the cape the most species-diverse Portuguese birding location in autumn. The Sines headland 40 kilometres north provides an additional seawatch point with a deeper-water offshore zone.

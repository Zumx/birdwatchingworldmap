Uruguay is small, green and gloriously easy to travel, and that makes it one of the most rewarding introductions to South American grassland birding. The country is essentially one great sweep of rolling pampas, stitched with rivers, palm savannas and a string of coastal lagoons along the Atlantic. The flagship birds are the grassland specialists: capuchino seedeaters in their many plumages, the running greater rhea, and a parade of declining pampas species that find refuge in Uruguay's cattle country. Add the shorebird-rich coast and the wetlands of the east, with viewpoints such as the Mirador de aves Camino de los Indios, and a week here delivers a deep, satisfying list.

## 1. Laguna de Rocha

A protected coastal lagoon in Rocha department, Laguna de Rocha is one of the finest shorebird and waterbird sites in the country. A narrow sandbar separates it from the Atlantic, and when it opens the salinity shifts draw flamingos, black-necked swans, coscoroba swans and large flocks of migrant sandpipers. American and Chilean flamingos feed in the shallows, and the surrounding grassland holds pampas species. Spring and autumn passage of Nearctic shorebirds is the highlight.

## 2. Laguna de Castillos and Monte de Ombúes

Also in Rocha, Laguna de Castillos shelters one of the world's largest stands of ombú, the giant herbaceous tree, in the Monte de Ombúes. The gallery and ombú woodland holds forest birds unusual for the open country around, while the lagoon itself draws waterfowl. Coypu and capybara share the marshes with herons, and the area is excellent for the secretive marsh and reedbed species of the eastern wetlands.

## 3. Esteros de Farrapos National Park

On the Río Uruguay in the northwest near Nuevo Berlín, the Esteros de Farrapos protect a maze of river islands, marshes and gallery forest. This Ramsar wetland is rich in waterbirds and riverine species, including the rare yellow cardinal in nearby grasslands. The islands hold breeding herons and ibis, and the gallery forest along the river adds species more typical of the subtropical north of the country.

## 4. Quebrada de los Cuervos

In Treinta y Tres department, the Quebrada de los Cuervos is a deep, humid ravine cutting through the hills, sheltering a relict subtropical forest unlike the surrounding grassland. The dense gallery forest holds species at the southern edge of their range, and the cliffs give the gorge its name from the black vultures that roost there. It is a protected landscape and one of the best forest-birding spots in a largely treeless country.

## 5. Cabo Polonio

The wild dune coast at Cabo Polonio in Rocha combines a sea-lion colony with excellent seawatching. From the headland, austral seabirds pass offshore: shearwaters, petrels and terns, with chances of albatross in the right winds. The scrubby dunes and lagoon behind the cape hold passerines and shorebirds, and the lack of roads keeps the place atmospheric and quiet outside the summer peak.

## 6. Bañados del Este and Laguna Negra

The vast Bañados del Este wetland complex, including Laguna Negra and the Potrerillo de Santa Teresa reserve, is a Biosphere Reserve covering marsh, palm savanna and lagoon in the east. This is prime habitat for the strange-tailed tyrant, black-and-white monjita and a host of marsh species. Greater rhea roam the open ground, and the butiá palm savannas add a distinctive Uruguayan landscape rich in seedeaters.

## 7. Santa Teresa National Park

On the Atlantic coast near the Brazilian border, Santa Teresa combines a historic colonial fortress with planted and native woodland that acts as a migrant trap. The mix of forest, coast and lagoon produces a varied list, and the gardens and arboretum concentrate passerines. It is an easy, comfortable base for exploring the eastern wetlands and the nearby coast.

## 8. Sierra de las Ánimas and the southern hills

The low granite hills of the Sierra de las Ánimas near Piriápolis, rising to around 500 metres, are among the highest ground in this gentle country. The rocky grassland and scrub hold species adapted to the hills, including raptors that hunt the slopes, and the area is good for grassland passerines. The views over the Río de la Plata add to a rewarding day within reach of Montevideo.

## 9. Mirador de aves Camino de los Indios

The Mirador de aves Camino de los Indios is a dedicated birdwatching viewpoint set in the interior grassland and rural landscape, the kind of cattle-country pampas that defines Uruguay. From here the open grassland is good for seedeaters, tyrants, the long-legged red-winged tinamou and the chance of pampas raptors quartering the fields. Viewpoints like this make the grassland birding accessible to travellers without local guides.

## 10. Montevideo coast and the Río de la Plata

Even the capital delivers. The coastal walk of the Rambla, the wetlands at Carrasco and the estuary of the Río de la Plata hold gulls, terns, grebes and migrant shorebirds within the city. The Río Santa Lucía wetlands on the western edge of Montevideo add marsh and reedbed species, making the capital itself a viable birding base for an arrival or departure day.

## Planning a Uruguay birding trip

Uruguay's mild climate makes it birdable year-round, but the seasons shape what you find. The austral spring and summer, roughly October to March, bring breeding grassland species in full song and the peak of Nearctic shorebird passage on the coast. Winter is gentle, with southern visitors and easier viewing of waterfowl on the lagoons. The country is small enough to cover its highlights in a week or ten days of driving.

Montevideo is the natural gateway, with the capital's own coast and the nearby Santa Lucía wetlands offering immediate birding. From there the eastern coastal route through Rocha reaches Laguna de Rocha, the Bañados del Este and Cabo Polonio, while the Río Uruguay route leads northwest to the Esteros de Farrapos. Roads are good, distances modest and a standard car is fine for most sites. Aves Uruguay, the national BirdLife partner, is the best source for current site information and grassland conservation news, especially for the threatened capuchino seedeaters.

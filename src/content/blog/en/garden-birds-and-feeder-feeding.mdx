Garden feeding is the most widely practiced form of birdwatching in the world. In the United Kingdom alone, more than half of all households provide supplementary food for birds, and surveys estimate that the combined supplement provided annually exceeds 150,000 tonnes — one of the most significant wildlife interventions carried out by private citizens in any country. The science on whether garden feeding is beneficial to bird populations is more nuanced than its ubiquity suggests, but the consensus is that well-managed feeding provides genuine support, particularly during the winter months and in urban environments where natural food sources are scarce.

## What to feed and when

Seed mix quality varies enormously. The cheapest mixes contain high proportions of cereal grains — wheat, millet, maize — that are attractive to pigeons and sparrows but ignored by most smaller finches and tits. A premium seed mix with sunflower hearts (dehusked sunflower seeds) as the primary component attracts the widest range of species with the least waste. Nyjer (thistle) seed in a fine-mesh nyjer feeder attracts goldfinches (Carduelis carduelis) and siskins (Spinus spinus) specifically. Peanuts in a wire mesh feeder attract nuthatches (Sitta europaea), great spotted woodpeckers (Dendrocopos major), and corvids; whole peanuts should never be offered on open trays where young nestlings could access them, as they present a choking risk.

Fat balls — compressed blocks of lard or suet with embedded seed — provide high-energy food that is particularly valuable in cold weather. Remove the plastic mesh netting before hanging; birds' feet and toes can become tangled. Loose suet pellets offered on a flat tray or ground feeder attract robins (Erithacus rubecula) and blackbirds (Turdus merula) that are reluctant to use hanging feeders.

Fresh water for drinking and bathing is at least as important as food and is often neglected. A shallow dish or birdbath, refilled daily and kept ice-free in winter (use warm water, never antifreeze or salt), attracts species that never use feeders. In summer, a clean water source may be the single most effective garden intervention for bird welfare.

## Feeder hygiene: the non-negotiable

Poorly maintained feeders are a disease vector. Trichomonosis — caused by the protozoan Trichomonas gallinae — has devastated UK greenfinch (Chloris chloris) and chaffinch (Fringilla coelebs) populations since 2005, spreading through saliva deposited on feeder surfaces. Salmonella outbreaks at poorly cleaned ground feeders kill siskins, sparrows, and other ground-feeding species. The evidence that garden feeding helps bird populations depends on feeders being cleaned properly; uncleaned feeders can cause net harm.

Best practice: clean feeders with hot water and a dilute disinfectant solution (5 percent bleach, rinsed thoroughly) every two weeks. Remove uneaten food before refilling. Move ground feeding areas regularly to avoid manure accumulation. If you observe birds with swollen bills, fluffed plumage, or difficulty swallowing near a feeder, remove and sterilize all feeding stations and suspend feeding for two weeks.

## Citizen science: Project FeederWatch and Garden BirdWatch

The British Trust for Ornithology's Garden BirdWatch (GBW) is the largest year-round survey of garden birds in the world, running continuously since 1995 with more than 10,000 participating households. Participants record the highest number of each species seen in the garden simultaneously (peak count, not cumulative total) in a standard weekly one-hour watch period. The resulting dataset tracks garden bird population trends, seasonal patterns, and the geographic spread of species like Ring-necked Parakeet (Psittacula krameri), which has expanded from the London area to appear in GBW gardens across southern England.

In North America, Cornell Lab's Project FeederWatch runs from November through April and operates on the same principle: standardized two-day count periods produce comparable data across thousands of sites. eBird also accepts garden checklists; a "stationary" checklist from a garden window creates a personal patch record that feeds the global database.

## Native plants and habitat structure

Feeders supplement but do not replace natural food and habitat. Native plants provide seeds, berries, and insects that are more nutritionally complete than commercial seed mixes, and their structural complexity supports nesting, sheltering, and foraging behaviors that feeders cannot replicate. An ivy-covered wall provides nesting habitat for spotted flycatchers (Muscicapa striata) and roosting sites for wrens (Troglodytes troglodytes) in cold weather. A rotting log harbors beetle larvae that attract thrushes. A tangle of bramble offers autumn berries and dense nest protection.

In North America, native plant gardens focused on species like serviceberries (Amelanchier), native oaks (Quercus), and sunflowers (Helianthus) support caterpillar diversity — the primary food for nestling songbirds regardless of what feeders offer — at a level that non-native ornamental planting cannot approach.

## Cats and window strikes: the garden hazards

Domestic cats kill an estimated 55 million birds annually in the UK and 1.3 to 4 billion in the United States. A garden feeding station that concentrates birds into a predictable location where an outdoor cat is present may cause net harm. Keeping cats indoors during peak feeding times, placing feeders at cat-inaccessible heights, and fitting cats with brightly colored collars (which reduce predation success by up to 40 percent in research studies) all mitigate the risk.

Window strikes kill millions of birds annually in every country with large glass-fronted buildings or residential windows. Birds see the reflected landscape rather than the glass. UV-reflective decals or external window screens break up the reflection without obstructing the view from inside.

## Building a productive garden birding setup

Garden birding has undergone a methodological transformation in the past decade as citizen science monitoring programs have demonstrated its scientific value for tracking population trends. The British Trust for Ornithology's Garden BirdWatch program, which collects weekly counts from over 10,000 UK gardens, has documented the decline of the House Sparrow (Passer domesticus) in urban gardens more sensitively than any field survey could achieve in the same area. This model has been replicated in North America (Project FeederWatch), Europe (the European Garden Bird Survey), and Australia (the Wild Bird Feeding Society programs).

The species composition of a garden feeder varies significantly by feeder type, food type, and garden habitat. Black sunflower seeds attract finches and sparrows most effectively; nyjer (thistle) seed attracts finches specifically; suet blocks attract woodpeckers, nuthatches, and tits; mealworms attract Robin, Blackbird, and Starling. Multiple feeder types producing a diverse food offering attract a wider species range. Ground feeding under a table or on a flat feeder surface attracts Dunnock, sparrows, and Blackbird, which rarely use hanging feeders.

The single most important variable in garden bird diversity is garden structure — the presence of dense low shrubs (for cover and nesting), mature trees (for woodpecker-suitable bark and cavity sites), and a water source. A garden with no cover other than a mown lawn will produce low diversity regardless of feeder quality; a garden with varied structure and height layers attracts a community comparable to a hedgerow.

Cat predation at feeders is a significant issue in suburban gardens. The most effective deterrent is positioning feeders at least 1.5 metres from cover that cats can use for concealment, and equipping feeders with anti-cat baffles on the pole. Some evidence suggests that brightly colored collar covers (the "cat bib" products) reduce predation on birds, though their efficacy on skilled garden predators is debated.

The RSPB Big Garden Birdwatch, conducted annually over a single January weekend, provides the largest single-event wildlife survey in the world, with over 700,000 UK participants submitting one-hour garden counts. Participating connects individual garden counts to the national dataset and provides a concrete measure of species change at the garden scale.

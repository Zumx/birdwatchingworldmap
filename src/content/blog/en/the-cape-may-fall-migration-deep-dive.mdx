On the right October morning, the southern tip of New Jersey can feel like the busiest airport in the natural world. A northwest wind has been blowing all night behind a cold front, and as dawn breaks over Cape May Point the trees and dune scrub are dripping with migrants: Yellow-rumped Warblers in their hundreds, kinglets flicking through the bayberry, a steady overhead stream of flickers and robins, and out over the hawk-watch platform the first Sharp-shinned Hawks of the day pulling low across the marsh. By mid-morning the sky may hold dozens of raptors at once. This is Cape May at its peak, and few places in North America concentrate autumn migration so reliably or so visibly.

Cape May sits at the southernmost point of New Jersey, a narrow peninsula where the Atlantic Ocean meets the Delaware Bay. Its geography is the whole story: birds moving south down the coast and the New Jersey shore are funnelled into an ever-narrowing point of land, then balk at the open water of the bay and pile up, waiting for the right wind to make the crossing toward Delaware.

## Why Cape May Concentrates Birds

The peninsula acts as a natural trap for southbound migrants. Songbirds, raptors, dragonflies, and monarch butterflies that drift down the Atlantic coast in autumn reach the tip of Cape May and find seventeen kilometres of open Delaware Bay barring their path. Many are reluctant to launch over water, especially in adverse winds, so they linger, feed, and accumulate in the woodlots, dunes, and gardens of Cape May Point. When the wind turns wrong for crossing, the buildup can be staggering.

The classic conditions follow a cold front: clear skies and brisk northwest winds that push migrants down the coast overnight and deposit them at the cape by dawn. Birders here, as at all great migration sites, watch the weather obsessively. A front passing through in the small hours is the signal for an early start, because the best of the flight often happens in the first hours after sunrise.

## The Hawk-Watch on the Platform

The Cape May Hawkwatch, run from the platform in Cape May Point State Park beside the old lighthouse, is one of the most famous raptor counts in the world. Official counters staff it daily from September through November, and the season total regularly runs into the tens of thousands. Cape May is especially renowned for falcons and accipiters: the Sharp-shinned Hawk flights can be extraordinary, and the cape records some of the highest concentrations of migrating Peregrine Falcons and Merlins anywhere in North America.

The cast shifts through the season. Early autumn brings Ospreys, American Kestrels, and Bald Eagles. Late September and October are the peak for Sharp-shinned and Cooper's Hawks, Northern Harriers quartering the marsh, and the famous falcon push. November adds Red-tailed and Red-shouldered Hawks and the occasional Golden Eagle. The data gathered here, like that from the great ridge counts inland, feed long-term monitoring of raptor populations across the eastern flyway.

## A Songbird Spectacle

For many birders the warblers are the heart of Cape May. The peninsula records an exceptional diversity of wood-warblers in autumn, from abundant Yellow-rumped and Palm Warblers to scarcer Connecticut, Cape May, and Blackpoll Warblers. The Higbee Beach Wildlife Management Area, just across the canal, is the legendary morning flight spot, where on good days a continuous stream of songbirds pours overhead at first light, reorienting after their overnight journey before settling to feed.

Beyond warblers, the autumn brings sparrows in great variety, thrushes, vireos, flycatchers, and the diurnal flight of flickers, blackbirds, and swallows. The Northern Saw-whet Owl migration through Cape May, monitored by banding each autumn night, is among the most significant in the region. The sheer turnover of species from week to week is what keeps birders returning day after day.

## Seabirds, Shorebirds and the Bay

Cape May is not only a landbird site. Sea-watching from the Cape May Point or from Avalon, a little up the coast, can produce streams of scoters, loons, gannets, and Cory's and Great Shearwaters, particularly during and after onshore blows. The famous Avalon Seawatch, near the eighth street jetty, counts hundreds of thousands of southbound waterbirds across the autumn.

The marshes and impoundments around the cape, including those at the Cape May National Wildlife Refuge and the Nature Conservancy's South Cape May Meadows, hold concentrations of shorebirds, herons, egrets, and waterfowl. While the global fame of Delaware Bay shorebirds belongs more to the spring horseshoe-crab spectacle further up the bay at places like Reeds Beach, autumn still delivers excellent wader watching at the cape's wetlands.

## Monarchs and the Wider Migration

Cape May is justly famous for more than birds. The autumn monarch butterfly migration funnels along the same coastline, and on peak days in late September and October the dunes and goldenrod can be alive with thousands of monarchs staging before their own crossing of the bay on the way to Mexico. The Monarch Monitoring Project has tagged butterflies here since the early 1990s, contributing to understanding of the insects' extraordinary journey.

Dragonflies migrate through too, sometimes in spectacular numbers, and the convergence of birds, butterflies, and odonates at a single narrow point makes Cape May a place to appreciate migration as a whole phenomenon rather than a single taxon. The interplay of all this movement, driven by the same winds and the same coastal geography, is part of what gives the cape its particular magic.

## Planning a Visit

The town of Cape May itself, with its Victorian architecture and walkable streets, makes a comfortable base, and the birding sites cluster within a short drive. Cape May Point State Park holds the lighthouse, the hawk-watch platform, and a network of trails through pond and dune habitat. Higbee Beach, the South Cape May Meadows, the state park, and Sunset Beach are all within minutes of one another.

The Cape May Bird Observatory, run by New Jersey Audubon, anchors the birding community with walks, programmes, and the annual Cape May Fall Festival, one of the largest birding events in the United States, usually held in October at the height of the season. For a first-time visitor, joining one of the observatory's morning walks is the quickest way to learn the rhythm of the cape and to read the wind like a local.

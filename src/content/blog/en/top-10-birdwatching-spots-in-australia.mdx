Australia's bird list of approximately 900 species is built on ancient Gondwanan lineages — the honeyeaters, lyrebirds, treecreepers, and fairy-wrens that evolved here while the continent drifted north — combined with a large contingent of waders, seabirds, and passerines that use the continent as wintering grounds or passage habitat. The country's sheer size means that any serious birding trip must choose a region rather than attempting the whole. These ten sites represent the most productive concentrations of endemic and specialty species. All are marked on the [map](/map).

## 1. Kakadu National Park, Northern Territory

Kakadu's 20,000 square kilometres of floodplain, escarpment, and woodland hold over 280 species, including 10 species found nowhere else outside the Top End. The Yellow Water Billabong near Cooinda is the defining experience: a flat-bottomed boat at dawn across a floodplain with Brolgas, Jabirus, and Magpie Geese overhead, and Comb-crested Jacanas walking on lily pads at eye level. The dry season (May to October) concentrates waterbirds at permanent water and is the only reliable access period. The walk to Nourlangie Rock adds Red-winged Parrots, Hooded Parrots, and, in the right woodland pockets, Chestnut-quilled Rock Pigeon.

## 2. Lamington National Park, Queensland

O'Reilly's Rainforest Retreat in Lamington NP sits on the edge of old-growth subtropical rainforest at 900 metres. The hand-feeding of native birds at O'Reilly's — Crimson Rosellas, Regent Bowerbirds, and Satin Bowerbirds at close range — is controversial but makes for genuinely memorable encounters. More substantially, the Green Mountains plateau trail network gives access to Albert's Lyrebird in its finest remaining habitat, along with Paradise Riflebird, Spotted Catbird, and the full complement of Queensland rainforest endemics. Mornings in October are peak for lyrebird display.

## 3. Kangaroo Island, South Australia

Kangaroo Island holds populations of several Australian species that are locally extinct or reduced on the mainland, including the Glossy Black Cockatoo, whose population of around 400 birds is managed intensively. The Flinders Chase National Park end of the island offers access to Rock Parrots, Regent Parrots, and large concentrations of waterbirds around salt lagoons. The island's isolation has kept introduced predators historically lower than the mainland, which benefits ground-nesting species. Winter visits (June to August) give the best access to the Glossy Black Cockatoo food trees in Flinders Chase.

## 4. Bruny Island, Tasmania

Bruny Island combines Tasmanian endemic land birds with extraordinary inshore seabird access. The Short-tailed Shearwater colony at The Neck is one of the most accessible in Australia; thousands of birds return to burrows after dark from February through April. Endemics including the Forty-spotted Pardalote, Swift Parrot, and Scrubtit are found in the eucalypt woodland along the Adventure Bay Road. Adventure Bay itself hosts Little Penguins at the standard twilight viewing area. Peak Swift Parrot access is October to December.

## 5. Werribee Treatment Plant, Victoria

A sewage treatment plant is not an obvious destination, but Werribee's lagoons hold some of the largest and most diverse waterbird concentrations in southern Australia. Brolgas, Australasian Bitterns, Sharp-tailed Sandpipers, Curlew Sandpipers, and regular vagrant shorebirds — including overseas rarities — make this the single best waterbird site in Victoria. Access requires a permit from Melbourne Water, available in advance, and visits are self-guided during daylight hours. August and September, when northbound shorebirds are building fat reserves before their Pacific and Siberian breeding journeys, is the peak period.

## 6. Hattah-Kulkyne National Park, Victoria

Hattah is mallee eucalypt country at its finest: an undulating semi-arid landscape that holds specialties absent from most of eastern Australia's birding circuit. Regent Parrot, Major Mitchell's Cockatoo, Mulga Parrot, and Chestnut Quail-thrush all occur. The lake system floods irregularly and in good water years concentrates extraordinary waterbird numbers. Camping is available within the park; the Hattah Lakes track gives access to both mallee and wetland habitats in a single morning circuit.

## 7. Capertee Valley, New South Wales

Capertee is the largest box-gum woodland canyon in the world and the key remaining habitat for the critically endangered Regent Honeyeater (Anthochaera phrygia). Fewer than 300 individuals remain in the wild; Capertee holds the largest single concentration in its spring migration. The surrounding box-gum woodland also holds Swift Parrot in winter, Brown Treecreeper, and several woodland species rare across their former range. Organised Regent Honeyeater roost counts in September and October allow observation without additional disturbance.

## 8. Iron Range National Park, Queensland (Cape York)

Iron Range is the most tropical and most remote of the Australian sites on this list. The lowland rainforest on Cape York Peninsula is the southernmost extension of New Guinea lowland forest, and the bird community reflects that origin: Eclectus Parrot, Palm Cockatoo, Green-backed Honeyeater, Magnificent Riflebird, and Fawn-breasted Bowerbird are all present. Access requires a 4WD vehicle and good preparation; the dirt road from Musgrave becomes impassable in the wet season. June to October is the accessible window.

## 9. Atherton Tablelands, Queensland

The Atherton Tablelands above Cairns offer the most accessible concentration of Queensland wet-tropics endemics. Lake Barrine and Lake Eacham are the entry points for Buff-breasted Paradise Kingfisher (November to April), Chowchilla, Fernwren, and Golden Bowerbird. Platypus viewing at Hasties Swamp near Atherton is easily combined with waterbird watching. The tablelands also give access to Tooth-billed Catbird display courts on the crater rim trails, active from September.

## 10. Eyre Bird Observatory, Western Australia

Eyre sits at the edge of the Nullarbor Plain on the Great Australian Bight coast, a six-hour drive from anywhere. The isolation means the guest list is small and the birding is serious. Inland from Eyre, spinifex mallee country holds Nullarbor Quail-thrush, White-winged Fairywren, and Southern Scrub Robin. Coastal walks give access to nesting Short-tailed Shearwaters and, in season, Cape Barren Geese. The observatory runs guided field sessions and provides accommodation; advance booking essential.

## Planning an Australian birding trip

Australia's birding geography divides cleanly into four independent circuits: the tropical north (Kakadu, Darwin region, Cape York), the southeast woodland and forest (Lamington, Capertee, Hattah), the southwest (Eyre, Western Australian mallee and heathland), and the island circuit (Kangaroo Island, Tasmania, Bruny). Each circuit requires at least a week; combining two in a fortnight is feasible with domestic flights but requires precise seasonal alignment.

The best all-round season is the southern autumn (March to May): the monsoon has ended in the north, summer heat has broken in the southeast, and resident breeding species are still displaying. The Swift Parrot (Lathamus discolor), one of the world's only migratory parrots, moves between Tasmania and mainland southeast Australia in a pattern tied to eucalypt flowering; tracking it requires checking eBird reports for current flowering and parrot movements in October and November each year. The Atherton Tablelands kingfisher species (including the Buff-breasted Paradise Kingfisher) arrive November to April; Regent Honeyeater at Capertee peaks in September. Seasonal misalignment is the most common cause of disappointing Australian birding trips.

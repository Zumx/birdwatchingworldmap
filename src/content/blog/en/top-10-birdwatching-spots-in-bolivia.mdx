Bolivia is among the most ornithologically rich countries in the Americas, and one of the least visited by international birders. Its combination of Andean habitats — from the world's highest navigable lake at Titicaca to the salt flats of Uyuni and the Yungas cloud forest — with Amazonian lowlands and transitional Cerrado produces extraordinary species diversity. Several sites in the country's map database reflect the growing ecotourism infrastructure, including the Mirador de Flamencos sites at high-altitude flamingo viewpoints and the condor monitoring camp at Campamento Proyecto Condor Andino.

## 1. Madidi National Park, Beni/La Paz

Madidi is one of the largest protected areas in South America and holds one of the highest bird lists for any protected area on earth — over 1,100 species. The park spans the Andean foothills-to-Amazonian-lowland gradient, combining highland cloud forest and puna with the full lowland forest community. Chalalan Ecolodge, community-operated within the park, provides base camp accommodation accessible by river from Rurrenabaque. Harpy Eagle, Sunbittern (Eurypyga helias), and all Bolivian macaw species occur within Madidi.

## 2. Amboró National Park, Santa Cruz

Amboró is the most accessible major reserve in Bolivia and covers the zone where the Andes meet the Amazon in Cochabamba and Santa Cruz departments. The resulting bird community combines Andean species with lowland forest species in a mosaic that nowhere else in Bolivia is as easily reached. Cock-of-the-rock (Rupicola peruvianus) leks occur in the steep cloud forest; Giant Antpitta (Grallaria gigantea) and several near-endemic subspecies occur within the park's elevational range.

## 3. Refugio Los Volcanes, Santa Cruz

This private reserve in the Amboró buffer zone, accessed from the Refugio Los Volcanes lodge, covers the forest on the volcanic hills of the Amboró massif. The combination of forest interior and valley edge habitats produces a diverse list including Red-fronted Macaw (Ara rubrogenys, Endangered) — a Bolivian near-endemic restricted to the dry intermontane valleys of central Bolivia.

## 4. Laguna Colorada and Uyuni, Potosí

The Reserva Nacional de Fauna Andina Eduardo Abaroa protects the high-altitude wetlands of the Bolivian Altiplano, including the red-algae-stained Laguna Colorada at 4,278 metres. All three Andean flamingo species breed here: Andean Flamingo (Phoenicoparrus andinus, Vulnerable), James's Flamingo (Phoenicoparrus jamesi, Near Threatened), and Chilean Flamingo (Phoenicopterus chilensis, Near Threatened). Puna Rhea (Rhea pennata tarapacensis), Giant Coot (Fulica gigantea), and Puna Plover share the high-altitude lake shores. The Mirador de Flamencos sites (documented in the map database) provide the best flamingo viewing points on this circuit.

## 5. Cochabamba Valley and Chapare

The transition between the Cochabamba Valley dry inter-Andean habitats and the humid Chapare to the east crosses several vegetation zones rapidly. Buffy Helmetcrest (Oxypogon stuebelii) and White-tufted Sunbeam are part of the highland hummingbird community; the Chapare lowlands produce the full western Amazonian forest community with additional Bolivian endemics.

## 6. Lake Titicaca, La Paz and Oruro

The world's highest large lake at 3,812 metres holds two endemic species: Titicaca Grebe (Rollandia microptera, Endangered) — a flightless grebe restricted to the lake — and Titicaca Flightless Grebe. The reed beds around the Bahía de Cohana and the Isla del Sol are the most accessible Titicaca Grebe sites. The lake's surface additionally holds Andean Gull (Chroicocephalus serranus) and Silvery Grebe (Podiceps occipitalis).

## 7. Campamento Proyecto Condor Andino, La Paz

The Andean Condor monitoring camp (documented in the map database as Campamento Proyecto Condor Andino) in the Apolobamba range of La Paz department provides researcher access to an active condor monitoring site. The organization behind the camp works on condor population ecology and provides an entry point for citizen scientists to participate in population monitoring at high altitude. Puna birds including the Giant Hummingbird (Patagona gigas) and Andean Flicker (Colaptes rupicola) are readily encountered.

## 8. Cerro Tunari, Cochabamba

The highest point in Cochabamba department at 5,035 metres, Cerro Tunari and its surrounding national park protect puna and Polylepis woodland — the wind-stunted, multi-stemmed tree that holds some of the most endemic bird communities in the Andes. Tit-like Dacnis (Xenodacnis parina), White-browed Tit-Spinetail, and Royal Cinclodes (Cinclodes aricomae, Critically Endangered) represent the Polylepis specialist community.

## 9. Puerto Suárez and the Pantanal margin, Santa Cruz

The Bolivian Pantanal, a small portion of the larger South American wetland system, is accessible from Puerto Suárez near the Brazilian border. Hyacinth Macaw (Anodorhynchus hyacinthinus, Vulnerable), Jabiru Stork, and Giant River Otter share this wetland with the Capped Heron and Sunbittern. The Bolivian Pantanal has been less intensively developed than the Brazilian sector and retains extensive primary wetland habitat.

## 10. Noel Kempff Mercado National Park, Santa Cruz

Noel Kempff, a UNESCO World Heritage Site on the Brazil border, covers one of the largest areas of intact Cerrado, Amazonian forest, and gallery woodland in South America. The Huanchaca Plateau provides the dramatic topography of the waterfall-edged meseta on which the Spielberg-produced BBC film sequence for "Blue Planet" was partially set. The park's bird list exceeds 620 species; Curl-crested Aracari (Pteroglossus beauharnaesii) and many Cerrado endemics occur here.

## Planning a Bolivia birding trip

Bolivia holds over 1,400 species — among the highest totals in South America — in a country where the extremes of Andean altiplano, cloud forest, and lowland Amazonia create the layered habitat stacking that drives Neotropical bird diversity. The country's relative lack of tourism infrastructure compared to Peru and Ecuador gives it an expedition-level quality that rewards self-reliant birders.

The most productive logistics centre is Santa Cruz de la Sierra in the Bolivian lowlands. Santa Cruz's Viru Viru International Airport receives flights from Lima, Buenos Aires, Bogotá, and São Paulo, and from Santa Cruz the lowland Chiquitano dry forests, the Beni wetlands, and the Madidi National Park lowland forests are accessible by road or domestic flight. Madidi National Park — accessible from Rurrenabaque by a one-hour flight from Santa Cruz — holds one of the highest bird diversities of any protected area on Earth, with over 1,000 species recorded.

The Cordillera Real and the Yungas cloud forest northwest of La Paz concentrate the most sought Bolivian endemics. The Yungas Road (the infamous "Road of Death" route from La Paz to Coroico) passes through an extraordinary altitudinal gradient from Puna grasslands at 4,700 metres to Yungas cloud forest at 1,200 metres in 80 kilometres. Birds of this transect include all three Bolivian endemic hummingbirds and a suite of altitudinal specialists found only in this narrow zone.

The saltpans of the southwest Altiplano — Uyuni and Laguna Colorado — are accessible from Uyuni by organized 4WD tours. The Mirador de Flamencos (documented in the map database) provides a designated flamingo viewing platform at one of the Altiplano's most productive flamingo lakes.

La Paz's El Alto airport at 4,061 metres is the world's highest commercial airport and serves both the Altiplano birding and the Yungas cloud forest via the La Paz to Coroico road. Santa Cruz is the better base for lowland Amazonian birding.

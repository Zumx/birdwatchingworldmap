The European Farmland Bird Index, maintained by the Pan-European Common Bird Monitoring Scheme (PECBMS), tracks the population trend of 39 species typical of European agricultural habitats: Skylark (Alauda arvensis), Lapwing (Vanellus vanellus), Grey Partridge (Perdix perdix), Corn Bunting (Emberiza calandra), Yellow Wagtail (Motacilla flava), Northern Lapwing, Tree Sparrow (Passer montanus), and others. Since 1980, the aggregate index has fallen by more than 55 percent. For some species, the decline is far worse: the Grey Partridge is down over 90 percent in Britain. The Corn Bunting, which was so common that its ratcheting song was a background noise of English farming landscapes through the mid-twentieth century, has declined by approximately 90 percent across its European range.

## What drove the collapse

Agricultural intensification is the proximate cause, operating through several simultaneous mechanisms. The switch from spring-sown to autumn-sown cereals, driven by yield improvements, eliminated the over-winter stubble fields that provided seed food for finches and buntings through January and February — the period of greatest winter food stress. Stubble fields were replaced by a bare-soil phase from August through March, providing no food and no cover.

The mechanization of harvesting eliminated the small-scale habitat complexity of pre-mechanization farming: rough field margins, uncut headlands, patches of wet ground drained for machinery access, and the varied mosaic of crop types and fallow stages that had characterized mixed farming for centuries. Modern large-field monocultures, uniform in structure and uniform in their management timing, provide a narrow window of habitat and food availability that cannot support populations of birds adapted to the varied, year-round resource base of traditional mixed farming.

Pesticide use — both insecticides and herbicides — reduced the invertebrate and seed food available within crop fields directly. The collapse of farmland invertebrate abundance has been documented by several long-term studies. The cereal aphid spraying that protects yield in modern arable farming eliminates the aphid supply that Grey Partridge chicks depend on in their first week of life. Without a high-protein invertebrate diet in the first ten days, chick survival drops to levels insufficient to maintain the population.

## The role of monitoring

The scale and precision of the European farmland bird decline is known because of long-running volunteer survey schemes. In Britain, the BTO/RSPB/JNCC Breeding Bird Survey (BBS) has operated since 1994, with volunteers visiting fixed transect routes twice annually and counting all birds detected. The Common Bird Census that preceded it ran from 1962 to 2000. Together, these surveys document population trends with statistical confidence for over 100 species.

The detection of the farmland bird crisis in the 1980s and 1990s was itself a product of these monitoring programs. Prior to BBS-quality data, the declines were locally visible but not statistically documentable at the national or continental level. The data provided the evidence that made the scientific and policy case for agri-environment payments.

## Agri-environment schemes: the partial response

The EU's agri-environment schemes, introduced as part of Common Agricultural Policy reform in the 1980s and expanded substantially in the 1990s, provided payments to farmers for management practices beneficial to biodiversity: leaving uncropped field margins, maintaining grass buffer strips, delaying mowing to allow ground-nesting success, and restoring hedgerows. Uptake has been highly variable by country and scheme design.

Evidence from UK studies shows that well-designed and well-implemented agri-environment schemes can stabilize or locally reverse farmland bird declines. Research plots in Hertfordshire under the Allerton Project have demonstrated recoveries of Grey Partridge, Lapwing, and Corn Bunting under targeted management on a 333-hectare mixed farm. The challenge is scaling these results across millions of hectares of diverse European farmland with variable compliance and monitoring.

## The continuing decline

Despite agri-environment payments, the farmland bird decline has continued in most European countries. More recent analysis suggests that the measures implemented have been insufficient in scale, management precision, and geographic targeting to reverse the underlying trends. A more fundamental transformation of farming's ecological relationship with the landscape — restoration of habitat heterogeneity, reduction of pesticide use, and permanent set-aside at meaningful scales — appears necessary but has not been implemented in Europe at the required scale.

The State of the World's Birds report, published by BirdLife International in 2022, placed the European farmland bird crisis in global context: agricultural intensification is the leading threat to bird populations on every continent, and the European experience provides a cautionary data record of what uniform intensification produces over forty years.

## The policy context and what comes next

The decline of European farmland birds is directly linked to the European Union's Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), which has historically subsidized production intensity in ways that reduce the habitat value of agricultural land for birds. Reforms to the CAP negotiated in 2021-2027 introduced "eco-schemes" — voluntary payment mechanisms for farming practices that benefit biodiversity — but the effectiveness of these schemes in reversing farmland bird declines depends on uptake rates and implementation at member state level.

The most effective farmland bird management interventions are well-documented from decades of Agri-Environment Scheme research in the United Kingdom: unharvested field corners (beetle banks) providing overwintering habitat for invertebrates and seeds; cultivated fallow plots providing bare ground for nesting; later hay-cutting dates allowing ground-nesting birds to complete their breeding cycle; and winter cover crops retaining seeds through the critical winter period.

The United Kingdom's Countryside Stewardship scheme and its predecessor Environmental Stewardship are the most evaluated farmland bird programs in Europe. Farms enrolled in Higher Level Stewardship with a full package of bird-targeted options show measurable population increases of Grey Partridge, Yellowhammer, and Corn Bunting compared to control farms. However, the 90% population collapses recorded in the BTO's Common Bird Census pre-date the current scheme framework and recovery from such depleted baselines requires decades of sustained habitat management.

The Farmland Bird Index — maintained by the European Environment Agency using standardized monitoring data from 28 member states — is the most widely cited barometer of the policy's effect on biodiversity. Birders can contribute to national farmland bird monitoring through Breeding Bird Survey participation in their country; in many European countries, farmland transect routes are the surveys most in need of additional volunteers.

Agricultural policy reform advocacy through BirdLife International's national partners and the RSPB's political engagement program provides a mechanism for birders to connect their field observations to policy outcomes.

## Monitoring and data

The Common Bird Census and its successor the Breeding Bird Survey, operated by the British Trust for Ornithology, have tracked UK farmland bird populations continuously since 1966 — the longest-running national bird monitoring program in Europe. The resulting dataset shows that the UK farmland bird index fell by 57% between 1970 and 2020. Comparable datasets from the Pan-European Common Bird Monitoring Scheme (PECBMS) show that the EU-27 farmland bird index fell by over 50% since 1980.

Volunteer participation in national Breeding Bird Survey (BBS) programs is the single most impactful data contribution available to ordinary birders for farmland bird monitoring. Survey squares are randomly allocated and cover a range of habitats; many squares in intensively farmed agricultural areas are under-surveyed due to lack of volunteer coverage in remote locations. Contacting the national BBS coordinator provides information on available squares needing coverage.

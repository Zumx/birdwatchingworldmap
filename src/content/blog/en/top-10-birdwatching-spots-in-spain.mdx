Spain is the most important country in Western Europe for birds. Its combination of Mediterranean scrub, Atlantic oak woodland, semi-arid steppe, large river systems, and its position at the junction of the East Atlantic and Black Sea-Mediterranean flyways produces a national list of over 600 species. The country holds the entire European breeding population of Spanish Imperial Eagle and holds Western Europe's largest populations of Black Stork, Black Vulture, and Great Bustard. A two-week circuit covering the main habitats produces a European birder's life-list trip. All sites are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Doñana National Park, Andalucía

Doñana at the mouth of the Guadalquivir is the largest wetland and the most important waterbird staging site in Western Europe. The park's combination of marismas (seasonal wetlands), cork oak woodland, stone pine scrub, and Atlantic beach holds breeding Spanish Imperial Eagle, Black-shouldered Kite, Marbled Duck, Purple Swamphen, Red-knobbed Coot, and Ferruginous Duck. In winter the wetlands hold up to 300,000 waterbirds; in spring and autumn the woodland holds a concentrated migration of Pied Flycatchers, Nightingales, and Golden Orioles. Access to the core marismas requires booking a guided jeep trip through the national park administration; peripheral areas are freely accessible from El Rocío and the northern entrance.

## 2. Monfragüe National Park, Extremadura

Monfragüe is the European capital of large raptor watching. The Tajo River cuts through granite and schist hills covered in Holm Oak and Mediterranean scrub; the cliffs support the largest concentration of cliff-nesting raptors in Europe. Black Vulture, Griffon Vulture, Spanish Imperial Eagle, Bonelli's Eagle, Black Stork, and White Stork all breed within the park. The Peñafalcón viewpoint above the Tajo gives the definitive raptor experience: multiple vulture species drifting past at eye level. Best in spring (March to May) when all species are active on breeding territories.

## 3. Coto de Doñana Migratory Routes, Andalucía

The peripheral areas of Doñana including the rice fields near Isla Mayor, the Brazo del Este marsh, and the woodland trails around El Acebuche are less regulated than the core park and often more productive for individual species. The Brazo del Este rice fields hold the largest concentration of breeding Whiskered Terns, Purple Herons, and Little Bitterns in Spain; Aquatic Warblers pass through in August on southbound migration. The woodland edge near El Acebuche is the most reliable access point for the Azure-winged Magpie in a landscape setting.

## 4. Tablas de Daimiel National Park, Castile-La Mancha

Tablas de Daimiel in the Manchegan plain protects a remnant of what was once a vast floodplain wetland at the confluence of the Cigüela and Guadiana rivers. The remaining water is maintained partly artificially. Breeding Purple Gallinule, Little Bittern, Glossy Ibis, and Ferruginous Duck occupy the central wetland; surrounding steppe holds Little Bustard, Pin-tailed Sandgrouse, and Roller. The main interpretation centre gives access to a boardwalk into the reedbeds.

## 5. Ebro Delta, Catalonia

The Ebro Delta is the most important wetland in eastern Spain. The rice fields, saltmarshes, and sandy spits at the delta mouth attract enormous concentrations of breeding Little Tern, Slender-billed Gull, Audouin's Gull, and Sandwich Tern in spring and summer; winter brings Greater Flamingo colonies and Eurasian Spoonbill roosts. The Delta holds the only breeding colony of Purple Heron in Catalonia and Spain's largest breeding Glossy Ibis colony. The town of Deltebre is the base; the CEBEM interpretation centre at La Marquesa gives organised access.

## 6. Sierra de Andújar, Andalucía

The Sierra de Andújar within the Sierra Morena is the best site in Spain for Spanish Imperial Eagle and offers the added possibility of Iberian Lynx — the former is nearly guaranteed on a morning drive; the latter occasionally seen around Charco del Aceite. The Sierra Morena is also the stronghold of the Black Vulture: thousands of birds breed here, and the colony around the Parque Natural de la Sierra Norte de Sevilla and adjacent Andújar territory is among Europe's largest. Honey Buzzard and Montagu's Harrier are summer breeders.

## 7. Belchite Steppes, Aragón

The Zaragoza steppes around the abandoned town of Belchite are the most reliable site in Western Europe for Dupont's Lark (Chersophilus duponti), a secretive skulking species of open dry steppe that is difficult to observe even with knowledge of its song and territory location. Calandra Lark, Black-bellied Sandgrouse, Pin-tailed Sandgrouse, and Lesser Kestrel colonies are additional steppe specialties. Dawn and dusk are the only productive periods for Dupont's; the surrounding flat steppe is unproductive in full daylight for most target species.

## 8. Aiguamolls de l'Empordà, Catalonia

Aiguamolls de l'Empordà, on the Gulf of Roses near the French border, is the most important migration watchpoint on the northeastern Spanish coast. The reserve's wetlands and woodland hold breeding Purple Heron, Little Bittern, and Night Heron; in migration the reedbeds attract Aquatic Warbler, Marsh Sandpiper, and large numbers of waders. The reserve has excellent infrastructure including a good visitor centre, observation towers, and boardwalks into the main wetland. Little Crake, Spotted Crake, and Baillon's Crake all occur but are largely secretive; listening at dawn produces the best results.

## 9. Lagunas de La Mancha, Castile-La Mancha

The complex of saline and freshwater lagoons across the provinces of Albacete, Cuenca, and Ciudad Real constitutes a chain of Important Bird Areas for Manchegan steppe waterbirds. Las Lagunas de Ruidera, Laguna de El Hito, and Laguna del Longar each hold distinct bird communities. El Hito is the most important single site: breeding White-headed Duck (Oxyura leucocephala), one of Europe's most threatened ducks, along with breeding Gull-billed Tern and Collared Pratincole. Water levels fluctuate dramatically year to year; checking current conditions before visiting is essential.

## 10. Tarifa, Cádiz — Strait of Gibraltar

Tarifa is the primary migration watchpoint for soaring birds crossing between Europe and Africa, and the concentration of species diversity is unmatched in Europe. The Fundación Migres count site gives access to up to 400,000 raptors per season crossing within a few kilometres of the shore. In addition to the raptor passage described in detail in the raptor migration post, Tarifa produces accessible observation of African species in the immediate vicinity: Black-crowned Tchagra, Barbary Partridge, and White-rumped Swift all breed on the Spanish hillsides around the town, reflecting the Moroccan influence on the Andalucian flora that reaches its northern limit here.

## Planning a Spain birding trip

Spain's birding geography divides into four productive zones: Andalucía (Doñana, Monfragüe, Sierra de Andújar, Tarifa), Extremadura (La Serena steppe for Great Bustard, Monfragüe raptors), Mediterranean east (Ebro Delta, Aiguamolls, Laguna de El Hito), and Aragón steppe (Belchite, Zaragoza plains). A two-week circuit of Andalucía and Extremadura alone provides over 200 species including almost all the Spanish target species.

March to May is the best general season: most species are breeding or about to breed, the steppe birds are displaying, and spring migration is active at Tarifa and Doñana. The Doñana wetlands reach peak waterbird concentrations in December to February. Belchite for Dupont's Lark must be visited at dawn and dusk; planning a night in the area rather than a day trip is essential.

BirdLife Spain (SEO/BirdLife) publishes the most detailed site guides for Spanish birding locations. eBird Spain is well-populated.

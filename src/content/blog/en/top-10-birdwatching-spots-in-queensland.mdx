Queensland is Australia's most biodiverse state for birds, with over 630 species recorded — roughly two-thirds of all Australian bird species. The state's size and its position astride the tropics and subtropics create exceptional habitat diversity. The Wet Tropics World Heritage Area in the northeast contains Australia's most intact tropical rainforest, supporting an endemic bird community found nowhere else. Cape York Peninsula at the far north holds species shared with Papua New Guinea accessible nowhere else in Australia. The outback Channel Country holds arid-zone specialists, and the wetlands of the Gulf Country and Atherton Tablelands attract enormous concentrations of waterbirds.

Queensland has a well-developed ecotourism infrastructure, and the Wet Tropics in particular around Cairns is one of the most visited birdwatching regions in Australia. The state's geographic range from the subtropical Sunshine Coast to the tropical Atherton Tablelands and the Cape York frontier means that different regions reward birding at different times of year. The dry season — May to November — is generally the most productive, though the wet season brings its own bird activity.

## 1. Wet Tropics Rainforest, Cairns and Atherton Tablelands

The rainforest around Cairns and the Atherton Tablelands is the centre of the Wet Tropics endemic community. Southern Cassowary — a large, dinosaurian ratite with a brilliant blue neck and red wattles — inhabits the dense rainforest and is regularly encountered on the roads through Mission Beach and Daintree. Buff-breasted Paradise Kingfisher arrives from Papua New Guinea each October to breed in the forest, nesting in terrestrial termite mounds. Golden Bowerbird — the male building elaborate stick bower decorated with white objects — inhabits the high-altitude rainforest above Ravenshoe. Tooth-billed Bowerbird sings from a cleared display court. Victoria's Riflebird, the Queensland bird-of-paradise, displays from rainforest perches.

## 2. Daintree National Park, Cairns Region

The Daintree is Australia's oldest tropical rainforest and holds the world's richest concentration of plant and bird species within a small area. The Daintree ferry crossing and the Cape Tribulation road are accessible to visiting birders and produce a range of Wet Tropics specialities. Pied Imperial Pigeon undertakes daily flights from offshore islands to the rainforest to feed — the mass flights at dawn and dusk are a spectacular sight. Tropical Scrubwren and Fernwren inhabit the dense rainforest floor. Spotted Catbird calls from the canopy — an Australian catbird whose fruity call echoes through the forest. The Mossman Gorge section provides excellent walking trails through lowland rainforest.

## 3. Mareeba Wetlands, Atherton Tablelands

Mareeba Wetlands near Atherton is a constructed wetland system that has become one of North Queensland's most productive waterbird sites. Brolga — Australia's native crane, grey and red-capped — is resident and breeds here in significant numbers alongside the much rarer Sarus Crane, which is regularly observed at close range. Radjah Shelduck, Plumed Whistling Duck, and Wandering Whistling Duck are all present. Royal Spoonbill and Yellow Spoonbill both feed in the shallows. The site offers elevated viewing platforms and well-maintained walking tracks. Black-necked Stork stands in the shallow margins, while Brolga dance in courtship displays during the breeding season.

## 4. Lake Eyre System and Channel Country, Southwest Queensland

The Channel Country floodplain in Queensland's far southwest, when flooded by the Diamantina, Cooper Creek, and their tributaries, produces the largest ephemeral wetland in Australia. When Lake Eyre (Kati Thanda) fills — an event that occurs perhaps once or twice a decade following exceptional inland rainfall — it attracts waterbirds from across the continent. Banded Stilt breeds in massive colonies on the salt lake shore, sometimes numbering hundreds of thousands. Silver Gull travels inland from the coast to breed. Grey Teal moves rapidly in response to flooding signals. In normal years, the dry Channel Country holds Flock Bronzewing, Bourke's Parrot, and other arid-zone specialists around the waterholes.

## 5. Iron Range National Park, Cape York Peninsula

Iron Range is the most important site in Australia for birds shared with Papua New Guinea and is often called Australia's most difficult and most rewarding birding destination. The park protects the largest area of lowland tropical rainforest in Australia outside the Wet Tropics, and its proximity to New Guinea means it holds species found nowhere else on the continent. Palm Cockatoo — the world's largest black cockatoo — inhabits the forest. Eclectus Parrot is conspicuous, with the red female and green male appearing to be different species. Magnificent Riflebird displays from rainforest perches. Green-backed Honeyeater and Magnificent Riflebird are both characteristic. The Yellow-legged Flyrobin inhabits the forest interior. Access requires a four-wheel drive vehicle and advance planning.

## 6. Undara Volcanic National Park, Gulf Savannah

The Undara lava tubes north of Mt Surprise create a network of dry volcanic caves supporting a remarkable bird community. Lava tubes function as temperature-stable roosts and breeding sites for a range of bat species, and the associated insectivory supports high bird diversity. Horsfield's Bronze Cuckoo parasitises fairy-wrens in the surrounding savannah. Pictorella Mannikin — a finch of tropical grassland found mainly in the far north — is resident. Gouldian Finch — arguably the world's most colourful finch — historically inhabited these tropical grasslands and is the subject of an ongoing recovery programme in the Gulf Country. Brown Honeyeater is abundant in the flowering grevillea woodland.

## 7. Townsville Town Common, Townsville

The Townsville Town Common Conservation Park on the southern edge of Townsville is a large area of tropical grassland, wetland, and mangrove managed for conservation. Brolga and Sarus Crane are regular, and Radjah Shelduck is conspicuous year-round. Pied Heron — a neat black and white heron of tropical Australia — forages in the shallow water. Wandering and Plumed Whistling Ducks are present in large flocks. The Black-throated Finch — a highly threatened finch whose decline has been linked to cattle grazing pressure and clearance of native grasses — still occurs at the common, making it one of the most accessible sites for this species. The mangrove fringe holds Mangrove Robin and Broad-billed Flycatcher.

## 8. Lamington National Park, Gold Coast Hinterland

Lamington on the Queensland-New South Wales border protects the largest remnant of subtropical rainforest in Australia. The park holds Albert's Lyrebird — a species restricted to the Queensland-NSW border ranges and rare even within its small range — inhabiting the densest rainforest gullies, where its extraordinary vocalisation carries far through the forest. Regent Bowerbird displays in the rainforest canopy, the male resplendent in black and gold. Paradise Riflebird inhabits the forest interior. The O'Reilly's Guesthouse, within the park, has maintained bird feeding stations for decades that provide excellent views of normally secretive forest species including various catbirds and fantails.

## 9. Hinchinbrook Island, Cardwell Area

Hinchinbrook is Australia's largest island national park, a granite massif rising from the sea and separated from the mainland by a narrow mangrove-fringed channel. The wilderness character of the island — no roads, no permanent accommodation, access by boat — means that visitor pressure is low. The island's rainforest holds Southern Cassowary. The mangroves of Hinchinbrook Channel are among the most pristine in Australia and hold Mangrove Robin, Little Kingfisher, and Large-billed Gerygone. The Thorsborne Trail, the island's famous multi-day walking route, passes through a complete cross-section of the island's habitats. Red-tailed Black Cockatoo occurs in the drier woodland on the island's northern tip.

## 10. Karumba Wetlands, Gulf Country

Karumba on the Gulf of Carpentaria is a remote fishing town at the mouth of the Norman River that provides access to extensive wetlands, mudflats, and mangroves of the Gulf coastal zone. Jabiru (Black-necked Stork) nests in the riverine woodland. The mudflats at low tide attract large numbers of migratory shorebirds in the non-breeding season. Far Eastern Curlew — globally endangered — is regularly recorded here. The Norman River mouth supports the largest population of Freshwater Crocodile in Queensland, and boat trips combine crocodile watching with waterbird viewing. The remote Gulf Country wetlands are at their most productive at the end of the dry season in October-November, just before the monsoon.

---

Queensland's sheer size means that different regions offer very different experiences, from the accessible rainforest of Cairns to the genuinely remote Iron Range and the Gulf wetlands. The state's birding community is active and well-organised, with regional bird clubs and guided tours available for most areas.

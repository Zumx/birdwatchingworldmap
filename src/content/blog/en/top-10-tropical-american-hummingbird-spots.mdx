Hummingbirds (family Trochilidae) are entirely restricted to the Americas, with 366 described species occurring from Alaska (Rufous Hummingbird, a summer visitor) to Tierra del Fuego (Green-backed Firecrown, Sephanoides sephaniodes, a resident of Andean beech forest). The family's center of diversity is the Andes and Andean cloud forest; a single day at a productive Ecuadorian or Colombian cloud forest lodge with multiple feeding stations can produce 20 to 30 species. The Sword-billed Hummingbird (Ensifera ensifera) has a bill longer than its body to access the corolla tubes of specific Passiflora flowers; the Marvelous Spatuletail (Loddigesia mirabilis, Endangered) has display plumes that constitute a significant fraction of its total body length. The following ten sites combine hummingbird species richness with accessibility.

## 1. Mindo Valley and Tandayapa, Ecuador

Mindo is the global center of accessible hummingbird diversity. The Tandayapa Bird Lodge, Refugio Paz de las Aves, and La Ruta de las Aves lodges collectively maintain hummingbird feeder arrays that attract up to 30 species in a single morning. Velvet-purple Coronet (Boissonneaua jardini), Booted Racket-tail (Ocreatus underwoodii), and the spectacular Long-tailed Sylph (Aglaiocercus kingii) are among the Mindo specialties. The cloud forest habitat above 1,500 metres provides the most species-rich zone.

## 2. Milpe Bird Sanctuary, Ecuador

Milpe, managed by Mindo Cloudforest Foundation, covers the foothill zone between Mindo and the Pacific lowland at 700 metres. The hummingbird community includes lowland species absent from the highland lodges: Green Thorntail (Discosura conversii), Club-winged Manakin (Machaeropterus deliciosus), and several species at the lower elevation boundary of the Andean cloud forest community.

## 3. Bosque de Paz, Costa Rica

Bosque de Paz at 1,800 metres on the Cordillera Central provides the Talamancan cloud forest hummingbird community. Coppery-headed Emerald (Elvira cupreiceps, endemic to Costa Rica) and Purple-throated Mountain-gem (Lampornis calolaemus) are among the 12 to 15 species attracted to the lodge's feeder arrays. The combination of endemic Costa Rican species and cloud forest quality makes this one of the best single sites in Central America.

## 4. Savegre Valley, Costa Rica

San Gerardo de Dota at 2,000 metres holds the highland hummingbird community of the Talamancan range. Fiery-throated Hummingbird (Panterpe insignis, endemic to Costa Rica and western Panama) and the Scintillant Hummingbird (Selasphorus scintilla) are characteristic. The valley's combination of quetzal and hummingbird diversity makes it Costa Rica's most productive single site.

## 5. San Isidro Lodge, Ecuador

The eastern Andes at San Isidro produce a different hummingbird community from the western Mindo zone: Napo Sabrewing (Campylopterus villaviscensio), Rufous-vented Whitetip (Urosticte ruficrissa), and Violet-fronted Brilliant (Heliodoxa leadbeateri) are eastern-slope species accessible here.

## 6. Cerro San Luis, Colombia (near Bogotá)

The Chingaza reserve complex in the Cordillera Oriental near Bogotá holds the Bogotá Sunangel (Heliangelus zusii, only known from one specimen; the area holds the related Longuemare's Sunangel Heliangelus clarisse, now split). The Bogotá region cloud forest and its approach from the capital make this the most accessible Colombian hummingbird site.

## 7. Paz de las Aves, Ecuador

Alex Bloch's Paz de las Aves reserve above Mindo is famous as the site where Antpitta feeding was developed as a tourism model, but the reserve's hummingbird feeders are equally spectacular. Empress Brilliant (Heliodoxa imperatrix), White-whiskered Hermit (Phaethornis yaruqui), and Club-winged Manakin occur alongside a full complement of Mindo cloud forest species.

## 8. Podocarpus National Park, Ecuador

Podocarpus in southern Ecuador's Loja province covers the transitional zone between the northern and southern Andes hummingbird communities. White-throated Daggerbill (Schistes albogularis), Greenish Puffleg (Haplophaedia aureliae), and Gorgeted Sunangel (Heliangelus strophianus) represent the southern Ecuador complement.

## 9. Pico Blanco, Colombia

The western Andes of Antioquia around Jardín and Cauca hold a distinctive cloud forest community with some of the most range-restricted and sought Colombian hummingbirds. Turquoise-throated Puffleg (Eriocnemis godini, Critically Endangered, possibly extinct) was recorded here; more reliably the Dusky Starfrontlet (Coeligena orina, Critically Endangered) has been seen near Urrao.

## 10. La Paz Cloud Forest, Bolivia

The Bolivian Yungas east of La Paz hold the southernmost expression of the Andean cloud forest hummingbird radiation. Gould's Jewelfront (Heliodoxa aurescens), Amethyst-throated Sunangel (Heliangelus amethysticollis), and the giant Great Sapphirewing (Pterophanes cyanopterus) — the world's second-largest hummingbird — occur in the accessible forest sections above Coroico.

## Planning hummingbird-focused trips in Tropical America

Hummingbirds in Tropical America are present year-round at most of the sites above, but species composition and diversity peaks vary by elevation and season. In Andean cloud forest, the highest diversity occurs when flowering resources are most abundant — which in Ecuador and Colombia typically means the drier months (June to August and December to February) when some species move altitudinally to follow food resources. The feeder stations at Mindo, Tandayapa, and San Isidro maintain artificial food year-round, effectively decoupling hummingbird accessibility from seasonal flowering patterns.

The Andean cloud forest zone between 1,000 and 2,500 metres elevation holds the highest hummingbird diversity. Species richness drops at both lower elevations (where the Trochilidae are replaced by fewer but more widespread generalist species) and higher elevations (where only cold-tolerant sunangels, mountain-gems, and the giant Great Sapphirewing maintain territories above the treeline). Planning an Andean hummingbird trip should prioritize the middle elevation zone and build in time at both east-slope and west-slope sites — the Andean divide separates distinct hummingbird communities.

At the feeder stations, patience and positioning matter more than timing. Most feeding activity occurs in the first two hours after dawn and the last hour before dusk; midday activity is lower as birds rest in the canopy. Positioning at 45 degrees to the feeders with the sun behind the observer maximizes the iridescence that makes hummingbird identification and photography most rewarding — the gorget colors that identify Fiery-throated Hummingbird or Velvet-purple Coronet are only visible when the angle of light reflection is favorable.

The most practical itinerary for a first hummingbird-focused trip to Ecuador: three nights at Tandayapa, two nights at San Isidro (eastern slope), two nights at Bellavista or equivalent at 2,000 metres for the high-elevation species. This covers approximately 25 to 30 species at reasonable close range. Costa Rica's Bosque de Paz and Savegre Valley can be incorporated into a separate 7-10 day itinerary that adds the Central American endemic complement.

## Hummingbird feeders and finding species in the field

The most efficient way to see a large number of hummingbird species in the Neotropics is to visit established feeder stations in cloud forest lodges. The feeders concentrate species that would otherwise require hours of forest walking to encounter individually. Many cloud forest lodges in Ecuador and Costa Rica maintain twelve to twenty feeders attracting ten to fifteen species simultaneously within a few metres — the visual density of these feeding stations produces a uniquely satisfying field experience impossible in most other bird groups.

Finding hummingbirds away from feeders requires knowledge of which flowering plants are currently in bloom. Hummingbirds track nectar resources closely; a heliconia patch in full flower in the lowland forest attracts Rufous-tailed Hummingbird and Long-billed Hermit (Phaethornis longirostris) regardless of feeder proximity. Local guides who know which plants are currently flowering guide clients to active feeding locations that can produce better views than a crowded feeder.

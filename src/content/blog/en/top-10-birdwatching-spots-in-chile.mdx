Chile is one of the most geographically extreme countries on earth. Its north is one of the driest places on earth; its south receives among the highest annual rainfall. The Andes form its eastern border along the entire length, from sea-level viewpoints to 6,893 metre peaks. The Humboldt Current running north along the Pacific coast drives marine productivity that supports one of the world's richest seabird communities. The result for birders is a country of extraordinary habitat variety with species from Andean flamingos and puna plovers in the north to Magellanic Penguins and steamer ducks in the south. Several locations from the map database — aviaries and birdwatching observation points — reflect Chile's growing birding infrastructure.

## 1. Lauca National Park, Arica y Parinacota

Lauca, at 4,500 metres on the altiplano near the Bolivian border, is Chile's premier high-altitude birding site. Andean Flamingo (Phoenicoparrus andinus, Vulnerable) and James's Flamingo (Phoenicoparrus jamesi, Near Threatened) breed at Lago Chungará — the world's highest navigable lake. Puna Rhea (Rhea pennata tarapacensis), Giant Coot (Fulica gigantea), and Andean Avocet share the altiplano wetlands. The road through Lauca from Arica to Putre crosses successively higher elevations, each with distinct species.

## 2. Torres del Paine National Park, Magallanes

Torres del Paine is Chile's most visited national park and one of the most photographically celebrated. The bird community reflects the Patagonian steppe-to-montane gradient: Andean Condor (Vultur gryphus) circles the towers daily; Torrent Duck (Merganetta armata) occupies every fast-flowing river section; Magellanic Woodpecker (Campephilus magellanicus) drums in the lenga beech forest. The iconic Grey-headed Duck (Heteronetta atricapilla) and Kelp Goose (Chloephaga hybrida) occupy the lake shores.

## 3. Chiloé Island, Los Lagos

The large island of Chiloé in the Lake District holds the most important overwintering concentration of shorebirds in Chile. Hudsonian Godwit (Limosa haemastica) winters in hundreds of thousands on the tidal flats of the Mechuque Archipelago and the Quicaví mudflats — one of the most important shorebird wintering sites in the Americas. Magellanic Penguin (Spheniscus magellanicus) breeds on Puñihuil Island; Black-necked Swan (Cygnus melancoryphus) occupies the estuaries.

## 4. Atacama Coast and Pan de Azúcar National Park

The Atacama coast between Antofagasta and Caldera is the most accessible section of the Humboldt Current seabird system. Humboldt Penguin (Spheniscus humboldti, Vulnerable) breeds at Punta de Choros and Pan de Azúcar. Peruvian Pelican (Pelecanus thagus), Peruvian Booby (Sula variegata), and Inca Tern (Larosterna inca) are characteristic of this cold-current coast. The Observatorio Tricahues in the Atacama region (documented in the map database) covers the inland canyon habitat of the Burrowing Parrot (Myiopsitta monachus).

## 5. Río Maipo Valley and Cajón del Maipo

The Maipo Valley east of Santiago provides access to central Chilean scrub and Andean foothill habitat within day range of the capital. Chilean Tinamou (Nothoprocta perdicaria), Moustached Turca (Pteroptochos megapodius), and Dusky Tapaculo (Scytalopus fuscus) are endemic tapaculos of central Chile found in the matorral scrub. Cajón del Maipo continues into the high Andes where Diademed Sandpiper-Plover and White-winged Diuca Finch occupy alpine streams.

## 6. Conguillío National Park, Araucanía

Conguillío protects the Araucaria (monkey puzzle) forest of the Chilean Lake District — a habitat type restricted to this region of Chile and adjacent Argentina. Chilean Pigeon (Patagioenas araucana) and Magellanic Horned Owl (Bubo magellanicus) occur in the Araucaria zone. The park's pehuén forest is among the most atmospheric birding settings in Chile.

## 7. Valdes Peninsula and Golfo Dulce, coastal Chile

The coastal wetlands of southern Chile, particularly around Puerto Montt and Chiloé's inner channels, hold the full southern waterbird community and are among the most productive sites for steamer ducks (Tachyeres spp.) in Chile.

## 8. Cape Horn National Park and Navarino Island

The subantarctic forests of Navarino Island hold the Magellanic forest community at its southernmost: Thorn-tailed Rayadito, Des Murs' Wiretail, and the southernmost Rufous-tailed Hawk (Buteo ventralis) nesting records. Weddell's Sparrow (Rhynchospiza stolzmanni) and the austral versions of several common South American species reach their range limits here.

## 9. Juan Fernández Archipelago

The remote oceanic islands 670 kilometres off Valparaíso hold three IUCN-threatened endemic landbird species: Juan Fernández Firecrown (Sephanoides fernandensis, Critically Endangered), Masafuera Rayadito (Aphrastura masafuerae, Critically Endangered), and Juan Fernández Tit-Tyrant (Anairetes fernandezianus, Vulnerable). Access by charter flight or ferry from Valparaíso; permit required.

## 10. Aviario Tricao, Valparaíso Region

Aviario Tricao (documented in the map database) is a large private aviarium near San Antonio in Valparaíso Region that maintains a collection of South American species in semi-free conditions. While not a wild site, it provides accessible views of species otherwise requiring remote expedition and has an educational function for domestic ecotourism.

## Planning a Chile birding trip

Chile's geography — a 4,300-kilometre ribbon between the Andes and the Pacific — means that birding the country requires moving along its extreme north-south axis. A comprehensive Chile trip must choose whether to prioritize the Atacama Desert and altiplano in the north, the temperate forests and lakes in the south, or the subantarctic zones further south still.

The Atacama altiplano of Region I and II (accessible from Arica and Iquique) is the most productive single region for concentrated endemic and near-endemic species. The flamingo lakes of Surire, Lauca National Park, and the Salar de Atacama hold all three South American flamingo species simultaneously — Greater, Andean, and James's — alongside a suite of high-altitude shorebirds and passerines found nowhere outside the puna zone. Arica's sea cliffs and fishing port are excellent for Peruvian Pelican (Pelecanus thagus) and Humboldt Penguin (Spheniscus humboldti, Vulnerable). Arica is served by regular domestic flights from Santiago.

Central Chile around Santiago provides the most accessible Mediterranean-climate endemic community: Dusky-tailed Canastero, Moustached Turca (Pteroptochos megapodius), and the full Chilean scrub-bird community. The Maipo Valley wine country immediately south of Santiago holds Chilean endemic species in the riverine scrub; La Campana National Park (two hours north of Santiago) is the primary site for the rare Rufous-tailed Plantcutter (Phytotoma rara).

Patagonian Chile — around Puerto Montt, Chiloé Island, and the Carretera Austral — holds the Magellanic Plover (Pluvianellus socialis, Near Threatened), the breeding Magellanic Woodpecker, and the Chilean Forest community. The Magellanic Penguin colonies at Magdalena Island (accessible from Punta Arenas) are the southernmost accessible in Chile. Torres del Paine National Park provides the iconic Patagonian scenery alongside Andean Condor (Vultur gryphus, Vulnerable).

Santiago's Arturo Merino Benítez airport serves as the main entry hub. Domestic flights to Arica, Puerto Montt, and Punta Arenas connect to the three main birding regions without the need for multi-day drives through unpopulated steppe and desert.

## Birding in Santiago and central Chile

Santiago's immediate surroundings offer productive birding without leaving the metropolitan region. The Río Maipo canyon and the Farellones highland road east of Santiago provide access to Andean Condor sightings within one and a half hours of the city centre. The Reserva Nacional El Yali wetland, two hours southwest of Santiago, holds Chilean Flamingo (Phoenicopterus chilensis, Near Threatened) and the full coastal waterbird community. The coastal town of Zapallar, 160 kilometres north, is the most reliable site for the endemic Seaside Cinclodes (Cinclodes nigrofumosus) — a coastal passerine found only on rocky shores in central Chile.

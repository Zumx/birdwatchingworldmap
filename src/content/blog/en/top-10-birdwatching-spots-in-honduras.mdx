Honduras is one of Central America's quietest birding secrets, a country of more than 770 recorded species packed into a landscape that climbs from steamy Caribbean lowlands to cloud forests above 2,800 metres in a matter of hours. The drawcard for many visitors is the Honduran Emerald, a hummingbird found nowhere else on Earth, clinging to the thorn scrub of the interior dry valleys. But beyond that single endemic lies an extraordinary cross-section of the New World tropics, where Resplendent Quetzals share ridgelines with Keel-billed Toucans and the dawn chorus mixes manakin snaps with the bell-like calls of bellbirds. The infrastructure is modest, the crowds are thin, and the rewards are correspondingly large.

## Pico Bonito National Park

Rising abruptly from the Caribbean coast near La Ceiba, Pico Bonito climbs from sea level to 2,435 metres in a single dramatic sweep. The Lodge at Pico Bonito is the most comfortable base in the country and one of the easiest places anywhere to watch lowland rainforest birds. Feeders and fruiting trees around the grounds attract Collared Aracaris, Keel-billed Toucans, and a constant rotation of tanagers and honeycreepers, while forest trails produce Lovely Cotinga, Great Tinamou, and the localised Keel-billed Motmot. The elevational gradient means a single property spans several life zones.

## Cusuco National Park

In the Merendón mountains above San Pedro Sula, Cusuco protects a dwarf cloud forest draped in moss and bromeliads. This is the most reliable site in Honduras for the Resplendent Quetzal, whose males trail iridescent emerald tail streamers nearly a metre long during the March-to-June breeding season. The park is also a stronghold for the Honduran subspecies of the endangered Exquisite Spike-thumb Frog, a reminder that the birding here sits inside one of Central America's most important biodiversity arks. Expect Wine-throated Hummingbird, Black-throated Jay, and Bushy-crested Jay along the steep trails.

## Lago de Yojoa

Honduras's largest natural lake, set between San Pedro Sula and Tegucigalpa, has tallied more than 400 species around its shores and is a justly famous birding magnet. The marshes, coffee plantations, and surrounding forest at sites like the D&D Brewery and the Panacam Lodge in Cerro Azul Meámbar National Park make for relaxed, productive watching. Northern Jacanas pick across the lily pads, Keel-billed Toucans call from the canopy, and the reedbeds hold Pinnated Bittern and Ruddy Crake. Boat trips at dawn add herons, kingfishers, and the occasional Sungrebe.

## The Aguán Valley and the Honduran Emerald

The dry interior valleys of the Aguán and Agalta hold the country's signature endemic, the Honduran Emerald. This small hummingbird depends entirely on the increasingly scarce thorn forest of the rain-shadow valleys, and reserves such as the one established near Olanchito have become essential to its survival. A morning spent scanning flowering Aphelandra and acacia in this arid scrub also turns up Lesser Roadrunner, White-bellied Wren, and Salvin's Emerald, a community of dry-country birds found in few other parts of the country.

## Celaque National Park

Celaque shelters Honduras's highest peak, Cerro Las Minas at 2,870 metres, and one of the best-preserved cloud forests in the country, near the colonial town of Gracias in the western highlands. The cool, dripping oak and pine forest holds Resplendent Quetzal, Emerald Toucanet, Mountain Trogon, and the elusive Fulvous Owl. Higher up, the stunted elfin forest is home to Wine-throated Hummingbird and Garnet-throated Hummingbird, and the trail to the summit passes through habitat that feels closer to the temperate world than the tropics.

## Cerro Azul Meámbar (PANACAM)

Overlooking Lago de Yojoa, this national park combines cloud forest, waterfalls, and a well-run lodge that has become a favourite among visiting birders. The trail network reaches into mid-elevation forest holding Tody Motmot, White-eared Ground-Sparrow, and a strong cast of furnariids and antbirds. The lodge gardens and hummingbird feeders are excellent, drawing Violet Sabrewing, Green-breasted Mango, and Stripe-tailed Hummingbird within easy camera range.

## La Tigra National Park

Just outside Tegucigalpa, La Tigra was Honduras's first national park and remains the most accessible cloud forest for anyone passing through the capital. The cool pine-oak and cloud forest, threaded with old mining trails, holds Resplendent Quetzal, Blue-throated Motmot, Singing Quail, and Rufous-browed Wren. Its proximity to a city of more than a million people makes it remarkable that quetzals still display here each spring.

## The Mosquitia and Río Plátano

The vast Río Plátano Biosphere Reserve in eastern Honduras is one of Central America's last great wildernesses, a UNESCO World Heritage Site of rainforest, savanna, and river. Reaching it requires real commitment, usually by light aircraft and dugout canoe, but the rewards include Harpy Eagle, Scarlet Macaw, Great Curassow, and Agami Heron in habitat barely touched by roads. This is birding for the adventurous, and a glimpse of how much of the country looked before settlement.

## The Bay Islands

Roatán, Utila, and Guanaja, strung along the Caribbean offshore, are better known for diving but offer rewarding coastal and migrant birding. The mangroves and lagoons of Roatán hold Yellow-naped Amazon and the endemic Roatán subspecies of several species, while spring and autumn passage brings warblers and shorebirds funnelling along the Caribbean coast. Combining reef and ridge makes the islands a relaxed coda to a mainland trip.

## Copán and the Western Borderlands

The famous Maya ruins of Copán, near the Guatemalan border, sit in a landscape of coffee farms, river forest, and the macaw breeding programme at Macaw Mountain. Scarlet Macaws, reintroduced to the archaeological site, now fly free over the stelae, and the surrounding forest holds Turquoise-browed Motmot, Blue-crowned Motmot, and a fine range of dry-forest species. Pairing archaeology with birding makes Copán an ideal end point.

## When to Go and How to Plan

The dry season from December to April offers the most reliable weather and coincides with the quetzal breeding display in the highland cloud forests, making it the prime window for a first visit. Resident birds are present year-round, while northern migrants swell the lists from October to March. Roads can be slow and a local guide is invaluable, both for finding skulking species and for navigating logistics in a country still developing its birding infrastructure.

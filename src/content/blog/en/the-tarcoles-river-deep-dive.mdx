The Rio Tarcoles meets the Pacific on Costa Rica's central coast in a broad, muddy estuary fringed with mangroves, and from the moment a small boat noses out from the dock at the village of Tarcoles, the birds are everywhere. A roseate spoonbill sweeps its bill through the shallows, a bare-throated tiger-heron freezes against the bank, and somewhere a mangrove hummingbird buzzes among the salt-tolerant trees. Then the guide cuts the engine beside a mudbank and points to a row of American crocodiles, some of them well over four metres long, basking with their jaws agape. Few birding boat trips anywhere combine such accessible diversity with such genuine drama.

## A River Between the Mountains and the Sea

The Tarcoles drains a large basin on the Pacific slope, gathering water from the Central Valley and the mountains above San Jose before spilling into the Gulf of Nicoya. It passes beneath the busy Costanera highway at the Tarcoles bridge, a famous roadside stop where travellers pull over to peer down at the crocodiles massed on the sandbars below. Downstream the river slows and widens into the estuary and the mangrove channels of the Guacalillo wetlands, while just inland rise the forested hills of Carara National Park. This is a transition zone where the dry forest of the north meets the humid forest of the south, and that overlap is precisely why the birding is so rich.

## The Estuary Boat Trip

The classic Tarcoles experience is the mangrove boat trip, run by local operators out of the riverside village. Boats leave most productively in the early morning or late afternoon, idling slowly through the main channel and into narrow mangrove creeks where the canopy closes overhead. Herons and egrets are the constant backdrop: little blue heron, tricolored heron, yellow-crowned night-heron, boat-billed heron roosting in shadowed branches, and the strikingly patterned bare-throated tiger-heron. Common black hawks perch over the water, and as the tide shifts, the mudflats fill with whimbrels, willets, spotted sandpipers, and other migrant shorebirds. A patient guide will often find the rarer prizes hiding in the tangle.

## Mangrove Specialists

The reason serious birders make the trip is the suite of mangrove specialists that are difficult to see anywhere else in Costa Rica. The mangrove hummingbird, a Costa Rican near-endemic restricted to Pacific mangroves, feeds on the flowers of the tea mangrove. The American pygmy kingfisher, smallest of the region's five kingfishers, perches low over shaded creeks, while the green, Amazon, ringed, and belted kingfishers complete a near-clean sweep of the family on a single trip. Other mangrove targets include the Panama flycatcher, the rufous-necked wood-rail stalking the exposed mud, and the elusive mangrove (yellow) warbler. Northern scrub-flycatchers and prothonotary warblers add to the haul in the boreal winter months.

## Crocodiles and the River's Other Wildlife

The American crocodiles of the Tarcoles are legitimately among the largest concentrations of big crocodiles accessible to ordinary visitors anywhere. The animals gather on the sandbars below the bridge and along the estuary, and several of the largest individuals are well known to local guides. While the once-common practice of guides feeding crocodiles to draw them close has rightly fallen out of favour for safety and welfare reasons, the animals are easily seen behaving naturally from the boats and the bridge. The river also supports green iguanas draped in the riverside trees, basilisk lizards skittering across the surface, white-faced capuchin and howler monkeys in the gallery forest, and, with luck, an otter or a coati along the banks.

## Carara National Park Next Door

No Tarcoles birding trip is complete without time in Carara National Park, whose entrance sits beside the river just east of the bridge. Carara is the most reliable place in the country to see wild scarlet macaws, which fly noisily over the canopy morning and evening and nest in the tall trees; the population here is a conservation success after decades of decline from poaching. The park's well-maintained trails, including the wheelchair-accessible Sendero Universal, run through transitional lowland forest holding trogons, motmots, manakins, antbirds, woodcreepers, the great tinamou, and, on the forest floor, the spectacular but secretive birds that draw photographers from around the world. The Laguna Meandrica trail follows an old river oxbow rich in waterbirds.

## Planning the Visit

The Tarcoles lies a little over an hour and a half by road from San Jose, on the way to the central Pacific beach town of Jaco, which makes it one of the easiest world-class birding destinations to reach in Costa Rica. The dry season, roughly December to April, brings the most comfortable conditions and the addition of North American migrants, while the green season delivers lush forest, fewer visitors, and dramatic afternoon skies, with mornings usually still clear. Boat trips can be booked directly at the village dock or through lodges in the area; an early start is essential both for bird activity and for cooler, calmer water. Bring sun protection, water, and binoculars suited to both the open estuary and the shaded mangrove channels.

## Why the Tarcoles Rewards a Return

What makes the Tarcoles special is the compression of so many habitats into a single morning: open estuary, mangrove channel, sandbar, gallery forest, and the transitional rainforest of Carara, all within a few kilometres. A single well-timed visit can produce well over a hundred species, from the gaudy scarlet macaw to the secretive rufous-necked wood-rail, with crocodiles and monkeys thrown in. It is a place that rewards both the first-time visitor chasing a big day list and the returning birder seeking out the harder mangrove specialists, and it remains one of the cornerstone stops on any Costa Rican birding itinerary.

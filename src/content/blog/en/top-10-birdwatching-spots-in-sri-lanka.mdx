Sri Lanka is one of South Asia's most important birding destinations, combining exceptional endemic species diversity with a compact size that allows a thorough country survey in two to three weeks. The island has 34 endemic bird species — a remarkable total given its area — and these endemics are distributed across radically different habitats: lowland wet-zone rainforest, central highland forest, dry-zone scrub, and coastal wetland. Several of Sri Lanka's endemics were formally described only in recent decades: the Serendib Scops Owl was not confirmed as a distinct species until 2001.

Sri Lanka's position in the Indian Ocean just south of the tip of India places it on the migratory route between Central and South Asia and the southern hemisphere. Winter brings numerous Palearctic migrants, with waders on the coastal wetlands, raptors over the highlands, and warblers in every scrubby patch. The birding infrastructure is well-developed, with specialist guides, comfortable guesthouses near key sites, and detailed field guides and site guides available. The dry zone — roughly the northern two-thirds of the island — is most accessible during November to April.

## 1. Sinharaja Forest Reserve, Ratnapura and Galle Districts

Sinharaja is the last viable tract of primary lowland rainforest in Sri Lanka and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is the single most important site on the island for endemic birds, holding nearly all the wet-zone endemics in dense proximity. The forest produces characteristic mixed-species flocks led by Orange-billed Babbler — a striking, chestnut and black babbler endemic to the island — that move through the forest and attract following species. Sri Lanka Blue Magpie, Green-billed Coucal, Sri Lanka Spurfowl, and Ashy-headed Laughingthrush are all found here. The trails at dawn are exceptionally productive, and local guides based in the villages around the forest boundary are essential for navigating the dense interior.

## 2. Horton Plains National Park, Nuwara Eliya District

Horton Plains sits at 2,100 to 2,300 metres on the central highland plateau and is the core area for Sri Lanka's montane endemic birds. The park is dramatically open — wet montane grassland and patanas alternating with patches of montane forest — giving excellent visibility for highland species. Sri Lanka Bush Warbler inhabits the dense grass tussocks and sings from low perches. Indian Blue Robin winters in the montane undergrowth. Yellow-eared Bulbul — one of the island's most charming endemics — feeds in the forest canopy. Dull-blue Flycatcher inhabits the forest interior. The cliff edge at World's End provides views across the lowland dry zone 3,000 metres below, with raptors soaring at eye level. Leopard is frequently seen on the park roads.

## 3. Yala National Park, Hambantota and Monaragala Districts

Yala is Sri Lanka's most famous wildlife park and simultaneously one of its most productive birding sites. The dry zone thorn forest, coastal lagoon, and scrubland hold species distinct from the wet-zone forest. Sri Lanka Junglefowl — the national bird, a spectacular red and green pheasant — walks along the park tracks in conspicuous groups. Sri Lanka Grey Hornbill is conspicuous in the woodland canopy. The coastal lagoon at Yala's Block 1 attracts flamingos, painted storks, and waders in large numbers. Crested Serpent Eagle perches on exposed branches throughout the park. The high density of Leopard in Yala means that safaris routinely produce excellent mammal viewing alongside the birds.

## 4. Bundala National Park, Hambantota District

Bundala is a Ramsar wetland on the southern dry-zone coast and one of the most important waterbird sites in Sri Lanka. Lesser Flamingo is far more regular at Bundala than at most South Asian sites. Greater Flamingo is common. The lagoons hold Eurasian Spoonbill, Painted Stork, Asian Openbill, and Black-headed Ibis in large numbers. Pied Avocet, Marsh Sandpiper, and various other waders are abundant during the October to March non-breeding season. The park also holds breeding Sri Lanka Junglefowl and Sri Lanka Grey Hornbill. Elephant herds visit the lagoon shores at dusk. The proximity of Bundala to Yala means both parks can be visited on a single southern Sri Lanka circuit.

## 5. Sinharaja Buffer Zone and Kudawa Area

The villages and forest edge around the Kudawa entrance to Sinharaja hold species not found in the primary forest interior, making this area complementary to the reserve itself. The secondary growth and garden habitat near Kudawa guesthouse provides excellent views of Crimson-backed Flameback — the endemic Sri Lankan woodpecker with a crimson back and golden crown — feeding on the bark of cultivated trees at close range. Sri Lanka White-eye and Sri Lanka Hanging Parrot inhabit the village gardens. Red-faced Malkoha inhabits the forest edge scrub. The forest streams at dawn produce Sri Lanka Frogmouth — a large, cryptically patterned nightjar relative that roosts in forest branches — reliably found by specialist local guides.

## 6. Kitulgala Forest, Kegalle District

The accessible riverine forest at Kitulgala on the Kelani River, famous as the filming location for the Bridge on the River Kwai, holds a productive bird community within easy reach of Colombo. Serendib Scops Owl — one of Asia's most recently described owl species, confirmed as distinct from Oriental Scops Owl only in 2001 — inhabits the dense riverine forest here and is the primary target species for visiting birders. The owl calls from dense undergrowth after dusk and requires patience and specialist guide knowledge to locate. The forest also holds Sri Lanka Spurfowl, Brown-capped Babbler, and Green-billed Coucal. White-throated Kingfisher is conspicuous along the river.

## 7. Udawalawe National Park, Sabaragamuwa and Uva Provinces

Udawalawe is centred on a large reservoir surrounded by open grassland, thorn scrub, and woodland. The site is the premier location in Sri Lanka for the Asian Elephant — the park's elephant density is exceptionally high — but the bird community is also very good, with grassland and wetland species dominant. Indian Pond Heron, White-breasted Waterhen, and Pheasant-tailed Jacana are common in the reservoir margins. Crested Hawk-Eagle perches on tall trees at the forest edge. The open grassland holds Streak-throated Swallow in large roosting flocks. Black-shouldered Kite hunts over the open ground. The wildlife safari approach from Embilipitiya covers both the elephants and a wide range of wetland and grassland birds.

## 8. Knuckles Mountain Range, Kandy and Matale Districts

The Knuckles Range, northeast of Kandy, holds a distinctive intermediate-altitude bird community between the lowland wet forest and the high-altitude patana grassland. The range is one of the most important sites in Sri Lanka for Dull-blue Flycatcher and Sri Lanka Bush Warbler at the lower altitudinal limit of both species. Layard's Parakeet — a small endemic parakeet with a short tail — inhabits the forest here at mid-altitude, where it is easier to observe than in the denser Sinharaja forest. The Knuckles Wilderness Area trails pass through cloud forest that holds Sri Lanka Hill Myna and Brown-capped Babbler. The area is accessible from Kandy and offers a less-visited alternative to Horton Plains for highland forest birding.

## 9. Adam's Peak (Sri Pada) and surrounding forests

The pilgrimage peak of Adam's Peak is surrounded by secondary forest and tea estate habitat in the central highlands. The walk up the pilgrimage trail passes through forest that holds Sri Lanka White-eye, Yellow-eared Bulbul, and Indian Black Robin at various altitudes. The forest near the base of the peak around Ratnapura holds Sri Lanka Junglefowl and Red-faced Malkoha in the denser scrub. The adjacent Bogawantalawa Valley tea estates, while managed landscape, attract Indian Roller and Brahminy Kite. The Suriyakanda Forest Reserve near the base of the peak holds good rainforest bird assemblages and has been the site of Sri Lanka Frogmouth records.

## 10. Mannar Island and Giant's Tank, North Western Province

Mannar Island off the northwest coast holds the most diverse shorebird community in Sri Lanka during the October to April non-breeding season. The island's shallow tidal flats attract Broad-billed Sandpiper — a species with a scattered Asian distribution — in numbers unusual for Sri Lanka. Crab Plover is an annual visitor to the Mannar tidal flats. The nearby Giant's Tank — a large ancient irrigation reservoir — holds Painted Stork, Black-headed Ibis, and Large-billed Tern. Lesser Flamingo occurs at the Giant's Tank during drier periods when water levels drop. The adjacent dry-zone scrub holds Indian Grey Hornbill and Bay-backed Shrike.

---

Sri Lanka's endemics are the primary draw for visiting birders, and they are distributed across enough distinct habitat types to require a structured tour combining rainforest, highlands, and dry-zone savannah. The island is compact enough that a two-week trip can cover all the major sites.

Pakistan's ornithological richness is one of the best-kept secrets in Asian birding. The country contains an extraordinary range of habitats within its borders — from the highest mountain ranges on Earth in the Karakoram and Hindu Kush, through forested Himalayan valleys and subtropical plains, to the arid Balochistan plateau and the mangrove coast of the Arabian Sea. Over 660 species have been documented. Pakistan lies on major migratory routes connecting Central Asia and Siberia with the Indian Subcontinent and Arabia, making it a critical transit and wintering country for waterbirds, raptors, and passerines.

The Siberian Crane historically wintered at the Keoladeo wetlands in India but now appears essentially extinct as a wild migrant in the region. The Indus Delta and the Thar Desert wetlands, however, remain globally significant for other migratory species. The northern Himalayan valleys are among the least ornithologically explored areas in the entire Western Palearctic, and expeditions to the Swat Valley, Gilgit-Baltistan, and Chitral continue to document species and ranges that extend avifaunal knowledge. Specialist tour operators work successfully in the northern areas.

## 1. Kirthar National Park, Sindh Province

Kirthar is one of the largest national parks in Pakistan, covering mountain and scrub desert habitat in the hills separating Sindh from Balochistan. The rugged limestone terrain holds breeding Lanner Falcon and Peregrine on the cliff faces. Sind Sparrow — a range-restricted endemic of riparian scrub in the Indus valley — occurs in the valley scrub. The park's open stony plains hold Houbara Bustard, which winters in large numbers across Pakistan's arid zones. Grey Francolin, Painted Francolin, and See-see Partridge are all resident. The drainage lines through the hills attract migrant raptors including Steppe Eagle and Imperial Eagle during autumn passage.

## 2. Taunsa Barrage Wildlife Sanctuary, Punjab Province

The Taunsa Barrage on the Indus River creates an extensive backwater wetland that is one of the most important wintering sites for waterbirds in Pakistan. Sarus Crane — the world's tallest flying bird, the Indian race of which breeds in the Punjab — winters here alongside Common Crane and Demoiselle Crane. Siberian Crane was historically recorded here. The shallow wetlands attract Greylag Goose, Bar-headed Goose, and large numbers of surface-feeding ducks. Pallas's Fish Eagle has been recorded from the larger trees along the river. Indian Skimmer nests on the sandbanks within the sanctuary. The site is one of a network of Indus wetlands that collectively hold enormous waterbird populations.

## 3. Chitral Valley, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Province

The Chitral District in the northwest provides access to some of the finest mountain birding in Pakistan, with habitats ranging from high-altitude steppe through coniferous forest to the poplar-lined valleys at lower elevation. Snow Partridge is found on the rocky slopes above the treeline. Himalayan Snowcock inhabits the highest boulder fields. The Western Tragopan — a globally vulnerable pheasant with spectacular spotted plumage — is present in the forest in the Chitral Gol National Park. Lammergeier soars above the mountain ridges. Tickell's Thrush, White-capped Redstart, and Slaty-backed Flycatcher are among the migrants and residents of the valley floor woodland.

## 4. Deosai National Park, Gilgit-Baltistan

The Deosai Plateau — described as the second-highest plateau on Earth after Tibet — sits at an average elevation of over 4,000 metres and covers approximately 3,000 square kilometres of alpine grassland and bog. In summer, the plateau holds breeding Long-legged Buzzard, Upland Buzzard, and numerous larks and pipits. Red Fox is common, and the park protects one of Pakistan's last Himalayan Brown Bear populations. The short growing season concentrates bird activity into the months of June through August when the snow melts. Gulled Pipit and Water Pipit breed in the bog meadows. Bimaculated Lark inhabits the drier areas. The plateau is reached by dramatic mountain roads from Skardu.

## 5. Chashma Barrage, Punjab Province

Chashma is a major impoundment on the Indus north of Taunsa that creates extensive shallows and reed marsh attracting large waterbird concentrations in winter. Dalmatian Pelican — one of the world's largest flying birds — occurs here in winter. Marbled Duck, a globally vulnerable species with a fragmented range, has been recorded in the backwater shallows. Western Marsh Harrier and Montagu's Harrier hunt over the marsh in winter. Greater Spotted Eagle and Eastern Imperial Eagle are regular winter visitors along the tree-lined river banks. The site is less well-known than Taunsa but complements it as part of an Indus wetlands circuit from Islamabad.

## 6. Nathiagali and Hazara Hill Forests, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa

The pine and oak forests of the Hazara hills above Abbottabad hold a rich community of Himalayan forest birds at accessible altitude. Kalij Pheasant walks through the forest undergrowth in the early morning. Koklass Pheasant is more secretive, heard calling at dusk from the oak woodland. White-crested Laughingthrush inhabits the forest undergrowth in noisy groups. Fire-breasted Flowerpecker feeds at mistletoe berries in the taller trees. Brown-fronted Woodpecker works the bark of the large oaks. Nuthatch species including White-tailed Nuthatch and Chestnut-bellied Nuthatch are present. The resort town of Nathiagali provides accommodation within the forest.

## 7. Haleji Lake, Sindh Province

Haleji is one of South Asia's most important freshwater wetlands, created as a reservoir south of Karachi to supply the city with water. Despite its utilitarian origin, the lake has become a nationally significant bird sanctuary. The reedbeds hold Pheasant-tailed Jacana and Purple Swamphen. Purple Heron breeds in the reedbed. Sarus Crane visits during migration. In winter, the open water holds large flocks of diving ducks including Ferruginous Duck and Tufted Duck. The surrounding scrub produces Hume's Short-toed Lark and Bimaculated Lark. Haleji is reached by road from Karachi and serves as the most productive wetland site near Pakistan's largest city.

## 8. Ayubia National Park, Punjab Province

Ayubia National Park in the Galliat hills north of Islamabad contains dense broadleaf forest of oak, horse chestnut, and blue pine at elevations between 1400 and 2800 metres. Western Tragopan has been confirmed breeding at the upper forest edge. Himalayan Monal — the iridescent national bird of Nepal — is resident in the forest and is best found by listening for its distinctive trumpet calls in the early morning. Monal Pheasant forages in cleared areas near the treeline. Streaked Laughingthrush inhabits the dense undergrowth. The park is also one of the most accessible sites near Islamabad for recording Himalayan passerines including various warblers, flycatchers, and robins during spring and autumn.

## 9. Astola Island, Arabian Sea, Balochistan

Astola Island — Pakistan's only significant offshore island — lies 39 kilometres south of the Makran coast. The uninhabited limestone island holds the largest seabird colony in Pakistan. Sooty Gull breeds in numbers on the rocky slopes. Brown Booby nests on the cliff ledges. Greater Flamingo visits the shallow coastal pools. During migration, the island can be covered in exhausted passerines completing their crossing of the Arabian Sea. Crab Plover is a regular visitor during winter. The island is accessed by fishing boat from Pasni, and permission from the Pakistan Navy is required. The surrounding sea holds Humpback Whale and Dugong.

## 10. Kachura and Shangrila Wetlands, Gilgit-Baltistan

The high-altitude lakes around Skardu in Gilgit-Baltistan, including Kachura Lake and the Shangrila (Lower Kachura) resort, provide mountain lake birding at around 2,400 metres with a backdrop of the Karakoram Range. Bar-headed Goose rests at the lakes during migration, having crossed the Himalayas at extreme altitude. Ruddy Shelduck is resident on the high-altitude lakes and ponds. Common Teal and Common Goldeneye occur in winter. The poplar and willow woodland around the lake shore holds Sulphur-bellied Warbler, White-browed Tit, and Sombre Tit. Himalayan Griffon and Lammergeier soar above the mountain walls surrounding the valley.

Pakistan's birding is best approached with a combination of northern mountain valleys in summer and Indus wetlands in winter. The country's ornithological records are still developing as access to remote areas improves.

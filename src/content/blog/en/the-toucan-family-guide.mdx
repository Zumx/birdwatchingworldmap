The toucans and their relatives — family Ramphastidae — are among the most visually striking birds of the Neotropical rainforest. The enormous, brightly colored bill that defines the family is so disproportionate to the bird's body that it appears structurally improbable, yet it is a model of engineering efficiency: honeycomb-like internal structure provides maximum strength at minimum weight, and the bill's mass is only a fraction of what a solid structure of the same dimensions would weigh. The function of the bill remains partly debated among researchers, but thermoregulation, fruit manipulation, and social signaling through bill pattern and color are all well-supported roles.

Ramphastidae contains 46 species grouped into five genera: Ramphastos (the largest toucans), Pteroglossus (the aracaris), Aulacorhynchus (the toucanets), Selenidera (the dichromatic toucanets), and Andigena (the mountain toucans). They are distributed from Mexico south through Central and South America, with the greatest diversity in the Amazon Basin and the Andes foothills, and they occur in virtually every forest type from lowland Amazonian rainforest to high Andean cloud forest above 3,000 metres.

## The Ramphastos Toucans

The large Ramphastos toucans are the most familiar members of the family and the birds most people picture when they hear the word toucan. The Keel-billed Toucan (Ramphastos sulfuratus) of Central America is the national bird of Belize and one of the most photographed birds in the Americas: its combination of black and yellow body, red undertail, and extraordinary bill in green, orange, yellow, and red is a standard image from ecotourism marketing. In Costa Rica and Panama it is abundant in lowland and mid-elevation forest and is one of the most easily seen species for visiting birders.

The Toco Toucan (Ramphastos toco) is the largest toucan and the one with the largest bill in absolute terms — the orange bill with its black patch at the base can reach 23 centimetres in length, nearly a third of the bird's total body length of around 65 centimetres. Unlike most toucans, the Toco is less dependent on closed forest and occurs in open woodland, forest edge, and savanna-forest mosaic from the Pantanal north through eastern Brazil and the cerrado. It is commonly seen in the Pantanal near the Transpantaneira road and at forest edges throughout central Brazil.

The Channel-billed Toucan and White-throated Toucan are the common large toucans of the Amazon Basin, where they occur alongside each other in lowland forest and are often seen perching in the canopy of emergent trees.

## Aracaris

The aracaris — genus Pteroglossus — are medium-sized, often colorful toucans with serrated bills and typically banded or patterned underparts. They are social birds that roost communally in tree cavities, with several birds sharing a hole, and move through forest in small flocks rather than as solitary pairs. The Collared Aracari (Pteroglossus torquatus) is a common and conspicuous species in Central America; the Chestnut-eared Aracari and the spectacularly colored Many-banded Aracari are among the most colorful Amazonian species.

The Curl-crested Aracari (Pteroglossus beauharnaesii) of the southwestern Amazon has a unique head pattern: glossy, disc-like black feathers on the crown that create an almost artificial appearance. It is most reliably found at clay lick sites in Peru near Puerto Maldonado and in the Madre de Dios region.

## Toucanets

The toucanets of the genus Aulacorhynchus are smaller, predominantly green birds of montane forest in the Andes and Central American highlands. The Emerald Toucanet (Aulacorhynchus prasinus) is the common, widespread species of the Central American highlands, found from Mexico through Costa Rica and western Panama in cloud forest and montane forest. The Blue-banded Toucanet and the Groove-billed Toucanet are among the less-known Andean species found in Bolivia and Peru.

The Dichromatic toucanets of genus Selenidera are forest-interior birds of lowland Amazonian forest, characterized by different head patterns in male and female. The Guianan Toucanet and the Yellow-eared Toucanet are typical species; they are generally less conspicuous than the large toucans and require more patience to observe.

## Mountain Toucans

The mountain toucans of genus Andigena inhabit Andean cloud forest between approximately 1,500 and 3,500 metres elevation. The Gray-breasted Mountain Toucan (Andigena hypoglauca) and Plate-billed Mountain Toucan (Andigena laminirostris) are the most accessible species, occurring in the cloud forests of Ecuador and Colombia at elevations reachable by road from Quito or Bogota. The Black-billed Mountain Toucan ranges from Colombia south to Bolivia and is often encountered on birding tours through the Andes.

These higher-elevation species are associated with Podocarpus and mixed cloud forest, moving along altitudinal bands in search of fruit, and are often encountered at fruiting trees alongside tanagers, barbets, and other montane specialists.

## Feeding and Ecology

Toucans are predominantly frugivorous — fruit-eaters — and play an important ecological role as seed dispersers for large-fruited tree species in Neotropical forests. Large seeds, including those of many palm species, are swallowed whole and defecated intact, often transported considerable distances from the parent tree. The decline of large frugivores — toucans, large cotingas, tapirs — through hunting and habitat loss creates cascading effects on forest regeneration and tree diversity.

Toucans also eat insects, lizard eggs, nestling birds, and other animal material when available, particularly during breeding season when protein requirements are higher.

## Conservation

Most toucan species are not currently threatened, though deforestation reduces population sizes and fragments ranges across the Neotropics. The Saffron Toucanet (Pteroglossus bailloni), restricted to the Atlantic Forest of southeastern Brazil, is Near Threatened due to ongoing habitat loss in this highly fragmented biome. Capture for the pet trade affects accessible populations in several countries.

The key to toucan conservation is the same as for most Neotropical forest birds: maintaining large, connected forest tracts that support the full suite of fruiting tree species these birds require.

Saudi Arabia is one of the last great frontiers of Middle Eastern birding, a country whose recent opening to tourism has put a remarkable avifauna within reach. The kingdom spans the breadth of the Arabian Peninsula, from the juniper-clad mountains of the southwest to the Red Sea coral coast and the immense sand seas of the Empty Quarter. The Asir highlands hold a cluster of Arabian endemics and near-endemics found almost nowhere else; the coastal wetlands gather migrant waders and herons on the Red Sea flyway; and the deserts support a hardy community of larks, sandgrouse, and coursers. The national list exceeds 500 species.

## 1. Raydah Escarpment, Asir

The Raydah Nature Reserve, on the steep western escarpment above Abha, is the single best site in the kingdom for the Arabian endemics. The juniper woodland and terraced slopes hold Arabian Partridge, Arabian Woodpecker, Arabian Wheatear, Yemen Thrush, Yemen Warbler, and the brilliant Yemen Linnet. Arabian Waxbill, Philby's Partridge, and the Arabian Serin frequent the slopes, while Verreaux's Eagle and Bonelli's Eagle patrol the cliffs. This dense pocket of specialties makes Raydah the essential first stop.

## 2. Abha and the Asir highlands

The plateau around Abha, the highest city in Saudi Arabia at around 2,200 metres, gives access to the wider Asir montane community. The Souda mountains above the city, reaching nearly 3,000 metres, hold Arabian Magpie (an isolated and threatened race), Tristram's Starling, Fan-tailed Raven, and African Stonechat. The terraced fields and gardens around Abha are reliable for Arabian Sunbird and Shining Sunbird feeding in flowering aloes.

## 3. Tanoumah and the southern junipers

The juniper forests around Tanoumah, between Abha and Al Bahah, hold the same endemic suite in a less-visited setting. Arabian Woodpecker, Yemen Thrush, and Yemen Warbler are present, and the area is good for Arabian Scops Owl after dark. The cooler, wetter microclimate at altitude supports a more extensive woodland than the surrounding lowlands.

## 4. Jizan and the Tihama lowlands

The Tihama coastal plain around Jizan, in the far southwest, holds Afrotropical species that reach their Arabian limit here. Arabian Golden Sparrow, Singing Bushlark, Zitting Cisticola, and African Collared Dove inhabit the cultivation and acacia scrub. The mangroves and mudflats of the Jizan coast hold Crab-plover, Goliath Heron, and large numbers of migrant and wintering waders.

## 5. Farasan Islands, Red Sea

The Farasan archipelago, a marine protected area in the southern Red Sea, holds important breeding seabird colonies and the endemic Farasan race of Arabian Babbler. White-eyed Gull, Sooty Gull, Bridled Tern, and White-cheeked Tern breed on the islands, while Pink-backed Pelican and Western Reef Heron feed in the lagoons. Brown Booby and Crab-plover occur along the shores.

## 6. Sabya and the southern wetlands

The agricultural lagoons and settling ponds around Sabya and Jizan attract concentrations of waterbirds rare elsewhere in Arabia. Greater Flamingo, Black-winged Stilt, and a wide array of migrant waders use the pools, while Glossy Ibis, Squacco Heron, and Pheasant-tailed Jacana have been recorded. These man-made wetlands are among the most productive birding patches in the south.

## 7. Taif and the western highlands

The highlands around Taif, in the Hejaz mountains east of Mecca, hold a northern outpost of the montane community. Arabian Partridge, Arabian Wheatear, and Tristram's Starling occur in the rocky wadis, while the Al Shafa escarpment is good for Bonelli's Eagle and Long-legged Buzzard. The Taif rose gardens and orchards are productive for migrants in passage seasons.

## 8. Riyadh wetlands and wadis

The capital region offers surprisingly rich birding at its constructed wetlands and the desert wadis nearby. The lagoons at Wadi Hanifah and the settling ponds attract migrant waders, ducks, and herons, while the surrounding desert holds Hoopoe Lark, Greater Hoopoe-Lark, Crowned Sandgrouse, and Cream-coloured Courser. Wadi Namar and the escarpment edge are reliable for desert specialties close to the city.

## 9. Tabuk and the northwestern deserts

The northwest, around Tabuk and the emerging NEOM region, holds the Saharan-Arabian desert community at its richest. Thick-billed Lark, Temminck's Lark, Bar-tailed Lark, and Spotted Sandgrouse inhabit the stony plains, while the rocky jebels hold Sinai Rosefinch, Desert Lark, and Mourning Wheatear. The wadis with palm cultivation concentrate migrants in spring.

## 10. Al Ahsa Oasis, Eastern Province

The vast date-palm oasis of Al Ahsa, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the largest oasis in the world, attracts migrants and holds a distinctive cultivated-land bird community. The springs, channels, and reed beds support Clamorous Reed Warbler, Graceful Prinia, and migrant warblers, while the nearby Gulf coast at Dammam adds Crab-plover, Greater Flamingo, and wintering Socotra Cormorant.

## Seasons across the kingdom

Saudi Arabia's birding seasons are governed by the extreme climate and the position of the peninsula on major flyways. The Asir highlands are best from spring through autumn, when the endemics are active and singing and the high country is cool and green after the summer monsoon rains drifting up from the Indian Ocean. The southwest receives more rain than anywhere else in the kingdom, and the terraced agriculture of the Asir is at its most verdant in late summer, when the breeding endemics are feeding young in the junipers.

Migration transforms the lowlands and coasts. The Red Sea coast is part of one of the world's great soaring-bird flyways, and spring passage from March to May brings raptors, storks, and bee-eaters north along the coastal plain. The same season floods the coastal wetlands and oasis lagoons with passage waders, warblers, and wheatears moving between Africa and Eurasia. Autumn passage from September to November reverses the flow, and the wetlands of the Tihama and the Eastern Province fill with herons, ducks, and waders staging on their southward journey.

Winter is the season for the wetlands and the Gulf coast. Greater Flamingo, Socotra Cormorant, and large numbers of wintering waders gather on the eastern shores, while the constructed wetlands of Riyadh and the southern lagoons hold ducks and herons. The deserts are most comfortably birded in the cooler months from November to March, when the larks, sandgrouse, and coursers can be sought without the punishing summer heat. The desert specialties are resident and present year-round, but the temperatures of June to September make midday birding on the open plains impractical.

## Practicalities for visiting birders

Saudi Arabia's tourist visa, introduced in 2019, has made independent birding travel possible for the first time. A hire car is essential, as the key sites are far apart and public transport does not reach the reserves. The Asir circuit centres on Abha, reachable by domestic flight, with the Raydah Escarpment and the Souda mountains within easy reach. The southwest lowlands and Farasan are accessed from Jizan, while the eastern sites cluster around Dammam and Al Ahsa. Local etiquette and dress codes should be respected, and photography near sensitive infrastructure avoided. The Saudi birding community and visiting tour groups have greatly improved site information in recent years.

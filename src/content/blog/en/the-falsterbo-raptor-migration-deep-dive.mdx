Stand on the dunes at Nabben, the very tip of the Falsterbo peninsula, on a clear morning in early September, and you may watch the sky fill with Honey Buzzards. They come in loose, drifting kettles, hundreds and then thousands, circling up on the first thermals before streaming southwest over the lighthouse and out across the Øresund toward Denmark. This is the moment that draws birders from across Europe to Sweden's southwesternmost corner. Falsterbo, a low spit of sand, pine, and heath at the bottom of the province of Skåne, is one of the continent's great migration bottlenecks, and in autumn it stages one of the finest raptor spectacles north of the Mediterranean.

## Why Falsterbo funnels the migration

The magic of Falsterbo is pure geography. Soaring migrants — raptors, storks, and other broad-winged birds — depend on rising warm air, the thermals that form over land, and they avoid long crossings of open water where no thermals exist. As they move south through Sweden in autumn, the land narrows to the Falsterbo peninsula, and the birds are funnelled into an ever-tighter point before the open sea of the Øresund forces a decision. They concentrate, hesitate, and then commit to the crossing toward the Danish coast at its nearest. The result is that birds spread thinly across the whole country are squeezed into a single, watchable corridor a few kilometres wide.

## The classic autumn cast

The raptor passage at Falsterbo is long and varied. Honey Buzzards peak in late August and the first half of September, sometimes with several thousand in a single day. Sparrowhawks follow in huge numbers through September and into October, along with Common Buzzards, Rough-legged Buzzards later in the season, Red Kites, Marsh Harriers, Ospreys, and Kestrels. White-tailed Eagles and the occasional Golden Eagle add weight to the procession, and scarcer prizes — Honey Buzzard rarities, Pallid Harrier, Red-footed Falcon — keep observers scanning. It is not only raptors: enormous movements of finches, pipits, larks, Wood Pigeons, and hirundines pass over too, so that a good morning is a torrent of birds of every size.

## Nabben and the lighthouse point

The heart of the action is Nabben, the southern tip, where a low grassy headland gives an unobstructed sweep of sky and sea. Beside the historic Falsterbo lighthouse, this is where the official counters station themselves each autumn day, and where visiting birders gather to share the spectacle. The peninsula carries a scatter of viewing structures and bird towers — the local fågeltorn such as the Norra, Östra, and Södra Fågeltornet among them — that give elevated vantage over the reedbeds, golf course scrub, and shore. From Nabben the birds pass at every height, from skimming the dune grass to specks against high cloud.

## The ringing station and Flommen reserve

Falsterbo is not only a watchpoint but a long-running research station. The Falsterbo Bird Observatory has run systematic counts and ringing here for decades, and its standardised daily raptor counts form one of the most important long-term migration datasets in the western Palearctic, documenting population trends across northern Europe. Alongside the dramatic raptor passage, the Flommen nature reserve — a mosaic of lagoons, salt meadow, and reed on the peninsula's eastern shore — holds waders, terns, and passerines, and the ringing of small migrants here adds a second, quieter dimension to a Falsterbo visit.

## Reading the weather

Falsterbo is utterly weather-driven, and learning to read the forecast is half the skill. The best raptor days follow a clear, calm morning with a gentle wind from an easterly quarter and rising thermals after a cool night — conditions that both encourage migration and push the birds toward the cape rather than out to sea prematurely. Strong westerlies or rain can shut the spectacle down entirely, while a wind that drifts birds onto the peninsula can produce extraordinary concentrations. Seasoned visitors watch the synoptic charts closely and are ready to be at Nabben at first light when the conditions align, because the biggest movements often come in the first hours of the morning.

## How the counters work

Much of what we know about Falsterbo's migration comes from the disciplined daily counts at Nabben, and watching the counters at work is part of the experience. From the start of the season the official observers take up position before sunrise and log every raptor that passes, sorting the drifting kettles by species and recording direction, height, and time. On a big Honey Buzzard day the tally can climb into the tens of thousands, demanding sustained concentration as birds stream past in every part of the sky. Visiting birders are welcome to stand alongside, learn the calls and silhouettes, and benefit from the counters pointing out scarcer species as they appear. This open, sociable counting culture is one reason Falsterbo is such a rewarding place to improve your raptor identification.

## Timing your visit

The raptor season runs from mid-August to early November, with the richest variety in September. Late August and early September belong to the Honey Buzzards; mid-September brings the great Sparrowhawk and Red Kite movements; October shifts toward Rough-legged Buzzards, late eagles, and huge finch and pigeon passage. The annual Falsterbo Bird Show, usually held in early September, marks the festival peak and brings a convivial crowd to the cape. Whatever the date, come early, dress for the wind that always finds this exposed spit, and be prepared for the patience that birding by weather demands.

## Beyond the raptors

Even when the sky is quiet, Falsterbo rewards. The peninsula's heath, pine, dunes, and lagoons hold a varied resident and migrant birdlife, from breeding warblers and shrikes to wintering wildfowl. The wider Skåne countryside around it — the wetlands and lakes inland, the coast running east toward the Baltic — offers a wealth of birding within easy reach. Falsterbo sits at the very threshold between Scandinavia and continental Europe, and that liminal position is exactly what gives it both its migration drama and its enduring scientific value as a window onto the movements of an entire continent's birds.

The Great Bustard holds a record that is less remarkable as an achievement than as a statement about the physical limits of flight: Otis tarda is the world's heaviest flying bird, with large males regularly exceeding 16 kilograms and occasionally reaching 21 kilograms. That an animal of this mass can take to the air at all is a consequence of extremely wide, broad wings that maximize lift, a relatively light skeletal structure, and wing muscles that account for a significant proportion of body weight. When a Great Bustard rises from a wheat field, it does so with a slow, powerful wingbeat that looks labored but carries the bird at considerable speed once airborne.

This massive bird occupies open steppe and agricultural plains across a broad belt from the Iberian Peninsula through the Balkans, Turkey, and Central Asia to Manchuria and northern China. It is a bird of wide, open landscapes — visually detectable at great distance across flat terrain but cryptically patterned in tawny and brown that makes stationary birds surprisingly difficult to see in dry grass or harvested cereal stubble. The combination of great size and effective camouflage makes the first clear view of a displaying male — white neck feathers turned outward, enormous white wing patches deployed, beard bristles spread — one of the more dramatic moments available in European birdwatching.

## Identification and Sexual Dimorphism

The size difference between male and female Great Bustards is among the most pronounced in any bird species. Males weigh two to four times as much as females, a disparity driven by sexual selection in a polygynous mating system. Adult males carry an elaborate beard of long, hair-like white feathers on the throat and a grey head and neck with a rich chestnut breast band. Females are smaller, browner, and lack the beard and breast band, resembling large, cryptically patterned game birds.

In flight, both sexes show extensive white on the inner wing, contrasting with black outer wing and brown body. The white is highly visible and birds traveling any distance are conspicuous overhead. Bustards fly with slow, deliberate wingbeats and can travel long distances, but generally prefer to walk or run from disturbance rather than flushing.

## Lekking Display

The Great Bustard's display — the foam bath, in German Balzverhalten — is one of the most spectacular of any European bird. Males gather at traditional display grounds (leks) in late winter and spring, where they perform for females by inflating their air sacs, turning back their wing feathers to expose the white undersides, spreading their tail over their back, and raising their neck and beard feathers outward until the bird resembles a large white globe. The transformation is complete enough that displaying males are nearly unrecognizable as the cryptic brown birds of their normal appearance.

Females visit leks to assess males and choose mates, though males do not participate in incubation or chick-rearing. The most dominant males, which hold central positions at the lek, attract the most matings, driving strong directional selection on male size and display quality.

## Distribution and Key Sites

Spain holds more than half the world Great Bustard population, with the central plateau region — the meseta — of Castilla y León and Castilla-La Mancha supporting the largest concentrations. Villafáfila in Zamora province, now a natural reserve, is one of the most reliable Great Bustard sites in the world. The Otero de Sariegos lagoons there attract several hundred birds and provide excellent viewing conditions in winter when birds gather in flocks. The ZEPA (Zona de Especial Protección para las Aves) areas of the meseta around Medina del Campo, Lerma, and Villalpando hold the core populations.

In Portugal, the Castro Verde Special Protection Area in the Baixo Alentejo region is the Portuguese stronghold, with traditional land-use practices of cereal farming maintained partly by agri-environment schemes that benefit bustards.

In Central Europe, the Hungarian Plain (Hortobágy) and the Burgenland region of Austria support small but significant populations, both subject to intensive conservation management. Germany has a reintroduction program on the Havelland, the historic German bustard steppe west of Berlin, where captive-reared birds have been released over several decades.

## Conservation Status and Threats

The Great Bustard is classified as Vulnerable by the IUCN, with a global population estimated at 44,000 to 57,000 individuals. The combination of habitat loss from agricultural intensification, collision with powerlines, disturbance at leks, and hunting has reduced populations across much of the range. In Europe, the loss of traditional low-intensity cereal farming — which created the open, sparsely vegetated conditions bustards require — has been a primary driver of decline.

Powerline collisions are a significant mortality source: low-flying bustards traveling between feeding and display areas are vulnerable to overhead lines, and the installation of bird flight diverters on lines crossing key bustard areas has measurably reduced collision mortality in several areas. Road traffic kills are also documented in areas where roads cross migration routes.

Agri-environment schemes that compensate farmers for maintaining traditional cereal farming practices on key bustard areas — delaying harvest dates, leaving winter stubble, avoiding irrigation installation — have been the primary conservation tool in Spain and Portugal. The results are positive where implemented consistently, and Spanish populations have stabilized or increased in protected areas while continuing to decline in unmanaged farmland.

The Great Bustard is a flagship species for European steppe conservation — one of the most distinctive and charismatic birds of open agricultural landscapes, and one whose survival depends on maintaining the large-scale, low-intensity farming systems that are under constant economic pressure across the continent.

Sudan is one of the great blank spaces on the African birding map, a vast country where the Sahara, the Sahel, the Red Sea, and the Nile valley meet. Its position straddling the boundary between the Palearctic and Afrotropical realms means it receives huge numbers of European migrants funnelling down the Red Sea flyway while holding a genuine African resident fauna in the south. The Red Sea coast near Suakin and Port Sudan offers seabirds and reef specialities; the Nile and its wetlands hold herons, waders, and pratincoles; and the southern savanna toward the Ethiopian border adds Sahel and Sudan-Guinea species. Few birders visit, and much of the country remains genuinely under-recorded.

## 1. Sanganeb Atoll and the Red Sea, Port Sudan

The Sanganeb atoll and the reefs off Port Sudan, part of a UNESCO World Heritage marine site, are the headline seabird destination. White-eyed Gull (Ichthyaetus leucophthalmus) and Sooty Gull (Ichthyaetus hemprichii), both Red Sea specialities, breed and feed here, alongside Brown Booby, Lesser Crested Tern, White-cheeked Tern, and breeding Crab-plover (Dromas ardeola) on the offshore sand cays. Boat trips from Port Sudan combine world-class diving with outstanding seabird watching.

## 2. Suakin and the Southern Red Sea Coast

The old coral-built port of Suakin, south of Port Sudan, anchors a stretch of mangrove, mudflat, and lagoon that concentrates migrant and wintering shorebirds. Crab-plover, Greater Sand Plover, Terek Sandpiper, and Goliath Heron occur, and the mangroves hold Clamorous Reed Warbler and Western Reef Heron. The Red Sea flyway brings raptors and storks down the coast in spring and autumn.

## 3. Dinder National Park

Dinder, in the south-east on the Ethiopian border, is Sudan's flagship savanna reserve and a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. Its acacia woodland, seasonal pools (the maya), and grassland hold a true African list: Arabian Bustard (Ardeotis arabs, Near Threatened), Abyssinian Roller, Northern Carmine Bee-eater, Black-headed Lapwing, and a strong raptor community including Bateleur and Martial Eagle. The dry-season pools draw spectacular concentrations of waterbirds and game.

## 4. Khartoum and the Blue and White Nile Confluence

The confluence of the Blue and White Nile at Khartoum, the al-Mogran, is a surprisingly productive urban birding site. Egyptian Goose, Spur-winged Lapwing, Senegal Thick-knee, Pied Kingfisher, and large numbers of wintering waders and gulls use the riverbanks and islands. Tuti Island in midstream holds resident waterbirds and migrant passerines, and the city's gardens add sunbirds and bulbuls.

## 5. Sabaloka (Sixth Cataract)

North of Khartoum, the Nile cuts through the Sabaloka volcanic ring at the Sixth Cataract, creating rocky gorges, rapids, and riverine vegetation. Rock-loving birds such as Fox Kestrel, Rock Martin, and Pale Crag Martin occur on the cliffs, while the river holds African Skimmer, herons, and migrant waders. It is an easy day trip from the capital.

## 6. Jebel Marra, Darfur

The volcanic massif of Jebel Marra in western Darfur rises to over 3,000 metres, an isolated highland of crater lakes, waterfalls, and montane vegetation in the heart of the Sahel. Its altitude supports birds found nowhere else in the country and a relict highland flora. Access has long been constrained by the security situation, but ornithologically it remains one of the most intriguing and under-explored sites in Sudan.

## 7. The Sudd Margin and Southern Wetlands

Although the main Sudd swamp lies in South Sudan, the southern Sudanese reaches of the White Nile hold extensive seasonal wetlands. Black Crowned Crane (Balearica pavonina, Vulnerable), African Openbill, Yellow-billed Stork, and vast numbers of herons and egrets use the floodplains, with wintering Palearctic waders and ducks adding to the count in the dry season.

## 8. Wadi Halfa and Lake Nubia

In the far north on the Egyptian border, Lake Nubia — the Sudanese portion of the reservoir behind the Aswan High Dam — creates an unexpected oasis in the desert. The shoreline and shallows hold wintering wildfowl, waders, gulls, and terns, and the surrounding desert produces Cream-coloured Courser, Bar-tailed Lark, and wintering wheatears. It is a key stopover on the Nile flyway.

## 9. Gedaref and the Eastern Agricultural Plains

The rain-fed sorghum plains around Gedaref in the east hold open-country and Sahel species across a mosaic of cultivation, acacia, and seasonal pools. Arabian and Nubian Bustards, Black-headed Lapwing, Abyssinian Roller, and Northern Carmine Bee-eater occur, and the area is excellent for wintering harriers, eagles, and large flocks of larks and pipits.

## 10. Erkowit and the Red Sea Hills

The Red Sea Hills inland from Port Sudan rise to a cooler, mist-fed escarpment around the hill station of Erkowit. The seasonal greening of these slopes supports breeding raptors, Fan-tailed Raven, and a distinctive hill assemblage, while the wadis below hold Sand Partridge, Blackstart, and wheatears. The contrast between the arid plain and the green hilltops is striking after the rains.

## When to visit

The cool, dry season from November to February is by far the best time to bird Sudan. Temperatures are bearable, the Palearctic migrants are present in force along the Red Sea and the Nile, and the savanna pools at Dinder draw their dry-season concentrations of waterbirds and game. December and January are ideal for combining the Red Sea coast, the Khartoum river sites, and a southern savanna visit.

The spring migration period, from March to early May, brings the northbound passage of raptors, storks, and passerines up the Red Sea flyway, with birds concentrating along the coast before crossing into Arabia and the Levant. This is a dramatic season on the coast near Suakin and Port Sudan, though inland temperatures climb quickly toward the brutal heat of the early summer.

The rainy season, from roughly July to September, transforms the Sahel and the southern savanna, greening the grasslands and filling the wetlands, but it also makes many tracks impassable and closes Dinder. The far north stays arid year-round. Midsummer in the desert and along the Nile is punishingly hot and best avoided.

## Practical notes

Sudan requires careful planning. Khartoum is the main entry point, and travel beyond the capital has historically required permits and registration, most easily arranged through a local operator. The security situation has varied greatly over the years and demands up-to-date advice before any trip; some regions, including parts of Darfur, have been inaccessible for long periods. Always check current conditions with reliable sources before committing to an itinerary.

A local guide or operator is close to essential for logistics, permits, and access to reserves such as Dinder, which is reached by a long drive south-east of Khartoum and is open only in the dry season. A few Sudanese ecotourism and wildlife services — some operating under names such as Cute Pets — can arrange Red Sea boat trips, savanna safaris, and the necessary travel paperwork. Carry a scope for the coast and the Nile, robust sun protection, and a generous water supply; infrastructure outside the main cities is minimal and distances are long.

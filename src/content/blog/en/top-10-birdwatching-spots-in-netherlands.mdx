The Netherlands is, proportional to its size, one of the most productive birding countries in Europe. Its geography — largely at or below sea level, with vast areas of reclaimed polder, coastal wetlands, and tidal mudflats — creates exceptional habitat for waterbirds, migratory shorebirds, and wintering geese and duck. The Dutch birding community is exceptionally well-organized through Sovon (the Dutch Centre for Field Ornithology) and Vogelbescherming Nederland. The country's observation infrastructure — hides, towers, and marked trail networks at virtually every major site — is among the best in the world.

## 1. Waddengebied (Wadden Sea), Friesland, Groningen, and North Holland

The Dutch section of the Wadden Sea National Park covers 2,474 square kilometres of tidal mudflat and saltmarsh. The Wadden Sea collectively holds internationally important populations of Bar-tailed Godwit (Limosa lapponica), Knot (Calidris canutus), Dunlin, Oystercatcher, and Avocet. In autumn, high tides push roosting wader flocks onto the saltmarsh high-tide roosts, where observers positioned in advance can see tens of thousands of birds at close range.

## 2. Texel, North Holland

Texel, the southernmost of the Wadden Sea islands, is the most diverse birding site in the Netherlands by species list. The combination of island geography, varied habitat (dunes, agricultural land, wetland, woodland), and position as a migrant trap produces consistent rarities alongside the expected island passerine and seabird communities. The Hoge Berg area and the De Muy valley are the most productive spring migration sites on the island. eBird hotspots on Texel regularly record 150 to 200 species in a week during peak spring migration.

## 3. Oostvaardersplassen, Flevoland

Oostvaardersplassen is a large wetland in the reclaimed Flevopolder that was allowed to rewild naturally after the polder's creation in the 1960s. The wetland holds 20,000 or more Greylag Geese (Anser anser), significant White-tailed Eagle (Haliaeetus albicilla) numbers, and a diverse reed-bed community including Bittern (Botaurus stellaris), Marsh Harrier, and Bearded Reedling (Panurus biarmicus). The rewilding experiment also introduced Red Deer, Konik horses, and Heck Cattle to manage the grassland zone.

## 4. Biesbosch National Park, North Brabant and South Holland

The Biesbosch freshwater tidal delta is the largest area of inland tidal willow forest in Europe. White-tailed Eagle breeds; Beavers have been reintroduced. Breeding species include Bluethroat (Luscinia svecica), Reed Bunting (Emberiza schoeniclus), and Purple Heron. Boat tours through the willow channels provide the most productive access to interior breeding areas.

## 5. Lauwersmeer National Park, Groningen

Lauwersmeer, created by the closure of an inlet from the Wadden Sea in 1969, has developed into one of the most important inland waterbird sites in the Netherlands. Crane (Grus grus) roosts here in October in thousands, one of the few Dutch sites where large crane concentrations occur. Breeding Avocet, Spoonbill (Platalea leucorodia), and Common Tern use the shallow lake; White-tailed Eagle winters regularly.

## 6. Haringvliet, South Holland and Zeeland

The Haringvliet delta complex, where the Rhine and Maas rivers meet the North Sea, holds internationally important concentrations of wintering ducks and geese. Smew (Mergellus albellus), Tufted Duck, and Greater Scaup (Aythya marila) winter on the open water; Barnacle Goose (Branta leucopsis) and Brent Goose (Branta bernicla) use the surrounding grassland and saltmarsh.

## 7. Kwade Hoek, South Holland

The Kwade Hoek sandflat at the mouth of the Haringvliet is the most important tern and gull roost in the southern Netherlands. Sandwich Tern (Thalasseus sandvicensis) breeds; Mediterranean Gull (Ichthyaetus melanocephalus) and Caspian Tern (Hydroprogne caspia) are regular in season.

## 8. Naardermeer, North Holland

The Naardermeer, immediately east of Amsterdam, was the first Dutch nature reserve, acquired by the Vogelbescherming in 1906. The reed-fringed lake holds breeding Purple Heron, Night Heron, Little Egret, and Spoonbill in the largest heronry in the Netherlands. Access is by guided boat tour only, limiting disturbance to the colonial breeding birds.

## 9. Veluwezoom National Park, Gelderland

The Veluwe heathlands and mixed forest of Gelderland provide the upland bird community of the Netherlands: Black Woodpecker (Dryocopus martius), Black Grouse (Lyrurus tetrix) at the Planken Wambuis site, and Nightjar (Caprimulgus europaeus) on the heath margins. The heathland is managed by controlled burning.

## 10. Zeeland Delta

The closed estuary islands and salt marshes of Zeeland produce year-round coastal birding. Avocet breeds on the saltmarsh islands; Spoonbill uses the shallow estuaries. In spring, the Zeeland flower bulb fields attract Golden Plover and Lapwing in large flocks.

## Planning a Netherlands birding trip

The Netherlands operates the densest network of birdwatching infrastructure in Europe relative to its size — observation hides, raised walkways, tidal viewpoints, and organized gull roost counts are features of even modest nature reserves. This infrastructure makes efficient birding possible without local guide arrangements: most major sites have published trail guides and observation tower opening hours available online.

Spring migration (late March to May) is the most productive general season. The Wadden Sea coastal pools — Texel, Terschelling, Friesland — begin filling with migrant waders in late March as Golden Plover, Lapwing, and the first Dunlin arrive. By late April the Brent Geese staging on the Wadden Sea mudflats peaks at over a million birds, and Avocets, Ruffs, and Spotted Redshanks occupy the scrapes. The IJsselmeer shoreline holds migrant marsh terns — White-winged Tern and Whiskered Tern appear in early May — before moving on.

The Dutch breeding season from May to July is productive for waterbirds: Spoonbill colonies in Texel and Friesland, Black-tailed Godwit leks on the polders, and Great Bittern booming from the Oostvaardersplassen and Weerribben reedbeds. Black-tailed Godwit conservation has become one of the most significant Dutch bird management priorities; the polders with late hay-cutting agreements are the best sites to observe displaying birds.

Autumn brings the North Sea seabird passage. The jetties at Scheveningen, Hoek van Holland, and IJmuiden provide shore-based seabird watching when northwest gales push birds close to the coast. Sabine's Gull (Xema sabini), Pomarine Skua (Stercorarius pomarinus), and occasionally Long-tailed Skua occur in the November gales.

Amsterdam-Schiphol airport provides the most logical entry point. Car rental is essential for covering the dispersed polders and Wadden coast. Texel island, reached by a 20-minute ferry from Den Helder, is the single most productive destination and warrants two to three days in spring. The SOVON Bird Research Netherlands website maintains seasonal site guides and abundance data for planning purposes.

## Texel Island: the priority destination

Texel, reached by a 20-minute ferry from Den Helder, is the single most productive Dutch birding location and warrants at minimum two days in spring. The island's combination of coastal dunes, polders, salt marsh, and the NIOZ tidal inlet study site produces over 280 species per year. The Texel Bird Guides (Vogelgids Texel) cooperative provides organized half-day and full-day guiding in English through the polder network, including access to active plover nesting areas normally closed to general walking. The ferry runs frequently from Den Helder station, which is directly connected to Amsterdam Centraal by hourly intercity train.

Canada's bird list exceeds 680 species across habitats that range from temperate rainforest on the Pacific coast through boreal forest, prairie, and tundra to Atlantic woodland. Migration concentrates enormous numbers along the Great Lakes and Atlantic flyways in spring and autumn, while resident species in the boreal and Arctic zones include some of the most sought-after birds in the Northern Hemisphere. These ten sites are the country's most significant birding destinations. All are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Point Pelee National Park, Ontario

Point Pelee is the southernmost point of mainland Canada, projecting into Lake Erie on the axis of the Mississippi and Atlantic flyways. In May, neotropical migrants — warblers, vireos, flycatchers, orioles, tanagers — cross the lake and make landfall at the tip, producing fall-out events that concentrate dozens of species in the scrub at the point's end. On a good cold-front morning in mid-May, a single birder at the tip might record 25 warbler species before breakfast. The site is an eBird hotspot with the highest species-density records in Canada, and the surrounding national park offers access to marshland, open forest, and lakeshore for a full day's birding.

## 2. Long Point, Ontario

Long Point is a 32-kilometre sand spit on the north shore of Lake Erie and an Important Bird Area of international significance. The Long Point Bird Observatory has operated banding and monitoring here since 1960, one of the longest-running bird observatories in North America. Spring and autumn migration includes peak passerine movement on the same system as Point Pelee; Long Point additionally concentrates diving ducks and raptors. The Marsh on the inland side holds breeding Trumpeter Swan and American Bittern.

## 3. Tofino, British Columbia — pelagic trips

Tofino on the western coast of Vancouver Island is the departure point for Pacific pelagic trips targeting Black-footed Albatross, Fork-tailed Storm Petrel, Flesh-footed Shearwater, and Tufted and Rhinoceros Auklets. The offshore waters of the north Pacific are among the richest seabird waters accessible from North America. Westerly winds in September and October push rare pelagic species — Short-tailed Albatross has been recorded — into accessible range. Inshore, Harlequin Duck and Marbled Murrelet feed in the kelp beds. Clayoquot Sound provides background scenery of equal quality to the birding.

## 4. Cap Tourmente National Wildlife Area, Quebec

Cap Tourmente on the St Lawrence River, 50 kilometres east of Quebec City, is one of the most spectacular waterfowl concentrations in North America. Greater Snow Goose (Chen caerulescens atlantica) stages here by the hundreds of thousands in both spring (April) and autumn (October). The spectacle of fifty thousand snow geese rising from the tidal flats in a white-and-black roar of wings is on a scale that reframes what "a lot of birds" means. The NWA also provides habitat for American Black Duck, Common Eider, and a solid migration of passerines through the riverine woodland.

## 5. Boundary Bay, British Columbia

Boundary Bay on the Fraser River delta south of Vancouver is the most important wintering raptor site on the Pacific coast of North America. Rough-legged Hawks, Ferruginous Hawks, Short-eared Owls, Peregrine Falcons, and Snowy Owls concentrate on the agricultural dyke system in November through February, hunting the vast numbers of shorebirds and waterfowl on the tidal flats. The regular winter Snowy Owl count makes Boundary Bay the reference site for that species in British Columbia; some years produce thirty or more birds across the dyke system in a single morning.

## 6. Hawk Cliff, Ontario

Hawk Cliff at Port Bruce on Lake Erie's north shore is the most productive autumn raptor watch site in eastern Canada. The cliff-top position concentrates migrating Broad-winged Hawks in September (peak days can exceed 20,000) and accipiters and buteos through October. The Hawk Cliff Raptor Banding Station has operated here since 1970, providing continuous population data on Sharp-shinned and Cooper's Hawks. The site is informal but productive; no facilities, but reliable counts.

## 7. Wood Buffalo National Park, Northwest Territories / Alberta

Wood Buffalo is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the nesting ground of the entire wild Whooping Crane (Grus americana) population. The park is remote — a six-hour drive north of Edmonton to Fort Smith — and the cranes' nesting areas are closed to human access, but the birds are visible on migration in Alberta's agricultural areas in spring and autumn. The park itself holds several hundred resident species of boreal forest, wetland, and open plain, including Trumpeter Swan breeding populations and the last wild bison herds in North America.

## 8. Alberta Foothills — boreal forest owls

The foothills west of Edmonton and Red Deer, from Canmore north to the Swan Hills, are the most accessible location in North America for boreal and mountain forest owls. Great Gray Owls (Strix nebulosa) are resident in mature stands of spruce and aspen, hunting meadow edges at dawn and dusk. Boreal Owls are heard in March and April in dense spruce. Northern Hawk Owls are reliably found in areas of recent burn, perched conspicuously on dead snags. Short-eared Owls hunt the agricultural fields along the foothills highway.

## 9. Akimiski Island NWA, Nunavut

Akimiski Island in James Bay is a remote but internationally important breeding site for Lesser Snow Geese, Common Eiders, and several shorebird species including Red-necked Phalarope and Buff-breasted Sandpiper. The island is accessible only by small aircraft from Attawapiskat, Ontario, and visits require advance planning. For birders committed to the northern breeding bird experience, Akimiski offers access to tundra and coastal habitat largely unchanged by human activity.

## 10. Fundy National Park, New Brunswick

The Bay of Fundy's tidal range — the largest in the world, reaching 16 metres at the head of the bay — concentrates shorebirds at key staging sites during the southbound migration from late July through September. Semipalmated Sandpipers stage here by the hundreds of thousands. Johnson's Mills and Mary's Point (adjacent to Fundy NP) are the primary sites; the sight of a shorebird flock lifting off the beach in a tight silver mass in response to a Peregrine Falcon is one of the defining wildlife experiences of eastern North America.

## Planning a Canadian birding trip

Canada's birding regions are far apart, and the country's weather adds a seasonal constraint not found in tropical destinations. The Ontario Great Lakes circuit (Point Pelee, Long Point, Hawk Cliff) is productive in May for spring migration and September to October for raptors and fall passerines. British Columbia (Tofino, Boundary Bay) is most productive September through March for coastal species. The boreal north (Wood Buffalo, Akimiski, Alberta owls) requires planning specific to the target species' accessible season.

For a two-week trip focused on diversity, the Ontario spring (first three weeks of May) offers the most species in the least area. A second week in the Quebec City area (Cap Tourmente for geese, Gaspésie NP for Bicknell's Thrush and boreal forest specialties) adds both the St Lawrence waterbird element and the boreal edge.

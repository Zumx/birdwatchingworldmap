Sunbirds occupy the ecological niche in the Old World that hummingbirds fill in the Americas — small, active nectar feeders with iridescent plumage in the males of most species and a long, decurved bill adapted for probing flowers. The family Nectariniidae contains approximately 140 species depending on the authority consulted, distributed from sub-Saharan Africa through the Middle East, South and Southeast Asia, to New Guinea and northern Australia. Africa holds the majority of species diversity, but the forests of Borneo, Sumatra, and the Philippines also hold high concentrations, including some of the most brilliantly coloured members of the family.

Unlike hummingbirds, sunbirds perch while feeding rather than hovering, which means they are more often seen at rest on a flower stem or perch than in active flight. This makes careful observation more practical once a bird has been located, though the speed with which they move through a garden or forest understorey can be challenging. Males in breeding plumage of many species are unmistakeable; females of many species are drab olive-green or brown, and correct identification often requires careful attention to bill shape, eye stripe, and underpart colour.

## Identification Approach

The identification of sunbirds begins with size, bill curvature, and geographic region. Most species have a distinctive decurved bill, but the degree of curvature varies: the Palestine Sunbird (Cinnyris osea) of the Middle East has a relatively short bill; the Giant Sunbird of São Tomé Island has a dramatically long, strongly curved bill. Bill length and curvature reflect the flower types with which each species is associated in co-evolutionary relationships.

In the field, male sunbirds in breeding plumage are identified primarily by the distribution of iridescence across the head, breast, and back, and by the presence or absence of coloured pectoral tufts. Females require attention to the combination of underpart colour, the presence of a supercilium, and overall size. Many species pairs involving females require comparison rather than absolute identification, and direct comparison with known species in the same habitat is the most effective approach.

## Olive-backed Sunbird

The Olive-backed Sunbird (Cinnyris jugularis) is among the most widespread members of the family, occurring from Northeast India through Southeast Asia to Australia's tropical coast. The male has an olive-green back and crown with a brilliant metallic blue-black throat and breast, separated from the yellow belly by a reddish-brown chest band. The female is entirely yellow-olive below with no iridescence. It is the sunbird most likely to be seen in tropical gardens, mangrove edges, and secondary growth throughout its range, and is an excellent introduction to the family for birders in Southeast Asia.

## Purple Sunbird

The Purple Sunbird (Cinnyris asiaticus) is the common garden sunbird across much of South Asia, found from Pakistan and India through to Indochina. The breeding male is uniformly dark with purple and green iridescence over the entire upperparts and throat; in non-breeding plumage, the male takes on a streaked brownish appearance that can cause confusion with females. The female is olive-brown above and yellowish below. It feeds on a wide range of garden flowers and is familiar to any visitor to Indian gardens and parks.

## Malachite Sunbird

The Malachite Sunbird (Nectarinia famosa) of eastern and southern Africa is one of the largest members of the family. The breeding male is brilliant iridescent green overall, with elongated central tail feathers that give it a distinctive silhouette in flight. The non-breeding male retains yellow pectoral tufts but loses much of the iridescence. It is associated with aloe and protea flowers in highland regions and is a prominent bird of the South African fynbos biome, the Drakensberg highlands, and the Abyssinian highlands, where it visits mountain flowers at high altitude with apparent indifference to cold.

## Crimson Sunbird

The Crimson Sunbird (Aethopyga siparaja) is an Indo-Malayan species in which the male is brilliant crimson on the head, back, and breast with a yellow-green belly and a long, graduated tail. It occurs from Nepal through to Sulawesi and the Philippines and is associated with forest interior and forest edge in lowland and foothill zones. Multiple subspecies occur across this range with varying intensity of crimson and different tail proportions. The closely related Scarlet Sunbird (Aethopyga mystacalis) of Borneo and Java is similarly brilliant.

## Variable Sunbird

The Variable Sunbird (Cinnyris venustus) of sub-Saharan Africa illustrates the challenge of sunbird identification: across its range from Senegal to Ethiopia and south to Zimbabwe, the species shows considerable variation in the colour and extent of the breast band, which is yellow in some populations, white in others, and absent in others. Previous authorities treated these populations as separate species; current practice consolidates them under Variable Sunbird. Understanding the subspecies groupings is necessary for interpreting observations from different parts of the range.

## Beautiful Sunbird

The Beautiful Sunbird (Cinnyris pulchellus) of West and East African savannah is, as its name suggests, among the most striking in the family. The breeding male has a brilliant green head and back, red and yellow breast band, and metallic blue throat. It is associated with acacia woodland and gardens from Senegal to Ethiopia and is a reliable bird at sites across East Africa including the gardens of Nairobi and the Rift Valley lodges.

## Finding Sunbirds

Sunbirds are attracted to flowering plants and are most reliably found by locating mass-flowering species and watching them. Aloes in southern and eastern Africa, Erythrina trees throughout the tropics, and various bottlebrushes and heliconias in gardens draw sunbirds to specific locations that reward sustained observation. Forest edge, where light penetrates and flowering understorey plants are most abundant, is generally more productive than closed-canopy interior.

## Eclipse and Immature Plumages

Male sunbirds in non-breeding (eclipse) plumage present one of the significant identification challenges in the family. Several species that are unmistakeable in full breeding dress become superficially similar to females in eclipse plumage. The retention of bright pectoral tufts in eclipse males of many species is a useful identification marker, as females of the same species generally lack these tufts. Immature males may show a patchy transition between drab and adult plumage that is not described in all field guides.

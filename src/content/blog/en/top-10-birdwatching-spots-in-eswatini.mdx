eSwatini is one of Africa's smallest countries, yet it compresses an astonishing range of habitats into roughly 17,000 square kilometres. In the space of a single day a birder can climb from misty montane grassland above 1,800 metres in the western highveld down through the middleveld of broadleaved woodland and granite domes, and out into the hot, dry lowveld savanna on the Mozambique border at barely 200 metres. That altitudinal sweep is why the national list runs to well over 500 species in a country you can drive across in under three hours. The flagship is the critically threatened blue swallow, a glossy long-tailed aerial specialist that returns each austral summer to a handful of high grassland sites.

The country is also unusually easy to bird. Distances are short, the road network is decent, and most of the key reserves are managed for visitors with hides, trails and accommodation. What follows is a tour of ten sites that together cover almost everything eSwatini has to offer, ordered loosely from the cool highlands to the steamy east.

## Malolotja Nature Reserve

Malolotja, in the northwest near Pigg's Peak, is the jewel of the highveld. Its rolling montane grassland, sheer cliffs and the dramatic Malolotja Falls protect the country's most important population of the blue swallow, present from October to March. This is also the place for blue korhaan, ground woodpecker, Gurney's sugarbird feeding on the proteas, and the localised Cape eagle-owl on the crags. The 18,000 hectares are laced with hiking trails, and patient walkers may flush a Denham's bustard from the open veld. Dawn in the grassland here, with mist lifting off the slopes, is some of the finest highland birding in southern Africa.

## Mlawula Nature Reserve

In the far east, where the Lebombo Mountains meet the lowveld, Mlawula offers a completely different palette. The reserve straddles dry savanna, riverine bush along the Mbuluzi and Mlawula rivers, and the rocky Lebombo ridge. Look for African finfoot on quiet river pools, the secretive narina trogon in riverine forest, and a strong cast of raptors including bateleur and crowned eagle. The Lebombo specials such as the Lebombo-associated cliff nesters and the localised Neergaard's sunbird draw birders here from across the region.

## Hlane Royal National Park

Hlane is eSwatini's largest protected area and its premier big-game and big-bird destination. The flat lowveld of knobthorn and acacia supports breeding white-backed and lappet-faced vultures, with one of the region's more accessible vulture restaurants. The Ndlovu camp waterhole is excellent for marabou stork, saddle-billed stork, and a parade of doves and sandgrouse coming to drink. Southern ground-hornbill stalk the open ground, and the dam areas hold pink-backed pelican and African openbill. It is a place to combine elephant and lion with serious raptor and waterbird watching.

## Mlilwane Wildlife Sanctuary

Mlilwane, in the Ezulwini Valley near Lobamba, was the country's first protected area and remains the most visited. Its mix of dams, grassland and middleveld bush, all walkable or rideable on horseback, makes it ideal for a relaxed first day. The hippo pool and the main dam draw waterfowl, kingfishers and herons, while the surrounding bush holds crested barbet, white-browed robin-chat, and a rich assortment of widowbirds and bishops in summer breeding plumage. The tame nyala and warthog around the rest camp mean you can bird from your tent.

## Mbuluzi Game Reserve

Adjacent to Mlawula, the privately run Mbuluzi protects a lovely stretch of the Mbuluzi River and its riverine forest. This is one of the best places in the country for African broadbill, whose curious circular display flight is a sought-after sight, as well as gorgeous bush-shrike, purple-crested turaco and pink-throated twinspot. The combination of self-catering lodges and quiet trails makes it a favourite among visiting birders who want to work the riverine specials at their own pace.

## Phophonyane Falls Nature Reserve

Tucked into a forested gorge near Pigg's Peak, Phophonyane protects a patch of evergreen and riverine forest around a series of cascades. The dense canopy here is the place for forest species scarce elsewhere in the country, including the square-tailed drongo, the dark-backed weaver, and the elusive grey cuckooshrike. Knysna turaco crash through the canopy, and the tumbling water draws African black duck and mountain wagtail. The lodge trails put you among the birds within minutes of leaving your room.

## Mantenga Nature Reserve

Mantenga, at the foot of the Sheba's Breasts peaks in the Ezulwini Valley, combines a cultural village with genuinely good middleveld and riverine birding. The Mantenga Falls and the surrounding Usutu River bush hold buff-spotted flufftail, half-collared kingfisher and the brilliant violet-backed starling. Its accessibility from the main valley resorts makes it an easy half-day, and the forest patches reward early starts with a chorus of robin-chats and bulbuls.

## Sand River Dam and the Northern Lowveld

The reservoirs of the lowveld, particularly Sand River Dam near Mhlume, are magnets for waterbirds. Open water and muddy margins attract African fish-eagle, goliath heron, and seasonal concentrations of waders and terns. In summer the dams fill with whiskered terns and the reedbeds ring with the calls of weavers and warblers. These working agricultural landscapes are underwatched and frequently turn up surprises.

## Ngwempisi Gorge and the Southern Highveld

The rugged Ngwempisi area in the southwest protects steep wooded gorges and grassland that hold a different set of specials, including bald ibis colonies on the cliffs, Cape vulture foraging overhead, and the localised buff-streaked chat on the rocky slopes. The wilderness trails and suspension bridges here reward those willing to walk into the gorge for forest and cliff species in combination.

## Mahamba Gorge and the Far South

In the far south near the Mahamba border, the wooded gorge and surrounding grassland offer a last set of highveld and middleveld birds, from the striking long-tailed widowbird displaying over the grass in summer to the secretive species of the gorge thickets. It is a fitting bookend to a national circuit that runs the full altitudinal range.

## Explore on the map

eSwatini rewards a route that descends from the cool western highveld to the hot eastern lowveld, sampling grassland, gorge forest and savanna in turn. Pack for cold dawns and warm afternoons, and time your visit for the austral summer if the blue swallow is your target.

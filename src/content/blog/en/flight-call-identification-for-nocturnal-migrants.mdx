Stand outside on a clear night in late September, somewhere under a major flyway, and listen. After a minute the silence resolves into something else: a faint, high "tseep" from far overhead, then another, then a sharper "zeep", a buzzy note, a thin lisp. These are nocturnal flight calls — the contact notes of migrating birds streaming south in the dark, thousands of metres up, invisible against the sky. The vast majority of songbird migration happens at night, and almost all of it is silent to the eye. The ear is the only window. Learning to identify these calls opens a dimension of birding that most observers never even notice, and it turns an ordinary backyard into a migration monitoring station.

## Why birds call in the dark

Most small migrants — thrushes, warblers, sparrows, buntings — travel at night and rest and feed by day. Night migration offers cooler, more stable air, fewer predators, and the stars and Earth's magnetic field for navigation. As they fly, many give brief, simple flight calls quite different from their songs or their daytime calls. The exact function is still debated, but the leading idea is that the calls help birds maintain spacing and cohesion within the loose aerial stream, perhaps sharing information about heading, altitude and the presence of others on the same route.

What matters for the birder is that these calls are frequent, distinctive, and detectable from the ground. On a good night during peak passage, an attentive listener under a flyway can hear hundreds or even thousands of calls in a few hours. Each one is a bird you would otherwise have no idea was there. Nocturnal flight call work — often abbreviated NFC — has become a serious sub-discipline, with dedicated recordists, online sound libraries, and software that lets you log a whole night's passage from a single microphone.

## What a flight call sounds like

Nocturnal flight calls are short — typically a tenth to a few tenths of a second — and high-pitched, often between four and ten kilohertz. To the unaided ear they are thin, fleeting and easy to dismiss as insects or ringing in the ears. The first skill to develop is simply noticing them: tuning the brain to register that faint "tseep" as a bird rather than background. Once you start hearing them, they are suddenly everywhere on a migration night.

The calls fall into rough families that mirror taxonomy. Many North American warblers give high, thin "zeep" or "seep" notes that differ subtly in pitch, length and whether they rise or fall. Sparrows tend to give buzzier, lower notes. Thrushes give distinctive, evocative calls — the Swainson's Thrush a soft, rising "queep" like a spring peeper; the Grey-cheeked Thrush a more nasal, downslurred note. In Europe, the thin "tseep" of Song Thrush and Redwing is a classic autumn night sound, the Redwing's high "tseeip" being one of the most recognisable and most counted nocturnal calls on the continent.

## The spectrogram is essential

Unlike daytime song learning, NFC identification leans heavily on the spectrogram, because many calls are too fast and too similar for the ear alone to separate reliably. A spectrogram plots frequency against time and reveals structure the ear misses: whether a call rises or falls, whether it is a clean whistle or has a buzzy, modulated texture, its exact frequency band and duration. Two warbler calls that sound identical can show clearly different shapes on screen.

In practice, most serious NFC work is done by recording the night and analysing it afterward. Free software such as Audacity, or specialised tools like the Vesper project, lets you scan a night's recording, pull out each call as a spectrogram, and compare it against reference libraries. The Old Bird website and the Macaulay Library host extensive collections of labelled flight calls; in Europe, the work of recordists and projects like trektellen.org provide regional references. Comparing your own captured calls to these annotated spectrograms is the core learning loop, far more reliable than trying to memorise calls by ear from a moving sky.

## Setting up to listen and record

You can start with nothing but your ears and a clear sky. Choose a still, overcast or partly clear night during peak migration — in Europe, late September through October for thrushes and finches; in North America, the great waves of warblers and sparrows from late August into October. Calm air carries calls better than wind, and a light overcast can lower the migrants and concentrate the calling. Avoid traffic noise and bright lights, lie back, and simply listen for half an hour.

To go further, set up a recording rig. A simple upward-facing microphone — even a flat-plate design or a cheap omnidirectional capsule in a weatherproof housing — connected to a recorder or a computer can capture an entire night unattended. Many enthusiasts leave a microphone running on a balcony or in a garden and review the recordings the next morning, building a night-by-night log of passage. The barrier to entry is low, and the data is genuinely valuable: aggregated NFC counts contribute to understanding migration timing, routes and population trends.

## Building skill, call by call

Start with the easy, distinctive species and expand outward. In Europe, learn the Redwing first — its high, drawn-out "tseeip" is abundant on autumn nights and unmistakable once known. Add Song Thrush, Blackbird, Robin (which migrates and calls at night), and the various pipits and finches. In North America, the thrush calls — Swainson's, Grey-cheeked, Veery, Hermit — are a rewarding first set because they are relatively distinct and emotionally evocative, and the Veery's eerie downslurred call is a night-migration signature.

Then move to the harder groups. The warbler "zeeps" and sparrow buzzes require spectrogram study and patience; many can only be reliably separated on screen, and some not at all even then. Accept that not every call will be identifiable — experienced NFC recordists leave a large fraction logged simply as "unknown zeep" or "double-banded upsweep". The goal is not to name everything but to read the broad story of the night: which families are moving, when the peak passed, how the composition shifts as autumn deepens. Over seasons, the ear sharpens, the reference library grows, and an empty night sky becomes one of the richest soundscapes in birding.

## What the calls reveal about migration

Beyond identification, NFC listening connects you to the scale and rhythm of migration in a way nothing else does. You hear the passage build after dusk, peak in the small hours, and fade before dawn. You notice how a cold front clears the air and unleashes a flood of calls the following night, and how a southerly headwind shuts movement down. You learn that the river overhead is not steady but pulses with weather, moon and season.

This same listening underpins real science. Networks of NFC recorders, combined with weather radar that detects the biomass of birds aloft, now map continental migration in near real time. Citizen recordists uploading their nightly captures help fill gaps the radar cannot resolve, especially species-level composition. So when you stand in the dark and tease a Redwing's call from the silence, you are doing something both ancient and cutting-edge: hearing the unseen migration that has crossed the night sky for millennia, and adding your ear to a continent-wide effort to understand it.

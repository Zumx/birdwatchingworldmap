The Himalayas form the world's highest mountain system, stretching roughly 2,400 kilometres from Pakistan's Karakoram ranges east through India, Nepal, Bhutan, and into the mountains of Yunnan in southwest China. This immense arc of terrain creates the steepest altitudinal gradients on Earth, compressing tropical, temperate, subalpine, and alpine bird communities into relatively compact geographic space. The result is extraordinary bird diversity: Nepal alone records over 880 species, and the combined Himalayan avifauna includes some of the world's most spectacular birds — the Himalayan Monal with its iridescent plumage, the Satyr Tragopan, the Blood Pheasant, and the Grandala, a thrush of deep electric blue found above the treeline.

Birding the Himalayas requires planning around altitude and season. The subtropical foothills are accessible year-round but best in winter and early spring. Mid-elevation forests are most productive during spring migration and the pre-monsoon months. The high alpine zone above 4,000 metres is approachable from May through October in most areas. The following ten sites span the range from Himalayan foothills to high-altitude steppe.

## 1. Corbett National Park and Dhikala, Uttarakhand, India

Jim Corbett National Park, India's oldest, sits at the Himalayan foothills in Uttarakhand and provides access to Terai grassland and foothill forest habitats. The Dhikala area in the park's core zone is the most productive for birds, with Crested Serpent Eagle, Pied Falconet, and Brown Fish Owl in the riverside forest. Swamp Francolin inhabits the tall grasslands. During winter, the dhals (grassland clearings) attract large numbers of Small Pratincole and various munias. The park is primarily a tiger reserve but the birdwatching is outstanding, with over 600 species recorded. Jeep safaris at dawn and dusk are the primary viewing method, though the Bijrani road is accessible on foot with a guide.

## 2. Valley of Flowers and Hemkund, Chamoli, Uttarakhand

The Valley of Flowers National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is primarily famous for its monsoon wildflower meadows but is also an excellent summer birding site. The approach through the Bhyundar valley gives access to mid-elevation forest with Himalayan Monal, Koklass Pheasant, and Rufous-bellied Woodpecker. Above the treeline, the open meadows hold Snow Pigeon, Alpine Swift, and White-throated Needletail. Rosy Pipit breeds in the highest meadows near the snowline. The valley is accessible only on foot from Govindghat and is free of vehicle traffic, creating ideal conditions for watching undisturbed birds across a full day's walk.

## 3. Chopta and Tungnath, Uttarakhand

Chopta, sometimes called the mini-Switzerland of Uttarakhand, is a high-altitude meadow accessed via a road from Ukhimath. The treeline here is around 3,300 metres, and the meadow above provides accessible open-country birding for species including Snow Bunting, Rufous-breasted Bush Robin, and Himalayan Rubythroat in summer. The forest below the meadow, especially the bugyals on the Tungnath ridge, is a reliable site for Koklass Pheasant at dawn. Lammergeier — the bearded vulture — is regularly seen soaring above the ridges. The lack of intensive tourism makes this one of the more peaceful birding spots in the Garhwal Himalaya.

## 4. Pangot and Kilbury, Nainital, Uttarakhand

Pangot, approximately 15 kilometres from Nainital, is a small settlement within dense oak-rhododendron forest that is consistently rated among the best birding sites in the western Himalayas. Kalij Pheasant and Koklass Pheasant are regular at dawn on the forest tracks. The forest holds Chestnut-headed Tesia, Rufous-bellied Niltava, and Verditer Flycatcher. During winter, the bird diversity is exceptional with flocks of Himalayan Bluetail, Yellow-browed Warbler, and Ashy-throated Warbler moving through the forest. The road from Nainital to Pangot passes through Kilbury, where the forest department maintains a birding track that consistently produces Speckled Wood Pigeon and Brown Wood Owl.

## 5. Sattal, Nainital District, Uttarakhand

The seven interconnected lakes at Sattal sit at around 1,350 metres in a forested valley of the Kumaon foothills. The combination of water, oak forest, and cultivated margins creates exceptional bird diversity. Long-tailed Broadbill is regularly seen in the forest near the lakes, along with Asian Barred Owlet and Brown-breasted Flycatcher. The lakes attract wintering diving ducks and, during migration, occasional vagrant waders. The Indian Pitta is a loud but elusive summer visitor in the forest undergrowth. The area has been a well-known birding destination since the 1920s, and the bird diversity is now well documented, making it easy to plan target species.

## 6. Kaziranga National Park, Assam

Kaziranga in the Brahmaputra floodplain at the Himalayan foothills in Assam is primarily known for one-horned rhinoceroses but the birding is outstanding. Bengal Florican persists here in the tall elephant grass, and the park holds one of the most important populations of this critically endangered bustard. The wetlands support Bar-headed Goose in winter, and Greater Adjutant Stork — now critically endangered globally — gathers in numbers near the park boundary. Eastern Imperial Eagle and Pallas's Fish Eagle hunt the river channels. The elephant-back safaris and jeep circuits give excellent access to the grassland and wetland habitats.

## 7. Shillong and Nongthliat, Meghalaya

The hills of Meghalaya in northeast India form a distinct biogeographic zone at the southern escarpment of the Himalayan foothills, and the forests here hold many species with affinities to the Indo-Chinese subregion. Nongkhyllem Wildlife Sanctuary near Nongpoh and the forests around Nongthliat village shelter Rufous-necked Hornbill, Blyth's Kingfisher, and a range of babblers rare in the rest of India. Clouded Leopard has been recorded in the area, and the Giant Nuthatch inhabits the large trees of undisturbed forest. The relative inaccessibility of Meghalayan forests has preserved bird communities that are disappearing elsewhere.

## 8. Koshi Tappu Wildlife Reserve, Nepal

Koshi Tappu on the Koshi river in the eastern Terai of Nepal is the country's most important wetland birding site, recognised as a Ramsar site for its waterbird diversity. The riverine wetlands support Swamp Francolin, Bengal Florican in very small numbers, and one of the last herds of wild Asian Water Buffalo. The oxbow lakes attract wintering ducks including Ferruginous Duck and Baer's Pochard, both of high conservation concern globally. The spring migration here is exceptional, with large numbers of waders using the river bars and floodplain. Over 500 species have been recorded at Koshi, including many rarities from Siberia and Central Asia passing through.

## 9. Langtang National Park, Nepal

Langtang, north of Kathmandu, is the Himalayan national park most accessible from Nepal's capital and provides a full altitudinal transect from subtropical forest to high-altitude alpine. The Langtang valley trail passes through rhododendron forest holding Himalayan Monal, Satyr Tragopan, and Snow Partridge at progressively higher elevations. The alpine meadows above Kyanjin Gompa at 3,800 metres support Red-billed Chough, Yellow-billed Chough, and Alpine Accentor. Lammergeier and Himalayan Griffon are constant presences in the mountain thermals. The valley is less commercially developed than the Annapurna circuit and feels genuinely wild despite its relative accessibility.

## 10. Phobjikha Valley, Bhutan

Phobjikha is a glacially carved valley in central Bhutan that functions as the primary wintering ground for the Black-necked Crane, a culturally revered bird in Bhutan. The cranes arrive from their Tibetan Plateau breeding grounds in October and depart in March, with peak numbers of several hundred birds in December and January. The wetland and grassland habitat of the valley floor, surrounded by conifer forest, also supports Hen Harrier, Peregrine Falcon, and large numbers of Common Crane. The nearby Gangtey Monastery provides an elevated viewpoint over the valley. Bhutan's conservation ethic and low tourism density make this one of the most tranquil birding experiences in Asia.

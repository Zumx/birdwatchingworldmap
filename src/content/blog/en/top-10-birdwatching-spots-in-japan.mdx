Japan occupies a narrow archipelago stretching from subtropical Okinawa to subarctic Hokkaido, and that 3,000-kilometre span encompasses bird communities with almost no overlap. The winter concentrations on Hokkaido are among the most dramatic cold-season spectacles in the Northern Hemisphere. The summer forest birds of the Japanese Alps are related to continental Palearctic species. The endemic birds of Okinawa and the Ryukyu chain exist nowhere else. A thorough Japanese birding itinerary requires multiple visits. These ten sites represent the most significant concentrations. All are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Hokkaido in Winter — Akan and Tsurui

The Kushiro area of eastern Hokkaido holds the wintering and breeding grounds of the Red-crowned Crane (Grus japonensis), one of the rarest cranes in the world. The Tsurui-Ito Tancho Sanctuary feeds cranes daily from November to March; up to 700 birds gather at the feeding station, performing their spectacular dancing displays against a background of snow-covered meadows. Akan International Crane Centre provides interpretation. The same Hokkaido winter also gives access to Steller's Sea Eagle and White-tailed Eagle hunting the pack ice and river mouths, and the remarkable Blakiston's Fish Owl (Bubo blakistoni) — the world's largest owl — on rivers flowing through mixed forest near Rausu and along the Shiretoko Peninsula.

## 2. Karuizawa, Nagano

The highland resort town of Karuizawa at 1,000 metres in the Japanese Alps is the premier summer birding site in central Honshu. The mixed forest around the town holds Japanese Green Woodpecker, White's Thrush, Eastern Crowned Warbler, and the full complement of Japanese forest passerines. The Karuizawa Wild Bird Forest, a managed reserve in the town, has boardwalks and a tower suitable for observing canopy species. The area is also the most reliable site in Japan for Narcissus Flycatcher and Blue-and-white Flycatcher in breeding habitat. May and June are peak.

## 3. Kushiro Marshland, Hokkaido

Kushiro Wetland National Park — Japan's largest wetland at 19,000 hectares — is the other core site for Red-crowned Crane, where the birds breed in late spring and summer rather than gathering at feeding stations. The marsh is viewed from several elevated observation platforms; canoe trips on the Kushiro River give water-level access to breeding Eurasian Bittern and nesting White-tailed Eagles. In winter the marsh edge and river produce foraging cranes alongside Whooper Swans.

## 4. Hokkaido — Steller's Sea Eagle on the Pack Ice, Rausu

Rausu on the eastern Shiretoko Peninsula is the primary site for close observation of Steller's Sea Eagle (Haliaeetus pelagicus) and White-tailed Eagle from winter tour boats that navigate between ice floes. Steller's Sea Eagle is the heaviest eagle in the world; viewing 30 to 50 individuals from a boat at close range in January through March is an experience not replicable anywhere else. Rausu boats also encounter Common and Brünnich's Murres (Guillemots), Harlequin Duck, Long-tailed Duck, and, occasionally, Sperm Whale.

## 5. Yatsu-higata Tidal Flat, Chiba

Yatsu-higata is a small remnant tidal flat preserved within the urban sprawl of Narashino, Chiba prefecture, a 40-minute train ride from Tokyo station. Its importance is disproportionate to its size: it is an eBird hotspot with over 200 species recorded and one of the most reliable shorebird sites in the greater Tokyo area. Dunlin, Dunlin's Asian sister, Grey Plover, Rufous-necked Stint, and on passage, Terek Sandpiper and Asian Dowitcher are regular. The flat is surrounded by a viewing path and observation shelter.

## 6. Tsurui-Ito Tancho Sanctuary area — Cranes in flight

The crane feeding ground at Tsurui near Akan in eastern Hokkaido is the centrepiece of a broader winter spectacle that includes morning departure flights of the full crane population from their roost on the Setsuri River. Arriving at the river in darkness and watching 500 cranes take off at first light — calling, displaying, and wheeling in the cold air — is one of the most widely published wildlife images in Japan and as impressive in reality as in photography.

## 7. Izu Islands — Seabirds

The Izu Islands south of Tokyo are part of the Pacific tectonic chain and support breeding colonies of Streaked Shearwater, Japanese Murrelet, and, on the remote outer islands, Short-tailed Albatross. Torishima Island is the single most important breeding site for Short-tailed Albatross (Phoebastria albatrus), a species reduced to under 50 individuals in the 1940s and recovered to several thousand through long-term protection. The ferry route from Takeshiba Pier to the Izu Islands passes through productive pelagic water and is an accessible introduction to North Pacific seabirds.

## 8. Okinawa Highlands — Okinawa Rail

The Okinawa Yambaru highlands in northern Okinawa Island are the only location in the world where the Okinawa Rail (Hypotaenidia okinawae) occurs. Described as a new species only in 1981, the rail is flightless and restricted to the wet subtropical forest of Kunigami, Ogimi, and Higashi villages. Searching for it along forest roads at night with a torch is the standard approach; the birds are active, and with local knowledge of current territories, finding one is realistic. The same forest holds Ryukyu Robin, Okinawa Woodpecker, and Okinawa Scops Owl — all range-restricted to the Ryukyu chain.

## 9. Tsushima, Nagasaki

Tsushima Island in the Korea Strait is a stepping stone for East Asian migrants and holds several species more characteristic of the Korean Peninsula than of Honshu. Tristram's Bunting, Yellow Bunting, and Grey Nightjar are specialties. In May and October, the island's mixed forest and scrub hold good migration falls: Yellow-rumped Flycatcher, Blue-and-white Flycatcher, and Asian Brown Flycatcher in their hundreds. The Tsushima Leopard Cat is the island's mammal specialty; the Tsushima Woodcock breeds in the denser forest.

## 10. Karuizawa Wild Bird Forest and Mt Fuji forest area, Yamanashi

The Aokigahara forest at the base of Mount Fuji's northwest slope is dense, mature, and structurally complex, holding a forest bird community that includes Japanese Grosbeak, Eurasian Nuthatch, Varied Tit, and winter visiting Brambling in good numbers. The Kawaguchiko wild bird sanctuary at the lake's western shore has managed woodland and a feeding area that attracts Hawfinch, Japanese Waxwing in winter invasion years, and Pale Thrush. Combined with a Fuji Five Lakes loop, the Mt Fuji foothills provide a productive one-day circuit for central Honshu forest species.

## Planning a Japan birding trip

Japan's birding divides naturally by season. A winter trip to Hokkaido (January to March) focused on cranes, eagles, and the fish owl is entirely different from a spring trip to Honshu (April to June) targeting forest passerines and raptors, which in turn differs from an autumn trip to Tsushima or the Izu Islands for migration. Attempting to combine Hokkaido winter birds with Okinawa forest birds in a single two-week trip is possible by domestic flight but requires precise weather-window management.

Japan's domestic transport infrastructure means most sites on this list are accessible without a rental car; the Shinkansen and local rail reach Karuizawa, Hakone, and the Kyushu gateway to Tsushima. Hokkaido requires either rental car or local bus; the crane sites near Tsurui and Rausu are not practically reached by public transport in winter.

The Wild Bird Society of Japan publishes detailed site guides; the Japan eBird portal is well-populated by Japanese birders. The [map](/map) shows all ten sites and their geographic relationships across the archipelago.

Zimbabwe carries a quiet reputation among African birders, overshadowed by Kenya and South Africa, yet the country list stands above 670 species packed into a compact and easily driven landscape. The Eastern Highlands along the Mozambique border catch montane mist that supports a handful of birds found almost nowhere else in the region, while the Zambezi River draws the country's most photographed riverine community. After difficult years for tourism, the guiding networks around Bulawayo, Harare and Mutare are active again, and well-maintained tar roads link most of the prime sites within a week-long loop. What makes the country so efficient for birders is its layering: in a single fortnight you can move from cool montane forest dripping with epiphytes, through broad miombo woodland, into the Kalahari sand country of the west, and finally to the hot riverine fringes of the Zambezi, each holding a distinct community.

## Bvumba Mountains and the Eastern Highlands

The Bvumba (Vumba) range east of Mutare rises to around 1,900 metres and holds the country's signature montane species. Seldomseen and the Bvumba Botanical Reserve are the classic access points, and the famous Benjhi Platform and forest hides around Seldomseen are where birders go for the localised Swynnerton's Robin, Roberts's Warbler, Chirinda Apalis and the Stripe-cheeked Greenbul. Robin numbers respond to the wet montane forest understorey, so early mornings after rain are most productive. The mist-belt forest here is patchy and shrinking, which makes the protected fragments disproportionately important.

## Chimanimani and Nyanga

Further along the highland spine, Nyanga National Park combines miombo woodland, montane grassland and the country's highest ground at Mount Nyangani (2,592 metres). Blue Swallow breeds in the damp upland grassland in summer, one of the most sought-after sightings in southern Africa, alongside Gurney's Sugarbird on the protea slopes and Cape Eagle-Owl on the rocky crags. Chimanimani to the south offers more rugged, less-visited terrain with similar specialties and the bonus of Scarce Swift over the quartzite peaks. These grasslands are best from October to January when the swallows return.

## Victoria Falls and the Zambezi

The spray forest at Victoria Falls is a permanently humid microhabitat unique on the river, and it shelters Schalow's Turaco, Trumpeter Hornbill and Half-collared Kingfisher within walking distance of the town. The wider Zambezi here adds African Finfoot on quiet channels, Rock Pratincole on the mid-river rocks during the dry season, and Collared Palm-Thrush in the riverside vegetation. Sunset boat cruises on the upper river are an easy way to find Long-toed Lapwing, White-crowned Lapwing and the occasional African Skimmer working the sandbars.

## Mana Pools National Park

Mana Pools, a World Heritage Site on the middle Zambezi, is the country's most atmospheric wilderness, where walking safaris are permitted among the cathedral-like Faidherbia albida woodland. Birding here is rich: Lilian's Lovebird, Meves's Starling, Southern Carmine Bee-eater colonies on the riverbanks in the dry season, and the elusive Pel's Fishing-Owl roosting in the riverine fig trees. The floodplain pools concentrate waterbirds as the land dries from June onward, and African Skimmer breeds on exposed sand. Access is seasonal, with the park typically closed during the heaviest rains.

## Hwange National Park

Hwange is Zimbabwe's largest park at around 14,600 square kilometres, dominated by Kalahari sand woodland and seasonal pans. The dry-country birds are the attraction: Bradfield's Hornbill, Arnot's Chat in the mopane, Racket-tailed Roller, and Southern Ground-Hornbill striding across the open grassland. Pumped waterholes such as Nyamandhlovu draw raptors and sandgrouse, and the camp gardens at Main Camp are reliable for Crimson-breasted Shrike and Violet-eared Waxbill. The cool dry months from May to August offer the most comfortable game-and-bird drives.

## Matobo Hills

The granite domes and balancing rocks of Matobo, south of Bulawayo, form one of the highest densities of breeding Black Eagle (Verreaux's Eagle) anywhere on the continent, feeding on the abundant rock hyrax. Long-running research at Matobo has tracked these eagles for decades, and a patient morning on a high viewpoint such as the World's View area will usually produce soaring pairs at close range. Beyond the eagles, the broken country holds Boulder Chat, a near-endemic of the Zimbabwean granite, along with Mocking Cliff-Chat and Freckled Nightjar after dark. The matrix of granite, msasa woodland and small dams also supports Augur Buzzard, African Hawk-Eagle and a good range of seedeaters. The combination of ancient rock art in the caves, whaleback kopjes and raptors makes Matobo a rewarding cultural-and-birding day from the city.

## Harare wetlands and miombo

The capital itself is surprisingly birdy and makes an easy soft landing for the jet-lagged. Lake Chivero and the Mukuvisi Woodlands inside Harare protect tracts of miombo where Miombo Tit, Wood Pipit, Whyte's Barbet, Miombo Rock Thrush and Spotted Creeper are found within the city limits, often in roving mixed-species parties that move through the canopy at mid-morning. The vleis (seasonal wetlands) scattered across the highveld around Harare flood in summer and attain Striped Crake, African Rail and large numbers of widowbirds and bishops in breeding plumage. These accessible patches make Harare a logical first or last stop on any itinerary.

## Gonarezhou and the southeast lowveld

Gonarezhou in the far southeast, part of the Great Limpopo Transfrontier Park, sits in hot lowveld dominated by the red Chilojo Cliffs above the Runde River. The dry savanna and riverine strips hold Three-banded Courser, Racket-tailed Roller, Lemon-breasted Canary in the ilala palms, and good numbers of vultures including White-headed and Lappet-faced. Far less visited than Hwange, it rewards birders willing to travel for a true wilderness feel and the chance of southern Mozambique influences in the avifauna.

## Practicalities and timing

The richest birding runs from November to March, the wet summer when migrants arrive, resident species breed, and the Eastern Highlands specials such as Blue Swallow are present. Intra-African migrants such as cuckoos, the Southern Carmine Bee-eater and the Woodland Kingfisher pour in, and the bush is alive with song. The trade-off is rain, mud and dense vegetation, and some dirt roads to remote sites become difficult. The dry season from May to October concentrates waterbirds at shrinking pools, makes game viewing far easier in Hwange and Mana Pools, and offers comfortable, midge-free highland walking. A practical two-week loop runs Harare to the Eastern Highlands, back across to Hwange and Victoria Falls, with Mana Pools as a fly-in extension from Kariba. Self-drive is feasible on the main tar routes between Harare, Bulawayo and Victoria Falls, but the dry-forest parks reward a 4x4 and a guide. Local birding guides arranged through BirdLife Zimbabwe and the lodges are knowledgeable and reasonably priced, and the country's general stability has improved markedly for visiting birders.

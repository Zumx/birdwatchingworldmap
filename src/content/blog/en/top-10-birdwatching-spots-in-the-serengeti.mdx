The Serengeti ecosystem is one of the most celebrated wildlife areas on Earth, and its reputation rests largely on the annual wildebeest migration — nearly two million animals moving in a great circuit between the Serengeti plains of Tanzania and the Masai Mara of Kenya. But the Serengeti's birds are equally remarkable and far less discussed. The ecosystem supports over 500 species across habitats ranging from short-grass plains and acacia savanna to riverine forest, seasonally flooded marshes, and rocky kopje outcrops. The Secretarybird strides the open plains in search of snakes. The Grey Crowned Crane, Tanzania's national bird, dances in the wetland margins. Martial Eagle, the largest eagle in Africa by weight, soars above the plains in search of prey visible from hundreds of metres up.

The Serengeti's birdwatching rewards both dedicated bird-focused trips and the incidental attention of visitors primarily there for the mammal migration. Birds are available in quantity and variety throughout the year, though the resident bird breeding season from October through December is particularly productive. The following ten locations within the greater Serengeti ecosystem represent the best birding nodes.

## 1. Seronera River Valley, Central Serengeti

The Seronera River is the most productive birding corridor in the central Serengeti, providing permanent water and riverine forest in an otherwise open grassland landscape. The fig trees along the river hold Bare-faced Go-away Bird, Superb Starling, and a reliable Verreaux's Eagle-Owl in the daytime roost. African Fish Eagle calls from the trees over the river, and Pied Kingfisher and Malachite Kingfisher both fish the shallower reaches. The rocky kopjes around Seronera are the stronghold of the Klipspringer and also hold Familiar Chat, Horus Swift nesting in rock crevices, and — during summer months — Rock Martin. The area around Seronera Lodge and the kopjes immediately east consistently produce 80 or more species in a full day.

## 2. Masai Mara National Reserve, Kenya

The Masai Mara is the Kenyan extension of the Serengeti ecosystem, and the Mara River crossing where wildebeest are taken by Nile Crocodile is famous globally. For birders, the riparian forest along the Mara River holds African Finfoot, Brown-necked Parrot, and Broad-billed Roller. The open plains support large concentrations of vultures: White-backed Vulture, Rüppell's Griffon, Lappet-faced Vulture, and White-headed Vulture are all regular. The seasonally flooded grass plains of the northern Mara attract Denham's Bustard and Buff-crested Bustard alongside the more common Kori Bustard. The Mara has a higher density of safari camps than the Serengeti and early morning bird walks from the main lodges are productive.

## 3. Ndutu Area and Southern Plains

The Ndutu lake area in the extreme south of the Serengeti, near the Ngorongoro Conservation Area boundary, is where the wildebeest calving takes place from January through March. The shallow soda lakes here attract Lesser Flamingo and Greater Flamingo in variable numbers, and the acacia woodland around the lake holds some of the most diverse bird communities in the ecosystem. White-bellied Go-away Bird, D'Arnaud's Barbet, and Red-and-yellow Barbet are all present. The open bush near Ndutu Lodge is a reliable site for Secretarybird in the early morning, walking with characteristic deliberate steps through the low grass.

## 4. Ngorongoro Crater

The Ngorongoro Crater is a collapsed volcanic caldera containing a self-contained ecosystem of about 250 square kilometres. The crater floor has a permanent lake, Magadi, which supports Lesser Flamingo in variable numbers alongside Great White Pelican and an exceptional diversity of waterbirds. African Marsh Harrier, Black-shouldered Kite, and Martial Eagle hunt over the crater floor simultaneously, and the concentration of raptors on the crater rim as they warm on thermals in the morning is remarkable. Common Ostrich stalks the open short-grass areas. The rim itself, at over 2,000 metres, holds a completely different montane forest community that includes Hartlaub's Turaco and Olive Thrush.

## 5. Grumeti River and Western Serengeti

The Grumeti River in the western corridor of the Serengeti supports perhaps the densest fig tree gallery forest in the ecosystem, and the fig crop cycle attracts large numbers of frugivorous birds when ripe. Ross's Turaco, Grey-capped Warbler, and African Golden Weaver are all found here and are difficult to see elsewhere in the ecosystem. The Grumeti River also holds enormous Nile Crocodiles and Hippopotamus, which create open water and mud banks that attract Hamerkop and African Spoonbill. The western corridor is less visited than the central Serengeti and feels genuinely wild, with extensive views and lower vehicle density.

## 6. Lobo Area and Northern Serengeti

The northern Serengeti around Lobo is where the wildebeest herds spend the dry season from July through September, but the area is productive for birds year-round. The rocky hills and granite outcrops around Lobo hold the Striped Pipit, which otherwise inhabits montane areas, along with Cinnamon-chested Bee-eater and Red-throated Twinspot in the denser vegetation. The mixed acacia-commiphora woodland in this area is more diverse in structure than the plains to the south, and the bird community reflects this: White-headed Buffalo Weaver, Donaldson-Smith's Nightjar, and Bare-cheeked Starling are all findable with a knowledgeable local guide. The Mara River entering from Kenya flows through this section.

## 7. Tarangire National Park

Tarangire, while not technically within the Serengeti ecosystem, is part of the same greater Tanzanian savanna landscape and is widely considered the best birding park in Tanzania per unit of area. The ancient baobab trees hold Yellow-collared Lovebird, a near-endemic to the Maasai steppe region. The seasonal Tarangire River attracts massive concentrations of elephants and the associated birding during the dry season is exceptional. The Silale Swamp within the park holds Rufous-tailed Weaver, the Ashy Starling (a near-endemic to the Maasai steppe), and large numbers of herons and egrets. The diversity of habitats in a compact park makes Tarangire ideal for bird-focused visits.

## 8. Lake Victoria Shore and Musoma

The western edge of the Serengeti reaches the shore of Lake Victoria near Musoma, and the lake margin adds a distinct community of freshwater specialists. African Skimmer nests on the sandy lake beaches. Papyrus Gonolek inhabits the papyrus fringe and is difficult to see but its loud calls betray its presence. Great Blue Turaco moves through the gallery forest in the lake-edge vegetation. Shoebill has been recorded in the papyrus swamps and is an extreme rarity, but the habitat is correct. The lake shore is rarely visited by birders, partly because the focus of Serengeti tourism is on the interior plains, making it one of the better options for avoiding crowds while maintaining species diversity.

## 9. Olduvai Gorge Vicinity

The Olduvai Gorge, famous for hominid fossil discoveries, sits in a dry volcanic landscape between the Serengeti plains and the Ngorongoro highlands. The gorge itself and the surrounding semi-arid scrub hold bird species typical of dry Maasai steppe: Masai Lark, a range-restricted endemic, inhabits the sparse grass and rocky edges of the gorge. Northern White-crowned Shrike is conspicuous in the acacia woodland. Red-fronted Barbet and Parrot-billed Sparrow occur in the drier acacia thickets. The area is visited primarily as a fossil tourism stop but the birding around the gorge during a midday rest is consistently productive for dry-country specialists.

## 10. Serengeti National Park Entrance Gate Area, Naabi Hill

The Naabi Hill gate at the southeastern entrance to the Serengeti National Park sits on a rocky kopje that marks the transition from the Ngorongoro Conservation Area to the park proper. The hill is a reliable site for Lanner Falcon and Red-necked Falcon, both raptors that nest in rock crevices. The open plains immediately west of Naabi are some of the most productive Secretarybird habitat in the ecosystem, with pairs regularly visible from the main track. Kori Bustard — the world's heaviest flying bird — moves across the open grass, and the stately gait of a large male in display is one of the more memorable sights on the southern plains. Cheetah hunting at dawn near Naabi frequently attracts following Tawny Eagles and Bateleur.

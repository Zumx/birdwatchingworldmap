Few countries pack as much avian variety into as little space as Nepal. In a horizontal distance of barely 150 kilometres the land climbs from the steamy lowland terai at under 100 metres to the summit of Everest at 8,849 metres, and the bird life rises with it. The subtropical grasslands and sal forests of Chitwan hold Bengal Florican and Great Hornbill; the broadleaf forests of the midhills shelter laughingthrushes and the spectacular Spiny Babbler, the country's only true endemic; and the rhododendron and conifer zones of the high Himalaya are the realm of blood pheasants, snowcocks and the iridescent Himalayan Monal, the national bird. More than 880 species have been recorded, an astonishing total for a country the size of a single large European region.

## 1. Chitwan National Park

Nepal's first national park and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, Chitwan protects a mosaic of tall elephant grass, riverine forest and oxbow lakes in the central terai. It holds an exceptional list: Bengal Florican (Houbaropsis bengalensis, Critically Endangered), Great Hornbill, Lesser Adjutant, Gharial-haunted rivers with Black-bellied Tern, and the localised Slaty-breasted Rail. A dawn canoe trip on the Rapti River produces kingfishers, storks and the chance of Ruddy Kingfisher.

## 2. Koshi Tappu Wildlife Reserve

In the eastern terai, the floodplain of the Sapta Koshi River forms Nepal's premier wetland and a Ramsar site. Koshi Tappu is the best place in the country for waterbirds: Swamp Francolin, Bristled Grassbird, Black-necked Stork, and vast numbers of wintering ducks and waders. It is the only reliable Nepali site for Indian Skimmer and the last stronghold of the wild water buffalo. The reserve records well over 480 species.

## 3. Phulchowki, Kathmandu Valley

The highest hill rimming the Kathmandu Valley at 2,762 metres, Phulchowki is the most accessible mid-elevation forest birding in the country. Its broadleaf and oak forest holds the endemic Spiny Babbler (Turdoides nipalensis) on the lower slopes, alongside Cutia, laughingthrushes, Rufous-bellied Niltava and many sunbirds. A single day on the road to the summit can yield over a hundred species across the altitudinal bands.

## 4. Shivapuri Nagarjun National Park

On the northern rim of the Kathmandu Valley, Shivapuri rises to 2,732 metres and protects extensive midhill forest within easy reach of the capital. The forest holds Hoary-throated Barwing, Spiny Babbler, Kalij Pheasant and a strong selection of warblers and flycatchers. The trails toward the Shivapuri peak pass through changing forest zones rich in mixed feeding flocks.

## 5. Pokhara and Phewa Lake

The lakeside city of Pokhara, with the Annapurna range as a backdrop, offers both wetland and foothill birding. Phewa Lake and the surrounding marshes hold cormorants, egrets and wintering ducks, while the World Peace Pagoda hill and the Begnas Lake area add forest species. Pokhara is also the gateway to the trekking routes that climb into true Himalayan habitat.

## 6. Annapurna Conservation Area

The trekking trails of the Annapurna region climb through every Himalayan forest zone. The rhododendron forests around Ghorepani and Poon Hill hold Himalayan Monal (Lophophorus impejanus), Satyr Tragopan, Kalij Pheasant and a wealth of laughingthrushes and rosefinches. Higher up, toward the Annapurna Sanctuary, the conifer and alpine zones produce Snow Partridge, Grandala and accentors.

## 7. Langtang National Park

North of Kathmandu, Langtang offers some of the most accessible high-altitude birding in Nepal. The valley climbs through oak and rhododendron into juniper and alpine meadow. Blood Pheasant, Himalayan Monal, Snow Partridge and Tibetan Snowcock occur, and the Kyanjin Gompa area at around 3,800 metres holds Grandala, Alpine Accentor and Snow Pigeon. Lammergeier and Himalayan Griffon ride the thermals above the ridges.

## 8. Sagarmatha (Everest) National Park

The Khumbu valleys around Everest hold a hardy high-altitude community. Above Namche Bazaar, the forests and scrub hold Blood Pheasant and Himalayan Monal, while the alpine zone above the treeline produces Tibetan Snowcock, Snow Pigeon, Brandt's Mountain Finch and the occasional Wallcreeper on the cliffs. Lammergeier (Gypaetus barbatus, Near Threatened) and Himalayan Griffon are constant overhead.

## 9. Bardia National Park

In the far western terai, Bardia is Chitwan's wilder, quieter counterpart, protecting sal forest, grassland and the Karnali and Babai rivers. It holds Bengal Florican, Sarus Crane, Great Hornbill, Lesser Adjutant and a strong raptor list. The riverine forest and the Karnali floodplain produce kingfishers, fishing eagles and the Gangetic specialities, and the park is among the best places in Nepal for both birds and tigers.

## 10. Rara National Park

In the remote northwest, Rara protects Nepal's largest lake at 2,990 metres, ringed by blue pine and spruce forest. The high lake holds wintering and migrant waterfowl unusual for the elevation, while the surrounding conifer forest produces Snow Partridge, Impeyan Pheasant, accentors and rosefinches. Its remoteness keeps numbers low, making it one of the most atmospheric birding destinations in the country.

## Planning a birding trip in Nepal

Nepal's prime birding season is spring, from March to May, when the resident montane birds are in song and breeding plumage, the rhododendrons flower across the midhills, and the high trails open up as the snow recedes. This is the time for the pheasants and the Himalayan specialities. Autumn (October to November) brings clear skies, returning migrants and the best mountain views, and is the classic trekking season. Winter (December to February) is the time for the terai wetlands of Koshi Tappu and Chitwan, when wildfowl and raptors are at their peak and the lowlands are comfortably cool.

A typical itinerary combines a terai park — Chitwan or Bardia — with a midhill site such as Phulchowki near Kathmandu, and, for the high birds, a trek into the Annapurna or Langtang region. Kathmandu is the hub for the central and mountain sites; the western parks are reached via Nepalganj. The road journeys are slow on mountain terrain, so internal flights to Pokhara, Bharatpur or Nepalganj save valuable time.

Practical notes: altitude must be respected on the high treks, with proper acclimatisation built into any itinerary climbing above 3,000 metres. The terai parks require a licensed guide and, for the grasslands, often an elephant or jeep to access the Bengal Florican habitat. Bird Conservation Nepal, the national BirdLife partner, maintains site information and trip-report archives that are invaluable for planning. With its compressed altitudinal gradient delivering everything from hornbills to snowcocks within a single country, Nepal offers a richness of birding found almost nowhere else on earth.

Finland sits at the northern edge of the Western Palearctic, and its vast boreal forest — the taiga — stretches from the southern coast to the treeline of Lapland. Approximately 73 percent of the country is forested, and a further 10 percent is covered by wetlands, bogs, and fens. This combination makes Finland one of Europe's most productive countries for boreal specialist species: owls of multiple species, woodpeckers, grouse, and the wading birds of the northern mires. Over 480 species have been recorded nationally. During the brief but intense Arctic summer, the long days compress enormous breeding activity into just a few weeks, making birding trips in late May and June exceptionally rewarding.

Migration in Finland is spectacular for those who know where to look. The southwestern archipelago acts as a funnel for passerine migrants leaving Finland in autumn. Coastal headlands and islands become congested with finches, thrushes, and raptors during the peak October weeks. Cranes gathering at traditional staging areas before their southwestern autumn journey are a defining Finnish wildlife spectacle.

## 1. Liminganlahti Bay, Northern Ostrobothnia

Liminganlahti is Finland's most important coastal wetland site, a broad shallow bay at the southern end of the Gulf of Bothnia that attracts tens of thousands of migratory waterbirds each spring and autumn. The bay is backed by traditional meadows, flooded grasslands, and reedbeds managed specifically for birds. Breeding species include Ruff, Whimbrel, Black-tailed Godwit, and Curlew. In May, the concentrations of waders and wildfowl are extraordinary — Bean Goose, Greater White-fronted Goose, and Barnacle Goose all stop here. The visitor centre at Liminka provides excellent facilities, and the network of dikes and observation towers allows comfortable birding across the most sensitive areas.

## 2. Oulanka National Park, Northern Ostrobothnia

Oulanka encompasses a dramatic river canyon system along the border with Russia, covered in old-growth taiga with extensive boreal wetlands. The park is one of the best places in Finland to find Siberian Jay, which is completely tame around regular feeding stations. Three-toed Woodpecker works the standing dead birches. Tengmalm's Owl and Pygmy Owl both nest in the forest cavities. During the brief Arctic spring, Common Crane and Lesser White-fronted Goose pass through the valley. The Karhunkierros Trail — one of Finland's most famous hiking routes — passes through the best habitats. Guided owl excursions operate from Kuusamo town nearby.

## 3. Hanko Bird Observatory, Uusimaa

The Hanko Peninsula at the southwestern tip of Finland is the principal migration watch point for the country. In autumn, the narrow peninsula acts as a gathering point for hundreds of thousands of birds converging before crossing the Baltic Sea. Raptor migration at Hanko is among the best in northern Europe, with Honey Buzzard, Osprey, all three European Accipiters, and both Rough-legged and Common Buzzard passing in large numbers during September and October. Passerine migration is even larger in scale — finch flights in October can involve tens of thousands of Chaffinches, Bramblings, and Siskins per hour. The observatory runs ringing operations and a daily migration count from the point.

## 4. Patvinsuo National Park, North Karelia

Patvinsuo is a large raised bog and lake complex in eastern Finland close to the Russian border. It is one of the most important sites in Finland for breeding waterbirds and mire-nesting species. Common Crane breeds in the bog pools. Whooper Swan nests on the larger lakes. Black-throated Diver is resident on the clear boreal lakes within the park. The undisturbed mire supports breeding Wood Sandpiper, Broad-billed Sandpiper, and Temminck's Stint. Great Grey Owl hunts over the open bog edges at dawn and dusk. The remoteness and minimal visitor infrastructure mean this site is best experienced by those prepared for long walks on boardwalked bog trails.

## 5. Vesijako Strict Nature Reserve, Pirkanmaa

The forests around Vesijako in Pirkanmaa Province contain one of the densest populations of boreal forest raptors in central Finland. Ural Owl is the resident speciality — large, pale, and less nocturnal than most owls, Ural Owls are often active in broad daylight, particularly during the breeding season in March and April when they perch openly near nest boxes. Lesser Spotted Eagle and Honey Buzzard breed in the mature forest. Hazel Grouse is common in the dense younger birch stands, typically detected by its distinctive whistle before being glimpsed in the undergrowth. White-backed Woodpecker, one of Finland's scarcest woodpeckers, uses the old birch woodland along the lakeshores.

## 6. Varanger Peninsula, Finnmark (border region)

While technically in Norway, the Varanger Peninsula is the Arctic destination most frequented by Finnish birders crossing the border from Lapland. The combination of frozen coastal tundra, fish-rich fjords, and seabird colonies makes this one of the most productive Arctic birding areas in Scandinavia. Steller's Eider winters in the sheltered bays in numbers unusual this far west. King Eider, Ivory Gull, and Ross's Gull are annual rarities. The accessible tundra around Vadsø and Nesseby holds breeding Lapland Bunting, Bar-tailed Godwit, and Grey Plover in summer. Many Finnish birders combine a Lapland driving trip with a Varanger extension.

## 7. Kvarken Archipelago, Ostrobothnia

The Kvarken archipelago in the Gulf of Bothnia is a UNESCO World Heritage Site noted for its ongoing land uplift following the last ice age. The shallow flint-grey sea between Finland and Sweden is rising from the sea at around eight millimetres per year, constantly creating new islands and skerries. The bird community is dominated by breeding waterbirds — Common Eider, Arctic Tern, Razorbill, and Black Guillemot on the outermost islands. Sea eagles, including White-tailed Eagle, nest throughout the archipelago and are easily observed from boat trips. The shallow waters attract Great Cormorant in large roosting flocks during summer.

## 8. Siikalahti Bird Sanctuary, South Karelia

Siikalahti is a shallow eutrophic bay on Lake Simpele that has been a designated bird sanctuary since the 1950s and is one of Finland's oldest protected wetlands. The reedbeds, sedge meadows, and open water support breeding Eurasian Bittern — heard much more than seen, its booming call carrying across the water at dusk. Great Reed Warbler and Marsh Warbler both sing from the reedbed edges. Garganey is a regular spring migrant. The hides overlooking the open bay are well-positioned for watching Osprey fishing. During spring migration in May, Siikalahti attracts large concentrations of diving ducks including Scaup, Goldeneye, and Tufted Duck.

## 9. Urho Kekkonen National Park, Lapland

Finland's largest national park covers over 2,500 square kilometres of the Saariselka fell area in northern Lapland. The treeless fells and river valley birch scrub hold a complete Arctic bird community. Long-tailed Skua — elegant and long-tailed in breeding plumage — nests on the open tundra. Dotterel breeds on the highest fells and is approachable to within a few metres during incubation. Rough-legged Buzzard quarters the open fell in search of lemmings. Whimbrel is the common large wader of the fell. The remoteness of the park means most birding is done on foot along the fell trails from Saariselka village, ideally during the Arctic midsummer when the sun does not set.

## 10. Espoonlahti Bay, Uusimaa

Espoonlahti is a suburban bay on the edge of Helsinki's western fringe, easily accessible by public transport from the city centre. Despite its proximity to dense urban development, the bay retains productive reedbeds and shallow margins that support a good variety of waterbirds. Caspian Tern fishes in the bay during summer. Great Crested Grebe and Black-necked Grebe both breed. During autumn migration, Espoonlahti attracts sea ducks including Velvet Scoter and Long-tailed Duck. It is a convenient introduction to Finnish birdwatching for visitors arriving in Helsinki who want to see boreal waterbirds without immediately travelling north.

Finland's birding calendar runs from the snow-melt cranes of March to the autumn raptor flights of October, with the boreal owl season in between. The country rewards patience and early mornings in equal measure.

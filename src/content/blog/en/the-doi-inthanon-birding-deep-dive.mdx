At 2,565 metres, Doi Inthanon is the highest point in Thailand, and it carries the country's birdlife into climatic territory found nowhere else within its borders. Drive up from the hot, dusty plains around Chiang Mai before dawn and you climb through bands of vegetation that compress an entire latitudinal gradient into a single morning. By the time you reach the summit boardwalk, the air is cold enough to fog your binoculars, sphagnum moss drips from gnarled rhododendrons, and the birds calling from the canopy belong to lineages that radiated across the eastern Himalaya. For many visiting birders, this mountain inside Doi Inthanon National Park is the single most productive day of a Thai trip.

## A mountain that compresses the Himalaya

What makes Doi Inthanon extraordinary is altitude. The peak rises far higher than anything else in the country, and that elevation creates a cool, humid summit forest that functions as an ecological island. Species with Himalayan and Sino-Himalayan affinities — birds you would otherwise have to seek in Bhutan, Nepal, or Yunnan — find their southernmost outpost here. The Green-tailed Sunbird, Ashy-throated Warbler, Yellow-bellied Fantail, and Dark-sided Thrush all hold on in this cool refuge, isolated from their main ranges by hundreds of kilometres of warm lowland. As the global climate warms, this summit habitat is precisely the kind of cool island that conservation biologists watch with concern, since species pushed upward have nowhere left to go.

## The summit bog and its boardwalk

The headline site is the Ang Ka Nature Trail, a short raised boardwalk that loops through dripping sphagnum bog and stunted, moss-laden forest just below the summit. This is the only easily accessible cloud forest of its type in Thailand. Walk it slowly in the first light and you can pick up the Green-tailed Sunbird flashing between flowers, the secretive Pygmy Cupwing skulking in the moss, and, with patience, the Dark-sided Thrush probing the leaf litter beside the planks. The Ashy-throated and Buff-barred Warblers work the canopy, while Snowy-browed Flycatcher and Rufous-winged Fulvetta forage in the understorey. Mornings are cold and often fogged; a fleece and patience are both essential.

## Working down the elevation bands

Below the summit, the road descends through a sequence of distinct birding zones, and the smart strategy is to bird your way downhill as the day warms. Around the kilometre-31 area and the twin chedis built for the king and queen, flowering gardens draw sunbirds, minivets, and the gaudy Mrs. Gould's Sunbird. Lower still, the evergreen forest near the kilometre-13 checkpoint holds laughingthrushes, the diminutive forktails along the streams, and mixed feeding flocks that can keep you occupied for hours. By the time you reach the warmer foothills, the birdlife shifts toward lowland forest species, drongos, barbets, and bulbuls, completing a remarkable cross-section of habitats in a single descent.

## The Mae Klang and the lower trails

Water shapes much of the lower mountain. The Mae Klang stream and the waterfalls along the lower road — Wachirathan and Mae Ya among them — provide habitat for the Slaty-backed and White-crowned Forktails, Plumbeous Water Redstart, and the Brown Dipper bobbing on midstream boulders. The dedicated Bird Watching Nature Trail near the lower forest, together with the หอชมนก viewing tower, gives birders a fixed vantage over the canopy and forest edge. These lower trails are warmer and more comfortable than the summit, and they reward slow, repeated walks rather than a single hurried pass.

## The Sirithan ponds and the famous hides

In recent years the network of small forest pools and feeding stations on the mountain has become legendary among photographers and listers alike. The pools known locally as บ่อลุงสิน 1 and บ่อลุงสิน 2, along with บ่อนก and บ่อน้ายาว, are managed water and feeding sites where shy ground-dwelling birds come to drink and bathe. From a quiet hide you may watch a White-tailed Robin, a Eurasian Woodcock, or a Lesser Shortwing materialise from the gloom — species that are maddeningly difficult to see in the open forest. Etiquette matters at these sites: arrive early, stay silent, and respect the local guardians who maintain the pools and keep the water topped up.

## Building a target list

Birders come to Doi Inthanon with a wishlist, and the mountain delivers a remarkable concentration of sought-after species in a small area. The headline targets are the highland endemics and near-endemics: the Green-tailed Sunbird, Ashy-throated Warbler, and Chestnut-tailed Minla near the summit; the Eyebrowed Wren-Babbler, Pygmy Cupwing, and Rufous-throated Partridge in the dense understorey; and the Dark-sided Thrush and White-browed Shortwing at the pools. Many visitors also hope for the wintering thrushes and accentors that drop onto the bog in cold spells. A focused birder working the summit boardwalk, the mid-elevation gardens, and the lower hides over two full days can realistically connect with the great majority of these specialties, which is why the mountain so often anchors a northern Thai itinerary.

## Seasons, weather, and timing

The best birding window runs from November through February, the cool dry season, when resident highland specialties are joined by wintering thrushes, warblers, and flycatchers from farther north. December and January bring the coldest summit mornings, sometimes with frost on the bog — a genuine novelty in tropical Thailand. The hot season from March drives haze and smoke from agricultural burning across northern Thailand, reducing visibility and comfort, while the southwest monsoon from June soaks the forest. Whatever the month, start before dawn: the summit is best in the first hour of light, and the lower roads grow quiet by mid-morning.

## Practicalities and conservation

Doi Inthanon National Park charges an entrance fee and is reached by a sealed road from Chiang Mai, roughly two hours away, so most birders base themselves in nearby Chom Thong or in the park's own bungalows and campground. A local guide is invaluable for finding the skulkers and for navigating the hide network with the right etiquette. The mountain's Karen and Hmong communities farm its lower slopes, and visitor income supports both the park and these villages. Treading lightly — staying on trails, keeping noise down at the pools, and not baiting birds beyond what the site managers permit — keeps this fragile summit island viable for the next generation of birders.

Hungary is a landlocked country whose bird diversity belies its modest size. The Pannonian Steppe — the westernmost extension of the Eurasian steppe — gives Hungary the puszta grasslands of Hortobágy and Kiskunság, which hold one of Europe's most important Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable) populations and the largest Crane (Grus grus) autumn roost in Central Europe. The wetland systems of the Tisza and Danube rivers support breeding terns, herons, and waders. The Aggtelek karst system provides cave-roosting and cliff-nesting birds.

## 1. Hortobágy National Park, Hajdú-Bihar

Hortobágy is Hungary's oldest national park and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, protecting the most intact section of puszta steppe in Central Europe. Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable) breeds on the dry steppe with a population of several hundred birds. In October, up to 100,000 Common Cranes stage on the Hortobágy fish ponds before crossing the Carpathians south. Saker Falcon (Falco cherrug, Endangered), Lesser White-fronted Goose (Anser erythropus, Vulnerable), and Red-footed Falcon (Falco vespertinus) are regular in season.

## 2. Kiskunság National Park, Pest and Bács-Kiskun

Kiskunság covers the sandy grassland, salt steppe, and shallow saline lakes of the Danube-Tisza interfluve. Great Bustard breeds; the salt lakes hold Avocet, Black-winged Stilt, and Red-necked Phalarope (Phalaropus lobatus) on passage. The Fülöpháza sand dunes provide dry grassland specialist breeding birds including European Roller (Coracias garrulus) and Lesser Kestrel (Falco naumanni, Vulnerable).

## 3. Aggtelek National Park, Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén

The Aggtelek karst hills near the Slovakian border hold deciduous forest birds of the eastern Central European community. Ural Owl (Strix uralensis), Lesser Spotted Eagle (Clanga pomarina), Black Stork (Ciconia nigra), and Middle Spotted Woodpecker (Leiopicus medius) breed in the hornbeam and oak forests. The cave system supports a large colony of Schreiber's Bent-wing Bat, but the relevant bird interest is the forest birds of the limestone hills.

## 4. Tisza Valley and Tiszaalpár

The middle Tisza between Szolnok and Szeged holds the most important Sand Martin (Riparia riparia) colony complex in Hungary in the riverbank clay cliffs, alongside White-tailed Eagle (Haliaeetus albicilla), Common Tern (Sterna hirundo), and Little Tern (Sternula albifrons) breeding on the river islands.

## 5. Biharugra Fish Ponds

The Biharugra fish pond complex on the Romanian border provides managed shallow water habitats for breeding and staging waterbirds. Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened), Squacco Heron (Ardeola ralloides), Little Egret, and Purple Heron breed; Spoonbill (Platalea leucorodia) and White Pelican are regular migrants. The Körös Valley Regional Landscape Protection Area encompasses this site.

## 6. Velencei-tó, Fejér

Lake Velence, southwest of Budapest, holds the most accessible combination of reed bed and open water birding near the capital. Little Bittern (Ixobrychus minutus), Great Reed Warbler, and Marsh Harrier breed; Common Terns nest on the lake's tern raft. The lake is accessible by suburban train from Budapest and is one of Hungary's best day-trip birding sites.

## 7. Hanság, Győr-Moson-Sopron

The Hanság, near the Austrian border, is a restored peatland complex that holds Common Crane roosting in October and November on the reclaimed pasture alongside Hortobágy. Breeding Curlew (Numenius arquata), Lapwing, and Black-tailed Godwit occur in the wet grasslands. The area's management by the Hungarian National Park system has produced gradual recovery of wet grassland bird populations.

## 8. Soproni-hegység, Győr-Moson-Sopron

The Sopron hills near the Austrian border hold mountain forest birds in the westernmost Hungarian upland. Black Grouse (Lyrurus tetrix) is present in small numbers; Tengmalm's Owl (Aegolius funereus) breeds in old woodpecker holes.

## 9. Ipoly Valley, Nógrád

The Ipoly (Ipel) River valley on the Slovakian border holds Kingfisher (Alcedo atthis), White-throated Dipper (Cinclus cinclus), and Common Sandpiper (Actitis hypoleucos) along the river. The adjacent hillside forests hold Collared Flycatcher (Ficedula albicollis) and Barred Warbler (Curruca nisoria) in suitable scrub patches.

## 10. Fertő-Hanság National Park

Fertő-tó (Neusiedler See on the Austrian side) holds one of the largest reed beds in Central Europe and a breeding waterbird community that includes Great White Egret (Ardea alba), Night Heron, Ferruginous Duck, and Marsh Harrier. The Hungarian side provides extensive cycle-path access around the lake's reed margins.

## Planning a Hungary birding trip

Hungary's birding calendar is dominated by two spectacles that define the country's ornithological reputation: the autumn crane roost and the Great Bustard breeding season. October is the month when Common Crane numbers at Hortobágy and Hanság peak, with tens of thousands of birds roosting on the fish ponds and marshes in morning and evening flights. The combination of the crane spectacle with the Hortobágy puszta landscape — flat, open, treeless, under an enormous Central European sky — produces one of the most atmospheric birding experiences in Europe.

The Great Bustard breeding season runs from March through May. Males display in groups (leks) on the open steppe of Hortobágy and Kiskunság, erecting the white beard feathers and tail in a striking balloon display visible from considerable distance. The Hortobágy National Park visitor center organizes guided Bustard viewings in the breeding season that place observers at appropriate distances while maximizing viewing quality.

Summer (June to July) is productive for wetland species: the Tisza Valley tern colonies are at full activity, and the reed-bed species of Kiskunság — Little Bittern (Ixobrychus minutus), Purple Heron, Whiskered Tern — are feeding young. Red-footed Falcon (Falco vespertinus) congregates in large numbers on passage in May and August, using transmission lines and roadside trees for perching in agricultural areas.

Budapest is a useful base with excellent rail connections to Hortobágy (Debrecen, 2.5 hours), Kiskunság (Kecskemét, 1.5 hours), and Velence Lake (Székesfehérvár, 45 minutes). Car hire extends access to the Hanság and Aggtelek. The BirdsHungary tour company and Hungarian Ornithological and Nature Conservation Society (MME) maintain well-developed visitor infrastructure at the key reserves.

## Hungary's birding calendar

Hungary's birding is distinctly seasonal, with the most spectacular events concentrated in spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November). Understanding the seasonal timing maximizes the value of any visit to the key sites.

Spring migration on the Hungarian puszta begins in late February with the arrival of the first Common Cranes following the Danube Valley and the Carpathian Basin south-to-north route. By late March, the steppe birds are establishing territories: Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable) males displaying on the Kiskunság puszta, and Eastern Imperial Eagle (Aquila heliaca, Vulnerable) pairs visible from the Hortobágy reed margins. The Ramsari flamingo ponds near Kiskunhalas hold the largest regular flamingo aggregation in Central Europe.

Budapest serves as an ideal base. Budapest Liszt Ferenc Airport receives direct flights from most European capitals. Car rental from Budapest allows day-trip access to all major sites: the Hortobágy National Park is two and a half hours east; the Kiskunság National Park puszta is one hour south; the Danube bend and the Ipoly valley (for Collared Flycatcher) are under one hour north.

The Hortobágy Madárgyűrűző Állomás (Bird Ringing Station) and the Tisza Lake complex organize seasonal birdwatching events and maintain visitor infrastructure at the most productive spots.

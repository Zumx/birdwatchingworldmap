A great many birds cannot build an open nest of twigs and grass; they need a hole. Some, the primary cavity nesters, excavate their own — woodpeckers chiselling fresh chambers each spring. Far more are secondary cavity nesters that cannot make holes and must find them ready-made: tits, nuthatches, flycatchers, starlings, swallows, owls and ducks among them. In a tidy modern landscape of young plantations, pruned street trees and felled standing deadwood, natural holes have become scarce, and that scarcity limits how many of these birds can breed. A well-made nest box is, quite literally, replacement habitat — and few interventions a gardener can make do more good for the species concerned.

## Know the species before the box

There is no universal nest box. The dimension that matters most is the entrance hole, because it admits the target species while excluding larger competitors and predators. A 25 mm hole suits the blue tit and other small tits; 28 mm admits the great tit; 32 mm serves house sparrows and nuthatches; and 45 mm or more is needed for starlings. Open-fronted boxes with a low front panel suit robins, wrens and spotted flycatchers, which prefer a partly concealed ledge to an enclosed chamber. Larger species need correspondingly larger structures: a tawny owl wants a deep box or an open chimney design, a kestrel an open-fronted tray, and a stock dove or jackdaw a chamber of generous proportions. Match the box to the bird you actually have, not the one you wish you had.

## Materials and construction

Untreated, rough-sawn timber at least 15 mm thick is the standard and the best choice. Thick wood insulates against both summer heat and spring cold, and a rough interior surface gives chicks the grip they need to climb to the entrance when they fledge. Avoid treated timber, plywood that may delaminate, and metal or thin plastic, which overheat dangerously in sun. The roof should overhang the entrance to keep rain out, and should be hinged or removable for cleaning. Drill two or three small drainage holes in the floor and ventilation gaps near the top. Crucially, do not fit a perch below the entrance: birds do not need one, and it only gives predators and competitors a foothold. Fix the box together with galvanised screws rather than nails, which work loose as the wood weathers.

## Where to put the box

Siting determines success more than any other factor. Most small-bird boxes should face somewhere between north and east, away from the strongest midday sun and the prevailing wet wind, which in much of Europe and North America means avoiding a south-west aspect. Tilt the box very slightly forward so rain runs off the roof rather than into the hole. Mount it at a sensible height — roughly two to four metres for tits, higher for owls — and keep the flight path to the entrance clear of dense foliage so the adults have an unobstructed approach. Spread boxes out: most small songbirds are territorial and will not tolerate near neighbours of their own kind, so a single garden rarely supports more than one or two boxes of the same type. House martin and swift boxes, by contrast, are colonial and can be grouped under the eaves.

## Timing and the breeding cycle

Put boxes up in autumn or winter, well before the breeding season. Birds prospect for sites through the cold months and may roost in a box overnight, and an early box has time to weather and lose its conspicuous newness. In temperate Europe most tits lay in April, timing their single large brood to coincide with the spring flush of caterpillars; a box installed in March may still be found, but one in place since the previous autumn stands a far better chance. Once a pair is in residence, leave them entirely alone. Repeated checks cause desertion, and in many countries disturbing an active nest is illegal. Resist the temptation to peer in; the proof of success is the steady traffic of adults carrying food.

## Predators and competitors

A box is only safe if predators cannot reach the brood. Cats, squirrels, woodpeckers and small mustelids will all take eggs or chicks through an enlarged or accessible hole. A metal hole plate fitted around the entrance prevents squirrels and woodpeckers from gnawing it wider. Keeping the box clear of branches that give climbing predators a launch point, and mounting it on a smooth pole with a baffle where cats are a problem, both help. Inside the box, the depth from hole to floor should be enough that a cat cannot reach down to the nest — at least 12 to 13 centimetres for the smaller species. Wasps and bumblebees sometimes take over a box; if they do, leave them be for the season, as both are valuable and will not return the following year.

## Cleaning and maintenance

Clean each box once a year, after the breeding season has finished and the young have fledged — in most of the temperate zone that means autumn or early winter. Remove the old nest material, which harbours fleas, mites and other parasites that would otherwise plague the next brood. Scrub the interior with boiling water, never with insecticides or detergents, and let the box dry before refitting the roof. A pinch of clean wood shavings or nothing at all can be left in the base; the birds will bring their own lining. Check the structure at the same time, tightening screws and replacing any wood that has rotted. A box that is maintained will serve generation after generation.

## Beyond the box: dead wood and natural cavities

Nest boxes are a stopgap, not a substitute for the real thing. The deeper solution is to let dead and decaying wood remain wherever it is safe to do so. A standing dead tree, a snag, a rotting limb or even a pollarded veteran provides not only nest holes but the insect food that fills them. Woodpeckers excavate fresh chambers in soft, decaying timber, and the holes they abandon become next year's homes for tits, owls and bats. Where you have room, a single retained dead tree may in time out-perform a dozen boxes. Boxes buy time for the birds; standing deadwood gives them back their habitat.

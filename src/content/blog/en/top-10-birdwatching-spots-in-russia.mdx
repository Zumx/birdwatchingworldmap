Russia spans eleven time zones and covers one-eighth of the world's land surface, encompassing habitats from the Black Sea coast and Central Asian steppe in the south to the Arctic tundra of Novaya Zemlya and the subarctic Pacific coast of the Russian Far East. Over 700 bird species have been recorded within Russian borders. The scale of the country means that several of its key birding sites are genuinely remote — accessible only by helicopter, boat, or multi-day drive — but the range of accessible sites near major population centres such as Moscow, St. Petersburg, and Vladivostok ensures that visiting birders can sample Russian avifauna without expedition-level logistics.

Russia holds the world's only breeding populations of several globally significant birds, including the Siberian Crane in the Yakutian tundra and Spoon-billed Sandpiper on the Chukotka coast. The Russian taiga is the world's largest forest biome and the breeding ground for hundreds of millions of boreal birds that winter across Europe, Asia, and Africa. Understanding Russian ornithology is fundamental to understanding the Palearctic flyway system.

## 1. Volga Delta, Astrakhan Oblast

The Volga Delta where Russia's longest river meets the Caspian Sea is one of Europe's largest and most productive wetlands, covering over 800,000 hectares. It is a Critical site for breeding Dalmatian Pelican — the largest global breeding colony of this species is here — alongside Great White Pelican, Pygmy Cormorant, and breeding Spoonbill. Grey Heron, Great Egret, and Night Heron nest in enormous mixed colonies on the delta islands. The lotus beds of the southern delta are spectacular during July and August and support a rich community of marsh birds. White-tailed Eagle is common. The Astrakhan Nature Reserve manages the most sensitive areas, and boat trips into the delta from Astrakhan city are well established.

## 2. Lake Baikal, Irkutsk and Buryat Regions

Lake Baikal, the world's deepest freshwater lake, holds the endemic Baikal Teal on its shores during migration — though this species winters in East Asia — and the summer period sees the lake shore and surrounding taiga fill with Siberian breeding species. Barguzin Valley on the northeast shore holds Pallas's Fish Eagle, one of Asia's largest eagles. Common Crane and Whooper Swan use the lake shore and delta of the Barguzin River. The shoreline forest holds Siberian Jay, Siberian Tit, and Three-toed Woodpecker. The Sikhote-Alin foothills species transition into Siberian taiga species here. In winter, the ice of Baikal is occasionally used by roosting eagles.

## 3. Primorsky Krai (Vladivostok Region), Russian Far East

The Primorsky region of the Russian Far East, bordering China and North Korea, holds one of the most distinctive bird communities in Russia — a mixture of Manchurian, Korean, and Siberian elements found together nowhere else in the Palearctic. Japanese Green Woodpecker, Pale Thrush, and Chinese Grosbeak are found in the mixed broadleaf forest. Scaly-sided Merganser breeds along the clear mountain rivers of the Ussuri taiga. Blakiston's Fish Owl — the world's largest owl — inhabits the large-tree riverside forest of the region. The spectacle of thousands of Shorebirds on the Yellow Sea coast during migration — including globally threatened Spoon-billed Sandpiper — is unmatched anywhere outside East Asia.

## 4. Yamal Peninsula, Yamalo-Nenets Autonomous Okrug

The Yamal Peninsula projecting into the Kara Sea is one of the most productive Arctic tundra birding areas in Russia. The tundra holds breeding Red-necked Stint, Little Stint, Dunlin, and numerous other waders at very high density. Snowy Owl hunts over the open tundra during lemming peak years. Rough-legged Buzzard and Peregrine nest on the eroding river banks. The Ob River estuary, which flanks the southern Yamal, supports enormous concentrations of migrating geese in spring — hundreds of thousands of Barnacle Goose, White-fronted Goose, and Bean Goose pass through in May. Access is by helicopter from Salekhard or by river vessel.

## 5. Kamchatka Peninsula

Kamchatka is one of the world's great natural wildernesses — a volcanic peninsula projecting south from the Russian Far East with no road connection to the rest of Russia. The combination of volcanoes, rivers packed with salmon, and untouched coniferous forest creates ideal conditions for the extraordinary density of Steller's Sea Eagle that inhabits the coast and river valleys. Kamchatka's seabird colonies on the Pacific cliffs include Tufted Puffin, Horned Puffin, Rhinoceros Auklet, and Crested Auklet in enormous numbers. Whiskered Auklet breeds on the Commander Islands offshore. The salmon rivers attract Steller's Sea Eagle and White-tailed Eagle that gather in concentrations unmatched elsewhere.

## 6. Taimyr Peninsula, Krasnoyarsk Krai

The Taimyr Peninsula, the northernmost part of mainland Russia, is the world's most important breeding area for Bar-tailed Godwit, Knot, Dunlin, and other high-Arctic waders. It is also the principal breeding area for the Taimyr population of Barnacle Goose and holds significant numbers of breeding Snow Goose. Siberian Crane once bred here but the western Siberia population is considered functionally extinct. The Taimyr Biosphere Reserve protects the core of the peninsula. Access is extremely challenging — typically charter flights from Norilsk — and the site is primarily visited by specialist ornithological expeditions rather than general birding tourists.

## 7. Oka Nature Reserve, Ryazan Oblast

The Oka Nature Reserve south of Moscow is the headquarters of Russia's Siberian Crane captive breeding programme and is one of the most accessible sites for seeing the results of crane conservation work. Captive Siberian Cranes breed here, and the reserve's floodplain meadows and ox-bow lakes hold a wide variety of typical central Russian waterbirds. Corncrake and Spotted Crake call from the floodplain meadows in summer — this region of central Russia is one of the last European strongholds of these declining grassland birds. White-tailed Eagle nests along the river. The reserve's educational facilities make it a productive destination for birders based in Moscow.

## 8. Caucasus Nature Reserve, Krasnodar Krai

The Greater Caucasus range reaches its highest points along the border between Russia and Georgia, and the Russian side of the mountain chain holds an endemic bird community including Caucasian Snowcock — one of the most sought-after snowcocks in the Palearctic, inhabiting boulder fields above 2,500 metres. Caucasian Grouse performs its extraordinary lek on snowfields in May. Güldenstädt's Redstart inhabits rocky montane habitat. The alpine zone holds Snowfinch and Alpine Accentor. The forests below the treeline contain several Caucasian endemics including Caucasian Tit and Persian Nuthatch. The resort area around Sochi provides accommodation with straightforward access to the mountain habitats.

## 9. Kenozersky National Park, Arkhangelsk Oblast

Kenozersky National Park in northern Russia protects a traditional landscape of old-growth boreal forest, lakes, and traditional villages that has been largely undisturbed by industrial development. The forest is among the best sites in European Russia for boreal species including Siberian Jay, Siberian Tit, and Three-toed Woodpecker. Ural Owl breeds at good density. Hazel Grouse and Black Grouse are resident. The park's lakes support breeding Red-necked Grebe, Osprey, and White-tailed Eagle. The traditional log villages within the park add cultural interest to what is genuinely productive birding in a beautiful northern landscape. The park is reached from Arkhangelsk.

## 10. Nurgush Nature Reserve and Kirov Oblast Floodplains

The Vyatka River floodplain in Kirov Oblast holds one of the highest concentrations of Corncrake in Russia — the region supports a significant proportion of the European population of this globally declining grassland bird. The flooded meadows and oxbow lakes along the Vyatka also support breeding Common Crane and White Stork. White-tailed Eagle is resident along the larger water bodies. In spring, the flooding of the meadows attracts enormous numbers of migratory ducks and waders. The Russian Corncrake research programme has conducted long-term monitoring of the Kirov population, contributing to global understanding of Corncrake ecology and decline.

---

Russia's birding geography rewards planning around specific regions rather than attempting to cover the whole country in a single visit. The accessible European Russia sites around Moscow and St. Petersburg are most practical for short trips, while the Russian Far East and the Siberian wilderness require dedicated expeditions.

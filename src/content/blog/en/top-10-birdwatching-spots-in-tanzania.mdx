Tanzania is famous for lions on the Serengeti plains and the great wildebeest migration, but its bird list of more than 1,100 species tells a richer story. The country sits at a crossroads of African avifauna, where the lowland forests of the Congo basin reach their eastern limit, the dry Somali-Masai savanna pushes south, and the ancient Eastern Arc Mountains harbour endemics found nowhere else on earth. These crystalline massifs, sometimes called the Galapagos of Africa, have been forested for over 30 million years, allowing species to evolve in isolation on each isolated block. Add the alkaline lakes of the Rift Valley, where flamingos gather in their hundreds of thousands, and Tanzania becomes one of the continent's three or four essential birding destinations.

The practical challenge is the sheer size of the country and the distance between its biological islands. A serious Tanzania trip combines lowland coast, montane forest and open savanna, often with internal flights to stitch the regions together. What follows is a selection of ten sites that, taken together, deliver endemics, megafauna and spectacle in equal measure.

## Usambara Mountains

The East and West Usambaras, part of the Eastern Arc, are the classic destination for Tanzanian forest endemics. Amani Nature Reserve in the East Usambaras protects submontane forest where the Usambara eagle-owl, Usambara weaver, Amani sunbird and the bizarre long-billed tailorbird can be found. The cool, misty forest around Magamba and the village of Lushoto in the West Usambaras holds the Usambara akalat and Usambara double-collared sunbird. Birding here is slow, forest work, but the reward is a suite of species with tiny world ranges.

## Lake Manyara National Park

Set beneath the towering escarpment of the Rift Valley wall, Lake Manyara packs an extraordinary range of habitats into a narrow strip. The groundwater forest at the entrance holds silvery-cheeked hornbill and crowned eagle, the open floodplain supports vast flocks of lesser and greater flamingo on the soda lake, and the acacia woodland is rich in raptors and rollers. More than 400 species have been recorded here, and the park is an easy and rewarding introduction to Rift Valley birding on the northern safari circuit.

## Ngorongoro Crater

The Ngorongoro Crater, a collapsed volcanic caldera nearly 20 kilometres across, combines its celebrated big game with excellent open-country and wetland birding. The soda lake of Lake Magadi within the crater attracts flamingos, and the grasslands hold kori bustard, secretarybird, grey crowned crane and Rüppell's vulture. The crater rim forest adds montane species, while the surrounding highlands of the Ngorongoro Conservation Area support Schalow's turaco and the localised mountain buzzard.

## Serengeti National Park

The Serengeti is much more than its famous predators. The endless short-grass plains support secretarybird, kori bustard, ostrich and a remarkable density of larks and pipits, including the localised Karamoja apalis in the whistling-thorn acacia near Seronera. Kopjes hold cliff chats and rock-loving cisticolas, and the seasonal wetlands draw storks, cranes and crowned plovers. The grey-breasted spurfowl, a near-endemic, is best looked for here.

## Tarangire National Park

Tarangire, with its iconic baobabs and the meandering Tarangire River, is one of the best dry-country birding parks in the north. The yellow-collared lovebird and the ashy starling are Tanzanian near-endemics best seen here, alongside northern white-crowned shrike, red-and-yellow barbet, and a wealth of hornbills. The swamps in the south of the park hold black heron and a variety of waders, and the open woodland is excellent for raptors.

## Udzungwa Mountains National Park

The Udzungwas, the largest and most intact block of the Eastern Arc, are reached on foot via forest trails climbing from the lowlands. This is the home of the rufous-winged sunbird, discovered as recently as 1981, and the Udzungwa forest partridge, found in 1991 and unlike any other African galliform. Iringa akalat, dappled mountain robin and the white-chested alethe inhabit the rich forest. Birding here is demanding but yields some of Africa's rarest species.

## Mikumi National Park

Often overlooked, Mikumi sits beside the main road from Dar es Salaam and offers accessible savanna birding. The Mkata floodplain supports bateleur, lilac-breasted roller, and seasonal concentrations of waterbirds, while the brachystegia woodland holds species of the miombo belt such as miombo wren-warbler and pale-billed hornbill. Its proximity to the coast makes it an easy first or last stop.

## Selous and Nyerere National Park

The vast Nyerere National Park, carved from the former Selous Game Reserve, is dominated by the Rufiji River and its network of lakes and channels. Boat safaris here put you among African skimmer, white-fronted bee-eater colonies on the riverbanks, Pel's fishing-owl in the riverine forest, and palm-nut vulture. The combination of water, woodland and wilderness makes this one of the finest all-round birding areas in the south.

## Lake Natron

Lake Natron, a searing alkaline lake at the foot of the active volcano Ol Doinyo Lengai, is the single most important breeding site in the world for the lesser flamingo. In good years over a million pairs nest on the caustic mudflats, an inaccessible spectacle that draws birders to the lake's southern shores. The surrounding desert and the Engare Sero area also hold dry-country specials and offer dramatic Rift Valley scenery.

## Pemba and the Coastal Forests

Pemba Island, off the Zanzibar archipelago, holds four endemics found nowhere else: the Pemba scops-owl, Pemba green pigeon, Pemba sunbird and Pemba white-eye. On the mainland, the coastal forests around Pugu Hills and the Arabuko-style thickets harbour east coast specials such as the Sokoke pipit at the northern edge of its range. These fragments are a final, distinct facet of Tanzanian birding.

## Explore on the map

A complete Tanzania itinerary links the Eastern Arc forests, the Rift Valley lakes and the great savanna parks, often with internal flights to cover the distances. Plan around the dry season from June to October for savanna and the rains from November for breeding plumage and migrants.

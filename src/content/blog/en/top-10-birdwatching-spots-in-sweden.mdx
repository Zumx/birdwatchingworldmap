Sweden stretches from the temperate farmland and beech forests of Skåne in the south to the subarctic tundra of Lapland in the far north — a latitudinal range that encompasses an extraordinary variety of bird habitats. The country has over 500 recorded species, and its network of protected areas, including large nature reserves managed by the Swedish Environmental Protection Agency and the county administrative boards, provides excellent public access to productive birding sites. The birding year in Sweden has two primary peaks: the spring migration and breeding season from March through June, and the autumn raptor and passerine migration that concentrates on southern coastal headlands from August through October.

Sweden's most famous birding spectacle is the Common Crane gathering at Lake Hornborga in Västergötland, where tens of thousands of cranes stage during March and April. The great grey owls of central Sweden, the mountain birds of Lapland, and the coastal seabird colonies of Bohuslän and the Baltic archipelagos contribute to a national birding tradition that is among the strongest in Europe.

## 1. Lake Hornborga (Hornborgasjön), Västergötland

Hornborga is Sweden's most celebrated birding spectacle and one of the most impressive wildlife events in Europe. During March and April, up to 20,000 Common Cranes gather at the lake and surrounding fields during their northward spring migration. The cranes arrive in waves from wintering grounds in Spain, staging at Hornborga before continuing to Scandinavian and Russian breeding grounds. The daily flights of cranes from roost to feeding areas — at dawn and dusk — are extraordinary in scale and sound. White-tailed Eagle is common around the lake year-round, with up to ten individuals visible simultaneously during peak migration. The viewing facilities are extensive, with car parks, hides, and observation towers.

## 2. Falsterbo Peninsula, Skåne

Falsterbo at the southwestern tip of Sweden is the most important raptor migration watch point in Scandinavia and one of the premier in Europe. The peninsula acts as a geographic funnel, concentrating migrating raptors that are reluctant to cross the Øresund strait. In September and October, Honey Buzzard, Osprey, Marsh Harrier, Hen Harrier, and thousands of Common Buzzards pass south in daily flights that are counted systematically by the Falsterbo Ringing Station. The passerine migration is equally impressive — the coastal scrub and dune woodland can hold enormous numbers of warblers, thrushes, and flycatchers in autumn. In winter, Falsterbo's beaches hold Snow Bunting, Shore Lark, and Sanderling.

## 3. Kvismaren Nature Reserve, Örebro County

Kvismaren is a restored lake and reedbed complex in south-central Sweden and is the country's most important site for breeding wetland birds. Bittern — a species whose booming call carries far across the reedbed — breeds here in significant numbers. Water Rail is common in the reedbeds. Marsh Harrier quarters the reserve's open areas. In spring and autumn, the open water attracts excellent wader diversity including Wood Sandpiper, Spotted Redshank, and Ruff. Sedge Warbler, Reed Warbler, and Great Reed Warbler all breed in the reedbed edge. White-tailed Eagle is seen year-round. The reserve visitor centre provides information on current breeding status and migration conditions.

## 4. Getterön Nature Reserve, Halland

Getterön near Varberg on the west coast is one of southern Sweden's finest wetland sites, a shallow bay and managed lagoon that produces excellent shorebird and waterbird diversity. The reserve is well known for turning up rare waders during migration, and the tidal conditions of the nearby Kattegat influence the species composition. In autumn, Curlew Sandpiper, Little Stint, and Broad-billed Sandpiper are among the regular migrants. The reserve holds breeding Avocet, Black-tailed Godwit, and Dunlin. Winter brings Steller's Eider — a sought-after sea duck from the Russian Arctic — offshore with reasonable regularity. Several public hides provide views over the main lagoon.

## 5. Öland and the Ottenby Bird Observatory

Öland is the second largest island in Sweden and holds the southern end of its famous ridge — the Alvar — a limestone pavement habitat with open, species-rich grassland unique in Sweden. Öland's Alvar is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and holds breeding Eurasian Dotterel, Stone Curlew, and Montagu's Harrier. The Ottenby Bird Observatory at the island's southern tip is one of Sweden's premier migration monitoring stations. During autumn, the point and the adjacent woodland produce large falls of migrants. Bluethroat, Barred Warbler, and various East European vagrants are annual. In winter, Short-eared Owl hunts over the Alvar and Rough-legged Buzzard quarters the open limestone grassland.

## 6. Tyresta National Park, Stockholm County

Tyresta is one of the largest areas of old-growth boreal forest in the Stockholm metropolitan region and provides accessible boreal forest birding within 30 minutes of the city centre. The ancient forest holds breeding Ural Owl, Black Woodpecker, and White-backed Woodpecker — species requiring old-growth forest with large dead standing trees. Hazel Grouse inhabits the younger birch forest. The park's lakes hold breeding Red-throated Diver and Common Goldeneye. Tengmalm's Owl occupies nest boxes installed through the forest. The proximity to Stockholm and the quality of the boreal forest birding make Tyresta exceptional among urban-edge nature reserves in Europe.

## 7. Padjelanta and Sarek National Parks, Lapland

The adjacent wilderness national parks of Padjelanta and Sarek in Swedish Lapland represent some of the most remote and undisturbed mountain country in Western Europe. The fell and mountain terrain holds breeding Long-tailed Skua, Purple Sandpiper, and Temminck's Stint. Shore Lark and Lapland Bunting breed on the fell. Rough-legged Buzzard and Golden Eagle are the characteristic large raptors of the mountain. The boreal forest and subalpine scrub below the fell hold Siberian Tit and Siberian Jay. Access is by foot from the nearest roads, typically via the Padjelanta Trail, requiring at least a week. The remote character means encounters with wildlife are unmediated by tourist infrastructure.

## 8. Lake Tåkern, Östergötland

Tåkern is one of Sweden's largest and most productive inland lakes, and its extensive reedbeds are the most important breeding site in Sweden for Great Bittern. The number of booming males counted each spring at Tåkern represents a significant proportion of the national population. Marsh Harrier, Bearded Reedling, and Savi's Warbler breed in the reedbed. In autumn, the open water holds spectacular concentrations of diving ducks — Tufted Duck, Pochard, and Greater Scaup in thousands. White-tailed Eagle breeds in the woodland surrounding the lake. The Tåkern Field Station provides hides and observation points around the lake perimeter and publishes daily sightings records.

## 9. Abisko National Park, Lapland

Abisko National Park, accessible by rail on the Kiruna-Narvik line, is the most easily reached subarctic birding site in Sweden. The park sits in a precipitation shadow created by the Norwegian coastal mountains, producing a drier and warmer microclimate than surrounding Lapland. The Abisko canyon and surrounding birch forest hold breeding Redwing, Bluethroat, and Arctic Warbler. The Abisko fell above the treeline is one of the more accessible areas in Sweden for Long-tailed Skua and Dunlin in their breeding territories. Ptarmigan is resident year-round on the higher fell. The park's accommodation and rail access make it the most practical entry point for Lapland birding.

## 10. Kosterfjord and Bohuslän Archipelago

The Kosterfjord on Sweden's west coast, near the Norwegian border, is the deepest fjord in Swedish waters and supports a rich marine environment. The offshore islands hold breeding Razorbill, Black Guillemot, and Common Guillemot on the outer skerries. Gannet is seen regularly offshore from autumn through spring. Eider is common in the sheltered waters year-round. Sea-watching from the outer islands during southerly storm conditions in autumn produces shearwaters and skuas. The grey seal colonies on the outer skerries are visible from wildlife boat trips. The Kosterhavet Marine National Park encompasses the outer archipelago and can be explored by kayak for close-up island seabird viewing.

Sweden's birding year is structured around distinct seasons — crane spectacles in spring, breeding waders and forest species in summer, raptor migration in autumn, and owls and northern ducks in winter. The country's strong tradition of accessible public nature areas makes independent birding straightforward.

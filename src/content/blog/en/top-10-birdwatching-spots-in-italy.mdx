Italy's long narrow geography — stretching 1,200 kilometres from the Alps to Sicily — spans multiple climate zones and bird communities. The Alpine north holds Central European montane species; the Po Valley and Adriatic coast provide internationally important wetlands; the Mediterranean south and the islands of Sicily and Sardinia hold distinctive Mediterranean endemic and specialist species. Italy is positioned centrally on the Central Mediterranean migration route; the Strait of Messina between Sicily and Calabria is one of the most important raptor and stork migration bottlenecks in Europe.

## 1. Po Delta, Emilia-Romagna and Veneto

The Po Delta Regional Park covers the most important coastal wetland system in Italy. The lagoons, fish ponds, and marsh systems of the Po Delta hold a breeding community of Mediterranean Gull (Ichthyaetus melanocephalus), Slender-billed Gull, Little Tern, Pied Avocet, Black-winged Stilt, and the only regular Greater Flamingo (Phoenicopterus roseus) breeding colony in Italy. Glossy Ibis has expanded here recently. The eBird hotspot network for the Po Delta documents over 250 species.

## 2. Saline di Trapani, Sicily

The Trapani saltpans in western Sicily hold one of the most productive shorebird and flamingo sites in Italy. Greater Flamingo is present year-round in thousands; Slender-billed Gull, Audouin's Gull (Ichthyaetus audouinii), and Sandwich Tern breed on the saltpan islands. The WWF Saline di Trapani reserve provides infrastructure for public access.

## 3. Gargano Peninsula, Puglia

The Gargano karst headland jutting into the Adriatic concentrates migrant raptors in autumn. Honey Buzzard, Short-toed Eagle (Circaetus gallicus), and Eurasian Hobby (Falco subbuteo) are among the regular autumn migrants. The Gargano National Park forest holds Middle Spotted Woodpecker and White-backed Woodpecker in its mature beech stands.

## 4. Orbetello Lagoon, Tuscany

The Orbetello lagoon complex in southern Tuscany is one of Italy's most productive Mediterranean waterbird sites. Breeding Red-crested Pochard (Netta rufina), Shelduck (Tadorna tadorna), and Black-winged Stilt are joined in winter by large numbers of wintering ducks. The WWF Orbetello reserve manages the main access point.

## 5. Gran Sasso and Monti della Laga National Park, Abruzzo-Lazio

Gran Sasso is Italy's second-highest mountain (2,912 metres), holding Alpine Chough (Pyrrhocorax graculus), Snowfinch (Montifringilla nivalis), and Alpine Accentor (Prunella collaris) on the high-altitude plateaux. The mountain forest below holds Lammergeier (Gypaetus barbatus, Near Threatened) — reintroduced to the Apennines since 2010 — alongside the recovering Central Apennine wolf and Marsican Brown Bear.

## 6. Strait of Messina, Calabria-Sicily

The narrow strait between Calabria and Sicily is one of Europe's most important raptor migration bottlenecks. In August and September, Honey Buzzard passes in thousands; the entire Western Palearctic Honey Buzzard population has been estimated to cross here or at Gibraltar. European Roller, Purple Heron, and White Stork are regular alongside the raptors. The Italian League for Bird Protection (LIPU) operates an observation and anti-poaching station at Punta Pezzo.

## 7. Circeo National Park, Lazio

Circeo on the Tyrrhenian coast holds Mediterranean macchia with Dartford Warbler (Curruca undata), Cetti's Warbler (Cettia cetti), and Sardinian Warbler alongside the Circeo Forest's woodpecker community. The coastal lagoon at Fogliano holds Greater Flamingo in winter and breeding waterbirds in the reed fringes.

## 8. Parco Nazionale d'Abruzzo, Lazio e Molise

The Abruzzo National Park holds the Central Apennine community with its recovering Marsican Brown Bear, Italian Wolf, and birdlife including Eurasian Dipper (Cinclus cinclus), Red-billed Chough (Pyrrhocorax pyrrhocorax), and Middle Spotted Woodpecker in the beech forest.

## 9. Sardinia: Molentargius-Saline Nature Park

The Cagliari lagoon and the Molentargius saltpan complex hold the largest Greater Flamingo colony on Sardinia and the only self-sustaining breeding flamingo colony in Italy on the islands. The saltpan edges hold Audouin's Gull, Slender-billed Gull, and Sandwich Tern. The Osservatorio naturalistico polivalente (documented in the map database) is located at the Cagliari coastal wetland.

## 10. Parco Nazionale del Gargano and Varano Lake

The Varano and Lesina coastal lagoons on the Gargano Peninsula hold European Shag, Shelduck, and Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened) in winter alongside a productive migration period in spring and autumn.

## Planning an Italy birding trip

Italy's birding calendar rewards visitors who time their trips around two distinct peak periods. Spring (April to May) is the most productive for breeding birds and northbound migration: the Po Delta pelican colonies are at their noisiest and most active, the saltpans of Sicily and Sardinia hold their peak flamingo numbers, and the Strait of Messina produces its most dramatic raptor stream as Honey Buzzards and Short-toed Eagles concentrate before crossing. The Gargano Peninsula is excellent in April, when migrant passerines pile into the macchia scrub after crossing the Adriatic.

Autumn (August to October) brings southbound migration through the same Messina Strait corridor — August produces the first Honey Buzzard movements, September sees the Steppe Eagle and Levant Sparrowhawk peak, and October closes with the last of the Marsh Harriers and Ospreys. Seawatch points on the Calabrian coast and the Sicilian headlands produce remarkable hour-by-hour counts during northwest wind conditions.

Winter in Italy is productive along the coasts: the Po Delta holds its largest waterbird concentrations from November through February, with Bittern, Ferruginous Duck, and the full suite of wintering ducks and grebes in the freshwater sections, while Greater Flamingo numbers in the southern saltpans reach their annual peak. The Apennine mountains hold their breeding raptors year-round; Bearded Vulture on the Gran Sasso can be seen displaying in January and February.

A practical itinerary covering the highlights: three days in the Po Delta (Comacchio lagoons and Po mouth) in late April; two days in Gargano for raptors and forest birds; two days in Sicily's Trapani saltpans in May or October. Sardinia's Molentargius lagoon is best combined with a visit to the Campidano plain for Short-toed Eagle and Roller in spring. Italian guides organized through LIPU (Lega Italiana Protezione Uccelli) provide local expertise at all major sites.

## Italy's birding seasons and logistics

Italy's diverse geography — from the Alpine arc to the Mediterranean islands — creates distinct birding seasons for each region. The Po Valley and the Venetian lagoon are at their most productive in late March to May and September to November for migrant and wintering waterbirds. The Alpine sites are most accessible from June to August. Sicily and Sardinia are productive for breeding endemic subspecies from April to June.

Raptor migration through the Straits of Messina between Sicily and Calabria is the most spectacular Italian birding event, occurring from late August through October when hundreds of thousands of Honey Buzzard, Black Kite, and marsh harrier species cross from Sicily to the Italian mainland. The observation post at Punta Faro near Messina counts over 500,000 raptors in a single autumn.

Rome, Milan, and Venice provide the main international entry points. All three cities provide rail access to the Po Valley sites (Piacenza, Ferrara, Comacchio) that concentrate waterbirds in the northern Italian lowlands. Cagliari airport serves Sardinia for the breeding Eleonora's Falcon and endemic island birds.

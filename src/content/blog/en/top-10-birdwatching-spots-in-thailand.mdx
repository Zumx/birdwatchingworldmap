Thailand holds nearly 1,000 bird species and a birding infrastructure — national parks, guides, and eBird coverage — that makes it one of the most accessible tropical birding destinations in the world. Its position between the Himalayan foothills and the Malay Peninsula places it at the junction of Himalayan and Sundaland faunas; its coast on the Gulf of Thailand and the Andaman Sea adds marine and coastal species. The birding community organized around the Bird Conservation Society of Thailand (BCST) has developed trail guides and eBird hotspot coverage that simplifies independent birding. The map database includes observation infrastructure in Kaeng Krachan and other major parks.

## 1. Kaeng Krachan National Park, Phetchaburi

Kaeng Krachan is the largest national park in Thailand and consistently produces the highest species lists in the country. The park's position at the southern end of the Western Forest Complex, receiving moisture from both the Gulf of Thailand and the Indian Ocean, produces a species-rich dry evergreen and moist deciduous forest community. Ratchet-tailed Treepie (Temnurus temnurus), Giant Pitta (Hydrornis caeruleus), and Blue Pitta (Hydrornis cyaneus) are among the park targets. The Ban Krang campsite area and the reservoir road are the most productive sections. The Bird Watching Nature Trail (documented in the map database) and the reservoir observation tower (หอชมนก) provide structured access.

## 2. Doi Inthanon National Park, Chiang Mai

Doi Inthanon, Thailand's highest mountain at 2,565 metres, holds the northern Thai highland community: Green-tailed Sunbird (Aethopyga nipalensis), Rufous-capped Babbler (Cyanoderma ruficeps), and Slender-billed Oriole (Oriolus tenuirostris) in the cloud forest zone. Mrs. Gould's Sunbird (Aethopyga gouldiae) and the endemic Mrs. Hume's Pheasant (Syrmaticus humiae, Near Threatened) represent the most sought highland species. The annual Doi Inthanon Bird Fair promotes local birding.

## 3. Khao Yai National Park, Nakhon Ratchasima

Khao Yai, a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Dong Phayayen-Khao Yai Forest Complex, holds the most accessible lowland evergreen forest in central Thailand. Wreathed Hornbill (Rhyticeros undulatus), Great Hornbill (Buceros bicornis, Vulnerable), and Malabar Pied Hornbill (Anthracoceros coronatus) can be seen along the main road through the park. Asian Elephant and Gaur are the mammal highlights.

## 4. Doi Lang and Doi Ang Khang, Chiang Mai

The high-altitude ridges near the Myanmar border hold the northern Thai highland specialties at their most accessible. Spiny Babbler (Turdoides nipalensis, Near Threatened), Chestnut-tailed Minla (Chrysominla strigula), and Gould's Shortwing (Heteroxenicus stellatus) represent the Himalayan foothills community at its Thai outpost.

## 5. Khao Sam Roi Yot National Park, Prachuap Khiri Khan

This coastal karst park holds the most important Watercock (Gallicrex cinerea) and Purple Swamphen (Porphyrio porphyrio) site in the Gulf of Thailand region, and the freshwater marshes host Painted Stork (Mycteria leucocephala), Open-billed Stork (Anastomus oscitans), and large concentrations of wintering ducks. The limestone hills hold the country's best Dusky Crag Martin (Ptyonoprogne concolor) records.

## 6. Phuket and Phang Nga Bay

The Phang Nga Bay mangroves provide the full Malay Peninsula mangrove community. Mangrove Pitta (Pitta megarhyncha), Mangrove Whistler (Pachycephala cinerea), and the endemic Irrawaddy Dolphin (Orcaella brevirostris) share the bay. Brown-winged Kingfisher (Pelargopsis amauroptera, Near Threatened) is accessible here.

## 7. Nam Nao National Park, Phetchabun

Nam Nao's deciduous and dry evergreen forest in the Central Thailand uplands holds an excellent selection of species not easily found elsewhere: Kalij Pheasant (Lophura leucomelanos), Hume's Pheasant (Syrmaticus humiae, Near Threatened), and the full woodland community of the northeastern plateau gradient.

## 8. Baan Pha Hee, Chiang Rai

The highest birding location near Chiang Rai, Baan Pha Hee provides access to montane species including Fire-tailed Myzornis (Myzornis pyrrhoura), Ward's Trogon (Harpactes wardi), and the spectacular Green Cochoa (Cochoa viridis) in the bamboo-forest interface.

## 9. Laem Pak Bia, Phetchaburi

The Laem Pak Bia mudflat is the most important shorebird staging site on the upper Gulf of Thailand coast. Spoon-billed Sandpiper (Calidris pygmaea, Critically Endangered) winters here in small numbers — one of only a handful of confirmed Thai wintering sites for this species. Nordmann's Greenshank (Tringa guttifer, Endangered) and Great Knot (Calidris tenuirostris, Endangered) are regular.

## 10. Doi Chiang Dao Wildlife Sanctuary, Chiang Mai

The imposing limestone massif of Doi Chiang Dao rises to 2,175 metres and holds the most complete set of northern Thai limestone specialties: Hainan Blue Flycatcher (Cyornis hainanus), Limestone Wren-Babbler (Napothera crispifrons), and the endemic Doi Chiang Dao Warbler (Phylloscopus davisonianus), described as a species from this site.

## Planning a Thailand birding trip

Thailand's birding scene is organized around three distinct regions that correspond to the country's main biogeographic zones: the northern highlands (Doi Inthanon, Doi Ang Khang, and the Chiang Mai mountain range), the central lowlands (Kaeng Krachan, Khao Sam Roi Yot), and the southern peninsula (Khao Sok, Trang, and the Malaysian border forests). A comprehensive Thailand trip requires coverage of all three zones.

The northern highlands are best in the cool-dry season from November to February, when the cloud forest birds are most active and the mountain roads are passable. Doi Inthanon above 2,500 metres holds the hill-myna community, wren-babblers, and the full Indochinese montane suite that doesn't appear in the lowlands. The Doi Inthanon summit bogs and the Kew Mae Pan ridge trail are the key habitats for the specialist species. Chiang Mai provides the base for northern highland birding, with easy day-trip access to Doi Inthanon (one hour south) and Doi Ang Khang (four hours north).

Kaeng Krachan National Park, four hours south of Bangkok on the Myanmar border, is the most productive single site in Thailand for lowland species. The Phanoen Thung ridge road produces the full Indomalayan lowland forest community including Giant Pitta (Hydrornis caeruleus, Vulnerable) and Green-legged Partridge (Arborophila chloropus). The park is open year-round; the dry season (November to May) provides the most consistent forest access.

The southern peninsula's most productive birding — the Trang coast, Thale Noi, and the Hala-Bala forest on the Malaysian border — requires a domestic flight from Bangkok to Hat Yai or Trang. Hala-Bala Wildlife Sanctuary contains Malaysian-affinity species not found north of the peninsula: Malaysian Rail-babbler (Eupetes macrocerus), Giant Antpitta equivalent species, and the full Sundaic lowland forest community.

Bangkok provides the easiest entry point. A typical two-week Thailand birding itinerary: three nights at Kaeng Krachan, three nights at Doi Inthanon (via Chiang Mai), two nights at Doi Ang Khang, then a domestic flight south for three nights in the peninsula. This covers the three main zones and around 350 to 400 species.

## Kaeng Krachan practicalities

Kaeng Krachan National Park requires a national park entry fee and accommodation booking either at the park's official guesthouse or at one of the private lodges outside the main gate. The park's Phanoen Thung road — the primary birding route — is open from dawn and provides vehicle-based birding through mature lowland forest. The road is one-way for much of its length, returning via the western side. A guide hired from the park gate significantly improves access to target species; the park guesthouse can arrange guide contacts on arrival. Birding is most productive in the first three hours after dawn before the forest heat peaks.

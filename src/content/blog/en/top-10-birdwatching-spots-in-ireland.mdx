Ireland sits at the extreme western edge of Europe, the first landfall for storms and migrants crossing the North Atlantic. Its position is the single fact that shapes its birding: the granite and sandstone cliffs of the west coast hold internationally important seabird colonies, while the soft mud of estuaries like the Shannon and the Wexford slobs feed tens of thousands of wintering waders and geese. Every autumn, deep low-pressure systems sweep across the ocean and deposit American landbirds, waders, and seabirds on the southwestern capes, making counties Cork and Kerry the most celebrated vagrant traps in Europe. The national list stands at over 480 species.

## 1. Cape Clear Island, County Cork

Cape Clear is Ireland's premier seawatching station and the spiritual home of Irish rarity-hunting. The island's bird observatory, established in 1959, has logged Ireland's first records of dozens of American passerines. From the southern cliffs in autumn, seawatchers count Cory's Shearwater, Great Shearwater, Sooty Shearwater, and large numbers of Manx Shearwater streaming past in southwesterly gales, with Sabine's Gull and Grey Phalarope offshore. The island's gardens and valleys trap migrant warblers, and rarities such as Red-eyed Vireo and Yellow-billed Cuckoo are near-annual.

## 2. Mizen Head and the Cork capes

The Mizen, Sheep's Head, and Dursey Island peninsulas form a cluster of southwestern headlands that funnel autumn seabirds and trap drift migrants. Northwesterly winds in September and October push shearwaters, skuas, and the occasional Wilson's Storm-petrel close inshore. The bramble-filled gardens of the small farms hold American wood-warblers in good vagrant years, and Galley Head near Clonakilty is one of the most reliable autumn rarity sites on the south coast.

## 3. Skellig Michael and the Kerry islands

The Skelligs, off the Iveragh Peninsula in Kerry, hold one of the world's largest Northern Gannet colonies on Little Skellig, with around 35,000 pairs crowding the rock. Skellig Michael, the UNESCO monastic site, supports breeding Atlantic Puffin, Manx Shearwater, and European Storm-petrel. Boats run from Portmagee in summer; the gannet rock alone, wheeling with white birds, is among the great seabird spectacles of the North Atlantic.

## 4. The Saltee Islands, County Wexford

The Great Saltee, off the Wexford coast, is Ireland's best-known and most accessible seabird island. Its cliffs hold thousands of Razorbill, Common Guillemot, Atlantic Puffin, and Kittiwake, along with a Gannet colony that has grown steadily since the 1920s. Day boats run from Kilmore Quay in early summer, allowing close views of densely packed auk ledges and gannets nesting almost within reach.

## 5. Wexford Wildfowl Reserve, the North Slob

The reclaimed polders of the North Slob, on the Wexford Harbour, host almost the entire Greenland White-fronted Goose population on their winter grounds — around 10,000 birds, a third of the world total. The reserve's hides and visitor centre give close views of the geese, along with wintering Brent Goose, Bewick's and Whooper Swan, and large numbers of Wigeon, Pintail, and Lapwing. Hen Harrier and Peregrine hunt the open ground.

## 6. The Shannon and Fergus estuaries, County Clare

The Shannon estuary is Ireland's largest wetland complex, holding over 50,000 wintering waterbirds. The intertidal mudflats and the adjoining Fergus estuary support internationally important numbers of Dunlin, Black-tailed Godwit, Redshank, and Golden Plover. The reserve at Shannon Airport lagoon and the callows upstream near Banagher add wintering Whooper Swan and breeding Corncrake in the wet meadows.

## 7. Loop Head, County Clare

Loop Head, the long peninsula closing the mouth of the Shannon, is the west coast's answer to the Cork capes. Its position projecting into the Atlantic makes it an outstanding seawatch point and a magnet for drift migrants. Autumn brings shearwaters, skuas, and Leach's Storm-petrel offshore, while the heather and walls trap migrants. The cliffs hold breeding Chough, Fulmar, and Razorbill.

## 8. Rathlin Island, County Antrim

Rathlin, off the north Antrim coast, holds Northern Ireland's largest seabird colony. The RSPB West Light Seabird Centre, perched at the foot of the cliffs, gives spectacular views of breeding Puffin, Razorbill, Guillemot, and Kittiwake on the stacks below. Rathlin is also one of the most reliable Irish sites for breeding Chough and for Corncrake calling in the island's meadows.

## 9. The Wicklow Mountains and uplands

For upland and woodland species, the Wicklow Mountains south of Dublin offer the most accessible high ground in the east. Red Grouse, Merlin, and Peregrine breed on the blanket bog, while the conifer forests around Glendalough hold Crossbill, Siskin, and breeding Common Redstart. The river valleys support Dipper and Grey Wagtail.

## 10. Lough Neagh and the inland loughs

Lough Neagh, the largest freshwater lake in Ireland and Britain, holds enormous concentrations of wintering diving ducks, including internationally important numbers of Tufted Duck, Pochard, and Goldeneye, along with Great Crested Grebe and Whooper Swan. The surrounding shores and the nearby Lough Beg support breeding waders and a passage of terns and gulls.

## Seasons and migration on the Atlantic edge

Ireland's birding year is dominated by two contrasting seasons. The summer seabird season, roughly May to early August, fills the western cliffs and islands with breeding auks, gannets, and petrels. This is the window for boat trips to the Skelligs and the Saltees, when ledges are packed and the air is loud with kittiwakes. By August the auks begin to leave the cliffs, and attention shifts to the sea itself.

The autumn is what gives Ireland its international reputation. From late August through October, deep Atlantic depressions sweeping in from the southwest drive seabirds toward the coast and carry displaced American birds across the ocean. Strong southwesterly gales are the trigger for the great seawatches at Cape Clear, the Bridges of Ross near Loop Head, and the Cork capes, when thousands of shearwaters, skuas, and storm-petrels pass within sight of land. The same systems deposit Nearctic waders — Pectoral Sandpiper, Buff-breasted Sandpiper, American Golden Plover — on the southwestern wetlands, and in the most dramatic years, American passerines such as Blackpoll Warbler and Northern Parula in the headland gardens. The Bridges of Ross, a narrow rock platform in west Clare, is arguably the finest single seawatch site in Europe during October northwesterlies.

Winter belongs to the estuaries and the wildfowl. The Wexford slobs hold their Greenland White-fronted Geese, the Shannon and Strangford fill with waders and Brent Geese, and Lough Neagh hosts its diving-duck multitudes. Light-bellied Brent Geese arriving from Arctic Canada concentrate at Strangford Lough and Dublin Bay in numbers that represent a large share of the world population. Spring brings the return of breeding waders to the callows, the calling of Corncrake on the western islands, and the first passage of skuas up the west coast.

## Getting around

Ireland is compact and the birding sites are reachable by car within a few hours of either Dublin or Cork. The southwestern rarity circuit centres on Cork and Kerry, with ferries to Cape Clear from Baltimore and to Skellig boats from Portmagee. The Saltees are reached from Kilmore Quay in Wexford, an easy drive from Rosslare. BirdWatch Ireland manages many of the key reserves, and the Irish birding community is highly active online, posting real-time rarity news that drives the famous autumn twitches to the capes.

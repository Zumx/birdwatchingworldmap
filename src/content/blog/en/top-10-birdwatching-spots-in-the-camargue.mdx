The Camargue is the largest river delta in Western Europe, formed where the Rhone divides into two branches before entering the Mediterranean Sea. The resulting landscape is a mosaic of brackish lagoons, salt pans, freshwater marshes, reed beds, and dry garrigue that supports an extraordinary diversity of birdlife throughout the year. Greater Flamingos breed here in their tens of thousands — one of only a handful of regular breeding colonies in Europe — and the wetlands function as a critical stopover for migrants moving along the Western European flyway. The Camargue Regional Nature Park covers nearly 100,000 hectares and was one of the first Ramsar Wetlands of International Importance designated in France.

For birders, the Camargue is accessible year-round, but each season reveals a different character. Spring migration from March through May brings a succession of waders, terns, and passerines. Summer sees breeding colonies at peak activity. Autumn migration reverses the flow, and winter concentrates large numbers of ducks, geese, and raptors in the flooded marshes. The following sites within and adjacent to the Camargue consistently produce the best birding.

## 1. Etang de Vaccarès and Its Surrounding Dikes

The Etang de Vaccarès is the largest lagoon in the Camargue, covering roughly 6,500 hectares at the heart of the Regional Nature Park. The dike roads around its perimeter give access to the water's edge without disturbing the interior. Flamingos are visible throughout the year, often in flocks of several thousand. Purple Heron, Night Heron, Little Egret, and Great Egret all nest in the reed beds on the eastern shore. Collared Pratincole breeds on the nearby dry plains. The northern shore near the town of Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer is accessible by car, while the southern and eastern margins require bicycles or walking. Marsh Harrier hunts over the reedbeds year-round.

## 2. La Capeliere Reserve, Mas du Pont de Rousty

La Capeliere is managed by the Société Nationale de Protection de la Nature and offers the most structured visitor experience in the Camargue. The reserve includes hides overlooking freshwater pools, nature trails through reed beds, and an information centre. Little Bittern nests in the phragmites and can be seen from the dike path in June and July. Bearded Reedling inhabits the reed beds year-round and is most visible in calm autumn mornings when it climbs to the tops of reed stems. The observatory hides overlook open water where Ferruginous Duck, Garganey, and Red-crested Pochard congregate during migration. Moustached Warbler is resident and audible from the boardwalk.

## 3. Pont de Gau Ornithological Park

Located near Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer, Pont de Gau is a private ornithological park with free-flying flamingos visible at close range alongside wild birds that choose to use the managed lagoons. The park's extensive network of paths covers several kilometres of marsh edge and gives access to areas otherwise difficult to reach. Black-winged Stilt nests here in open numbers, and the area around the car park is reliably good for Eurasian Hoopoe in spring. The park's proximity to the coast means that sea movements are sometimes visible from the higher ground, and Mediterranean Gull and Slender-billed Gull are regularly present on the adjacent mudflats.

## 4. Les Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer Beach and Mouth of the Petit Rhône

The beach at Saintes-Maries fronts the Mediterranean and the river mouth nearby attracts terns and gulls in numbers. Audouin's Gull, a Mediterranean endemic, is regularly present on the sandbars. Little Tern and Common Tern nest on the beach in colonies marked by conservation fencing. The river mouth itself attracts Caspian Tern during migration. The area between the town and the Petit Rhône mouth is worth scanning carefully for resting waders: Curlew Sandpiper, Dunlin, and Kentish Plover all occur. In autumn, migrating raptors including Osprey pass regularly over the coastal scrub.

## 5. Salins de Giraud Salt Pans

The industrial salt pans at Giraud on the southern Camargue are managed partly for wildlife and attract some of the largest concentrations of flamingos in France, sometimes exceeding 20,000 birds. The main flamingo breeding colony is located on an island within the salt pans and is visible from the dike roads at a respectful distance. Avocet, Black-winged Stilt, and Kentish Plover all breed on the pan margins. Slender-billed Gull nests in the area. The access road along the southern salt pan edge is one of the most productive drives in the entire Camargue for sheer numbers of birds and variety.

## 6. Marais du Vigueirat, Arles

The Vigueirat marshes lie at the northern edge of the Camargue near Arles and are managed by the Conservatoire du Littoral. The reserve combines freshwater marsh, seasonally flooded meadow, and tamarisk scrub with carefully managed water levels to maximise habitat diversity. Squacco Heron breeds here, one of its most reliable French sites. Glossy Ibis has established a breeding colony that has grown substantially over the past decade. The observation tower gives wide views across the flooded meadows where wintering White-fronted Geese and Greylag Geese graze. Guided tours are available and are especially valuable for locating the Squacco Herons in the denser vegetation.

## 7. Domaine de la Palissade, Salin de Badon Area

This eastern sector of the Camargue near the Salin de Badon is owned by the Conservatoire and offers walking trails through a relatively wild section of the delta. The area around the brackish pond at Salin de Badon historically attracted enormous numbers of ducks in winter, and while water management has altered conditions over the decades, it remains a productive site. Bluethroat is regularly found in the tamarisk scrub during autumn migration. The eastern fringe of the Camargue here transitions toward the Crau plain, and Short-toed Lark and Calandra Lark can be found where the habitats mix.

## 8. La Crau Dry Plain

The Crau is a unique steppe landscape east of the Camargue, formed by ancient alluvial deposits of the Durance river. It is the only true steppe in France and supports species otherwise absent from the country. The Little Bustard, now reduced to a small French population, persists on the Crau. Calandra Lark is common and conspicuous. Pin-tailed Sandgrouse occurs in variable numbers depending on the year. European Roller breeds in old buildings and tree cavities at the steppe margin. Lesser Kestrel has recently recolonised the area. The flat terrain and short vegetation make scanning for ground-nesting and ground-feeding species straightforward, and the open sky makes raptors easy to track.

## 9. Les Alpilles Hills

The Alpilles are a small limestone range rising from the flat Camargue plain northeast of Arles, visible from much of the delta. The rocky garrigue supports a completely different community from the wetlands below. Subalpine Warbler, Dartford Warbler, and Spectacled Warbler all breed on the scrubby slopes. Short-toed Eagle is a reliable summer visitor hunting lizards in the open areas. Egyptian Vulture was once resident but has declined; sightings still occur. The combination of a day at the Alpilles with a morning or evening at the nearby wetland makes efficient use of a Camargue visit.

## 10. Berre Lagoon Margins

The Etang de Berre northeast of Marseille sits at the eastern edge of what might be considered greater Camargue influence, and while it is more heavily industrialised than the Camargue proper, the northern shores near Marignane and Istres retain wetland margins worth visiting. Flamingos regularly use the lagoon, and the salt pan margins at Pont de l'Etoile attract similar species to the main Camargue. In winter, the lagoon holds good numbers of diving ducks including Common Pochard and Tufted Duck. Kentish Plover breeds on the sandy margins in spring.

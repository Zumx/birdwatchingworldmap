The first view of a male Resplendent Quetzal (Pharomachrus mocinno) in breeding plumage is an event that experienced birders describe with the same vocabulary used for very few other birds. The iridescent green of the head, breast, and back shifts through gold and blue with changing light. The red belly is a shock of pure color. The upper tail coverts — which in a breeding male can exceed 60 centimetres in length — trail behind the bird in flight or hang below the perch, moving in any breeze. Its cultural weight among the Maya and Aztec was such that the quetzal feather was more valuable than gold, and the bird has carried the distinction of the Guatemalan national currency, the national bird, and the central motif of the Guatemalan flag for a century.

## Taxonomy and distribution

The Resplendent Quetzal is the northernmost of the five quetzal species in the genus Pharomachrus, itself part of the trogon family (Trogonidae). It ranges from southern Mexico (Chiapas) through Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, and Costa Rica to Panama, occupying cloud forest and montane oak forest between roughly 1,200 and 3,000 metres elevation. Two subspecies are recognized: P. m. mocinno in the northern part of the range (southern Mexico to Honduras) and P. m. costaricensis in Costa Rica and western Panama. The subspecies differ subtly in the extent of the upper tail covert length and the saturation of the red belly in breeding males.

## The cloud forest specialist: frugivory and Ocotea

The Resplendent Quetzal is one of the most important seed dispersers in Mesoamerican cloud forests. Its primary food is the fruit of Ocotea (laurel family) trees, which it swallows whole and regurgitates the large seeds intact — the seeds too large for the quetzal's digestive tract to break down. The bird is the principal disperser of several Ocotea species, including the avocado relative Ocotea tenera, whose large fruits are almost exclusively dispersed by quetzals. The ecological relationship is mutualistic and obligate; where quetzal populations have been reduced by hunting or habitat loss, Ocotea regeneration in secondary forest has declined.

Quetzals supplement their frugivory diet with insects, small lizards, and frogs, especially when raising chicks. The protein requirement of nestlings drives a significant shift toward invertebrate and small vertebrate prey that is concentrated in the breeding season.

## Breeding behavior and nesting

Male quetzals begin their spectacular territorial display flights in February, coinciding with the onset of the dry season in Mesoamerican cloud forest. The long upper tail coverts serve as the display's primary visual signal; they develop fully in males' third calendar year and are molted after breeding. The display flight — a rising-and-falling undulation over the forest canopy, with the tail coverts streaming — has been described by observers as one of the most theatrical bird displays in the Americas.

Nesting occurs in cavities excavated in dead or decaying trees. Both sexes incubate; when the long-tailed male sits in the nest hole, the tail coverts trail over the entrance like a decoration. Two to three eggs are laid; incubation lasts approximately 18 days. Chick development is rapid — the young are altricial at hatch and fledge at approximately 30 days.

## Best sites: Monteverde, Savegre, and beyond

In Costa Rica, the two most reliable quetzal sites are Monteverde Cloud Forest Reserve and the Savegre Valley (San Gerardo de Dota). Monteverde provides the most consistent breeding-season views (February to April) when displaying males use territories at forest edges near the reserve infrastructure. The Savegre Valley, accessed from San José via the Cerro de la Muerte highway, holds quetzals year-round; the apple orchards and secondary forest near the lodges at San Gerardo de Dota are particularly productive in the early morning. eBird hotspot data for Savegre shows quetzal on virtually every complete checklist submitted between November and May.

In Guatemala, the Sierra de las Minas Biosphere Reserve and the cloud forest around Cobán hold the core Guatemalan population. The Quetzal Reserve (Biotopo del Quetzal) near Purulhá, Baja Verapaz, protects a segment of cloud forest on the route between Guatemala City and Cobán and provides infrastructure for quetzal-focused visits.

## Conservation status: Near Threatened

The Resplendent Quetzal is assessed as Near Threatened (NT) on the IUCN Red List. The primary threat is cloud forest loss through agricultural conversion, primarily in Guatemala and Honduras where lowland-to-highland migration for agricultural expansion has reduced cloud forest coverage substantially. Cloud forest fragments below 100 hectares are considered too small to maintain viable quetzal territories. In areas where cloud forest has been reduced to isolated hilltop patches, quetzal populations cannot persist.

Climate change presents an additional threat: montane cloud forest species are constrained in their ability to shift elevation with changing temperature gradients because the mountain tops above the cloud forest belt are too cold and exposed to support the fruiting trees on which the quetzal depends.

## Where and when to see Resplendent Quetzal

The Resplendent Quetzal's breeding range in the cloud forests of southern Mexico and Central America aligns with a set of well-known birding sites that have organized guide infrastructure and predictable viewing during the breeding season. The breeding season (February to June, varying by latitude) coincides with the dry season in many range-state cloud forests, making road access and trail conditions at their most reliable.

Costa Rica's most reliable Quetzal sites are in the Cerro de la Muerte highlands on the Pan-American Highway south of San José, and in the Monteverde cloud forest. The Cerro de la Muerte (meaning "Hill of Death") cloud forest at around 3,000 metres elevation is consistently the most productive site for breeding Quetzal in Costa Rica — male birds with full breeding tail plumes are present from February to May. The Savegre Hotel and La Georgina area along the Pan-American Highway have eBird hotspot records showing daily Quetzal records during the breeding season.

Guatemala's Sierra de los Cuchumatanes and the Biotopo Mario Dary Rivera (Quetzal Reserve) in Alta Verapaz provide additional Quetzal viewing with different forest character. The Biotopo Quetzal is Guatemala's most organized Quetzal conservation site, with a trail network through cloud forest managed specifically for Quetzal protection and ecotourism.

The Quetzal's frugivore biology — it feeds primarily on wild avocado (Persea spp.) and other Lauraceae family fruits — makes it possible to predict Quetzal presence by the fruiting status of the forest. Local guides who monitor which trees are in fruit on any given week provide information that dramatically improves viewing probability.

Outside the breeding season, Quetzals make altitudinal movements — descending from the high cloud forest to lower-elevation fruiting zones in the post-breeding period (July to October). These movements are poorly understood and less predictable than the breeding season, making February to June the recommended visiting window for birders prioritizing Quetzal encounters.

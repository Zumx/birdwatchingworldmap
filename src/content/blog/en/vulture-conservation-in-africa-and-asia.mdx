Vultures are among the most ecologically important birds on Earth, and the least appreciated. They are the cleanup crew of terrestrial ecosystems, capable of consuming carcasses in quantities and at speeds that prevent the build-up of disease, block feral dog population explosions, and recycle nutrients from dead animals back into the ecosystem. A carcass that would take weeks to decompose in the absence of scavengers is stripped to bone by a vulture congregation in hours. Their stomach acid is strong enough to destroy anthrax spores, botulinum toxin, and cholera bacteria — pathogens that would persist and spread if carcasses remained on the landscape. Vultures are, in this sense, a public health service provided for free by functioning ecosystems.

Both African and Asian vulture populations are now in various states of crisis, threatened by different but overlapping sets of pressures. The collapse of Asian vulture populations from the 1990s onward is the most dramatic wildlife die-off in recorded history outside of direct species extinction: three Gyps vulture species lost more than 95 percent of their populations across South Asia within a decade. The causes in Africa are somewhat different but the trajectory of decline is similarly alarming for several species. Understanding the threats is essential to understanding what conservation responses are needed.

## The Asian Crisis: Diclofenac

The crash of vulture populations across India, Pakistan, and Nepal beginning in the early 1990s was initially mysterious. Indian populations of White-backed Vulture, Long-billed Vulture, and Slender-billed Vulture, which had historically been among the most numerous large birds in the world, began dying in large numbers. Researchers eventually traced the cause to a single veterinary drug: diclofenac, a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory widely used to treat cattle and other livestock. When cattle treated with diclofenac die and are left for vultures to consume, the drug accumulates to toxic levels in vultures, causing visceral gout and kidney failure. A single carcass treated with diclofenac can kill dozens of vultures at a feeding.

The ban on veterinary diclofenac in India, Pakistan, and Nepal in 2006 was a landmark conservation intervention, but population recovery has been slow because vultures are long-lived and breed very slowly: most Old World vulture species raise a single chick per year, meaning that population growth is inherently limited. The drug meloxicam was identified as a safe alternative and promoted to replace diclofenac, and captive breeding programs for the most threatened species — Gyps vultures — were established in South Asia. Population counts suggest that the rate of decline has slowed significantly since the ban, but recovery is measured in decades.

## African Threats: Poisoning and the Bushmeat Crisis

African vultures face a different combination of threats. Primary among them is deliberate and accidental poisoning. Deliberate poisoning takes two main forms: farmers poison carcasses to kill predators that have taken livestock, and vultures are among the most conspicuous victims of this untargeted approach. More insidious is poisoning motivated by the bushmeat and illegal wildlife trade: poachers poison carcasses to kill vultures specifically, because the circling of vultures over a killed elephant or rhinoceros acts as a visible signal to rangers that a poaching event has occurred. Removing the vulture surveillance capacity allows poachers to operate more covertly.

Traditional medicine demand in parts of Africa, particularly in southern Africa, creates a direct market for vulture body parts — heads and feet are believed in some traditions to convey powers of foresight and intelligence. This demand for vulture body parts has created a commercial incentive for illegal killing on a scale that conservation organizations have struggled to address through enforcement alone.

Seven of the eleven African vulture species are now classified as Critically Endangered or Endangered on the IUCN Red List. White-backed Vulture, the most numerous African vulture species and formerly one of the most abundant large birds on the continent, has declined by more than 90 percent in West Africa and shows significant declines across East and Southern Africa. Hooded Vulture, a smaller and formerly familiar scavenger in and around African towns and markets, has declined catastrophically across West Africa.

## Where Vultures Can Still Be Seen

Despite population declines, several sites in Africa still support concentrations of vultures that are both spectacular and conservation-significant. The Masai Mara-Serengeti ecosystem maintains one of the largest remaining concentrations of Old World vultures, with White-backed Vulture, Rüppell's Griffon, Lappet-faced Vulture, and White-headed Vulture all regularly present around large mammal carcasses. The dynamic of vulture species at a kill follows a rough hierarchy: the large, aggressive Lappet-faced dominates when present; Rüppell's Griffon and White-backed Vulture crowd the carcass in numbers; and the smaller Hooded Vulture picks at scraps on the periphery.

The Cape Griffon or Cape Vulture of southern Africa concentrates at breeding colonies on cliff faces, most notably at the Kransberg colony in Limpopo, the Blouberg colony in northern South Africa, and the Manyeleti area adjacent to Kruger National Park. Cape Vulture tours at active colonies during the breeding season give remarkable close-range views of thousands of birds on the cliff faces.

In Asia, the Kathmandu Valley in Nepal and the Ranthambhore area of Rajasthan, India, maintain Gyps vulture populations that are monitored by the Vulture Conservation Breeding Programme. Jatayu Conservation Breeding Centre at Pinjore in Haryana, India, operates a captive breeding program for all three critically endangered Gyps species.

## Gyps Vultures in Europe and Their Spread

Western Europe maintains its own Gyps vulture populations in the Griffon Vulture, which has made a remarkable recovery from its 20th-century lows. Spain holds the largest Griffon Vulture population in Europe, with tens of thousands of birds breeding in the Pyrenees, the Cantabrian Mountains, and the Meseta ranges. The Jura mountains of France, Switzerland, and the Balkans of Bulgaria and Serbia also hold significant populations. The recovery of Griffon Vulture in Western Europe is attributable partly to supplementary feeding programs and partly to legal protection. Bearded Vulture, reintroduced to the Alps in 1986 after extinction in the 19th century, has established a self-sustaining population that is slowly expanding.

## What Conservation Looks Like

Vulture conservation is unusually difficult because the species travel vast distances, routinely crossing international borders, and the threats are diverse, spanning pharmaceutical industries, agricultural practice, traditional medicine markets, and anti-poaching enforcement. The multi-pronged approach required includes drug regulation across multiple South Asian countries, carcass monitoring programs in Africa, community education about the ecological value of vultures, and legal frameworks that protect vulture body parts from trade. The Vulture Multi-species Action Plan (Vulture MsAP) adopted under the African-Eurasian Migratory Waterbird Agreement in 2017 is the most comprehensive international framework yet established for vulture conservation, identifying priority actions for all relevant range states.

Where vultures persist in significant numbers, they remain one of the most spectacular components of the birding experience. Watching a congregation of a hundred vultures descend on a carcass, the first individuals circling high in distant thermals and gradually drawing others in across several hours, is a reminder of the ecological processes that large vertebrate ecosystems depend upon.

Bhutan is the rare country where conservation is written into the constitution: more than 70 percent of the land remains under forest, and the kingdom commits to maintaining at least 60 percent forest cover in perpetuity. For birders this translates into something almost unimaginable elsewhere in the Himalayas — vast, unbroken altitudinal gradients running from subtropical foothills at 200 metres to alpine meadows above 4,000 metres, with intact forest at every level in between. The result is a bird list approaching 700 species packed into a country smaller than Switzerland, including some of the most sought-after birds in Asia. A single transect along the lateral road can take you from hornbills to snow partridges in a day.

## Phobjikha Valley

The glacial valley of Phobjikha, also written Gangtey, sits at around 3,000 metres in central Bhutan and is the kingdom's most famous birding destination. Each year from late October to mid-February, roughly 500 black-necked cranes migrate from the Tibetan Plateau to winter in its marshes, and their arrival is celebrated with the Black-Necked Crane Festival at Gangtey Monastery in early November. The Royal Society for Protection of Nature runs a crane information centre on the valley floor, where spotting scopes are trained on the feeding flocks. Beyond the cranes, the dwarf bamboo and blue pine support Himalayan Monal, satyr tragopan in the surrounding forest, and wintering raptors.

## Bumthang Valleys

The four valleys of Bumthang — Chokhor, Tang, Ura, and Chhume — form the spiritual heart of Bhutan and lie between 2,600 and 4,000 metres. The conifer forests of blue pine, spruce, and hemlock here hold ibisbill along the braided rivers, where the species probes the shingle for invertebrates, as well as wood snipe, Himalayan Monal, and Spotted Nutcracker. The high pastures above Ura are reliable for snow pigeon and the occasional Tibetan snowcock. Bumthang is also where many tours base themselves to access the alpine specialities near the Thrumshing La pass.

## Thrumshing La National Park

Thrumshing La pass crests at 3,780 metres and the national park that surrounds it protects one of the finest temperate forest transects in the eastern Himalayas. The descent eastward toward Sengor and Yongkola drops through fir, then hemlock, then dense rhododendron and broadleaf forest, and this corridor is legendary among birders. Fire-tailed Myzornis, fire-tailed sunbird, gold-naped finch, and a remarkable diversity of laughingthrushes occur along the road. Mixed feeding flocks here can include dozens of species moving through the canopy at once.

## Yongkola and the Lingmethang Road

The stretch of road between Sengor and Lingmethang, often simply called Yongkola, is widely regarded as one of the single best birding roads on earth. Dropping from roughly 3,000 metres to 700 metres over a few dozen kilometres, it passes through wet broadleaf forest dripping with epiphytes and orchids. Beautiful nuthatch, rufous-necked hornbill, ward's trogon, the elusive rufous-throated wren-babbler, and the brilliant golden-throated barbet all occur here. Dawn choruses along this road are among the densest in the Himalayas.

## Royal Manas National Park

In the far south, bordering India's Manas Tiger Reserve, Royal Manas National Park protects subtropical lowland forest along the foothills at 200 to 1,500 metres. This is Bhutan's richest park for lowland species: great hornbill, wreathed hornbill, Pallas's fish eagle along the Manas River, and the rare Bengal florican in the grasslands. The park forms a transboundary conservation landscape with India and is one of the last refuges in the region for intact lowland forest birds.

## Jigme Dorji National Park

Covering much of the northwest, Jigme Dorji is Bhutan's second-largest protected area and spans subtropical valleys up to permanent snow and glaciers along the Tibetan border. Birders trekking the Jomolhari route can find blood pheasant, Himalayan snowcock, snow partridge, grandala, and the spectacular wallcreeper working the cliff faces. The high-altitude lakes and screes also support brandt's mountain finch and various accentors.

## Tashithang and the Punakha Valley

The warm, low Punakha valley at around 1,200 metres, where the Pho Chhu and Mo Chhu rivers meet beneath the magnificent Punakha Dzong, offers gentle, accessible birding. The road up the Mo Chhu toward Tashithang follows the river through subtropical forest holding white-bellied heron — one of the world's rarest birds, with fewer than 60 mature individuals remaining worldwide. Pallas's fish eagle, brown dipper, and various forktails patrol the river, while the warm slopes hold sultan tit and small niltava.

## Chele La Pass

At 3,988 metres, Chele La is the highest motorable pass in Bhutan, linking the Paro and Haa valleys. The fir and rhododendron forest just below the pass is one of the most reliable sites for Himalayan Monal, with displaying males catching the morning light, and for kalij pheasant lower down. The pass itself is good for blood pheasant, Himalayan griffon riding the thermals, and, in the rhododendron blooms of April and May, fire-tailed sunbird.

## Sengor

The high settlement of Sengor, perched around 3,000 metres on the eastern road, deserves its own mention as a base for the wet fir and hemlock forest immediately above Yongkola. This is the core range of the satyr tragopan, whose males display in spring among the moss-laden trees, and of rufous-throated and other wren-babblers. The damp, mist-shrouded forest here also holds fulvettas, parrotbills, and the elusive hill partridge.

## Royal Botanical Park, Lamperi

Between Thimphu and Punakha on the Dochula pass road, the Royal Botanical Park at Lamperi sits in beautiful mixed forest around 2,500 metres and offers an easy, well-marked introduction to mid-elevation Bhutanese birding. The 108 chortens of Dochula provide a dramatic backdrop, and on clear days the eastern Himalayan peaks are visible. The park's trails are good for cutia, yellow-billed blue magpie, hoary-throated barwing, and a fine selection of warblers and tits.

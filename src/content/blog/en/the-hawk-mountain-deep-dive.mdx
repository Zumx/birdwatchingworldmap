Stand on the North Lookout at Hawk Mountain Sanctuary on a crisp afternoon in mid-September, when a cold front has cleared overnight and the wind is pushing steadily from the northwest, and you will understand why this rocky outcrop changed the course of conservation. The Kittatinny Ridge falls away below you in folds of hardwood forest, the Appalachian spine running blue toward the horizon, and out of that blue come the raptors: Broad-winged Hawks in spiralling kettles of hundreds, Sharp-shinned Hawks beating low along the rock, the occasional Bald Eagle riding the updraft at eye level. People have counted birds from this exact ledge every autumn since 1934. No other watch site on Earth has a continuous record like it.

Hawk Mountain sits in Berks and Schuylkill counties, in the ridge-and-valley country of eastern Pennsylvania, about ninety minutes northwest of Philadelphia. It is not a tall mountain — the lookouts stand near 1,500 feet, roughly 460 metres — but its position relative to the prevailing autumn winds makes it one of the great natural funnels for migrating birds of prey in eastern North America.

## How the Ridge Concentrates Migration

The secret of Hawk Mountain is the Kittatinny Ridge itself, the long unbroken Appalachian fold that runs for hundreds of kilometres from the Hudson Valley southwest into Pennsylvania. When autumn cold fronts arrive and northwest winds strike the ridge, they are deflected upward, creating a continuous band of updraft along its leeward edge. Migrating hawks, eagles, and falcons exploit this lift, gliding south for kilometres at a time while expending almost no energy. The ridge becomes a highway, and Hawk Mountain a tollbooth where the traffic is visible and countable.

This is why wind direction matters so much to anyone planning a visit. On days with northwest winds following the passage of a front, the flight hugs the ridge close and the views are spectacular. On southerly or easterly winds the birds drift wide, fly high, or simply do not move, and the lookout can be quiet. Seasoned hawk-watchers read the weather maps as carefully as they read the sky.

## The Story of Rosalie Edge

For all its geological good fortune, Hawk Mountain exists because of one determined woman. In the early twentieth century the ridge was a notorious shooting gallery, where gunners gathered each autumn to blast migrating hawks out of the sky by the thousands, driven by the bounty-fuelled belief that birds of prey were vermin. Photographs of hillsides littered with dead raptors reached a New York conservationist named Rosalie Edge, a suffragist turned fierce campaigner against the establishment conservation groups of her day.

In 1934 Edge leased the mountain, posted it against shooting, and hired a young naturalist, Maurice Broun, as the first warden. He and his wife Irma began systematically counting the raptors that now passed unmolested. Edge bought the land outright the following year and opened it to the public, creating the world's first refuge for birds of prey. The act was radical: at the time, even mainstream ornithology largely accepted the killing of hawks.

## The Longest Raptor Count in the World

What began as Maurice Broun's daily tally has become the longest-running record of raptor migration anywhere. Official counters staff the North Lookout from mid-August through mid-December every year, logging each bird to species, recording the hour and the weather, following protocols that have stayed consistent enough to make decades of data comparable. The season total typically runs between 18,000 and 20,000 raptors of some sixteen regularly occurring species.

These numbers are far more than a spectator's scorecard. Because the methodology has remained stable, the Hawk Mountain dataset functions as a long-term monitor of raptor population health across eastern North America. It documented the catastrophic crash of Bald Eagle, Osprey, and Peregrine Falcon numbers during the DDT era of the 1950s and 1960s, providing some of the earliest hard evidence that the pesticide was devastating top predators. The same record later charted their recovery after DDT was banned, and today tracks the worrying long-term decline of the American Kestrel.

## The Birds and the Calendar

The migration unfolds in waves through the autumn. The earliest movement, in late August and early September, brings Bald Eagles, Ospreys, and American Kestrels. The great spectacle peaks around the third week of September, when Broad-winged Hawks pass in enormous kettles — on a single classic day the count can exceed several thousand, and exceptional days have topped 20,000 of this one species alone as they ride thermals south toward Central and South America.

October shifts the cast. Sharp-shinned and Cooper's Hawks dominate, along with Red-shouldered Hawks, Northern Harriers, and the first Red-tailed Hawks. Peregrine Falcons and Merlins streak through, and Golden Eagles begin appearing. November belongs to the heavyweights: Red-tailed Hawks in numbers, Golden Eagles riding the cold updrafts, and the occasional Northern Goshawk. For many regulars, a late-autumn Golden Eagle banking against a grey sky is the prize of the season.

## Walking the Sanctuary

Hawk Mountain is more than a single lookout. The sanctuary protects around 2,600 acres of ridge-top forest, threaded by roughly thirteen kilometres of trails. The South Lookout is an easy, nearly level walk from the visitor centre and is accessible to most. The North Lookout, the classic counting station, lies about 1.2 kilometres up the rocky Lookout Trail and rewards the climb with a panorama spanning more than 110 kilometres on a clear day. The more ambitious River of Rocks Trail loops down through a striking boulder field below the ridge.

The Acopian Center for Conservation Learning and the modern visitor centre anchor the experience, with exhibits, a bookstore, and the warmth of a wood stove on cold November days. An admission fee supports the non-profit that owns and runs the sanctuary entirely outside any government system. Members count for free, and the official counters are usually happy to help newcomers tell a distant Sharp-shinned Hawk from a Cooper's.

## The Legacy Beyond the Ridge

Hawk Mountain's influence reaches far past Pennsylvania. The counting protocols pioneered here helped seed the network of hawk-watch sites that now stretches across the Americas, coordinated through the Hawk Migration Association of North America. The sanctuary runs an international training programme that has educated raptor conservationists from dozens of countries, many of whom return home to establish protected areas and monitoring of their own. The very phrase hawk-watching, as a recreational and scientific pursuit, traces its modern form to this ridge.

For the visitor, none of that history is necessary to enjoy a clear September afternoon with binoculars raised and a kettle of Broad-wings boiling up over the valley. But it lends the place a particular gravity. Every raptor that crosses unharmed does so because of a decision made here nearly a century ago, on a mountain that taught the world to look up and count rather than shoot.

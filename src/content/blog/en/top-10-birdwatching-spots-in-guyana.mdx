Guyana is one of the last great wild corners of South America, a sparsely populated country where roughly 85 percent of the land is still covered in forest. It sits on the ancient Guiana Shield, and its birdlist passes 800 species, including charismatic giants like the Harpy Eagle and the flame-orange Guianan Cock-of-the-rock. What sets Guyana apart is not just the species but the experience: birding here means small Amerindian-run lodges, river travel by dugout, and forest so intact that top predators are present in healthy numbers. The sites below trace the classic route from the coastal mudflats up the Essequibo and into the vast Rupununi savannas of the deep south.

## 1. Kaieteur National Park

Kaieteur Falls plunges 226 metres in a single drop on the Potaro River, one of the most powerful single-drop waterfalls on earth, set in pristine Guiana Shield forest. The plateau around the falls is the classic site for the Guianan Cock-of-the-rock, whose males display in leks on the rocky cliffs. At dusk the Kaieteur (White-collared) Swift pours over the lip of the falls to roost behind the curtain of water — a spectacle of thousands of birds wheeling into the gorge.

## 2. Iwokrama Forest

Iwokrama is a one-million-acre rainforest reserve in the heart of the country, managed for sustainable use and research, and the centrepiece of Guyana birding. The Iwokrama River Lodge on the Essequibo and the canopy walkway at Atta give access to a staggering list, including the Crimson Fruitcrow, Pompadour Cotinga, Capuchinbird, and Guianan Toucanet. The reserve is famous as one of the most reliable places on earth to see a Jaguar from the road.

## 3. Atta Rainforest Lodge and Canopy Walkway

Atta, within the Iwokrama forest, is built around a canopy walkway rising into the treetops, where dawn brings mixed canopy flocks and fruiting trees draw cotingas and toucans. The clearing around the lodge is a known site for the Crimson Topaz, one of the most spectacular hummingbirds in the world, and the surrounding trails hold the Rufous-winged Ground Cuckoo and a rich understorey antbird community.

## 4. Surama Eco-Lodge

Surama is an Amerindian village in the North Rupununi running a community ecolodge that has become a benchmark for responsible tourism in Guyana. Set where forest meets savanna, it offers two habitats from one base: the Burro Burro River for Agami Heron and Sungrebe, and the forest trails for the Rufous-winged Ground Cuckoo and trogons. Village guides know the local leks and roosts intimately.

## 5. Karanambu Lodge

Karanambu, on the Rupununi River, is a former cattle ranch turned conservation lodge long associated with giant river otter rehabilitation. Its mosaic of riverine forest, ponds, and savanna is excellent for the Agami Heron, Crestless Curassow, and the Rio Branco Antbird. Boat trips at dusk to see Victoria amazonica giant water lilies open are paired with chances at Capped Heron and Boat-billed Heron along the banks.

## 6. Rupununi Savannas

The Rupununi is a vast grassland in the deep south near the Brazilian border, dotted with termite mounds, gallery forest, and seasonal wetlands. This is the place for open-country species absent from the forest: the Red Siskin, a Critically Endangered finch with a Guyanan stronghold, plus Burrowing Owl, Bearded Tachuri, and the Jabiru stork. The Sun Parakeet, a brilliant golden-orange endemic of the region, has its core range here.

## 7. Manari Ranch and Lethem area

Around Lethem, the southern gateway town near the Brazilian frontier, ranches like Manari sit in transitional savanna and gallery forest along streams. This area is one of the most accessible places to look for the Sun Parakeet and the Hoary-throated Spinetail, a Critically Endangered gallery-forest endemic of the Rio Branco basin. The mix of grassland, scrub, and creekside woodland produces long day lists.

## 8. Georgetown Botanical Gardens and seawall

Georgetown, the coastal capital, offers superb birding within the city. The Botanical Gardens hold the Blood-coloured Woodpecker, a near-endemic of the Guianas, alongside Festive Parrot, Toco Toucan, and roosting Snail Kites. The seawall and the Atlantic mudflats are a vital stopover on the shorebird flyway, with vast flocks of Semipalmated Sandpiper and Hudsonian Godwit on northbound and southbound passage.

## 9. Mahaica River

The Mahaica River, a short drive east of Georgetown, is the most reliable place in Guyana to see the Hoatzin, the country's national bird, a bizarre leaf-eating species that clambers through riverside vegetation. Morning boat trips along the mangrove-lined river also produce Rufous Crab Hawk, Boat-billed Heron, and a range of kingfishers and herons, making it an easy half-day from the capital.

## 10. Shell Beach Protected Area

Shell Beach stretches along the remote northwest coast near the Venezuelan border, a 120-kilometre arc of beaches, mangrove, and mudflat that is one of the most important sea-turtle nesting sites in the region. For birders it offers vast concentrations of Scarlet Ibis, which roost in the mangroves in flame-red flocks at dusk, alongside herons, terns, and migratory shorebirds. Access is by boat and requires arrangement through local operators.

## Planning a Guyana birding trip

Guyana rewards a two-week trip following the classic overland-and-air route: fly into Georgetown, work the coast and Mahaica, then take the small aircraft or the rough Linden-Lethem road south into Iwokrama and the Rupununi. Distances are long and roads are unpaved, so most itineraries combine charter flights with river and 4WD travel between community lodges. Internal logistics are best handled by a specialist operator, as the lodges are remote and booking ahead is essential.

The dry season, roughly September to April, with a peak from October to March, gives the most reliable travel and the best forest birding. The savanna is most accessible when the seasonal floods recede, and the cock-of-the-rock leks at Kaieteur are active year-round. The northbound shorebird passage on the coast peaks in spring, while the giant water lilies and dusk wildlife trips at Karanambu run best in the dry months.

Guyana is physically demanding birding — humid forest, long boat days, and basic lodges — but the reward is a level of wilderness rare in the modern Neotropics. The presence of Harpy Eagle, Jaguar, and giant river otter in natural densities reflects how intact the ecosystem remains. A good local guide is indispensable, both for finding skulking forest species by voice and for the cultural insight that the Amerindian-run lodges provide.

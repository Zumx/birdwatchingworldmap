Cyprus occupies a strategically important position in the eastern Mediterranean, lying along one of the most heavily used migratory flyways in the Western Palearctic. Hundreds of millions of birds funnel through the region each spring and autumn, moving between wintering grounds in Africa and breeding grounds across Europe and central Asia. The island serves as a critical stepping stone, offering wetlands, scrubland, and forests where exhausted migrants can rest and refuel. Combined with resident and breeding species, including the island's own endemic subspecies and full species, Cyprus rewards visiting birders with an impressive and varied list.

The island's habitats are diverse for its modest size — salt lakes near the coast, river valleys cutting through limestone hills, dense pine forests on Troodos, and the dry garrigue of the central mesaoria plain. Winter brings wildfowl and waders in numbers. Spring migration peaks in April and May, when shrubs along the coast can be dripping with warblers, flycatchers, and chats. Cyprus's endemic Cyprus Warbler is findable year-round, and the Cyprus Wheatear breeds on the upper slopes of Troodos from March onwards.

## 1. Akrotiri Salt Lake, Limassol District

Akrotiri Salt Lake is the most productive wetland on Cyprus and one of the best birding sites in the entire eastern Mediterranean. In winter, Greater Flamingos gather here in flocks that can exceed ten thousand birds, feeding on the brine shrimp and algae that thrive in the shallow hypersaline water. The surrounding scrub and reedbeds hold Little Bittern, Purple Heron, and Marsh Harrier. Resident Black Francolin can be heard calling from dense vegetation around the lake's margins. Waders are excellent during migration, with Temminck's Stint, Little Stint, Curlew Sandpiper, and Marsh Sandpiper all recorded regularly in season. The site is a Ramsar wetland and Special Protection Area, and access is straightforward via tracks south of Akrotiri village.

## 2. Cape Greco, Famagusta District

The rocky headland at Cape Greco on the southeastern tip of Cyprus is a classic migrant trap. Birds funnelling south in autumn, or arriving from Africa in spring, concentrate here before or after the sea crossing. Eleonora's Falcons patrol the skies during late summer and autumn, hunting migrant passerines. On a good morning in April, the low scrub and sea caves area holds dozens of Collared Flycatchers, Pied Flycatchers, Redstarts, and various warblers. Bee-eaters stream overhead on warm sunny days. Rarities recorded here include Cyprus's first Rufous-tailed Rock Thrush sightings and various East Mediterranean overshoots. The National Forest Park walking trails provide easy access.

## 3. Troodos National Forest Park, Nicosia/Limassol Districts

The forested massif of Troodos, rising to nearly 2000 metres at Mount Olympos, holds the island's forest endemics and specialities. The Cyprus Warbler — a compact black-and-white species related to Sardinian Warbler but found only on Cyprus — inhabits the maquis and lower pine zones. Cyprus Wheatear breeds on the open rocky slopes above the treeline. Short-toed Treecreeper, Jay, and Coal Tit are resident in the forests. During winter, migratory thrushes and finches move through in numbers. The Caledonian Forest trails and the Cedar Valley in the Paphos Forest section are particularly good for quiet morning walks in June when resident species are singing.

## 4. Oroklini Lake, Larnaca District

Oroklini is a small but reliable freshwater wetland near Larnaca that attracts waders and wildfowl throughout the year. Unlike the large salt lakes, Oroklini offers fresh and brackish margins that attract species preferring less saline conditions. Little Ringed Plover and Black-winged Stilt breed here. In winter the lake holds Teal, Shoveler, and Pochard. The surrounding scrub regularly produces Cyprus Warbler, Spectacled Warbler, and Cetti's Warbler from the reedbeds. A hide and walkway have been constructed by the BirdLife Cyprus partner organisation, making this an accessible site for visiting birders arriving at Larnaca Airport who want to begin birding immediately.

## 5. Larnaca Salt Lake Complex, Larnaca District

Immediately adjacent to Larnaca International Airport, the Larnaca Salt Lake complex is in some ways even more dramatic than Akrotiri. The juxtaposition of flamingos and taxiing aircraft makes this one of the more visually striking birding experiences in Europe. The Hala Sultan Tekke mosque, set among palm trees on the lake's western shore, provides an atmospheric backdrop. As at Akrotiri, winter flamingo numbers are the headline attraction, but autumn wader passage includes Broad-billed Sandpiper, and both Slender-billed and Audouin's Gulls occur on the fringes. The walking path around the mosque grounds gives good views across the shallows.

## 6. Paralimni Lake, Famagusta District

In the north of the government-controlled area, Paralimni Lake is a seasonal wetland that floods in winter and dries almost completely by midsummer. This natural flooding cycle makes it particularly attractive to waders during the spring drawdown, when muddy margins expose feeding habitat. Greater Spotted Eagle has been recorded here in winter, and Spur-winged Lapwing — an established resident and breeder in Cyprus — is easily seen. The lake is surrounded by orange groves and agricultural land that attracts migrant Rollers, Hoopoes, and Golden Orioles in spring. Stone Curlew is found in the dry fields around the lake perimeter.

## 7. Akamas Peninsula, Paphos District

The Akamas Peninsula in northwestern Cyprus remains one of the least developed and most naturally intact parts of the island. The rough limestone terrain, covered in phrygana and maquis, supports breeding Eleonora's Falcon, Peregrine, and Bonelli's Eagle. Cyprus Warbler is common throughout. The coastal cliffs along the Avakas Gorge hold breeding Griffon Vulture — a population that has been the subject of long-term conservation monitoring. Sea watching from the western tip can produce Scopoli's Shearwater and Yelkouan Shearwater passing offshore in spring. The challenging terrain means visitor pressure remains low, and early morning walks on the Aphrodite Trail reward patience with excellent close views of passerines.

## 8. Polis Wetland and Chrysochou Bay, Paphos District

The wetland at Polis, where the Chrysochou River meets the sea, is an underrated site on the northwestern coast. The combination of freshwater marsh, river mouth, and adjacent scrub creates a diverse microhabitat. Purple Swamphen has been recorded breeding here, and Little Egret and Squacco Heron are regular. In spring, the bay is used by passing Ospreys and the nearby citrus groves attract large numbers of Nightingales. The beach immediately south of Polis town has scrub where Cyprus Warbler and Black-eared Wheatear both occur. Evening visits during April produce Scops Owl calling from the olive groves.

## 9. Athalassa National Park, Nicosia District

Athalassa provides accessible birding for visitors based in Nicosia. The park encompasses a reservoir surrounded by planted woodland and open grassland. Black-headed Buntings breed in the scrubby areas and are easy to find in May and June. Rollers nest in old trees near the reservoir banks. Raptors including Long-legged Buzzard hunt over the open areas. The water attracts Little Grebe, Great Crested Grebe, and Mallard year-round. Autumn migration through Athalassa can be productive for warblers and flycatchers in the woodland strips. A marked walking circuit of around five kilometres covers the main habitat zones.

## 10. Pentadaktylos Range, Northern Cyprus

The Kyrenia Mountain range — known in Greek as Pentadaktylos — running along the northern coast of Cyprus holds significant populations of migratory and resident birds despite political complexities of access. Griffon Vulture is resident on the limestone crags. The forests below the ridge hold many of the same species found on Troodos, including Cyprus Warbler and Short-toed Treecreeper. The proximity to the sea on the northern face creates ideal conditions during spring migration, with birds landing directly from Turkey into the dense scrub. Bellapais and the trails above Kyrenia offer good access points. Coordination with local guides familiar with the area simplifies logistics considerably.

---

Cyprus is a compact island that can be birded thoroughly in a week, combining wetland sites for flamingos and waders, coastal headlands for migration watching, and mountain forests for endemics. The best months are April for spring migration and December through February for wintering waterbirds.

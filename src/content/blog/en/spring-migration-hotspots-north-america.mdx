Spring migration in North America is one of the continent's defining wildlife spectacles. Between April and early June, hundreds of millions of birds move northward from wintering grounds in Central America, South America, the Caribbean, and the southern United States toward breeding territories that stretch from the Gulf Coast to the Arctic. The route concentrates birds through specific geographic bottlenecks — peninsulas, shorelines, and woodland refugia at the edges of major water barriers — creating the celebrated fallout events where coastal woodland fills overnight with colourful warblers, tanagers, and thrushes that have been crossing the Gulf of Mexico.

The sites that produce the most spectacular spring migration watching share geographic features: they sit at natural concentration points on the flyway, they offer the first or last suitable habitat at a water crossing, and they have sufficient vegetation to attract and hold migrants during the day. The ten sites below represent the most consistently productive spring migration destinations across the continent.

## 1. Magee Marsh, Ohio

Magee Marsh Wildlife Area on the Lake Erie shoreline in northwestern Ohio is widely regarded as the single best spring warbler watchpoint in North America. In the second and third weeks of May, the narrow strip of lakeshore woodland between the open water of Lake Erie and the marshes behind it concentrates northbound warblers at densities that are difficult to believe. With the lake presenting a barrier to further northward movement, birds stack up in the lakeside trees and feed actively, allowing observation at extremely close range. More than 35 warbler species have been recorded in a single day during peak movement. The annual Biggest Week in American Birding festival, held in early May, attracts birders from across the world.

## 2. High Island, Texas

High Island is a slightly elevated forested area on the Texas coastal plain, surrounded by open grass and salt marsh, which makes it an irresistible landfall for birds crossing the Gulf of Mexico from the Yucatan. On days following northerly cold fronts in late April, which produce headwinds that force northbound migrants to the ground at the first available cover, the trees at High Island's Boy Scout Woods and Sabine Woods reserves are crammed with exhausted birds. Wood Thrushes, Scarlet Tanagers, Rose-breasted Grosbeaks, and dozens of warbler species can be seen at close range in remarkable concentrations. The Houston Audubon Society manages the sites and provides facilities for visiting birders.

## 3. Point Pelee, Ontario

Point Pelee National Park is a long, narrow peninsula extending south into Lake Erie from southern Ontario — the southernmost point of Canada's mainland — and it acts as a funnel for spring migrants moving northward. Birds reaching the tip of the peninsula before dawn are reluctant to make the final crossing and concentrate in the park's dense second-growth woodland and swamp forest. May is the prime month, with peak warbler diversity typically in the second week. The park's accessibility from Toronto and Detroit makes it one of the most heavily visited birding sites in Canada.

## 4. Hawk Mountain Sanctuary, Pennsylvania

Hawk Mountain in the Kittatinny Ridge of the Pennsylvania Appalachians is the world's first raptor sanctuary, established in 1934 after conservationists succeeded in stopping the mass shooting of migrant raptors at the site. The ridge creates a thermal lift that channels broad-winged hawks, accipiters, falcons, and eagles along a predictable flight line. The south lookout provides views of hawks passing at close range throughout September and October, but Broad-winged Hawk movements in September — when thousands can pass in a single morning — are the signature spectacle. Spring flights are more diffuse but still include excellent Osprey and Golden Eagle movement.

## 5. Cape May, New Jersey

Cape May occupies the southern tip of the New Jersey coastline and concentrates migrants that hesitate before crossing Delaware Bay. In spring it is best known for raptor and songbird passage, with excellent flycatcher, vireo, and warbler diversity in the woodland and scrub of Cape May Point State Park. The hawkwatch platform records Broad-winged Hawks and Bald Eagles in spring, and the ponds and mudflats attract migrant shorebirds. In May, the adjacent Delaware Bay beaches host one of North America's most remarkable concentration events: horseshoe crabs spawning in the shallows while thousands of northbound Red Knots, Semipalmated Sandpipers, and other shorebirds gorge on the eggs before their final push to Arctic breeding grounds.

## 6. Bosque del Apache, New Mexico

The Rio Grande valley floodplain at Bosque del Apache National Wildlife Refuge hosts spectacular concentrations of migrant Sandhill Cranes and Snow Geese in November and continuing through winter, with spring departure in February and March providing the northward migration spectacle. The arrival of the cranes each autumn, when tens of thousands descend onto the agricultural fields, is a celebrated event in American birding.

## 7. Whitefish Point, Michigan

Whitefish Point on the southeastern shore of Lake Superior is famous for hawk migration in April and May and for owl migration in March. The point is the closest land to the western Lake Superior shore before a long overwater crossing, and northbound raptors concentrate here before crossing. Long-eared Owls and Short-eared Owls move through in late March. The hawk season peaks with Broad-winged Hawks in May, but Ospreys, Bald Eagles, and Rough-legged Buzzards add variety.

## 8. Rondeau Provincial Park, Ontario

Rondeau on the north shore of Lake Erie has a climate significantly warmer than surrounding Ontario and a rich woodland and marsh habitat that holds breeding and migrant species uncommon elsewhere in the province. Prothonotary Warbler breeds here at the northern limit of its range. Spring migration brings similar diversity to Point Pelee, with a more accessible woodland trail network.

## 9. Santa Ana National Wildlife Refuge, Texas

The Rio Grande valley at Santa Ana is a critical stopover on the Central Flyway and holds species at the northern edge of their Mexican and Central American ranges. Green Jay, Plain Chachalaca, Altamira Oriole, and Buff-bellied Hummingbird are year-round residents. In spring, wave after wave of northbound migrants pass through, and the combination of resident subtropical species and migrant diversity gives species list totals exceptional by any North American standard.

## 10. Dry Tortugas, Florida

The Dry Tortugas, accessible only by seaplane or ferry from Key West, are a cluster of small islands seventy miles offshore that are the first land encountered by birds crossing from Cuba and the Yucatan. Fort Jefferson, the massive nineteenth-century fortification on Garden Key, serves as the landing point for exhausted migrants, and in May the grounds can hold extraordinary numbers and diversity of warblers and other songbirds that have no other landfall available.

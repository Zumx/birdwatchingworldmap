Parrots are among the most trafficked animals on Earth. The combination of their intelligence, coloration, and ability to mimic human speech has made them desirable as companion animals across cultures and centuries, and the trade in wild-caught parrots has driven multiple species toward extinction while suppressing the populations of hundreds more. The crisis is not historical: despite the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) listing most parrot species under Appendix II, and many of the rarest under Appendix I, illegal poaching and trafficking continues to remove wild birds from habitats at ecologically significant rates.

Understanding the parrot trade crisis requires recognising that it intersects with habitat loss, political instability, poverty, and inadequate law enforcement in ways that make simple solutions impossible. The regions of highest parrot diversity — the Neotropics, sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and the Pacific — are largely tropical developing nations where the economic incentive to poach can be substantial relative to local wages and where enforcement capacity is often limited.

## The Scale of the Trade

Estimates of the illegal parrot trade are difficult to make precisely because accurate data on clandestine commercial activity is inherently incomplete. The best available figures, compiled by TRAFFIC and CITES monitoring bodies, suggest that millions of wild parrots have been extracted from natural populations over the past four decades. Even before CITES protections were strengthened in the 1990s, legal imports of wild-caught parrots into the United States and Europe alone numbered in the hundreds of thousands annually.

The shift toward documented captive-breeding following strengthened CITES controls has been real but incomplete. Captive-bred birds carry paperwork that distinguishes them from wild-caught birds for customs purposes, but fraudulent documentation is widely reported. Wild-caught birds are laundered through operations that nominally appear as captive breeders but depend on wild stock. In Mexico and Central America, surveys of parrot populations in known trapping zones have documented significant annual extraction even in protected areas.

## Species Most Affected

The Spix's Macaw (Cyanopsitta spixii) is the most famous casualty of the parrot trade. Confined to a small area of caatinga scrubland in Bahia, Brazil, this small blue macaw was reduced to a single known wild individual by the late 1990s — the last survivor of a species emptied from the wild primarily by collectors, with habitat loss compounding the impact. That bird disappeared in 2000, and the species is now extinct in the wild. A captive population held at the Loro Parque Foundation in Tenerife and the Al Wabra Wildlife Preservation in Qatar forms the basis of a reintroduction program, and the first birds were returned to the Brazilian caatinga in 2022.

The Lear's Macaw (Anodorhynchus leari) of northeastern Brazil, with a wild population that reached a low of around 60 individuals before intensive protection, has recovered to several hundred birds through a combination of anti-poaching enforcement, community engagement with local landowners, and protection of licuri palm groves that provide its primary food resource.

In the Pacific, the kaka and the kakapo of New Zealand have been affected by habitat loss and introduced predators rather than trade, but other Pacific parrots — including the Ultramarine Lorikeet of the Marquesas and the Blue Lorikeet of Tahiti — are critically endangered partly as a result of capture for captive collections.

## African Grey Parrot

The African Grey Parrot (Psittacus erithacus) is among the most commercially significant species in the trade, prized for its exceptional mimicry ability and cognitive capacity. It was estimated that more than 21 percent of the wild population of African Grey Parrots was extracted annually at the peak of the trade in the 1990s and early 2000s. The species was uplisted to CITES Appendix I in 2016, which bans all commercial trade in wild-caught individuals, but enforcement across Central and West Africa remains patchy.

Population surveys in the Congo Basin, where the species is most abundant, suggest significant ongoing trapping pressure in areas accessible from road and river networks. The subspecies timneh, occurring in West Africa, has a smaller population and is considered more vulnerable.

## Community-Based Conservation

The most successful long-term parrot conservation programs combine enforcement with the creation of economic alternatives for local communities that previously participated in the trade. In the Yucatan Peninsula of Mexico, programs run by Pronatura and the Loro Parque Foundation have trained and supported local community wardens who monitor nest sites, protect nesting cavities from trappers, and receive direct financial benefit from ecotourism that values the living birds in their habitats.

In Bolivia, Armonía runs community parrot conservation programs for the Blue-throated Macaw (Ara glaucogularis), a species confined to a small area of Beni savannah and estimated to number fewer than 500 individuals in the wild. Local landowners who maintain intact palm stands and allow nest monitoring receive support and recognition that creates a direct economic value for the living birds.

## Ecotourism as a Conservation Tool

Parrots and macaws are among the most spectacular attractions at wildlife lodges and reserves in their native ranges. The clay lick at Manu Wildlife Center in Peru, where dozens of macaw species gather to ingest mineral-rich clay, is one of the most photographed wildlife spectacles in the Amazon and generates significant ecotourism revenue for local operations. That revenue creates a constituency for conservation among people who might otherwise view the birds primarily as extractable resources.

Croatia stretches in a long arc from the Pannonian floodplains of the northeast to the karst islands and limestone coast of the Adriatic, and this geography gives it one of the most varied bird faunas in southeastern Europe. Inland, where the Drava, Danube, and Sava rivers meander through ancient floodplain forest, lie some of the largest and least altered wetlands left on the continent, alive with herons, eagles, and breeding waterbirds. On the coast, the bare limestone islands hold Mediterranean specialists, cliff-nesting Griffon Vultures, and seabirds, while the Velebit mountains rising sheer from the sea add an alpine dimension. For a country you can drive across in a day, Croatia offers a startling transition from steppe-edge wetland to Mediterranean cliff.

## Kopacki Rit Nature Park

Where the Drava meets the Danube in the far northeast lies Kopacki Rit, one of the largest and most important intact inland wetlands in Europe. This vast complex of floodplain forest, oxbow lakes, and seasonally flooded marsh holds spectacular breeding colonies of herons, including Great White and Purple Heron, Pygmy Cormorant, and Spoonbill, alongside White-tailed Eagle at one of the highest densities on the continent. Black Stork and Black Kite breed in the forest, and the spring floods draw enormous numbers of waterbirds. Trails and a boardwalk from the visitor centre near Kopacevo give access to the heart of the marsh.

## Lonjsko Polje Nature Park

Along the Sava River south of Zagreb, Lonjsko Polje is a vast retention area of seasonally flooded meadows and ancient oak forest, famous as the village of storks. The settlement of Cigoc is designated a European Stork Village, its wooden houses crowded with White Stork nests each summer. The wet meadows hold breeding Corncrake, Spotted Crake, and waders, while the oak forests support White-tailed Eagle, Black Stork, and woodpeckers. The traditional grazing by Posavina horses and Turopolje pigs maintains the open habitat.

## The Velebit Mountains

The Velebit range runs for 145 kilometres along the Adriatic coast, rising abruptly from sea level to over 1,700 metres, and is protected as a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and two national parks, Paklenica and Northern Velebit. The dramatic limestone gorges of Paklenica hold breeding Golden Eagle, Peregrine, Alpine Swift, and Rock Partridge, while the higher forests support woodpeckers, Ural Owl, and Capercaillie. Wallcreeper haunts the cliffs in winter, and the contrast between Mediterranean slopes and montane summits packs many species into a short ascent.

## Cres and the Griffon Vultures

The island of Cres in the Kvarner Gulf holds one of the last colonies of Griffon Vultures in Croatia, nesting on the sea cliffs of the island's eastern coast. The colony is centred on the Eco-Centre Caput Insulae and the cliffs near Beli, where the birds soar on thermals rising from the sun-warmed limestone. The island's interior of dry grassland and scrub holds Short-toed Eagle, Eastern Subalpine Warbler, and Black-eared Wheatear, while the freshwater Lake Vrana supplies the only drinking water and attracts other birds.

## Vransko Jezero Nature Park

Not to be confused with the lake on Cres, the larger Vransko Jezero near Biograd is the biggest natural lake in Croatia, a shallow, partly brackish water body just behind the coast. Its reedbeds and the protected ornithological reserve at the northwestern end hold breeding Purple Heron, Little Bittern, Bearded Reedling, and one of the few Croatian colonies of Whiskered Tern. The lake is a vital wintering and staging site for ducks, grebes, and coot, and observation points around the reserve give excellent views.

## The Neretva Delta

In the far south, where the Neretva River reaches the Adriatic near Ploce, the delta is the most important wetland on the eastern Adriatic coast. Although much has been drained for agriculture, the remaining lagoons, reedbeds, and wet meadows are a magnet for migrating and wintering waterbirds, including Glossy Ibis, Pygmy Cormorant, and large numbers of duck and waders. It is a key stopover for migrants crossing the Adriatic and holds breeding Collared Pratincole and Black-winged Stilt.

## Krka National Park

The Krka River carves a series of travertine waterfalls and lakes through the Dalmatian karst, and the national park surrounding them combines river, gorge, and Mediterranean scrub. The reedbeds and open water hold breeding and migrating waterbirds, while the surrounding cliffs and maquis support Golden Eagle, Eagle Owl, Blue Rock Thrush, and Eastern Orphean Warbler. The visnovac and Roski slap areas are particularly productive for combining scenery with birding.

## Plitvice Lakes National Park

Croatia's most famous national park, a UNESCO World Heritage site of terraced turquoise lakes and waterfalls, lies in forested karst country. While best known for scenery, the surrounding beech and fir forests hold Ural Owl, Tengmalm's Owl, woodpeckers including White-backed and Three-toed, Capercaillie, and Hazel Grouse. Dipper and Grey Wagtail work the fast streams, and the forest birding away from the busy lakeside trails can be excellent in early morning.

## The Kvarner and Northern Adriatic Islands

Beyond Cres, the cluster of northern Adriatic islands including Krk, Rab, and Pag offers classic Mediterranean island birding. The dry karst grasslands and scrub hold Tawny Pipit, Black-eared Wheatear, Eastern Subalpine Warbler, and Woodchat Shrike, while the salt pans on Pag attract migrating waders. The bare, wind-scoured eastern slopes facing the bora wind contrast sharply with the wooded western sides.

## Mljet National Park

The forested island of Mljet, off the Dalmatian coast near Dubrovnik, protects a national park of dense Aleppo pine and holm oak forest surrounding two saltwater lakes. The lakes and surrounding forest hold breeding Cormorant, Shag, and herons, while the maquis supports Sardinian Warbler, Subalpine Warbler, and Scops Owl, whose monotonous call fills the warm nights. Mljet offers some of the most peaceful Mediterranean woodland birding in the country.

## Explore on the map

Croatia rewards a trip that links its two faces: spring, from April to early June, is the richest, when the inland floodplains throb with breeding herons and storks and the coast fills with returning Mediterranean migrants. A determined birder can stand among the storks of Cigoc and watch Griffon Vultures soar over the Adriatic within a couple of days' drive.

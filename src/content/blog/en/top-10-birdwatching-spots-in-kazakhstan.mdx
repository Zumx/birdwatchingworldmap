Kazakhstan is the ninth-largest country on Earth, and birding here means scale that is hard to grasp until you stand on the open steppe with the horizon unbroken in every direction. The country bridges Europe and Asia, hosting a remarkable mix: Palearctic breeders, Central Asian desert specialists, and the great migration of waterbirds along the West Siberian flyway. Sandgrouse cross the dawn sky over the Kyzylkum, demoiselle cranes gather in their thousands before autumn, and the world's tiny breeding population of relict gulls clings to a handful of saline lakes. Observation points such as the watchpoints at Erketau and Akdym mark the way into landscapes few foreign birders ever reach.

## 1. Korgalzhyn State Nature Reserve

A UNESCO World Heritage Site on the Tengiz-Korgalzhyn lake system southwest of the capital Astana, Korgalzhyn is the northernmost breeding ground of the greater flamingo and the heart of Kazakh wetland birding. The shallow saline and freshwater lakes hold breeding white-headed duck, Dalmatian pelican, black-winged pratincole and sociable lapwing, a critically endangered steppe wader for which Korgalzhyn is a global stronghold. Spring and autumn bring vast passage of geese, cranes and shorebirds.

## 2. Lake Alakol and the Alakol-Sasykkol depression

In the southeast near the Chinese border, the Alakol-Sasykkol lake system is the breeding refuge of the relict gull, one of the rarest gulls on the planet, nesting on a few barren islands. The reed-fringed shores also hold white-headed duck, ferruginous duck and ruddy shelduck, while the surrounding semi-desert supports desert species. The contrast between the open water and the parched plains around it makes for memorable mixed-habitat birding.

## 3. Charyn Canyon and the Almaty foothills

The Charyn Canyon, a deep red-rock gorge east of Almaty, slices through arid steppe near the Tien Shan. Rock-loving species such as blue rock thrush, eastern rock nuthatch and chukar work the cliffs, while the relict Sogdian ash grove along the river bottom adds woodland birds. The drive from Almaty crosses semi-desert good for desert wheatear and the striking white-winged woodpecker, a Central Asian near-endemic.

## 4. Big Almaty Lake and the Tien Shan

Above Almaty, the road climbs to Big Almaty Lake at around 2,500 metres and into the spruce forest and alpine zone of the Tien Shan. This is the place for high-mountain specialties: Himalayan snowcock, brown accentor, white-winged grosbeak, blue whistling thrush and the localised Severtzov's tit-warbler. Lammergeier and Himalayan griffon soar over the ridges, and the higher meadows hold rosefinches and accentors found nowhere lower.

## 5. Aksu-Zhabagly Nature Reserve

Kazakhstan's oldest reserve, Aksu-Zhabagly protects the western spurs of the Tien Shan in the south near Shymkent. Its juniper woodland, alpine meadow and deep Aksu canyon shelter paradise flycatcher, blue-cheeked bee-eater on the approaches, and breeding raptors including golden eagle and booted eagle. The reserve is famous for spring wildflowers, and the elevational range from foothill steppe to high alpine produces a long and varied bird list.

## 6. Kyzylkum and Karakum desert margins

The deserts of southern Kazakhstan are home to a suite of true desert birds rarely seen elsewhere in the Western Palearctic. Pallas's sandgrouse and black-bellied sandgrouse fly to drink at dawn; macqueen's bustard, greater sand plover, desert warbler and Pander's ground jay haunt the saxaul scrub. The saxaul woodlands near the Syr Darya are the most reliable habitat for the secretive Pander's ground jay, a Central Asian endemic.

## 7. Sorbulak Lake system

Northwest of Almaty, the Sorbulak reservoir and its surrounding lakes form one of the most productive waterbird sites in the southeast, especially on migration. The lakes concentrate ducks, grebes, terns and waders, and the reedbeds hold breeding herons and bitterns. Spring and autumn passage can produce huge counts, and the open shores are good for steppe raptors hunting the margins.

## 8. Ustyurt Plateau and Mangystau

The vast Ustyurt Plateau in the far west, between the Caspian and Aral basins, is a stark limestone tableland cut by dramatic cliffs. This remote region supports houbara-type bustards, cream-coloured courser, desert larks and the Caspian plover on its breeding grounds. The Mangystau region's chalk canyons and the shores of the Caspian add a different cast of species, including coastal waterbirds and passage migrants.

## 9. Markakol Lake and the Altai

In the far east, Markakol Lake sits in a mountain basin at the edge of the Altai, surrounded by larch taiga unlike anywhere else in the country. The forest holds Siberian birds at the southern edge of their range: black-throated accentor, Pallas's rosefinch and various owls. The lake itself draws breeding waterfowl, and the surrounding meadows and taiga make this the most boreal-feeling corner of Kazakhstan.

## 10. Erketau and Akdym steppe watchpoints

On the central steppe, dedicated observation points such as the watchpoints at Erketau and Akdym overlook the open grassland that defines the country. This is demoiselle crane and steppe eagle country, where black larks and white-winged larks display over the spring grass and Pallid harriers quarter the plain. In late summer demoiselle cranes gather in pre-migration flocks, and the sheer openness lets a watcher track distant raptors for kilometres.

## Planning a Kazakhstan birding trip

Timing is everything on this scale of country. Late April through June is the prime window: the steppe is green, breeding is in full swing, and the mountain passes are opening. July heat dries the lowlands, while August and September bring the spectacular crane and raptor passage. Winter is harsh across most of the country, though the south and the Caspian shore remain accessible for wintering waterfowl and raptors.

Almaty and Astana are the two main gateways. Almaty gives the fastest access to the Tien Shan, Charyn, Sorbulak and the southern deserts, while Astana is the base for Korgalzhyn and the central steppe watchpoints. Distances are immense and roads vary wildly in quality, so a sturdy vehicle, a driver who knows the tracks, and plenty of time are essential. The Association for the Conservation of Biodiversity of Kazakhstan (ACBK), the national BirdLife partner, is the best source for site access and the latest on sociable lapwing and relict gull conservation.

The Atlantic Forest of Brazil ranks among the most important and most endangered tropical biomes on Earth. Once stretching continuously along 3,000 kilometres of coastline from Rio Grande do Norte to Rio Grande do Sul and extending inland across parts of Paraguay and Argentina, the forest has been reduced to roughly 12 percent of its original extent through centuries of agriculture, logging, and urban expansion. What remains holds extraordinary birdlife. The biome supports more than 1,000 bird species, with over 200 endemic species found nowhere else on the planet. These include some of the world's most spectacular birds: the Seven-colored Tanager, the Brazilian Merganser, and an array of cotingas, antbirds, and hummingbirds found only within its remaining fragments.

For birders, the Atlantic Forest presents both opportunity and urgency. Many endemic species are already restricted to single mountain ranges or protected areas, making targeted site visits essential. The following ten locations represent the best-preserved and most accessible nodes in this fragmented biome, offering consistent results for core Atlantic Forest endemics.

## 1. Serra dos Orgaos National Park, Rio de Janeiro State

The jagged peaks of the Serra dos Orgaos rise above the city of Petropolis and contain one of the best-preserved mid-elevation Atlantic Forest remnants in southeastern Brazil. The park is reached from Teresopolis and holds a remarkable density of endemic species. Black-and-gold Cotinga, Swallow-tailed Cotinga, and Saffron Toucanet are all reliable in the forest at around 1,000 metres. The Barroco trail near the Teresopolis entrance is particularly productive. The mountain climate moderates temperatures, and the undisturbed canopy makes wildlife movement predictable around fruiting trees. The Tijuca area nearby, technically a restored urban forest within greater Rio, adds Black-capped Piprites and White-throated Woodpecker.

## 2. Serra Bonita Private Reserve, Bahia

The Serra Bonita Reserve in the municipality of Camacã in southern Bahia protects a patch of lowland and mid-elevation Atlantic Forest that is one of the most endemic-rich sites in the biome. The reserve was established specifically for conservation and receives visiting birders under a managed access system. Target species include the Banded Cotinga, the rare Stresemann's Bristlefront, and the Bahia Tyrannulet. The reserve also holds Pink-legged Graveteiro and the Slender Antbird, both Bahian endemics with highly restricted ranges. The primary forest on the reserve has been largely intact, providing habitat quality rarely matched elsewhere in the region.

## 3. Intervales State Park, São Paulo State

Intervales is the flagship birding site of the São Paulo segment of the Atlantic Forest, protecting a large block of forest along the Serra de Paranapiacaba escarpment. The park spans from sea-level lowlands to over 1,000 metres, giving access to the full altitudinal range of forest endemic species. White-bearded Antshrike, Robust Woodpecker, and Helmeted Woodpecker are all present. The lodge at the park headquarters allows multi-day stays with guides, and the dawn forest walks regularly produce Brazilian Antthrush, Spotted Bamboowren, and multiple antbird species. The mist-filled valleys at first light are particularly atmospheric, and mixed-species flocks in the canopy reward patience with a succession of endemic tanagers and flycatchers.

## 4. REGUA (Reserva Ecologica de Guapi Assu), Rio de Janeiro State

REGUA sits in the lowland Guapi Assu valley north of Rio and operates as a conservation project combining reforestation with birder accommodation. The reserve offers access to lowland Atlantic Forest, wetland, and former pasture in various stages of restoration. The diversity is exceptional: over 480 species have been recorded here. Target birds include the Pinto's Spinetail, the Brazilian Tanager, and the rare Spotted Bamboowren. The combination of a resident guide team and extensive restored habitat makes REGUA especially productive for visitors who can spend several days. The reed beds around the wetland support the near-threatened Marsh Antwren, and the forest edge has yielded Ochre-bellied Flycatcher and Gilt-edged Tanager.

## 5. Serra da Canastra National Park, Minas Gerais

Canastra is not classic Atlantic Forest — it bridges between the Atlantic Forest and the Cerrado savanna — but its position makes it one of the best sites for species that inhabit both biomes and for a suite of grassland and gallery forest birds. The Giant Anteater and Maned Wolf are visible alongside the birds, and the park supports the near-threatened Brazilian Merganser in its rocky rivers. Cock-tailed Tyrant, Campo Miner, and Cinereous Harrier hunt the elevated grassland. The dramatic Casca D'Anta waterfall is also a productive birding area with Torrent Duck and Rufous-tailed Attila present in the spray zone vegetation.

## 6. Itatiaia National Park, Rio de Janeiro State

Brazil's oldest national park, Itatiaia sits on the border between Rio de Janeiro and Minas Gerais at the highest elevations of the Mantiqueira range. The upper park above 2,000 metres holds open rocky campo vegetation with species found nowhere else: Itatiaia Spinetail, Itatiaia Thistletail, and Grey-winged Cotinga inhabit the high elevation shrubs and rocky slopes. The lower park hotel area has feeders that attract up to a dozen hummingbird species simultaneously, including Scale-throated Hermit and Saw-billed Hermit. The middle elevation forest holds Red-ruffed Fruitcrow and Blue-billed Black-Tyrant. The park's altitude range and intact forest make it one of the most consistently productive sites in the biome.

## 7. Boa Nova National Park, Bahia

Boa Nova protects caatinga scrubland transitioning to semi-deciduous Atlantic Forest in the interior of Bahia, and it is the only reliable location in the world for the Boa Nova Tapaculo. The park was established in 2010 partly in response to the tapaculo's discovery as a new species in this area. Other specialties include the Slender Antbird, the Sincora Antwren, and the Bahia Spinetail. The endemic-rich nature of this transitional zone, between the arid caatinga and the humid forest, creates a species composition found nowhere else. Access requires a guide familiar with the tapaculo and other skulking species of the dense undergrowth.

## 8. Chapada Diamantina National Park, Bahia

The Chapada Diamantina tableland is primarily a landscape of rocky plateau and semi-arid vegetation, but its gallery forests, waterfalls, and moist escarpments support a suite of Atlantic Forest species in an unexpected interior location. The Diamantina Tapaculo is found here, along with the Bahia Warbling-Antbird. The open grasslands support Cinereous Harrier and Crowned Eagle. The park's dramatic scenery makes it a popular hiking destination independently of birdwatching, and the combination of accessible infrastructure with genuine endemics makes it a viable stop on a wider Brazilian birding itinerary.

## 9. Ubatuba Region, São Paulo State

The Ubatuba coastline in northern São Paulo State protects some of the best-remaining lowland Atlantic Forest along the Brazilian coast. The Picinguaba sector of Serra do Mar State Park nearby contains primary lowland forest down to sea level. Red-billed Curassow, now extremely rare, persists here alongside Spot-billed Toucanet and Atlantic Royal Flycatcher. The nearby Fazenda Angelim hosts Harpy Eagle and Black-fronted Piping-Guan. The combination of easily accessible lowland forest with relatively intact community makes this one of the more reliable areas for large forest birds in the entire biome.

## 10. Augusto Ruschi Biological Reserve, Espírito Santo

This reserve in the mountains of Espírito Santo protects mid-elevation forest known especially for its hummingbirds. The reserve has historically been associated with the research of Brazilian naturalist Augusto Ruschi, who documented much of Brazil's hummingbird fauna from here. Hook-billed Hermit, Frilled Coquette, and Black-bellied Thorntail are among the hummingbirds recorded. The surrounding forest also supports a full range of Atlantic Forest endemics including Ochre-marked Parakeet and the locally endemic Kaempfer's Tody-Tyrant. The reserve is managed for conservation and scientific research but accepts visiting ornithologists with prior arrangement.

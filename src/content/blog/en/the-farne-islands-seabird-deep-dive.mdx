The Farne Islands lie a few kilometres off the Northumberland coast in northeast England, scattered between the village of Seahouses and the dark basalt outcrop of Bamburgh Castle on the mainland. There are around fifteen to twenty-eight islands depending on the state of the tide, low stacks and skerries of the Whin Sill dolerite, and together they form one of the most important seabird sites in the British Isles. In late spring the air above them is a churning, screaming mass of birds. Arctic terns dive-bomb visitors' heads on Inner Farne, eiders sit tight on nests beside the boardwalks, and puffins shuttle in and out of burrows utterly unbothered by people. It is one of the rare places where you can stand inside a working seabird colony.

## Getting There from Seahouses

Boats run from the harbour at Seahouses, operated by several long-established local companies, from roughly April to October. Sailings range from non-landing cruises around the outer islands to landing trips on Inner Farne or Staple Island, which are owned and managed by the National Trust. Landings are seasonal and tightly regulated to protect the breeding birds; during the core nesting period only certain islands are open and only at certain times. The crossing is short but the North Sea can be lively, and trips are often cancelled in onshore winds. On Inner Farne you should wear a hat — the Arctic terns nest right beside the path and will strike the highest point of any intruder, drawing blood from the unprepared.

## The Tern City

The Farnes are above all a tern stronghold. Four species breed here: Arctic, common, Sandwich and the scarcer roseate tern. Arctic terns are the most numerous and the most aggressive, and the colony on Inner Farne brings you within touching distance of incubating birds. The Arctic tern is famous for the longest migration of any animal, travelling from these northern breeding grounds to the Antarctic pack ice and back each year — a round trip that, over a lifetime, can total a distance equivalent to several journeys to the Moon. Sandwich terns, larger and with shaggy black crests and yellow-tipped bills, form dense, raucous sub-colonies. The roseate tern, one of Britain's rarest breeding seabirds, occurs in small numbers and benefits from intensive protection.

## Puffins, Guillemots and the Auk Colonies

The Farnes hold a substantial population of Atlantic puffins, with tens of thousands of pairs nesting in burrows on the grassy islands, particularly Staple Island and the Wamses. As at Skomer, the absence of ground predators makes them confiding, and on Staple Island you can watch them at close range against the backdrop of the stacks. Guillemots pack onto the flat tops of the Pinnacles off Staple Island in dense black-and-white ranks, one of the most photographed seabird scenes in England. Razorbills nest in the crevices nearby, and shags — the Farnes are a national stronghold for the species — build seaweed nests on accessible ledges, their oily green plumage and crests unmistakable at close quarters. Kittiwakes occupy the steeper faces.

## Eiders and the Cuthbert Connection

The common eider has a special place in Farnes history. The species is known locally as the cuddy duck, after Saint Cuthbert, the seventh-century monk who lived as a hermit on Inner Farne and is said to have given the eiders protection — making this perhaps the world's first bird protection law, more than thirteen centuries ago. Female eiders nest among the rocks and vegetation, sitting so tight that visitors walk within a metre of them. The drakes, in immaculate black and white with a pistachio nape and a soft cooing call, gather in rafts offshore. Cuthbert's chapel still stands on Inner Farne, and his legacy of stewardship runs through the islands' conservation ethos to this day.

## Grey Seals and Other Wildlife

Beyond the birds, the Farnes hold one of England's largest grey seal colonies, with several thousand pups born on the islands each autumn. In summer the seals haul out on the rocks and bob curiously around the boats, and non-landing cruises pass close to their favoured skerries. The islands' surrounding waters are rich, and porpoises are seen regularly. The rocky shores and rock pools hold their own life, and migrant birds drop in during spring and autumn passage, occasionally bringing scarce visitors blown off course across the North Sea. The combination of breeding seabirds, seals and the dramatic dolerite scenery makes the Farnes a destination that rewards both the dedicated birder and the casual visitor.

## Best Time to Visit and Practicalities

The peak landing season runs from May to late July, when the terns, puffins and guillemots are all present and breeding. By August the auks have largely left for the sea, and the seal season takes over into the autumn. Booking ahead is wise in summer, and you should always check whether landings are running before travelling, as both weather and National Trust restrictions affect access. Waterproof clothing is essential even in fine weather, as the boat trip is exposed and the islands offer little shelter. A hat for the tern colony is genuinely necessary. Binoculars in the 8x42 range and a camera with a moderate zoom serve well — the birds are so close that no extreme telephoto is needed. The nearby mainland coast at Bamburgh and Lindisfarne adds depth to any trip.

## Conservation and the Long View

The National Trust has cared for the Farnes since the 1920s, and the rangers who live on the islands during the season carry out long-term monitoring of every breeding species. This data set is among the most complete for any British seabird colony and is invaluable for tracking the effects of climate change, shifting fish stocks and disease such as the avian influenza outbreaks that struck North Atlantic seabirds severely in recent years. Visitors who follow the rules — keeping to the paths, not disturbing nesting birds, and respecting the seasonal closures — directly support that work. The Farnes show what a protected colony can be: not a remote, untouchable wilderness, but a thriving seabird city you can walk through.

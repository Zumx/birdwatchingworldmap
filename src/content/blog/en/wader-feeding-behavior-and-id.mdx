A flock of small waders scatters across a tidal flat at three hundred metres, heat-haze shimmering off the mud, the light flat and grey. The plumage details that field guides emphasise — wing-bars, supercilia, leg colour — are simply not resolvable at that range, even through a good scope. Yet an experienced shorebirder names most of the birds within a minute. They are not reading feathers. They are reading behaviour: the tempo of the walk, the angle of the bill as it strikes the mud, the way a bird stitches across the flat versus the way another stands and waits. Feeding action is one of the most powerful and most neglected identification tools in birding, and on distant shorebirds it is often the only one that works.

## Why behaviour beats plumage at range

Wader plumage is a minefield. Most species moult between distinct breeding and non-breeding plumages, and many show a confusing range of juvenile and transitional states across autumn passage. Two birds of the same species can look strikingly different in August, while two different species can converge on near-identical grey-and-white winter dress. Distance compounds the problem: the subtleties that separate, say, a Dunlin from a Curlew Sandpiper in worn winter plumage vanish at any real range.

Behaviour, by contrast, is conservative. A species' feeding method is shaped by its bill morphology, leg length and ecological niche, and it changes far less than plumage does across age and season. A Sanderling runs like a Sanderling whether it is breeding-rufous in May or pale-grey in November. Once you learn to read the action, you carry an identification cue that survives bad light, long range and moult chaos — exactly the conditions under which plumage fails.

## The vocabulary of feeding action

Start by naming what you see. Waders feed by a small number of recognisable techniques, and learning the terms sharpens the observation. **Probing** is driving the bill deep into soft substrate to reach buried prey — the signature of long-billed species such as godwits, curlews and snipe. **Stitching** or sewing-machine feeding is rapid, rhythmic vertical probing in one spot, the classic action of Dunlin and dowitchers. **Pecking** or picking is taking visible prey from the surface with a downward jab, used by plovers and many smaller calidrids on firm ground.

Then there is **scything**, the side-to-side sweep of an Avocet's upturned bill through shallow water; **foot-trembling**, where a plover vibrates one foot on the mud to startle prey into moving; and **running-and-pausing**, the stop-start hunting of plovers that scan visually rather than probe by touch. Each action maps onto a body plan. Tactile feeders with long bills probe and stitch; visual feeders with short bills and large eyes run and peck. Naming the action is the first step to naming the bird.

## Plovers versus sandpipers: the fundamental split

The most useful single distinction in shorebird behaviour is between the run-stop-peck plovers and the steadily-probing sandpipers. Plovers — Ringed, Grey, Golden, Kentish — feed visually. They sprint a short distance, freeze, tilt forward, and snatch a single item, then run again. The motion is jerky and punctuated, with long stationary pauses while the bird scans. Their large eyes and short bills are built for this surface hunting.

Sandpipers and calidrids work the substrate continuously. A feeding Dunlin head-down stitches its way across the mud almost without pause, the body moving in a steady glide while the bill works like a needle. A Common Redshank walks deliberately and probes at intervals; a Bar-tailed Godwit wades belly-deep and drives its long bill down to the hilt. The contrast in tempo — punctuated sprints versus continuous shuffling — is often visible at a range where no plumage detail is, and it instantly splits the flock into two camps.

## Calibrating gait, tempo and posture

Within the sandpipers, finer behavioural distinctions separate confusing species. A Sanderling on a sandy beach is unmistakable in action: it runs at high speed along the wave-edge, chasing the retreating swash and fleeing the next wave like a clockwork toy, a tempo no other small wader matches. A Little Stint feeds with quick, busy, pecking movements close to the waterline, often crouched and hunched; a Temminck's Stint, by contrast, creeps mouse-like with a more horizontal, deliberate carriage and tends to slink rather than scurry.

Posture is part of the picture too. Greenshanks feed actively, sometimes running through shallows and even chasing fish, an animation quite unlike the more sedate Spotted Redshank. A Wood Sandpiper picks delicately and elegantly at the surface; a Green Sandpiper bobs nervously and flushes with a towering, snipe-like escape. Even the rear-end bobbing of a Common Sandpiper — its constant tail-pumping teeter as it works a stony shore — is diagnostic from a distance no plumage could bridge.

## Putting it together at a real site

Imagine a working session on an estuary such as the Wash in eastern England, Le Hâble d'Ault on the Baie de Somme, or the vast flats of the Wadden Sea. The tide is dropping and thousands of waders spread out to feed. Rather than fixing on individual birds, read the flock as a set of behaviours. The dense carpet of small birds stitching head-down across the near mud, moving as one — Dunlin. The slightly larger birds running and freezing among them, upright and clean — Grey Plover. The tall, elegant waders striding through the channels and sweeping their long bills — godwits or Greenshank. The frantic sprinter on the sand at the water's edge — Sanderling.

This behavioural triage gets you most of the flock before you check a single feather. Then, and only then, do you bring the scope onto candidates that the action could not separate — the Curlew Sandpiper hidden among Dunlin, the Little Stint among Sanderling — and use structure and plumage to finish the job. Behaviour narrows the field from dozens of possibilities to two or three; plumage clinches the rest.

## Training your eye for movement

Reading behaviour is a learned skill, and it rewards deliberate practice. When you have a confidently identified bird in the scope, do not move on immediately — watch it feed for a full minute and consciously register the tempo, the bill angle, the stride length, the pauses. Build a mental library of how each species moves, the way a musician learns rhythms. Video is a powerful aid: many phone-scoped clips on eBird and the Macaulay Library capture feeding action, and watching them in slow motion fixes the patterns.

Pay attention to how the same species changes its action with substrate and conditions. A Dunlin stitches on soft mud but pecks more on firm sand; godwits probe deep in soup-like mud but pick at the surface on harder ground. The action is a response to where the prey is, so it shifts with the habitat. Understanding that flexibility keeps you from over-reading a single behaviour, and turns feeding action from a party trick into a reliable, repeatable identification system you can use anywhere shorebirds gather.

Drive two hours east from Agra and the dry Rajasthan plain suddenly dissolves into a maze of shallow lagoons, half-submerged acacia, and reed-fringed dykes alive with the clatter of wings. This is Keoladeo National Park, the wetland the world still calls Bharatpur after the princely town on its doorstep. It is one of the strangest of all great bird sites because it is almost entirely artificial: a flood-control basin sculpted in the late nineteenth century that became, by accident, one of the densest concentrations of wintering and breeding waterbirds in Asia. On a December morning the sheer biomass is staggering — thousands of ducks lift in waves, painted storks bicker on their nest platforms, and a kingfisher streaks blue across the bund.

## A Wetland Built by Maharajas

Keoladeo owes its existence to the Maharaja of Bharatpur, who in the 1850s diverted water from the Gambhir and Banganga rivers via the Ajan Bund to create a hunting marsh for waterfowl shoots. For decades the reserve was a slaughterhouse for visiting dignitaries, and a stone plaque near the entrance still records a single day's bag in 1938 that ran into the thousands. After Indian independence the shooting wound down, the area was declared a sanctuary in 1956, gained national park status in 1982, and was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985. The same engineered hydrology that once served the guns now sustains the birds, and managing the inflow of monsoon water through the sluices remains the single most important conservation task in the park.

## The Great Heronry

The defining spectacle at Keoladeo is its monsoon heronry. When the rains fill the basin between July and September, the flooded acacia and babool trees erupt into a breeding colony of extraordinary density. Painted storks build untidy stick platforms by the hundred, jostling alongside Asian openbills, black-headed ibis, Eurasian spoonbills, little, intermediate and great cormorants, grey herons, purple herons, and several egret species. Darters dry their wings on dead snags between dives. The noise, smell, and constant traffic of birds ferrying frogs and fish to gaping chicks make this one of the most accessible large heronries anywhere in the subcontinent, all visible from the central causeway without leaving the path.

## Winter and the Wintering Wildfowl

From October the character of the park changes as Palearctic migrants pour in. Northern pintail, common teal, gadwall, northern shoveler, common pochard, tufted duck, and red-crested pochard gather on the open water in enormous rafts, joined by greylag and bar-headed geese. The bar-headed goose is itself a marvel, a species that crosses the Himalaya at extreme altitude to reach wintering grounds like this one. Bharatpur was historically the most reliable site in the world for the critically endangered Siberian crane, whose central Asian flock wintered here until the last confirmed birds appeared in 2002. Their loss is a sober reminder that even a protected wetland depends on an entire flyway remaining intact.

## Raptors, Cranes and the Resident Cast

Keoladeo is not only about waterfowl. The drier woodland and grassland fringes hold sarus cranes, the world's tallest flying bird, often seen in pairs performing their bugling duets near the Ajan Bund. Raptors are abundant in winter: greater spotted eagle, imperial eagle, marsh harrier, and the occasional Pallas's fish eagle hunt over the marsh, while shikra, Indian spotted eagle, and a roosting concentration of dusky eagle-owls add to the list. Nightjars, painted francolin, and Indian courser occupy the scrub, and the park's mammals — spotted deer, nilgai, golden jackal, and a healthy population of rock python basking in winter sun near Python Point — make every walk a broader safari.

## Birding the Park on Foot and by Rickshaw

One of Keoladeo's great pleasures is that no motor vehicles are allowed beyond the entrance. The 29-square-kilometre park is explored along a single metalled spine and a web of raised earthen bunds, on foot, by bicycle, or aboard the cycle-rickshaws whose drivers double as sharp-eyed guides — many have ringed-bird-level knowledge accumulated over decades. Key vantage points carry names every visiting birder learns: the central watchtower for a panorama over the heronry, the Salim Ali viewpoint honouring the ornithologist whose advocacy helped save the park, Python Point, and the temple, or Mandir, near the heart of the wetland where the path crosses open water rich in herons and jacanas. A boat can sometimes be hired at the central lake when levels are high.

## When to Go and How to Plan

The prime window runs from November to early March, when wintering numbers peak and the weather is cool and dry; mornings can be genuinely cold, so a fleece is wise even in Rajasthan. The breeding heronry is best from August to October, though paths may be wet and some birders prefer the spectacle once water has receded a little. Avoid the searing pre-monsoon heat of May and June, when much of the basin can dry out entirely if the rains and the Ajan Bund releases have been poor. Bharatpur is easily reached by train or road from Delhi, Agra, and Jaipur, sitting almost exactly within the Golden Triangle, which makes a two- or three-night birding stop simple to combine with the region's monuments.

## Conservation Pressures Worth Knowing

The park's man-made nature is also its vulnerability. Keoladeo depends on an assured supply of monsoon water, and disputes over irrigation allocation from upstream dams have in some years left the basin dangerously dry, with knock-on collapses in breeding. Invasive water hyacinth and the spread of the exotic shrub Prosopis juliflora demand constant management, and the loss of the Siberian crane underscores how a single wetland cannot compensate for pressures along thousands of kilometres of flyway. Recent years have seen more reliable water management and renewed pumping when monsoons fail, and the heronry has bounced back strongly, but Bharatpur remains a vivid lesson in how thoroughly a great natural spectacle can rest on human decisions.

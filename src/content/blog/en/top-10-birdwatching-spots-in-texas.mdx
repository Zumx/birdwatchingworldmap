Texas holds a unique position in North American ornithology. With more than 650 recorded species, it supports more bird diversity than any other state in the continental United States. That breadth reflects the state's geography: the Gulf Coast migration corridor funnels millions of neotropical migrants northward each spring, the Trans-Pecos desert supports arid-adapted western species, and the fertile brushlands of the Rio Grande Valley shelter Mexican species found nowhere else in the country. For birders, Texas is not merely a destination but an entire birding region unto itself.

Planning a Texas birding trip requires some sense of season. Spring migration along the coast peaks between late March and mid-May, when exhausted warblers, vireos, and tanagers make landfall after crossing the Gulf of Mexico. Winter brings irruptive sparrows and raptors to the interior grasslands. Year-round residents in the Valley include species that require special trips elsewhere in their range. This list covers ten sites that consistently produce outstanding results across seasons.

## 1. High Island, Bolivar Peninsula, Upper Texas Coast

High Island is a slight topographic rise — barely 38 feet above sea level — that acts as a magnet for migrants crossing the Gulf. When weather systems stall spring migrants over the water, birds land exhausted in their thousands in the oak mottes maintained by the Houston Audubon Society. Smith Oaks sanctuary is the premier location, with a heronry visible from a viewing platform where Roseate Spoonbills, Great Blue Herons, and Tricolored Herons nest at eye level. On a good fallout day in April, a single morning can produce 20 or more warbler species. The Boy Scout Woods sanctuary next door offers a water drip that attracts painted buntings, rose-breasted grosbeaks, and scarlet tanagers simultaneously.

## 2. Santa Ana National Wildlife Refuge, Hidalgo County

Santa Ana is often called the jewel of the U.S. wildlife refuge system, covering roughly 2,000 acres of native Tamaulipan thornbrush along the Rio Grande. The refuge supports more than 400 recorded species, including year-round residents like Altamira Oriole, Plain Chachalaca, Green Jay, and Buff-bellied Hummingbird. The woodland interior harbors Hook-billed Kite and Gray Hawk. In winter, look for Northern Beardless-Tyrannulet and Clay-colored Thrush near the refuge headquarters feeding stations. Tram tours operate in season, but the walking trails through the resaca wetlands and dense scrub are where most specialties are found. The combination of subtropical climate and intact native vegetation makes this refuge irreplaceable.

## 3. South Padre Island Birding and Nature Center

South Padre Island is the easternmost point of Texas where Gulf-crossing migrants first make landfall on the barrier island chain. The Convention Centre's Birding and Nature Center maintains boardwalks over coastal marsh and a butterfly garden that also attracts migrants. The beach jetties are productive for shorebirds during southbound migration in July and August, with species like Stilt Sandpiper and Buff-breasted Sandpiper moving through. The flats adjacent to the Queen Isabella Causeway support large numbers of wintering shorebirds including American Avocet, Long-billed Curlew, and Marbled Godwit. Pelagic species including Magnificent Frigatebird are occasionally visible from the beach during storms.

## 4. Laguna Atascosa National Wildlife Refuge, Cameron County

Laguna Atascosa is the largest protected natural area in the lower Rio Grande Valley, encompassing coastal prairie, thorn scrub, and wetlands along Laguna Madre. The Bayside Drive auto tour produces spectacular concentrations of wintering waterfowl, including Redhead ducks in the tens of thousands alongside American Wigeons and Northern Pintails. Aplomado Falcon, once extirpated from the United States, has been reintroduced here and is now reliably seen hunting over the coastal grasslands. Rarities from Mexico appear regularly, and Ferruginous Pygmy-Owl is resident in the brushy areas. The combination of habitats in one contiguous protected area makes this refuge especially productive for a full day's birding.

## 5. Falcon State Park and Falcon Dam, Starr County

The Falcon Reservoir area on the Rio Grande is renowned for producing Mexican rarities in winter. Greater Pewee, Tropical Parula, and Northern Beardless-Tyrannulet occur in the scrub along the lakeshore, while the dam and tailrace attract unusual gulls and terns. White-collared Seedeater, a tiny tanager-relative, can be found in tall grasses near the water. Ferruginous Pygmy-Owl is present year-round in the mesquite woodland. The campground area at the state park provides good habitat access, and early mornings in the dense brush beside the Rio Grande can produce Ringed Kingfisher, Green Kingfisher, and the occasional Muscovy Duck on the water.

## 6. Big Bend National Park, Brewster County

Big Bend occupies a remote bend of the Rio Grande in the Chihuahuan Desert, and the mix of desert, riparian cottonwood gallery forest, and Chisos Mountains oak-juniper woodland supports a bird community unlike anywhere else in Texas. Colima Warbler, which breeds nowhere else in the United States, nests in the Boot Canyon area of the Chisos Mountains above 6,000 feet. The Rio Grande Village campground cottonwoods are famous for vagrant warblers in fall. Zone-tailed Hawk, Lucifer Hummingbird, and Black-capped Vireo all occur regularly. The park's remote character and low light pollution make dawn and dusk especially productive for owls: Elf Owl, Whiskered Screech-Owl, and Western Screech-Owl are all present.

## 7. Davis Mountains State Park, Jeff Davis County

The Davis Mountains form a sky island of pine-oak woodland in the Trans-Pecos, separated from the Sierra Madre by the intervening desert. Montezuma Quail is the target species for many visitors, best found on the grassy slopes above Limpia Creek at dawn. The forest holds Acorn Woodpecker, Steller's Jay, and Hutton's Vireo, species typical of the Arizona mountains but largely absent from the rest of Texas. Scaled Quail and Crissal Thrasher inhabit the drier lower slopes. The Skyline Drive road within the state park traverses multiple habitat zones efficiently. Adjoining Fort Davis National Historic Site adds additional habitat and sometimes produces Varied Bunting along the brushy drainages.

## 8. Bentsen-Rio Grande Valley State Park, Hidalgo County

Bentsen is the World Birding Center flagship site, offering some of the best accessible thornbrush birding in the valley. The hawk watch tower in the park sees impressive kettles of Broad-winged and Swainson's hawks during September migration. Year-round, the park supports Groove-billed Ani, Long-billed Thrasher, and Verdin. The resaca (oxbow lake) system provides freshwater habitat for wintering ducks and resident herons including Yellow-crowned Night-Heron. White-tipped Dove and Red-billed Pigeon are regular. The park's morning tram tours are designed around accessible wildlife viewing, but the established trails through the old-growth native brush produce consistent results for self-guided birders.

## 9. Attwater Prairie Chicken National Wildlife Refuge, Colorado County

The Attwater's Prairie-Chicken is one of North America's most critically endangered birds, reduced from millions of individuals to a population maintained only through intensive management and captive breeding. This refuge on the coastal prairie is one of the few places where released birds survive and lek in spring, with males displaying in the dawn hours of March and April. The surrounding coastal prairie also supports Sprague's Pipit and Le Conte's Sparrow in winter, with Sedge Wren in the wetland margins. Access to the lek viewing area during the display season is managed, but the refuge also supports a rewarding mix of grassland and wetland species accessible on the auto tour road year-round.

## 10. Muleshoe National Wildlife Refuge, Bailey County

In the Texas Panhandle, Muleshoe is the oldest national wildlife refuge in the state and hosts one of North America's largest wintering concentrations of Sandhill Cranes. From November through February, tens of thousands of Lesser and Greater Sandhill Cranes roost on the playa lakes, with their calls audible for miles at dawn. The surrounding grasslands support Mountain Bluebird, Ferruginous Hawk, and Prairie Falcon in winter. Snow Geese and Canada Geese are present in large numbers alongside the cranes. The spectacle of cranes rising from the playas at first light against a Panhandle sky is one of the most memorable sights in American birding.

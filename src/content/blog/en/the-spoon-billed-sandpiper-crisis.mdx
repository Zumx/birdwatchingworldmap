In the summer of 2010, ornithologists counting Spoon-billed Sandpipers at the breeding grounds on the Chukchi Peninsula of Russia came back with numbers that forced an emergency reassessment. The global breeding population — which a decade earlier had been estimated at several thousand pairs — appeared to have collapsed to fewer than 400 pairs, possibly far fewer. The decline, driven by a combination of factors along one of the world's longest migratory routes, was accelerating at a rate that placed the species years, not decades, from functional extinction.

Calidris pygmaea breeds only on the coastal tundra of the extreme northeast of Russia, winters in the intertidal mudflats of Bangladesh, Myanmar, and southern China, and travels between the two along a route that crosses the Yellow Sea — one of the most heavily developed and ecologically degraded coastal regions on Earth. Every component of this life history creates vulnerability, and every vulnerability has been exploited simultaneously.

## The Spoon-Shaped Bill

The spatula-shaped bill that gives this small sandpiper its name is its most immediately distinguishing feature. The spoon — a flattened, spatulate tip approximately the same width as the bird's head — is unique among waders and serves to detect invertebrate prey through touch as the bird sweeps its bill through wet sediment, shallow water, and algal mats. Spoon-billed Sandpipers feed in a characteristic side-to-side sweeping motion, exploiting the mechanoreceptors densely concentrated in the bill tip.

The bill shape makes the bird unmistakable in the field, but identification requires views of the face — the small size (roughly the size of a Little Stint) and brownish-reddish breeding plumage can be overlooked at distance in mixed wader flocks. The combination of the spatulate bill, the rufous head and breast in breeding plumage, and the characteristic feeding action distinguishes it from all other small waders. In winter plumage, the whitish underparts and grey upperparts are less distinctive, but the bill remains diagnostic.

## The East Asian-Australasian Flyway

The Spoon-billed Sandpiper's migration route traverses the East Asian-Australasian Flyway — the passage corridor used by millions of shorebirds traveling between Arctic breeding grounds and non-breeding areas in Southeast Asia, Australia, and New Zealand. The flyway depends on a series of staging sites along the East Asian coast, particularly the intertidal mudflats of the Yellow Sea, where birds stop to refuel on invertebrate-rich sediment before or after the longest overwater legs of their journey.

These staging sites are under severe pressure. The Yellow Sea's intertidal zone has lost an extraordinary proportion of its area to reclamation — the conversion of coastal mudflats to industrial land, port facilities, and agricultural land through sea walls and land-filling operations. South Korea has reclaimed an estimated 60 percent of its tidal flat area since the 1970s. China's Jiangsu province coast has been dramatically altered by reclamation. These are precisely the sites that Spoon-billed Sandpipers, and millions of other waders, depend on for survival during migration.

## Breeding and Nesting

On the Chukchi tundra, the breeding season is brutally short. Spoon-billed Sandpipers arrive in late May or early June and must complete courtship, nesting, and chick-rearing before the brief Arctic summer ends. The nest is a grass-lined scrape concealed in tundra vegetation; four eggs are incubated by both parents for about 21 days. The male takes primary responsibility for incubation and chick care — the female departs before the chicks fledge. The chicks are precocial, leaving the nest within hours of hatching and feeding themselves from the start.

Breeding success is variable and affected by weather, lemming cycles (which influence predator pressure), and, increasingly, climate change effects on tundra phenology. Low breeding success in individual years may be tolerable in a species with adequate adult survival, but when survival is being depleted by trapping in wintering areas and stopover loss through habitat destruction, even good breeding seasons cannot compensate.

## The Wintering Ground Problem

Myanmar's Irrawaddy delta, the coast of Bangladesh, and the Guangdong coast of China provide the non-breeding habitat for the majority of the population. Bird trapping along the Myanmar coast — using nets and sticky traps targeting small birds for food and trade — has been documented as a significant mortality source. The coastal mudflats of Bangladesh are subject to shrimp farming expansion that reduces available intertidal feeding habitat.

BirdLife International and partner organizations have worked with local communities along the Myanmar coast to promote alternative livelihoods that reduce dependence on bird trapping. The involvement of local village communities in Spoon-billed Sandpiper monitoring has created local stakeholders in the species' survival.

## Conservation Responses

The Wildfowl and Wetlands Trust (WWT) launched a headstarting program — collecting eggs from the wild, incubating them in captivity in Russia, and rearing the chicks before release — to boost recruitment. The program has produced hundreds of birds over more than a decade and demonstrated the technical feasibility of increasing fledgling numbers, though the critical bottleneck of adult survival along the flyway means that headstarting alone cannot reverse decline.

Protection of specific Yellow Sea mudflat sites has been the other major conservation priority. The designation of the Yellow Sea-Bohai Gulf of China Migratory Bird Habitats as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2019 represented a significant step, formally recognizing the international importance of China's remaining intertidal habitats. Continued expansion of World Heritage protection to key South Korean and additional Chinese sites is essential for the flyway's viability.

The Spoon-billed Sandpiper is a test case for international flyway conservation: whether its trajectory can be reversed depends on coordinated action across Russia, China, South Korea, Japan, Myanmar, Bangladesh, and multiple other countries simultaneously. The technical knowledge exists; what is required is the political will and the sustained funding to act on it.

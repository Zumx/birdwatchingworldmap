Namibia is one of Africa's premier birding destinations, combining a diverse bird list of over 670 species with spectacular desert scenery and a strong ecotourism infrastructure. The country occupies a particularly interesting biogeographical position, holding species from the Namib-Karoo zone, the southern African savannah, the Angolan-Zambian woodland, and the Kalahari Desert. Several species are endemic or near-endemic to Namibia, including the Dune Lark — found only in the Namib Sand Sea — and Namibia holds key populations of globally threatened coastal seabirds including African Penguin and Bank Cormorant.

Namibia's accessibility is exceptional for an African country. Good roads, well-managed parks, and a network of private game reserves and lodges — many of which specifically market to birding tourists — make self-drive birding feasible for well-prepared visitors. The summer rainfall season from November to April brings migrants from the northern hemisphere and European breeding visitors, and this is also when resident species are most vocal and active. The winter months provide better conditions for desert birding and coastal seabird watching.

## 1. Etosha National Park, Oshikoto and Kunene Provinces

Etosha's vast salt pan and surrounding savannah are the centrepiece of Namibian wildlife tourism and hold an outstanding bird community. The pan itself — a blinding white salt flat covering over 4,700 square kilometres — floods partially during good rainy seasons, drawing enormous concentrations of Lesser and Greater Flamingo. Chestnut-banded Plover breeds on the pan margins, a range-restricted species of Namibian salt pans. The waterholes around the pan, particularly at Okaukuejo and Halali, are active around the clock and produce a constant procession of birds at close range. Kori Bustard — the world's heaviest flying bird — stalks through the sparse grass. Secretary Bird and Martial Eagle are regularly observed from the park roads.

## 2. Namib Sand Sea and Sossusvlei, Hardap Region

The Namib Sand Sea is the oldest desert on Earth and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Dune Lark — a small, pale, nondescript but highly localised lark found only in the Namib — inhabits the sparse grass at the base of the dunes. Gray's Lark is found on the gravel plains. Tractrac Chat and Namibian Scrub Robin are resident in the scrubby areas around Sesriem. The dry riverbed of the Tsauchab, flooded episodically, is lined with trees that hold Rosy-faced Lovebird, Mountain Kaokochat, and migrant warblers during summer. Early morning walks on the dunes before the heat builds produce the best conditions for finding all the sand sea specialities.

## 3. Walvis Bay Lagoon and Pelican Point

The sheltered lagoon at Walvis Bay is one of the most important coastal waterbird sites in southern Africa. Greater Flamingo and Lesser Flamingo gather here in flocks that can exceed 150,000 birds — one of the largest flamingo concentrations on the continent. The tidal flats attract enormous numbers of Palearctic-breeding shorebirds during the October to March non-breeding season: Curlew Sandpiper, Sanderling, Grey Plover, and Bar-tailed Godwit are all present in thousands. The nearby Pelican Point — a sand spit extending into the bay — hosts a resident Cape Fur Seal colony alongside African Penguin, Bank Cormorant, Cape Cormorant, and Cape Gannet. Boat trips from Walvis Bay give exceptional close views of marine wildlife and seabirds.

## 4. Caprivi Strip (Zambezi Region), Zambezi Province

The Caprivi Strip — renamed Zambezi Region — is a narrow corridor of northeastern Namibia sandwiched between Angola, Botswana, and Zambia. Its position gives it access to the Zambezi and Chobe River systems and the associated miombo and mopane woodland. The region holds a dramatically different bird community from the rest of Namibia, with over 400 species recorded, including numerous Afro-tropical woodland species absent from drier Namibia. African Skimmer nests on Zambezi sandbanks. Chirping Cisticola inhabits the floodplain grasslands. Pel's Fishing Owl haunts the larger riverine trees. Lilian's Lovebird inhabits Mopane woodland. Bwabwata National Park in the eastern Caprivi provides the best access.

## 5. Swakopmund and the Namib Coast

The coastal town of Swakopmund sits at the meeting point of the Namib Desert and the cold Benguela-current Atlantic, and the surrounding area holds species found at few other sites in Africa. Damara Tern — a small, globally vulnerable tern endemic to the Namibian coast — breeds on the gravel plains inland from the coast and forages in the near-shore zone. Hartlaub's Gull is the characteristic gull of the Namibian coast. Dune Lark is findable in the sand sea within a short drive of town. The Swakop River flood plain holds Carp's Tit — endemic to the central Namib drainage — and Cinnamonbreasted Warbler. The coastal road north and south of town is productive for coastal seabirds and shorebirds.

## 6. Waterberg Plateau Park, Otjozondjupa Province

The Waterberg Plateau rises abruptly from the surrounding Kalahari sandveld in central Namibia, its forested summit plateau holding species not found in the surrounding lowlands. White-backed Vulture and Cape Vulture both breed on the cliff faces and are easily observed from the plateau rim. Brown-necked Parrot inhabits the taller woodland. The Waterberg's enclosed nature — the plateau acts as a natural enclosure for relocated rhino and other large mammals — means it is intensively managed for wildlife, and the combination of mammals and birds makes it one of Namibia's most rewarding single-site destinations. Birding is done by guided walks on the summit plateau.

## 7. Brandberg Mountain, Erongo Region

The Brandberg, Namibia's highest mountain at 2,573 metres, rises dramatically from the surrounding desert plain. The mountain's varied altitudinal zones hold species not found in the surrounding lowlands. Herero Chat — a range-restricted species of rocky hillsides in Namibia and Angola — is found on the lower rocky slopes. Ruppell's Korhaan is seen on the stony plains around the mountain base. Cinnamonbreasted Warbler inhabits the dry rocky kloofs. The Tsisab Gorge approach to the White Lady rock painting passes through excellent bird habitat. The mountain is best birded in the early morning before heat builds, and the view across the desert from the upper slopes is remarkable.

## 8. Khaudum National Park, Otjozondjupa Province

Khaudum in the northeast Kalahari sandveld is one of Namibia's wilder and least-visited parks, protecting deep Kalahari bushveld and open savannah. The tree-lined dry riverbeds called omatandos cut through the deep Kalahari sand and hold the most productive birding corridors. Southern Ground Hornbill walks through the park in small family groups. African Wild Dog has been reintroduced. The woodland holds Racket-tailed Roller, African Barred Owlet, and Sharp-tailed Starling — species more characteristic of the Zambezi woodland but reaching into northern Namibia here. The park requires a high-clearance four-wheel drive vehicle and carries low visitor numbers even by Namibian standards.

## 9. Fish River Canyon Area, Karas Province

The Fish River Canyon in southern Namibia is Africa's largest canyon and is surrounded by the arid succulent Karoo — an extreme landscape of dry rocky hills and sparse shrubby vegetation. The bird community of the Karoo is distinct from the rest of Namibia, with Mountain Wheatear, Pale-winged Starling, and Verreaux's Eagle all characteristic species. Lanner Falcon breeds on the canyon walls. The cliff faces hold nesting Ruppell's Griffon Vulture. The canyon area is best birded in the early morning when temperatures allow comfortable walking on the rim trails. The nearby Ai-Ais hot spring resort provides accommodation.

## 10. Mahango Game Reserve and Popa Falls, Zambezi Province

Mahango, west of Bwabwata in the Caprivi, protects a productive section of the Okavango floodplain where the river is broad, shallow, and tree-fringed. The combination of water, riverine forest, and open floodplain grassland produces exceptional waterbird diversity. African Pygmy Goose — a small, richly coloured duck that nests in tree cavities — is conspicuous in the water lily-filled backwaters. White-backed Night Heron roosts in the riverine trees. The Popa Falls area just downstream is a rocky rapid section of the Okavango where Malachite Kingfisher and Half-collared Kingfisher both occur. Black-collared Barbet and Broad-billed Roller are found in the woodland. The site combines easily with cross-border trips into the Caprivi floodplain.

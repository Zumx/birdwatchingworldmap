Mexico is one of the ornithologically richest countries in the world, with over 1,100 bird species and more than 100 endemic species restricted to Mexican territory. Its biogeographic position at the junction of the Nearctic and Neotropical regions, its extraordinary elevational and habitat range — from the Sonoran Desert to the cloud forests of Chiapas and the tropical lowlands of the Yucatán — and its extensive coastline on both the Pacific and Gulf of Mexico combine to produce a bird list that challenges many countries with far larger land areas. The birding infrastructure is growing rapidly; eBird coverage has expanded substantially in the last decade.

## 1. San Blas, Nayarit

San Blas is Mexico's most celebrated accessible birding site and the location that Roger Tory Peterson and James Fisher visited on their 1953 "Wild America" tour. The mangrove estuaries, lagoons, and tropical dry forest produce a species list of over 300 in a single productive weekend. Boat trips through the mangrove channels at dawn produce Military Macaw (Ara militaris, Vulnerable), Rufescent Tiger Heron (Tigrisoma lineatum), and Mangrove Cuckoo (Coccyzus minor). The Vida Natural birding area (documented in the map database) covers the San Blas estuary zone.

## 2. Veracruz, Veracruz

The Gulf coast plain at Veracruz is the most important land corridor for autumn raptor migration in the Americas. In October, counts at the Cardel hawkwatch have recorded over 5 million raptors in a single autumn season — the largest migration flight count for any site in the world. Broad-winged Hawk (Buteo platypterus), Swainson's Hawk (Buteo swainsoni), and Mississippi Kite (Ictinia mississippiensis) form the bulk; the daily peak can reach several hundred thousand birds per hour in optimal conditions.

## 3. Sumidero Canyon, Chiapas

The Sumidero Canyon National Park near Tuxtla Gutiérrez holds breeding Military Macaw, Elegant Euphonia (Euphonia elegantissima), and the full tropical dry forest community of the Central Depression of Chiapas. Boat trips through the canyon see cliff-nesting swifts and herons at close range.

## 4. San Cristóbal de las Casas highlands, Chiapas

The highlands around San Cristóbal are the most accessible entry point for Chiapas's montane forest birds. Pink-headed Warbler (Cardellina versicolor, Vulnerable), Blue-throated Motmot (Aspatha gularis), and Wine-throated Hummingbird (Selasphorus ellioti) are among the highland endemics and near-endemics accessible from the El Bosque trail system on the outskirts of the city.

## 5. Palenque, Chiapas

The ruins of Palenque sit within a buffer of tropical forest that holds the full Petén lowland forest community: Keel-billed Toucan (Ramphastos sulfuratus), Ocellated Turkey (Meleagris ocellata), and Yucatán Jay (Cyanocorax yucatanicus). The early morning hours before tourist buses arrive produce the most productive birdwatching in the forest surrounding the main plaza.

## 6. Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve, Yucatán

This UNESCO Biosphere Reserve on the eastern coast of the Yucatán Peninsula protects mangrove, sea grass, tropical forest, and coral reef in combination. The full Yucatán endemic suite — Yucatán Woodpecker (Melanerpes pygmaeus), Yucatán Vireo (Vireo magister), and Yucatán Jay — occurs here alongside Jabiru Stork and American Flamingo (Phoenicopterus ruber) in the coastal lagoons.

## 7. Rio Lagartos, Yucatán

The Reserva de la Biosfera Ría Lagartos on the north Yucatán coast holds the largest American Flamingo (Phoenicopterus ruber) breeding colony in Mexico. Boat trips from Las Coloradas produce close views of the flamingo colony and the hypersaline lagoon bird community. Snowy Plover (Charadrius nivosus), American Avocet (Recurvirostra americana), and Roseate Spoonbill (Platalea ajaja) share the lagoon.

## 8. Oaxacan highlands: Monte Albán and Yagul

The open grassland, oak woodland, and agricultural mosaic of the Central Valleys of Oaxaca holds the narrowly endemic Oaxaca Sparrow (Aimophila notosticta) and Slaty Vireo (Vireo brevipennis) alongside a diverse endemic and near-endemic group. The Ubelilek Cooperativa de turismo comunitario (documented in the map database) represents the community-based ecotourism infrastructure developing in Oaxacan communities.

## 9. Sierra de Manantlán Biosphere Reserve, Jalisco-Colima

The Manantlán reserve holds the endemic Sierra Madre del Sur bird community with Military Macaw, Golden Vireo (Vireo hypochryseus), and White-throated Flycatcher (Empidonax albigularis). The reserve is best known for the maize ancestor teosinte discovered here but holds significant bird endemism.

## 10. Celestún, Yucatán

The Celestún lagoon system holds one of the most accessible American Flamingo populations in Mexico. Boat trips from the beach town of Celestún navigate the mangrove channels and hypersaline pools where flamingos feed year-round. The full Caribbean coastal mangrove community accompanies the flamingo viewing.

## Planning a Mexico birding trip

Mexico's position at the meeting of the Nearctic and Neotropical biogeographic zones means that a well-planned circuit can cover both North American breeding species and Central American tropical endemics in a single trip. The country's accessibility from the United States, well-developed domestic air network, and large number of eBird-documented hotspots make it one of the most visited international birding destinations in the Americas.

The most productive single region for endemic diversity is Oaxaca — the Sierra Juárez, Sierra de Miahuatlán, and the Sierra Norte combined hold over 50 Mexican endemic or near-endemic species accessible within a two-hour radius of Oaxaca City. The high-altitude pine-oak forests above San José del Pacifico produce species like Dwarf Jay (Cyanolyca nana, Vulnerable), Dwarf Vireo (Vireo nelsoni), and the full Oaxacan endemic suite. The cloud forest corridor from Oaxaca City to Tuxtepec via the Sierra Norte is the most efficient route through the diversity gradient.

Chiapas in the south — accessible from Tuxtla Gutiérrez airport — provides the Neotropical component: the Cañón del Sumidero's cliff-breeding raptors, the El Triunfo Biosphere Reserve's highland forest (home to Horned Guan, Eriocnemis nigrivestis, Critically Endangered), and the Palenque lowlands for Yucatan-endemic species. El Triunfo requires permits and the logistics of the cloud forest trail.

The Yucatán Peninsula is the most accessible region for North American visitors flying into Cancún. Celestún Flamingo reserve (one of the largest flamingo concentrations in North America), the Calakmul Biosphere Reserve (Yucatan Woodpecker and Ocellated Turkey), and the Sian Ka'an wetland complex are all accessible from the peninsula's road network.

Spring migration (April to May) brings Wood Warblers in enormous concentrations through the Gulf Coast and eastern Mexico. The Sierra de Tamaulipas is one of the highest-volume passerine migration corridors. Mexico City itself provides access to the Bosque de Chapultepec and the volcanic ranges of Popocatépetl and Iztaccíhuatl for high-altitude endemic species.

## Mexico City as a birding base

Mexico City itself offers productive urban birding within easy reach. Chapultepec Park's old-growth ahuehuete trees hold Elegant Trogon on occasion during winter; the Xochimilco canal system south of the city is the last stronghold for the Critically Endangered Axolotl and also holds Black-crowned Night-Heron, Great Blue Heron, and a range of waterbirds. The Pedregal ecological reserve at UNAM is the most productive urban site, with endemic Mexican species in the scrubby lava fields.

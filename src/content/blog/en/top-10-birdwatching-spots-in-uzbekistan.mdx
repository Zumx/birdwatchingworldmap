Uzbekistan is the birding heart of Central Asia, a double-landlocked country where desert, mountain, river, and lake collide within a single itinerary. The Kyzylkum desert holds larks, sandgrouse, and the elusive Pander's Ground Jay; the gorges of the Western Tien Shan offer rosefinches, accentors, and the chance of Himalayan Snowcock; and the reedbeds and lakes of the lower Amu Darya and Syr Darya hold pelicans, pheasants, and wintering wildfowl. Around 460 species have been recorded, and a focused two-week loop from Tashkent can produce a long list of birds found nowhere in Europe and barely accessible elsewhere.

## 1. Aydar-Arnasay Lakes

This vast system of saline and freshwater lakes in the central steppe formed largely in the twentieth century from Syr Darya overflow, and it is now one of the most important waterbird sites in Central Asia and a Ramsar wetland. Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened), Great White Pelican, flamingos, and huge numbers of wintering ducks and grebes use the lakes, while the surrounding desert holds larks and Asian Desert Warbler.

## 2. Kyzylkum Desert near Nurata

The clay and sand desert of the Kyzylkum is the place for true Central Asian desert birds. Pander's Ground Jay (Podoces panderi), a charismatic ground-dwelling corvid almost endemic to the region, occurs in the saxaul scrub, alongside Pallas's Sandgrouse, Greater Sand Plover, Desert Wheatear, and a suite of larks including Bimaculated and Calandra. Dawn drives across the desert tracks near Nurata are the classic way to find the ground jay.

## 3. Nuratau Mountains

The Nuratau range and the Nuratau-Kyzylkum Biosphere Reserve rise abruptly from the desert, holding walnut and juniper woodland in their valleys. The reserve protects the rare Severtzov's (Tien Shan) argali sheep, but for birders it produces Eastern Rock Nuthatch, Rufous-naped Tit, White-winged Woodpecker, and breeding raptors including Golden Eagle and Egyptian Vulture (Neophron percnopterus, Endangered). Community guesthouses in the valley villages make a comfortable base.

## 4. Western Tien Shan and Chimgan

The mountains east of Tashkent, including the Chimgan area and the Ugam-Chatkal National Park, give access to the montane birds of the Western Tien Shan, a UNESCO World Heritage range. Himalayan Snowcock, White-winged Grosbeak, Red-fronted Serin, Brown Accentor, and Blue Whistling Thrush occur on the high slopes and in the gorges, with Wallcreeper on the cliffs and Sulphur-bellied Warbler in the juniper.

## 5. Chatkal and Pskem Valleys

The deep valleys north-east of Tashkent hold riverine and montane forest birds along fast mountain rivers. Ibisbill, that strange wader of stony riverbeds, occurs along the Pskem and Chatkal, alongside White-capped Redstart, Brown Dipper, and breeding rosefinches. The valleys are also among the most scenic walking country in the country.

## 6. Lower Amu Darya and Badai-Tugai

The Badai-Tugai reserve near Urgench protects one of the last large stands of tugai — the riverine poplar and tamarisk forest of the Amu Darya floodplain. This is the place for Pheasant of the local subspecies, Turkestan Tit, White-tailed Lapwing, and breeding herons, with reintroduced Bukhara deer in the undergrowth. The tugai is a globally threatened habitat, and Badai-Tugai is its best-protected fragment.

## 7. Talimarjan Reservoir

This large reservoir in the south near the Turkmen border is one of the most important wintering and staging waterbird sites in the country. White-headed Duck (Oxyura leucocephala, Endangered), Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened), and large flocks of dabbling and diving ducks gather here, with raptors hunting the surrounding semi-desert and pelicans on passage.

## 8. Zarafshan Valley and Reserve

The Zarafshan reserve near Samarkand protects a strip of riverine tugai along the Zarafshan River. Yellow-eyed Stock Dove, White-winged Woodpecker, Turkestan Tit, and the localised Zarafshan race of pheasant occur, and the river attracts kingfishers, herons, and wintering wildfowl. It pairs naturally with a visit to the Registan and the great monuments of Samarkand.

## 9. Aral Sea Lakes and the Sudoche Wetlands

In the far north-west of Karakalpakstan, the Sudoche lake system in the former Amu Darya delta is a remarkable wetland surviving amid the catastrophe of the shrinking Aral Sea. Pelicans, flamingos, and large concentrations of breeding and migrant waterbirds use the remaining lakes, set against the surreal landscape of the dried seabed and the ship cemetery at Moynaq.

## 10. Gissar (Hissar) Mountains

The Gissar range south-east of Samarkand, including the Gissar Nature Reserve, holds high-mountain and gorge birds in dramatic limestone scenery. Himalayan Snowcock, Güldenstädt's Redstart, Fire-fronted Serin, and Wallcreeper occur at altitude, while the lower juniper woodland holds Rufous-naped Tit and breeding raptors. The reserve protects some of the most pristine high country in southern Uzbekistan.

## When to visit

Spring, from late April to early June, is the prime birding season. The desert larks and sandgrouse are displaying, the migrant warblers and shrikes are passing through, and the mountain birds are on territory as the snow retreats up the Tien Shan and Gissar slopes. May is the classic month for a combined desert-and-mountain trip, when Pander's Ground Jay is feeding young in the Kyzylkum and Himalayan Snowcock is calling on the high ridges.

Autumn, from September to October, brings southbound migration through the lakes and reservoirs, with pelicans, waders, and wildfowl building at Aydar-Arnasay and Talimarjan. The desert is more comfortable than in the searing summer, and raptor passage moves through the foothills. Winter is harsh on the steppe but excellent for waterbird concentrations at the southern reservoirs, where White-headed Duck and large duck flocks gather on the ice-free water.

Midsummer is best avoided in the lowlands, where the Kyzylkum and the Amu Darya floodplain can exceed 40°C and bird activity collapses to the first hours after dawn. The high Tien Shan and Gissar, however, are at their most accessible in July and August once the snow has cleared the passes.

## Practical notes

Tashkent is the entry point, with a modern airport and good onward connections, including the high-speed Afrosiyob train to Samarkand and Bukhara. A hired vehicle with a driver is the standard way to bird Uzbekistan, since the desert tracks and mountain roads demand local knowledge and a robust four-wheel drive. Distances are large: Tashkent to the Aral region in Karakalpakstan is a long journey best broken with internal flights to Nukus or Urgench.

Several reserves, including Badai-Tugai, Zarafshan, and the Nuratau biosphere reserve, require permits, most easily arranged through a local operator. A number of Uzbek birding and ecotourism services — some trading under names such as Ptisa — handle permits, desert logistics, and community guesthouse bookings. English is limited outside the tourist cities, and Russian remains the lingua franca for travel. Carry a scope for the lakes and a robust water supply for the desert, where shade is scarce and distances between settlements are long.

India's bird diversity reflects the country's size and its position at the junction of four major biogeographic zones: the Himalayan region with its Palearctic and Oriental species, the Indo-Gangetic plain, the Western Ghats (a biodiversity hotspot), and the Andaman and Nicobar Islands. Serious Indian birding requires visiting multiple zones; this list covers the most productive sites in each. All are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Keoladeo Ghana National Park (Bharatpur), Rajasthan

Keoladeo Ghana is the most celebrated waterbird habitat in South Asia. The reserve was originally a hunting ground of the Bharatpur maharajas and was notified as a national park in 1982. In winter (October to March), the flooded grassland and scrub woodland supports over 230 species, including breeding Indian Sarus Crane, Painted Stork, Asian Openbill, and Spot-billed Pelican, overlaid by Central Asian and Siberian winter visitors: bar-headed geese, ducks, and waders in enormous variety. The critically endangered Siberian Crane formerly wintered here; the last records were in the 1990s. Cycle rickshaws and walking are the transport modes; no motor vehicles enter the core zone.

## 2. Bhitarkanika National Park, Odisha

Bhitarkanika on the Odisha coast protects mangrove forest and tidal creek habitat. The park holds the largest nesting colony of Open-billed Storks in South Asia, along with nesting Darters and Night Herons in the mixed heronry at Bagagahana. Irrawaddy Dolphins are present in the tidal channels; saltwater crocodiles make walking outside marked trails inadvisable. The bird list includes Mangrove Pitta, Collared Kingfisher, and several species specific to mangrove habitat that are absent from most inland sites. Access by boat from Rajnagar.

## 3. Kaziranga National Park, Assam

Kaziranga is better known for the Indian One-horned Rhinoceros, but the park's mosaic of grassland, forest, and wetland in the Brahmaputra floodplain holds outstanding birds. Greater Adjutant Stork (Leptoptilos dubius) — fewer than 1,200 individuals remain globally — is regular here and in nearby Panbari area. Bengal Florican, one of the most endangered bustards in the world, is found in Kaziranga's tall grasslands. Elephant-back safaris in the core zone bring observers close to grassland species that remain inaccessible on foot.

## 4. Eaglenest Wildlife Sanctuary, Arunachal Pradesh

Eaglenest at 200 to 3,250 metres on the Arunachal Himalayan foothills is the most significant birding site in northeastern India. The sanctuary holds at least 450 species and was the location where Bugun Liocichla (Liocichla bugunorum) — a new bird species — was described in 2006. It also harbours Rufous-necked Hornbill, Ward's Trogon, Beautiful Nuthatch, and several globally threatened species found nowhere else in India as reliably. Access requires Inner Line Permits issued by the Arunachal Pradesh government, and foreign visitors must arrange permits through recognised travel agents in advance.

## 5. Sattal and Pangot, Uttarakhand

The Sattal and Pangot areas near Nainital in the Kumaon Himalayan foothills give the best accessible introduction to Himalayan forest birds from Delhi. At 1,500 to 2,300 metres in mixed oak and rhododendron forest, species include Khalij Pheasant, Koklas Pheasant, Himalayan Woodpecker, Orange-flanked Bush Robin, and the full suite of Himalayan laughingthrushes. Sattal's lake complex adds wintering waterfowl including Baikal Teal in good years. Pangot's Woodpecker Trail is the specific focus for picids; Cutia, Variegated Laughingthrush, and Brown-capped Pygmy Woodpecker are all regular.

## 6. Thattekad Bird Sanctuary, Kerala

Thattekad in the Western Ghats of Kerala was described by ornithologist Salim Ali as the richest bird habitat in peninsular India. The sanctuary lies in the lowland Periyar River valley between two Ghats ranges, giving access to both hill forest and riverine woodland birds. Malabar Trogon, Rufous Babbler (endemic to the Western Ghats), Small-billed False Sunbird (Iora), and Sri Lanka Frogmouth are sanctuary specialties. The forest floor holds Indian Pitta in winter; Malabar Grey Hornbill and Great Hornbill both breed in the taller forest.

## 7. Chilika Lake, Odisha

Chilika is Asia's largest coastal brackish lagoon, covering approximately 1,100 square kilometres. In winter (November to February), it holds the largest concentration of migratory waterbirds in the Indian subcontinent: flamingos, Pallas's Gull, Lesser Florican stragglers, Painted Stork, and Ruddy Shelduck. The island of Nalabana in the centre of the lake is designated a Bird Sanctuary and holds the densest concentrations; boats hired from Barkul or Chilika give access. The Irrawaddy Dolphin population in the southern lake is an additional draw.

## 8. Sultanpur National Park, Haryana

Sultanpur, 15 kilometres from Gurugram (Gurgaon) at the edge of the Delhi metropolitan area, is the best-known example of what was once a much larger network of seasonal wetlands on the Gangetic plain. The lake and its surrounding scrub hold over 250 species recorded, including winter visitors from Central Asia: Demoiselle Crane, Bar-headed Goose, and Sociable Lapwing in some years. Its proximity to Delhi makes it the primary day-birding destination for the city's large birding community; eBird checklists here are among the most detailed for any Indian site.

## 9. Nameri National Park, Assam

Nameri on the Assam-Arunachal border protects lowland tropical forest on the foothills, giving access to the lowland extension of the Himalayan foothills bird community. Bengal Florican occasionally wanders from the Brahmaputra plain; the forest holds White-winged Wood Duck — one of the most endangered ducks in Asia — in the sluggish forest streams. River rafting on the Jia Bhoroli is the standard activity, and the combination of the rafting route's bird viewing with forest walks from the camp at Potasali gives a productive two-day session.

## 10. Andaman Islands

The Andaman Archipelago in the Bay of Bengal has been isolated from mainland Asia long enough to produce a suite of endemic birds — Andaman Crake, Andaman Treepie, Andaman Drongo, Andaman Coucal, and Andaman Woodpecker among them — found nowhere else. The lowland tropical rainforest on South Andaman and Neil Island is the most accessible; Havelock Island (now Swaraj Dweep) has basic forest trail access. The sea between islands produces Red-footed Booby, Brown Booby, Bridled Tern, and Nicobar Pigeon between stops.

## Planning an India birding trip

India's birding circuits require choosing between the northeast (Eaglenest, Kaziranga, Nameri), the Himalayan foothills (Sattal, Pangot, Corbett NP), the subcontinent (Bharatpur, Sultanpur, Ranthambore), and the south (Thattekad, Periyar, Chilika). A two-week trip can meaningfully cover one or two of these zones; attempting four in a fortnight produces superficial views of all.

November to February is the optimal season for most Indian birding: winter visitors have arrived from Central Asia and Siberia, the heat is manageable, and the monsoon ground conditions have dried out. March to May brings heat but some species are at peak breeding display. The northeast monsoon runs June to September and makes most forest trails difficult; Eaglenest is inaccessible from June to September.

Inner Line Permits for Arunachal Pradesh must be arranged several weeks in advance and require a registered Indian tour operator; this is not negotiable. Indian eBird checklists are detailed and growing rapidly; the [map](/map) provides geographic context for the site distances that any itinerary must account for.

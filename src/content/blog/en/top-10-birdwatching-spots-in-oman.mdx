Oman is the most rewarding birding destination on the Arabian Peninsula, a compact and welcoming country where coastal lagoons, gravel deserts, and the unique monsoon-fed hills of the south sit within a day or two of each other. The coastal khawrs — brackish creek mouths fringed with reed and mudflat — concentrate migrant waders, herons, and gulls in spectacular numbers. The Dhofar region in the far south, watered each summer by the Indian Ocean monsoon, holds Afrotropical species that occur nowhere else in Arabia. The national list exceeds 540 species, and the relaxed infrastructure makes self-drive birding easy.

## 1. The Dhofar khawrs, Salalah

The chain of coastal lagoons around Salalah — Khawr Rori, Khawr Taqah, Khawr al Mughsayl, and others — is the birding heart of Oman. These reed-fringed creek mouths hold concentrations of waders, herons, and gulls that include regional rarities: Pheasant-tailed Jacana, Greater Spotted Eagle, Glossy Ibis, and a roll-call of Asian and African waterbirds. The khawrs are the single most productive birding area in the country and the focus of any southern trip.

## 2. Tawi Atair and the Dhofar mountains

The Jabal Samhan and the hills above Salalah are clothed in a unique seasonal woodland fed by the summer khareef monsoon. Tawi Atair sinkhole and the surrounding wooded slopes hold the Arabian endemics of the south: Arabian Partridge, Arabian Wheatear, Yemen Serin, and the African-affinity species such as African Paradise Flycatcher, Shining Sunbird, and Bruce's Green Pigeon. Verreaux's Eagle and Bonelli's Eagle patrol the escarpments.

## 3. Mughsayl and the western Dhofar coast

The cliffs and beaches west of Salalah toward Mughsayl hold breeding seabirds and an outstanding seawatch in the right conditions. Jouanin's Petrel, Persian Shearwater, and Masked Booby pass offshore, while the lagoon at Mughsayl holds waders and the area is reliable for Singing Bushlark and Black-crowned Sparrow-Lark on the surrounding plains.

## 4. Al Beed Farm and the Nejd

Inland from Salalah, the agricultural fields of Al Beed and the central Nejd desert hold a different community. The irrigated fields attract migrant raptors, larks, and pipits, and are one of the most reliable sites in Arabia for sought-after vagrants. The surrounding gravel desert holds Cream-coloured Courser, sandgrouse, and wheatears.

## 5. Masirah Island

Masirah, off the central east coast, is one of Oman's great seabird and wader islands. The beaches hold internationally important wintering wader flocks — Crab-plover, Greater Sand Plover, and Terek Sandpiper among them — while offshore boat trips produce Jouanin's Petrel, Persian Shearwater, and skuas. The island is reached by ferry from Shannah and rewards a multi-day stay.

## 6. Ras al Hadd and the eastern khawrs

The easternmost point of Arabia, around Ras al Hadd and Ras al Jinz, combines turtle-nesting beaches with productive khawrs. Khawr al Hajar and the creek mouths hold waders and herons, while the headland is a seawatch point for migrating terns, gulls, and the occasional Socotra Cormorant. The fishing harbours attract gulls including Sooty Gull and the regional large-gull complex.

## 7. Al Ansab Lagoons, Muscat

On the edge of the capital, the Al Ansab wetlands — a series of reed-fringed treatment lagoons — are the most accessible quality birding in Oman. The pools attract migrant ducks, waders, crakes, and warblers, with regular records of Pheasant-tailed Jacana, Citrine Wagtail, and a long list of passage migrants. A hide and managed access make this an easy half-day from the city.

## 8. Qurum Nature Reserve, Muscat

The Qurum mangrove and khawr in the heart of Muscat is the capital's other key site, a green wedge of reed and mangrove between the city's beaches and buildings. Western Reef Heron, Clamorous Reed Warbler, and Indian Pond Heron occur, and the reserve concentrates migrant warblers and waders in passage seasons within easy reach of the airport.

## 9. Al Hajar Mountains and Jabal Akhdar

The high limestone massif of the Al Hajar in the north, rising above 3,000 metres at Jabal Shams, holds the montane community of northern Arabia. Plain Leaf Warbler, Hume's Wheatear, Striolated Bunting, and Long-billed Pipit inhabit the rocky slopes, while the cultivated terraces of Jabal Akhdar attract Eastern Orphean Warbler and migrants. Lammergeier and Bonelli's Eagle occur in the gorges.

## 10. The Batinah coast and Sohar

The northern Batinah coast, with its beaches, fish markets, and the lagoons toward Sohar, holds the Gulf of Oman waterbird community. Wintering and passage waders crowd the shoreline, Socotra Cormorant occurs offshore, and the fishing harbours concentrate gulls and terns. The date plantations inland add Grey Francolin, Indian Roller, and Purple Sunbird.

## Seasons and the monsoon

Oman's birding is shaped by two contrasting climatic worlds. The north follows the standard Arabian pattern: blistering summers and a cool, comfortable winter from October to March that brings wintering waders to the coasts, ducks to the lagoons, and migrant passerines to the gardens and wadis. Spring and autumn passage along the coast is heavy, with raptors, shrikes, wheatears, and warblers moving through, and the khawrs and treatment lagoons fill with waders.

The south is a different country entirely. The Dhofar region is touched each summer by the khareef, the Indian Ocean monsoon that rolls cloud and drizzle over the coastal hills from June to September and turns the brown slopes a startling green. This monsoon is the reason Dhofar holds African species found nowhere else in Arabia, and the period just after the khareef, from late August into the autumn, is one of the best times to find the southern specialties when the hills are lush and the breeding birds are active. The khawrs of Salalah are productive year-round but reach their peak during the autumn and spring wader passage.

Winter is the prime season for the coastal khawrs and Masirah, when wintering waders are at their maximum and the lagoons hold ducks and herons. The seawatching off Mughsayl and Masirah is best from the spring through autumn when Jouanin's Petrel and Persian Shearwater are present in the Arabian Sea. The northern mountains can be birded year-round, though the high Jabal Akhdar is most comfortable outside the summer heat. A typical itinerary combines a few days around Muscat and the Al Hajar with a southern leg to Salalah and the Dhofar khawrs and mountains.

## Practicalities and getting around

Oman is the easiest Arabian country for independent birding. A tourist visa is straightforward, English is widely spoken, and the road network is excellent. A hire car, ideally a 4WD for the desert tracks and the Masirah ferry approach, is the key to the country. The north can be covered from Muscat, with Al Ansab, Qurum, the Batinah coast, and the Al Hajar mountains all within a day's drive. The south requires a flight or a long drive to Salalah, where the khawrs, the Dhofar mountains, and the western coast cluster within an easy radius. The Oman Bird Records Committee and active local birders keep site and rarity information current.

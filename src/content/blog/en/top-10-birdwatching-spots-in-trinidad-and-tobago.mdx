Trinidad and Tobago offers a unique proposition for birders: South American birds at island scale. Lying just off the Venezuelan coast, the two islands share the avifauna of the mainland tropics, yet pack it into a compact, English-speaking country with excellent infrastructure and short distances. More than 480 species have been recorded, including 18 hummingbirds, and the islands are famous as one of the gentlest introductions to Neotropical birding anywhere. The legendary Asa Wright Nature Centre, perched in the Northern Range, has hosted visiting birders for more than half a century, and from its veranda you can watch a dozen tanager species and a constant blur of hummingbirds before breakfast.

## Asa Wright Nature Centre

Set at around 360 metres in the forested Arima Valley of Trinidad's Northern Range, Asa Wright is one of the most famous birding lodges in the world. The veranda overlooks fruit and hummingbird feeders that draw Purple Honeycreeper, Green Honeycreeper, Silver-beaked Tanager, White-necked Jacobin, and Tufted Coquette in a constant rotation. The surrounding 1,500-acre reserve holds Bearded Bellbird, Channel-billed Toucan, and the famous breeding colony of Oilbirds in Dunston Cave, one of the most accessible roosts of this nocturnal, echolocating species anywhere.

## Caroni Swamp

This vast mangrove wetland on Trinidad's west coast is the roosting site of the Scarlet Ibis, the national bird, and one of the great wildlife spectacles of the Caribbean. Boat trips depart in the late afternoon, drifting through the mangrove channels as flocks of brilliant scarlet ibis stream in to roost on the islands at dusk, turning the trees red against the sunset. The swamp also holds Boat-billed Heron, Tricolored Heron, and a range of egrets, and the journey out often produces Common Potoo and tree boas.

## Nariva Swamp and Bush Bush

The largest freshwater wetland in Trinidad, on the east coast, Nariva is a Ramsar site of palm forest, marsh, and lagoon. It is the stronghold of the Red-bellied Macaw and the reintroduced Blue-and-yellow Macaw, and the marshes hold Pinnated Bittern, Azure Gallinule, and the localised White-headed Marsh Tyrant. The Bush Bush forested island within the swamp is rich in primates and parrots, and boat trips through the moriche palm stands are among the most atmospheric birding in the country.

## The Northern Range and Blanchisseuse Road

The forested spine of Trinidad, the Northern Range rises to El Cerro del Aripo at 940 metres, and the road from Arima to Blanchisseuse climbs through some of the best montane forest birding on the island. Specialities include Trinidad Motmot, found only on these two islands, along with Speckled Tanager, Golden-headed Manakin, and the spectacular White-bearded Manakin at its forest leks. Mixed flocks move through the canopy, and the road offers easy roadside access to forest interior species.

## Pointe-a-Pierre Wildfowl Trust

Set incongruously within an oil refinery complex on Trinidad's west coast, this wetland reserve is a conservation success story and an easy, productive birding stop. The lakes and surrounding habitat hold Black-bellied Whistling-Duck, Wattled Jacana, Purple Gallinule, and a range of herons, and the Trust runs a breeding programme for endangered waterfowl. It makes a relaxed introduction to the island's wetland birds and is especially good for families.

## Aripo Savannahs

This protected area of open savanna and palm marsh in central Trinidad supports a distinctive grassland avifauna found in few other parts of the island. Red-bellied Macaws fly over the moriche palms, and the savanna holds Sulphury Flycatcher, Savanna Hawk, Grassland Yellow-Finch, and Plumbeous Kite. The open landscape is a striking contrast to the forested Northern Range and adds a different suite of species to a Trinidad list.

## Tobago's Main Ridge Forest Reserve

Tobago's mountainous spine is protected by the Main Ridge Forest Reserve, established in 1776 and reckoned to be the oldest legally protected forest reserve in the western hemisphere. The rainforest holds the endemic Tobago specialities, including the Blue-backed Manakin, White-tailed Sabrewing, and Collared Trogon, and the famous Gilpin Trace trail is the classic way to find them. Tobago's avifauna differs notably from Trinidad's, making the island an essential second leg of any trip.

## Little Tobago and the Seabird Colonies

The small island of Little Tobago, off Speyside, hosts one of the most important seabird colonies in the southern Caribbean. Red-billed Tropicbirds nest on the cliffs, and the island holds Brown Booby, Magnificent Frigatebird, Audubon's Shearwater, and Brown Noddy. Boat trips from Speyside cross the reef-rich channel to the island, and the spectacle of tropicbirds wheeling over the cliffs is one of the highlights of a Tobago visit.

## Buccoo Marsh and the Bon Accord Lagoon

The wetlands at the southwestern tip of Tobago, including Buccoo Marsh and the Bon Accord Lagoon, are the island's premier site for waterbirds and migrant shorebirds. The mangroves and lagoons hold Mangrove Cuckoo, herons, and a range of waders, and the famous Buccoo Reef offshore adds seabirds. The area is an easy and rewarding contrast to the rainforest of the Main Ridge.

## Grafton Caledonia and the Tobago Lowlands

The Grafton Caledonia Wildlife Sanctuary, a former cocoa estate on Tobago's leeward coast, is an excellent and accessible site for lowland forest birds. The feeding station draws Rufous-vented Chachalaca, the island's national bird, along with Blue-grey Tanager, Bananaquit, and Crested Oropendola. The reserve's trails and the surrounding dry forest make it a productive half-day for anyone based on the south of the island.

## When to Go and How to Plan

The dry season from January to May offers the most comfortable weather, while the wet season brings breeding activity and lush forest. Scarlet ibis roost at Caroni year-round, and the islands' resident species are present throughout, with northern migrants adding to the lists from October to April. A classic trip combines several days at a Trinidad forest lodge with the Caroni boat trip, then crosses to Tobago for the Main Ridge and the seabird colonies, capturing the islands' full range. A short domestic flight or the inter-island ferry connects the two; both should be booked ahead. Engaging knowledgeable local guides not only improves the birding but channels income into the reserves and communities that safeguard these habitats, and visitors should keep a respectful distance from the ibis roost at Caroni and from the tropicbird cliffs of Little Tobago.

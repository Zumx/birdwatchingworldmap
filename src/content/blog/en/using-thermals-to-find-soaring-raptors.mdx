At a good hawkwatch on a sunny October morning, the first hours can feel empty — a few low birds, long quiet stretches, scanning an apparently birdless sky. Then, around mid-morning, the air begins to work. A distant Common Buzzard rises in a tight spiral, climbing without a single wingbeat, joined by a second, then a third, until a rotating column of birds hangs over a sunlit slope. They reach the top, peel off one by one, and glide southward in a long shallow line toward the next lift. Understanding why this happens — and where and when — is the difference between staring at empty sky and reading the day's migration as it unfolds.

## What a thermal is and why raptors need it

A thermal is a column or bubble of warm air rising through cooler surrounding air. As the sun heats the ground, the ground heats the air just above it; that warm air becomes buoyant and lifts off, often from a particular triggering feature, rising in an invisible column that can reach a kilometre or more in height. Soaring raptors — buzzards, eagles, vultures, many hawks — exploit these columns to gain altitude almost for free, circling within the rising air to climb, then gliding off to lose height slowly while travelling forward, then finding the next thermal to climb again.

This soar-glide cycle is vastly more efficient than flapping flight. A migrating Honey Buzzard or Broad-winged Hawk crossing a continent does so largely without powered flight, stepping from thermal to thermal across hundreds of kilometres a day. The corollary for the birder is decisive: soaring raptors go where the lift is. Find where thermals form and where they line up, and you find the birds. The sky is not uniform — it has structure, and that structure is what concentrates migration into the streams that hawkwatches exist to count.

## Where thermals form

Thermals are triggered by surfaces that heat faster than their surroundings. Bare rock, dry fields, ploughed earth, south-facing slopes, towns and roads all warm quickly in sun and launch thermals; forests, wet ground, lakes and shaded north slopes stay cooler and rarely do. The first skill in finding soaring raptors is reading the ground for these hot spots. A sunlit, south-facing rocky slope above a valley is a thermal factory; the cool forested valley floor beside it is dead air. Birds will rise over the slope, not the forest.

Terrain shapes thermals as much as surface does. Slopes and ridges focus the rising air, and where a thermal drifts against a hillside it can be reinforced. Ridges also produce a second kind of lift — orographic or slope lift — where wind striking a ridge is deflected upward, letting raptors soar along the ridge line without any thermal at all. The great ridge hawkwatches, such as Hawk Mountain in Pennsylvania, work on exactly this principle: a long ridge meeting the prevailing wind generates a continuous upward cushion that birds ride for kilometres. Knowing whether a site is a thermal site or a ridge-lift site tells you where to look and what weather to hope for.

## The role of time of day

Thermals are creatures of the sun, and so soaring migration follows a daily clock. In the first hour or two after sunrise the ground is still cool and few thermals form; raptors that roosted overnight wait, often perched, until the air begins to work. As the sun climbs and the ground heats through mid-morning, thermals strengthen and multiply, and the soaring birds lift off. Through late morning and early afternoon the lift is strongest and migration peaks; this is the prime window at most thermal-dependent hawkwatches, typically from roughly three hours after sunrise to mid-afternoon.

Toward evening the ground cools, thermals weaken and collapse, and soaring migration shuts down — the birds drop to roost. This rhythm has practical consequences. Arriving at a thermal hawkwatch at dawn often means a slow start; the action builds with the heat. On overcast or cold days thermals may barely form at all, and even strong migration weather can produce few soaring birds if the sun never breaks through. Reading the day means watching the sky come alive as the heat builds, and knowing that a dead early sky may transform completely by eleven o'clock.

## Reading weather: wind, sun and fronts

Beyond the daily cycle, broader weather sets the stage. Clear or partly cloudy skies with strong sun produce the best thermals; total overcast suppresses them. Cumulus clouds are a gift to the hawkwatcher — each fair-weather cumulus marks the top of a thermal, where rising moist air condenses, so a sky dotted with cumulus is a map of the lift, and raptors climb toward the cloud bases. Learning to read the clouds tells you where the columns are even before a bird appears.

Wind direction interacts with terrain to decide everything. At ridge sites, a wind blowing into the ridge produces strong slope lift and big flights; a wind off the ridge can shut the site down. After the passage of a cold front in autumn, with clearing skies, falling temperatures and a following northwesterly wind, conditions often align for major raptor movements across temperate latitudes — the classic post-frontal hawk flight that fills the sky. Tracking the synoptic weather, especially the timing of fronts, is how experienced hawkwatchers predict the big days before they arrive.

## Finding and following the birds in the sky

When you know where thermals form, scanning becomes targeted rather than random. Concentrate on the sunlit slopes and ridges, the warm valley sides, the airspace below and around fair-weather cumulus. A rising raptor often appears first as a tiny circling speck low over a hot slope, then climbs into clearer view. Once one bird marks a thermal, others frequently join it — a single soaring buzzard is an advertisement that lift is present, and following birds use the same column.

Watch the whole cycle to anticipate where birds go next. A raptor that tops out of a thermal will glide in a fairly straight line, losing height, until it reaches the next source of lift — often the next ridge or hot slope downwind along the migration axis. Skilled observers track a bird across this glide and pick it up again as it begins to circle in the following thermal, and they learn the local sequence of lift sources that birds chain together. At a well-known site this becomes almost predictable: the birds rise here, glide there, and re-form over that hill, day after day, because the terrain dictates the route.

## Putting it all together at a hawkwatch

The classic raptor migration bottlenecks of the world combine all these factors. Soaring raptors avoid long water crossings, where no thermals form, so they funnel along coasts and converge on the narrowest sea crossings — the Strait of Gibraltar between Spain and Morocco, the Strait of Messina, the Bosphorus at Istanbul, Falsterbo at the southern tip of Sweden, Veracruz in Mexico, Eilat in Israel. At each, geography forces a continent's worth of birds through a narrow gate, and within that gate the daily thermal cycle decides the hour they pass.

To make the most of a thermal hawkwatch, arrive knowing the pattern: scan the hot slopes from mid-morning, read the cumulus, watch the wind relative to the terrain, and expect the peak in the warm middle of the day. Track each rising column, follow the glides toward the next lift, and let the structure of the air guide your eyes. Done well, this turns a patient watch into an unfolding story of heat and flight — the invisible architecture of the atmosphere made visible by the birds that ride it.

The Yucatan Peninsula is a flat limestone platform projecting into the Gulf of Mexico and the Caribbean, encompassing the Mexican states of Yucatan, Campeche, and Quintana Roo, along with northern Guatemala and Belize. The lack of surface rivers — rainfall disappears underground through the porous limestone into a network of cenotes and subterranean rivers — gives the peninsula a distinctive dry character despite adequate rainfall. The resulting tropical dry forest and seasonal forest support a distinctive avifauna with a high degree of endemism: the Yucatan Jay, the Ocellated Turkey, the Yucatan Poorwill, and the Black Catbird are among the species found only within this peninsula or with their core range centered here.

For birders, the Yucatan rewards those who spend time in the less-touristed forest interior as much as on the well-visited coasts. The Maya ruins at Chichen Itza, Uxmal, and Palenque sit within or adjacent to excellent bird habitat, and morning birding before the tourist crowds arrive often produces the best results. The following ten sites represent the most productive birding locations across the peninsula.

## 1. Chichen Itza Ruins and Surrounding Forest

The great Maya pyramid complex of Chichen Itza sits within semi-deciduous tropical forest, and the early morning before the tourist buses arrive is one of the best birding times in the Yucatan. Turquoise-browed Motmot, arguably the most visually stunning bird of the region, is common in the forest edge and often perches conspicuously at eye level. Yucatan Jay moves in noisy flocks through the forest, and the loud calls of Plain Chachalaca emanate from the densest vegetation before dawn. Black-headed Trogon and Violaceous Trogon both occur, and the forest around the cenote holds Ferruginous Pygmy-Owl and Mayan Antthrush. The site is productive for 30 or more species before the visitor crowds diminish birding quality.

## 2. Uxmal and Ruta Puuc

The Puuc hill region in southern Yucatan state has a slightly more varied terrain than the flat northern peninsula and the associated forest is noticeably more diverse. Uxmal's well-preserved pyramid complex is surrounded by taller forest than Chichen Itza, and the compound walls provide elevated perches for Canivet's Emerald hummingbird and Lesser Kiskadee. The Ruta Puuc road through the Puuc hills passes through forest where Yucatan Woodpecker, Yucatan Flycatcher, and Orange Oriole are all findable. The roadside milpa cultivation adds agricultural habitat birds including Blue Seedeater and Blue-black Grassquit. The Kabah ruins nearby have a Crested Guan presence.

## 3. Rio Lagartos Biosphere Reserve

The Rio Lagartos estuary on the northern Yucatan coast is one of the most important flamingo nesting sites in Mexico, supporting several thousand Greater Flamigos that breed on the salt flats of the shallow coastal lagoon. Boat tours from the fishing village of Rio Lagartos are the standard approach, gliding at low speed through the shallow water to view nesting flamingos at a respectful distance. The estuary mangroves hold Black Catbird, a Yucatan endemic, in the dense vegetation. Roseate Spoonbill, Wood Stork, and Jabiru all use the area. The adjacent coastal scrub along the highway has Yucatan Poorwill, detectable only at night by its distinctive call.

## 4. Dzilam de Bravo State Reserve

West of Rio Lagartos, the Dzilam reserve protects a large area of coastal mangrove and dry forest accessible on the road from Dzilam Gonzalez. The mangroves here hold both Mangrove Vireo and Mangrove Warbler, two species with specialized habitat requirements rarely found outside coastal mangroves. Northern Jacana is common on any open freshwater lagoon. The forest between the road and the coast has Mexican Sheartail, a hummingbird at the northern edge of its range, hovering at flowering shrubs. Cozumel Thrasher occasionally wanders to the mainland coastal areas from Cozumel Island. The combination of coastal and forest habitats on a single drive makes Dzilam efficient.

## 5. Calakmul Biosphere Reserve, Campeche

Calakmul is the largest protected area in Mexico and one of the most important Maya forest sites in the world, protecting a vast block of tropical semi-deciduous forest in southern Campeche. The forest here is taller and denser than in northern Yucatan, and the bird community reflects this with species typical of the Peten lowland forest of Guatemala. Ocellated Turkey — one of the most remarkable birds in the Americas, with iridescent scaled plumage and brilliant facial ornamentation — is reliable in the forest clearings near the archaeological zone at dawn. White-bellied Emerald, Mealy Amazon, and Crested Guan are all present. The forest trails accessible from the ruins parking area are productive for a full morning of birding.

## 6. Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve, Quintana Roo

Sian Ka'an on the Caribbean coast of Quintana Roo is a UNESCO World Heritage Site protecting a mix of tropical forest, mangrove, and coastal lagoon habitat. The reserve is most famous for its aquatic habitats, and the boat tours through the canal system give access to Mangrove Swallow, Snail Kite over the freshwater wetlands, and Agami Heron in the deepest shadowed channels of the mangrove interior. Magnificent Frigatebird is conspicuous over the coast, and the beach strand holds Wilson's Plover and Snowy Plover nesting in the open sand. The forest interior of Sian Ka'an north of Boca Paila adds Yucatan endemics including Black Catbird and Yucatan Flycatcher.

## 7. Palenque, Chiapas

Although technically at the edge of the Yucatan lowlands in Chiapas rather than the Yucatan Peninsula itself, Palenque is part of the same Peten lowland forest system and holds species otherwise difficult to find in the region. The ruins sit in intact rainforest, and the morning before the site opens is productive for Keel-billed Toucan — the banana-bright billed species common throughout the lowland Peten — and Collared Aracari. The forest here is wetter than elsewhere in the Yucatan, and the higher moisture supports Barred Antshrike, Dot-winged Antwren, and Wedge-billed Woodcreeper. Palenque town waterfall trail is a reliable local birding walk.

## 8. Merida and the Henequen Zone

The henequen agave haciendas around Merida, the capital of Yucatan state, create an open agricultural landscape dotted with cenotes and secondary forest that adds open-country and edge-habitat species to the Yucatan list. Groove-billed Ani is common in the agricultural margins. The cenotes around Merida, accessible as day trips from the city, have verdant vegetation that attracts concentrations of species including Ruddy Ground Dove, Olive-throated Parakeet, and the ubiquitous Great Kiskadee. The Merida Plaza Central has a resident Peregrine Falcon that hunts pigeons from the colonial buildings.

## 9. Bacalar Lagoon, Southern Quintana Roo

The Laguna Bacalar, known as the Lake of Seven Colors for its turquoise gradients, is a freshwater lagoon connected to the Hondo River system near the Belize border. The forested shores of the lagoon hold Sungrebe in the shaded margins and Bare-throated Tiger Heron in the open shallows. The town of Bacalar is a convenient base for exploring the southern Quintana Roo forest, which grades toward the Belize lowlands and adds Ivory-billed Woodcreeper and Pale-vented Pigeon to the Yucatan list. The early morning around the lake is productive for King Kingfisher and Ringed Kingfisher simultaneously visible on the same stretch of shore.

## 10. Holbox Island

Holbox is a barrier island at the northeastern tip of the Yucatan where the Gulf of Mexico meets the Caribbean, accessible by ferry from Chiquila. The shallow Yalahau lagoon between the island and the mainland is one of the more accessible flamingo viewing sites in the peninsula, with flocks visible from the ferry crossing. The island's beaches hold nesting Least Tern and Sandwich Tern from April through July. The island interior has Black Catbird in the dense coastal scrub and Tropical Mockingbird in the more open areas. The island's car-free policy and shallow lagoon access by small boat to flamingo areas make it a different experience from the mainland reserves.

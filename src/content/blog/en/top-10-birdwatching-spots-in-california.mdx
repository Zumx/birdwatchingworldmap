California supports a larger bird list than any other US state, with over 680 species recorded. The state's extraordinary geographic diversity — the Pacific coastline, the Central Valley, the Sierra Nevada, the Mojave and Sonoran deserts, and the chaparral hills of the south — creates a corresponding diversity of habitats supporting everything from shorebird concentrations of international significance to desert species found nowhere else in the United States. The state's position on the Pacific Flyway makes it one of the most important migration corridors in North America. ## 1. Point Reyes National Seashore, Marin County

Point Reyes is California's preeminent birdwatching destination, a peninsula extending into the Pacific that functions as a migration trap for both vagrant landbirds and concentrations of seabirds offshore. The Point Reyes Bird Observatory (now Point Blue Conservation Science) has operated a banding station at Palomarin since 1966, producing one of the longest continuous bird population datasets in North America. The peninsula holds 490 recorded species and is particularly notable for vagrant landbird occurrence in October, when unusual weather patterns produce remarkable accumulations. The estero system at Drakes Bay and the Tomales Bay shore are excellent for shorebirds and waterfowl.

## 2. Salton Sea, Imperial County

The Salton Sea, California's largest lake, is a hypersaline inland sea in the Sonoran Desert that has become one of the most important waterbird sites in the state despite significant ecological problems. The sea and its adjacent wetlands support enormous concentrations of migrant shorebirds and waterfowl, and the surrounding desert and agricultural land produce an impressive list of desert specialties. Burrowing Owl nests in the earthen banks; Eared Grebe masses in the tens of thousands in autumn; and the site is one of the most reliable in the United States for wintering Yellow-footed Gull, a Mexican species not regularly found elsewhere in the country.

## 3. Mono Lake, Mono County

Mono Lake is an ancient saline lake at the eastern base of the Sierra Nevada, ringed by tufa towers and supporting a simple but enormously productive ecosystem based on brine shrimp and alkali flies. The combination of abundant invertebrate food and isolated position on the eastern flank of the Sierra makes Mono Lake a critical shorebird staging site: eared grebes, Wilson's Phalaropes, and Red-necked Phalaropes stop in thousands on migration. California Gulls nest on the island. The reserve at South Tufa provides excellent access to the lakeshore.

## 4. Elkhorn Slough, Monterey County

Elkhorn Slough is the largest contiguous area of tidal marsh in California outside of San Francisco Bay, and its combination of mudflat, eelgrass beds, upland scrub, and open water provides habitat for an outstanding selection of resident and migratory birds. Tricolored Blackbird colonies nest in emergent vegetation. The slough is a consistent site for white morphs of Reddish Egret and regular shorebird concentrations. Sea kayaking the slough channels provides one of the best wildlife-watching experiences in California.

## 5. San Diego Bay and Mission Bay, San Diego County

San Diego Bay and its associated mudflats are among California's most productive estuarine sites. The endangered Light-footed Ridgway's Rail breeds in the coastal cordgrass marshes; Western Snowy Plover nests on salt pan and sandy areas near the bay margin. In winter the bay holds large concentrations of scaup, surf scoter, and other diving ducks alongside egrets and large numbers of waders. The Famosa Slough and Sweetwater Marsh National Wildlife Refuge units provide the best access.

## 6. Bolinas Lagoon, Marin County

Bolinas Lagoon south of Point Reyes is a small but highly productive tidal estuary that reliably holds large shorebird concentrations at low tide and roosting herons and egrets in the fringing marsh. The site is particularly good for viewing close-range Marbled Godwit, Long-billed Curlew, and Dunlin in winter. The adjacent Audubon Canyon Ranch protects Great Blue Heron and Great Egret nesting colonies in tall redwoods.

## 7. Upper Newport Bay Ecological Reserve, Orange County

Upper Newport Bay is a tidal estuary in suburban Orange County that functions as a significant stopover and wintering site for Pacific Flyway waterbirds. The reserve and adjacent state marine park protect around 1,000 acres of marsh and open water used by migrating White-faced Ibis, Long-billed Dowitcher concentrations, and the full range of California wintering waterfowl. The resident population of Belted Kingfisher, Black-crowned Night-Heron, and breeding Least Tern make it productive year-round.

## 8. Joshua Tree National Park, Riverside and San Bernardino Counties

Joshua Tree National Park sits at the transition between the Mojave and Sonoran Deserts and holds a combination of desert bird communities found together nowhere else in the state. Pinyon Jay, Scott's Oriole, and Ladder-backed Woodpecker are characteristic species. The oases at Cottonwood Spring and Twentynine Palms concentrate migrant landbirds in spring and fall, making the park an excellent destination for Neotropical migrants passing through desert. Le Conte's Thrasher is a sparse resident in the creosote flats.

## 9. Arcata Marsh and Wildlife Sanctuary, Humboldt County

The Arcata Marsh, a constructed wetland at the edge of Humboldt Bay in the heart of California's redwood country, is one of the state's most productive per-acre birding sites. The combination of open water, mudflat, freshwater marsh, and proximity to Humboldt Bay tidal habitat produces an outstanding bird list concentrated in a small, easily walkable area. Rare vagrant shorebirds appear regularly, and the site has a strong record of attracting unusual gulls and terns.

## 10. Kern River Preserve, Kern County

The Nature Conservancy's Kern River Preserve protects riparian forest along the South Fork Kern River in Kern County, in the southern Sierra Nevada foothills. The preserve is the most important site in California for breeding Yellow Warbler, Yellow-breasted Chat, and Summer Tanager, and is the best location in the state for Willow Flycatcher and other riparian obligate species. Breeding Bird surveys at the preserve contribute to long-term population monitoring data for riparian-dependent species that have declined severely across the California lowlands.

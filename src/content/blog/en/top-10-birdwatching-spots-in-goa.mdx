Goa is small by Indian standards — roughly 3,700 square kilometres — but its ecological diversity is extraordinary. The state straddles the transition between the coastal plain and the Western Ghats, one of the world's eight biodiversity hotspots. Within a distance of fifty kilometres, habitats range from tidal estuaries and mangrove creeks through scrub, paddy fields, and cashew plantations, to dense evergreen forest in the ghats. More than 450 bird species have been recorded here, including numerous Western Ghats endemics that are highly sought by visiting birders from across India and overseas.

The birding year in Goa has two distinct peaks. The breeding season — roughly October to April in the lowlands and a little later in the forest — coincides with the dry season when forest is open and birds are vocal. Migratory species arrive from October onwards, with shorebirds, ducks, and raptors filling the wetland sites. The monsoon months of June through September are wet and often impractical for travel, though forest clearings can hold active breeding activity.

## 1. Bondla Wildlife Sanctuary, North Goa

Bondla is the smallest of Goa's wildlife sanctuaries but contains dense moist deciduous forest on the lower slopes of the Western Ghats. It is one of the most accessible sites in Goa for forest endemics and specialities. Malabar Trogon — a jewel-like bird with a crimson belly and iridescent green back — inhabits the middle storey of the mature forest and is reliably found here. Malabar Whistling Thrush sings from stream gullies. Indian Pitta is common during winter, its rich eight-note call audible throughout the forest floor. Crested Serpent Eagle perches openly and is frequently seen. The small zoo and forest department facilities make this a comfortable entry-level forest birding destination.

## 2. Salim Ali Bird Sanctuary, Chorao Island, Panjim

Named for India's most celebrated ornithologist, the Chorao Island mangrove sanctuary is reached by ferry across the Mandovi River. The dense mangrove forest and tidal creek system is the best mangrove birding in Goa. Mangrove Whistler — a small, rare Whistler species with a highly restricted distribution — occurs here. Collared Kingfisher calls from the mangrove canopy. Brahminy Kite and White-bellied Sea Eagle hunt over the tidal channels. Waders including Terek Sandpiper and Greenshank work the exposed mudflats at low tide. The site is easily combined with a visit to Panjim and the Mandovi waterfront for casual estuarine birding.

## 3. Bhagwan Mahavir Wildlife Sanctuary and Mollem National Park, South Goa

The largest protected area in Goa, Bhagwan Mahavir encompasses the most extensive tract of undisturbed forest in the state. The forest here transitions from moist deciduous to semi-evergreen and holds the full suite of Western Ghats endemics. Malabar Grey Hornbill calls raucously from the emergent trees. Sri Lanka Frogmouth — cryptically patterned and almost invisible by day — roosts in dense forest and is best found by night with a guide. White-rumped Shama sings from the undergrowth. Jungle Owlet calls from dusk. The Dudhsagar Waterfall at the Karnataka border is surrounded by high forest and gives access to birds of the upper storey during the dry season.

## 4. Carambolim Lake, North Goa

Carambolim is a shallow freshwater lake near Khandepar that serves as a gathering point for migratory waterbirds and resident wetland species. In winter, the lake holds large numbers of ducks including Common Pochard, Ferruginous Duck, and Northern Pintail. Pheasant-tailed Jacana is common in the floating vegetation. Purple Swamphen is easily seen. Bronze-winged Jacana and Cotton Pygmy Goose breed. The lake margins support Pied Kingfisher, which hovers over the open water in conspicuous numbers. The adjacent agricultural land contributes species such as Baya Weaver, Indian Silverbill, and large flocks of munias. The site is visible from the main highway and can be quickly scanned for waders at low water levels.

## 5. Backwoods Camp, Goa-Karnataka Border

Backwoods Camp near Mollem is a private eco-camp that has developed a strong reputation as a base for serious forest birding in the Goa-Karnataka transition zone. The camp guides specialise in finding Goa's most difficult species, including Malabar Parakeet, Grey-headed Bulbul, and Brown-backed Needletail in the forest clearings at dusk. Crested Treeswift perches on exposed dead branches. The night walks here are particularly productive for Sri Lanka Frogmouth and Indian Scops Owl. The proximity of both Goa forest and the Karnataka side of the ghats means the bird list achievable during a three-day stay is among the highest available anywhere in the Western Ghats.

## 6. Cumbarjua Canal, North Goa

The narrow tidal canal connecting the Mandovi and Zuari rivers is fringed with mangroves and tidal mudflats and represents one of the best estuarine birding routes in coastal India. Scanning from road bridges and boat trips reveals Collared Kingfisher, White-throated Kingfisher, and Common Kingfisher at close range. Herons and egrets — Great Egret, Little Egret, Grey Heron, Purple Heron — line the canal banks. Horseshoe crabs are visible on the mudflats at low tide, and the associated birds include Whimbrel, Eurasian Curlew, and Bar-tailed Godwit during migration. Crocodiles resting on mudbanks are a further wildlife attraction, and the crocodile boat tours from Cortalim offer excellent birding simultaneously.

## 7. Cotigao Wildlife Sanctuary, South Goa

Cotigao in the southernmost part of Goa is the least visited of the state's sanctuaries and contains some of the most pristine dry deciduous and mixed deciduous forest in the ghats foothills. The forest watchtower over a waterhole is excellent at dawn for Indian Giant Squirrel, Gaur, and the birds that come to drink — including Painted Spurfowl, Grey Junglefowl, and Malabar Pied Hornbill. Nilgiri Wood Pigeon feeds in the fruiting trees. Red Spurfowl calls at dusk. The sanctuary is most productive during the dry season when animals concentrate at water sources and the undergrowth thins enough to allow views into the forest interior.

## 8. Baina Beach and South Goa Mudflats, Vasco da Gama

The extensive tidal mudflats and mangrove-fringed inlets around Vasco da Gama in South Goa are among the best shorebird sites in the state during the October to March migratory season. Large-billed Tern fishes in the sheltered waters. Lesser Sand Plover, Greater Sand Plover, Dunlin, and Knot all occur on the mudflats in winter. Crab Plover — a distinctive, pied, crab-eating wader with a heavy bill — is an annual visitor to these flats, unusual this far north along the Indian west coast. The industrial character of the area is offset by its genuine ornithological value, and local birding clubs run regular site visits.

## 9. Netravali Wildlife Sanctuary, South Goa

Netravali, in the extreme southeast of Goa along the Karnatakam border, is the newest and in some respects the most exciting protected area in the state. The forest is primarily semi-evergreen and supports both Nilgiri Langur and Indian Leopard, and the bird community is correspondingly rich. Malabar Trogon, Indian Pitta, and Malabar Whistling Thrush are all present. The Netravali Bubble Lake — a curious natural feature where bubbles of carbon dioxide emerge from the forest floor — is a known gathering point for wildlife and birds in the dry season. The sanctuary has minimal visitor facilities, requiring self-sufficient planning, but rewards those who invest the effort with genuine wilderness birding.

## 10. Anjuna and Vagator Cliffs, North Goa

The laterite sea cliffs and cashew scrubland around Anjuna and Vagator are surprisingly productive for birds beyond the better-known sanctuary sites. Coppersmith Barbet and Brown-headed Barbet are conspicuous in the fruiting trees. Indian Roller perches on telephone wires and fence posts and is conspicuous in flight. Shikra hunts over the gardens and scrub. The cliff faces and rocky outcrops hold Indian Peafowl year-round. During April and May, Large Cuckooshrike and Thick-billed Cuckoo are present in the taller trees. The evening light on the cliffs and the accessibility by scooter along the coastal roads make this an enjoyable add-on to a beach holiday that incorporates casual birding.

---

Goa's combination of Western Ghats forest endemics and coastal migratory species makes it a genuinely diverse destination that repays a birding trip structured around habitat variety rather than a single flagship site.

Ethiopia is the great endemic stronghold of north-east Africa, a high tableland cut by the Great Rift Valley and ringed by escarpments that have isolated bird populations for long enough to produce more than thirty species found nowhere else. For birders the appeal is twofold: the highland endemics of the Abyssinian plateau and the Bale Mountains, and the spectacular abundance of the Rift Valley lakes, where flamingos, pelicans, and storks gather in the tens of thousands. A typical circuit climbs from Addis Ababa onto the moorlands above 4,000 metres, drops to the acacia savanna and lakes of the Rift, and pushes south into the arid borderlands, sampling four distinct avifaunas in a single loop. The combination of accessibility, scenery, and a long endemic list makes Ethiopia one of Africa's premier birding destinations.

## Bale Mountains National Park

The Bale Mountains hold the largest area of Afro-alpine habitat on the continent, and the Sanetti Plateau above 4,000 metres is the single best place in Ethiopia for highland endemics. The plateau is home to the Blue-winged Goose, Spot-breasted Lapwing, Rouget's Rail, and Ethiopian endemics such as the Abyssinian Catbird and the Bale-restricted form of warblers, while the Ethiopian wolf hunts giant mole-rats across the open moorland. Below the plateau, the Harenna Forest on the southern slopes holds Abyssinian Owl, Yellow-fronted Parrot, and a different montane forest community. Bale is the indispensable highland stop on any Ethiopian itinerary.

## Gaysay Grasslands and Dinsho

The Gaysay valley near the Dinsho park headquarters, at around 3,000 metres, is an easy and immensely productive area of highland grassland, juniper woodland, and marsh. Mornings here produce Abyssinian Longclaw, Wattled Ibis, Blue-winged Goose, and Rouget's Rail in the wet grassland, with Abyssinian Catbird and White-backed Black Tit in the woodland. Mountain Nyala and Menelik's bushbuck graze openly. The gentle terrain and concentration of endemics close to the road make Gaysay one of the most rewarding birding mornings in the whole country.

## Lake Awassa

Lake Awassa, a freshwater Rift Valley lake fringed by reedbeds and fig forest, is one of the most accessible and bird-rich sites in Ethiopia. The lakeshore and the famous fish market attract Marabou Storks, pelicans, herons, and African Fish Eagles at close range, while the surrounding scrub and gardens hold Black-winged Lovebird, Spotted Creeper, and Bruce's Green Pigeon. A boat trip out onto the lake adds jacanas, crakes, and an array of kingfishers. Awassa is an excellent introduction to Rift Valley birding and a comfortable base on the southern route.

## Lake Ziway and Lake Langano

The chain of Rift Valley lakes south of Addis offers a sequence of contrasting wetlands. Lake Ziway, shallow and reed-fringed, holds enormous numbers of pelicans, herons, storks, and waders, with the jetty area allowing close photography of feeding waterbirds. Nearby Lake Langano, brown with sediment and ringed by acacia woodland, is a base for the surrounding bush, where Black-winged Lovebird, woodpeckers, and a range of bush species are found. The Bishangari and Bilen woodlands near Langano are reliable for many Rift Valley specialities.

## Lake Abijatta-Shalla National Park

This twin-lake park protects an alkaline lake, Abijatta, and a deep crater lake, Shalla, separated by a narrow neck of acacia woodland. Abijatta is the great flamingo lake of Ethiopia: in good years tens of thousands of Lesser and Greater Flamingos gather on its soda flats, alongside Great White Pelicans, avocets, and migrant waders. The hot springs at the lake edge and the surrounding woodland add Ostrich, bustards, and a range of larks and wheatears. Water levels fluctuate dramatically, so flamingo numbers vary, but at its best Abijatta is a spectacle.

## Awash National Park

Awash, on the road east toward Dire Dawa, protects acacia savanna, riverine forest along the Awash River, and the dramatic Awash Falls. The park is a stronghold for dry-country species: Arabian Bustard, Hartlaub's Bustard, Secretarybird, Northern Carmine Bee-eater, and a wealth of larks and coursers on the plains. The riverine forest holds different birds again, with hornbills, barbets, and kingfishers, while the falls area is good for swifts and raptors. Awash gives a taste of the hot, arid avifauna that dominates the lowlands east and south.

## Ankober Escarpment

The escarpment near the historic town of Ankober, northeast of Addis, drops dramatically from the highland plateau to the Rift lowlands and is the type locality of the Ankober Serin, a tiny endemic finch restricted to the cliffs. The grassy plateau edge also holds Erckel's Francolin, Moorland Chat, and large raptor numbers riding the updrafts along the escarpment. Gelada baboons graze the slopes in troops. The combination of a near-guaranteed endemic and the spectacular cliff scenery makes Ankober a classic day trip from the capital.

## Debre Libanos and the Jemma Valley

The gorge country north of Addis around Debre Libanos and the Jemma Valley is the best area for several northern highland endemics. The cliffs and gorges hold White-billed Starling, White-winged Cliff Chat, and the much-wanted Harwood's Francolin in the Jemma Valley, alongside lammergeier and other cliff-nesting raptors. The famous Portuguese Bridge at Debre Libanos offers Gelada baboons at close range and Rüppell's Vultures overhead. The descent into the Jemma gorge is one of the premier endemic-hunting routes in the country.

## Negele Borana and the Liben Plain

The arid south around Negele Borana holds some of Ethiopia's rarest and most localised birds. The Liben Plain (Liben/Daka Dima) is the last refuge of the critically endangered Liben Lark, one of Africa's most threatened birds, on its shrinking patch of natural grassland. The surrounding acacia bush holds Stresemann's Bushcrow and White-tailed Swallow, two endemics restricted to a tiny area around Yabello and Mega. This remote corner is essential for the southern endemics but requires real effort to reach.

## Yabello and Mega

The Yabello area in the far south is the core range of two of Ethiopia's most prized endemics, Stresemann's Bushcrow and the White-tailed Swallow, both confined to a small zone of acacia savanna around Yabello and Mega for reasons still not fully understood. The Yabello Wildlife Sanctuary and the surrounding bush also hold Vulturine Guineafowl, Magpie Starling, and a rich dry-country community of larks, weavers, and bustards. This is the southernmost stop on the classic endemic circuit and rounds out an Ethiopian list with species shared with neighbouring Kenya.

## Explore on the map

An Ethiopian birding circuit is essentially a loop through altitude and aridity: the high moorlands of Bale and the northern escarpments for highland endemics, the Rift Valley lakes for waterbird spectacle, and the southern lowlands for the bushcrow, the swallow, and the Liben Lark. Distances are considerable and roads vary, so most birders take ten days to two weeks to link the key sites.

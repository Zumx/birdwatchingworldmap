Papua New Guinea is the spiritual home of the birds-of-paradise, and no other country on Earth concentrates such evolutionary extravagance. The island of New Guinea hosts the majority of the world's roughly 45 birds-of-paradise species, alongside bowerbirds that build elaborate decorated stages, jewel-like kingfishers, and the world's only known poisonous birds, the pitohuis. Birding here demands effort — trails are steep, accommodation is basic, and dawn comes early — but the reward is witnessing courtship displays unchanged for millions of years, from the head-shaking of the Raggiana to the cleared dance courts of the Carola's parotia. The country's bird list exceeds 750 species, with high endemism throughout.

## Varirata National Park

Just an hour from the capital Port Moresby, Varirata National Park is the most accessible quality birding site in the country and protects savanna and hill forest on the Sogeri Plateau at around 800 metres. It is the classic site for the Raggiana bird-of-paradise — the national bird — whose males gather at traditional display trees to shake their orange flank plumes. Growling riflebird, the bizarre eastern riflebird, dwarf cassowary, and the brown-headed paradise-kingfisher also occur, and the forest edges are alive with honeyeaters and fruit-doves.

## Tari and the Tari Gap, Southern Highlands

The Tari basin in the Southern Highlands, homeland of the Huli people, sits around 1,600 metres and rises into the mossy montane forest of the Tari Gap at over 2,700 metres. This is one of the richest birds-of-paradise areas on Earth. Around Ambua and the gap you can find King of Saxony bird-of-paradise with its absurd head wires, ribbon-tailed astrapia with the longest tail feathers relative to body size of any bird, blue bird-of-paradise, Lawes's parotia, and brown sicklebill. The Huli wigmen famously incorporate these plumes into their ceremonial dress.

## Kumul Lodge, Mount Hagen area

In the Western Highlands near Mount Hagen, Kumul Lodge sits at about 2,860 metres and is celebrated for its feeding table, where birds come to fruit at close range. Ribbon-tailed astrapia, brown sicklebill, Belford's melidectes, and the brilliant crested berrypecker visit the platform within metres of observers. The surrounding moss forest holds the elusive Archbold's bowerbird and a range of high-altitude honeyeaters and robins, making it one of the easiest places to photograph montane specialities.

## Kiunga and the Fly River lowlands

The town of Kiunga on the Fly River in Western Province is the gateway to superb lowland rainforest birding. Boat trips on the Fly and Elevala rivers and walks to known display sites give access to the greater bird-of-paradise, the king bird-of-paradise, the magnificent riflebird, and the twelve-wired bird-of-paradise displaying on dead snags over the swamp. The lowland forest also holds Pesquet's parrot, southern crowned pigeon, and a wealth of parrots, hornbills, and kingfishers.

## Karawari and the Sepik lowlands

The Karawari river region, a tributary system of the Sepik in East Sepik Province, offers river-based access to extensive lowland forest from rustic lodges. The forest here holds the magnificent and lesser birds-of-paradise, palm cockatoo with its drumming display, the Sepik specialities, and a rich community of fruit-doves and pigeons. Travel is largely by dugout and motorboat through a landscape of meandering rivers and oxbow lakes.

## Mount Wilhelm and the Central Range

Papua New Guinea's highest peak, Mount Wilhelm at 4,509 metres, offers a remarkable altitudinal transect from montane forest through subalpine grassland to alpine zones. Birders ascending from the Keglsugl trailhead pass through the range of the crested satinbird, painted tiger-parrot, and the alpine pipit near the summit lakes of Piunde and Aunde. The high-altitude shrubbery holds snow mountain robin and various honeyeaters found only at these elevations.

## Crater Mountain, Eastern Highlands

The Crater Mountain Wildlife Management Area protects a vast tract of forest spanning lowland to montane elevations in a community-run conservation area. It is one of the best sites for the MacGregor's bowerbird, whose maypole bowers are decorated meticulously, and for a range of birds-of-paradise across the altitude gradient. The remoteness and the involvement of local landowners make this a model of community conservation and a genuinely wild birding experience.

## Lake Kutubu

The largest highland lake in Papua New Guinea, Lake Kutubu in the Southern Highlands sits at about 800 metres surrounded by forested hills and limestone. The lake and its catchment support a mix of waterbirds and forest species, and the surrounding forest holds birds-of-paradise and the rare New Guinea harpy eagle hunting over the canopy. The lake itself is famous for its endemic fish and its strikingly clear water, and the local communities maintain strong conservation traditions.

## New Britain and the Bismarck Archipelago

The island of New Britain, off the eastern mainland, holds a distinct set of island endemics not found on the New Guinea mainland. Around Walindi and the Kimbe Bay forests you can find the New Britain kingfisher, Bismarck hanging parrot, New Britain boobook, and the spectacular nesting colonies of the Melanesian megapode, which incubates its eggs in volcanically heated sand. The island's lowland forest and volcanic landscapes make it a rewarding extension to a mainland trip.

## Wau and the Bulolo Valley, Morobe

The Wau valley in Morobe Province, historically a goldfield, sits in a forested basin around 1,100 metres and rises into montane forest on the surrounding ranges. The area around the former Wau Ecology Institute is good for the superb bird-of-paradise, Lawes's parotia, and a range of montane honeyeaters and robins. The mosaic of regenerating forest, gardens, and primary forest on the slopes provides varied birding within a compact area.

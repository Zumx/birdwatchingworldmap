Visit a marsh in late July and the ducks look wrong. The Mallard drake with his glossy green head has become a mottled brown bird barely distinguishable from his mate. The Northern Pintail's elegant chocolate-and-white pattern has dissolved. The Wood Duck male, normally one of the most ornate waterfowl on earth, looks like a dull female with a strange red eye. This is eclipse plumage — the cryptic, female-like plumage that male dabbling ducks adopt for several weeks each summer — and it briefly turns familiar species into a puzzle that defeats many birders who only watch ducks in winter.

## Why drakes go into eclipse

The reason is wing molt. Ducks, unlike most birds, shed all their flight feathers simultaneously and become flightless for roughly three to four weeks in mid to late summer. A bright, conspicuous drake is dangerously visible to predators while it cannot fly. Eclipse plumage solves this by replacing the gaudy breeding feathers with a drab, brown, female-like body plumage that provides camouflage during the vulnerable flightless period. Once the new flight feathers grow in, the drakes begin a second molt back toward their bright alternate plumage through autumn, so that most are recognizable again by the time winter arrives.

The result is a window, roughly June through September depending on species and latitude, when male dabbling ducks look very different from their familiar winter selves.

## Forget head color and read structure instead

The single most important adjustment is to stop relying on the bright head and body colors that define drakes in winter. In eclipse those colors are gone or muted. What does not change is structure: the size and shape of the head, the length and shape of the bill, the proportions of the body and neck, and the bird's overall jizz on the water.

The Northern Pintail keeps its long, slim neck and elongated rear even in eclipse. The Northern Shoveler keeps its enormous spatulate bill, instantly diagnostic regardless of plumage. The American Wigeon and Eurasian Wigeon keep their short, blue-gray bills and round heads with a steep forehead. The Gadwall keeps its trim, gray, steep-foreheaded look and orange-edged dark bill. Learn the structural silhouette of each species and eclipse plumage stops being an obstacle.

## Bare parts are the secret weapon

Bill color and pattern, leg color and eye color frequently remain diagnostic when body plumage does not. This is the eclipse-season specialist's most reliable tool. The male Mallard retains a yellow-olive bill while the female's bill is orange blotched with black — so an eclipse Mallard with a clean yellowish bill is a male. The male Northern Shoveler keeps a black bill; the female's is orange. The drake Wood Duck retains a red eye and a red-and-white patterned bill even in eclipse, when the female shows a dark eye in a teardrop white eye-ring.

Among teal, the Green-winged Teal keeps a small dark bill while the Blue-winged and Cinnamon keep larger bills, and the powder-blue forewing patch shown in flight is diagnostic across all plumages. Always check the bill before the body — it often answers the question outright.

## The speculum never lies

The speculum — the iridescent patch on the secondary flight feathers — is present in both sexes and across all plumages, and it is one of the most reliable identification features for confusing brown ducks. Get a bird to stretch, preen or fly and note the speculum's color and the color of the bars bordering it.

The Mallard shows a blue speculum bordered by white on both sides. The Gadwall shows a white speculum, unique among dabbling ducks and visible even at rest. The Green-winged Teal shows a green speculum with a buff leading bar. The Northern Pintail shows a bronze-green speculum with a broad cinnamon leading edge and white trailing edge. Even a brief flap as a duck settles can reveal the speculum and clinch an identification that the dull body plumage would never deliver.

## Diving ducks and the easier cases

Diving ducks generally show less dramatic eclipse change than dabblers, and many are easier in late summer. Still, structure remains key. The Common Goldeneye and Bufflehead keep their peaked or puffy head shapes; the Lesser and Greater Scaup keep their head profiles and bill shapes; the Canvasback and Redhead keep their distinctive head-and-bill silhouettes that separate them even when the rich head colors fade.

Mergansers undergo eclipse too: the male Common Merganser and Red-breasted Merganser take on female-like plumages but retain their long, thin, serrated red bills, an unmistakable structural feature. When in doubt with any diving duck, prioritize the head-and-bill shape over color, just as with the dabblers.

## A practical late-summer routine

Eclipse season is an excellent time to deepen duck skills precisely because it forces good habits. Treat every brown duck as an exercise: first the structure and size, then the bill shape and bare-part colors, then the speculum if the bird obliges, and only last any residual body coloration. Carry the assumption that a flock of brown Mallard-shaped birds in August contains both sexes and that bill color will sort them.

Use the season's timing to your advantage. By September many drakes are already showing patchy returns of breeding color — a few green feathers on a Mallard's head, a hint of chestnut on a teal's flank — and these transitional birds, awkward as they look, are often easier than the full eclipse of high summer. Keep notes on which species you found hardest; the bare-parts and speculum habits you build now will serve you through every winter that follows.

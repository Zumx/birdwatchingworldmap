The United States has a national birding culture of remarkable depth, driven by some of the world's largest birding organizations — the American Birding Association, the National Audubon Society, and Cornell Lab of Ornithology — and supported by the most comprehensive eBird data coverage of any country on earth. Its bird list of over 900 species reflects the country's extraordinary geographic range, from Arctic Alaska to subtropical Florida and Texas. The ten sites below are selected for their combination of species diversity, proven birder infrastructure, and significance within the North American birding tradition. The map database includes numerous named birdwatching sites in the United States, from Texas blinds to Midwestern hawk watches.

## 1. Magee Marsh, Ohio

Magee Marsh on the Lake Erie shore is the premier spring warbler site in North America. During the peak week in early May, migrating warblers pile up at the lake edge as they prepare to cross the lake northward; the result is dozens of species at close range in the lakeside boardwalk forest. Cape May Warbler (Setophaga tigrina), Blackburnian Warbler (Setophaga fusca), and Bay-breasted Warbler (Setophaga castanea) are regularly countable in the hundreds. The annual Biggest Week in American Birding festival, held during peak migration, attracts thousands of participants.

## 2. Cape May, New Jersey

Cape May is the most celebrated multi-season birding site in eastern North America. The peninsula's autumn raptor migration, visible from Cape May Point State Park's hawk watch platform, counts hundreds of thousands of raptors in a good season. Sharp-shinned Hawk (Accipiter striatus), Merlin (Falco columbarius), Peregrine Falcon (Falco peregrinus), and Cooper's Hawk (Accipiter cooperii) are the primary species; in a northwest-wind October morning, all four can be counted simultaneously overhead. The hawk watch platform is an eBird hotspot with one of the most complete autumn data records in the country.

## 3. High Island, Texas

High Island is a small wooded hill on the Gulf Coast plain of Texas, a critical landfall for Neotropical migrants crossing the Gulf of Mexico in spring. After a "fallout" — migrants grounded by headwinds or cold fronts — the woods at Boy Scout Woods and Smith Oaks become filled with exhausted warblers, tanagers, and orioles at arm's reach. Prothonotary Warbler (Protonotaria citrea), Baltimore Oriole (Icterus galbula), and Indigo Bunting (Passerina cyanea) crowd every branch.

## 4. Hawk Mountain Sanctuary, Pennsylvania

Hawk Mountain, established in 1934 as the world's first refuge for birds of prey, counts raptor migration from its South and North Lookouts overlooking the Kittatinny Ridge. Broad-winged Hawk, Red-tailed Hawk (Buteo jamaicensis), and Sharp-shinned Hawk make up the bulk of the 20,000 to 30,000 raptors counted annually. The Hawk Watch Platform site (documented in the map database) represents the tradition of US hawk watching this site established.

## 5. Bosque del Apache National Wildlife Refuge, New Mexico

Bosque del Apache in the Rio Grande Valley produces one of the most spectacular winter wildlife spectacles in North America. Sandhill Crane (Antigone canadensis) winter in the thousands alongside Snow Goose (Anser caerulescens), Ross's Goose (Anser rossii), and a complete suite of wintering ducks. The annual "Festival of the Cranes" in November coincides with peak crane concentrations.

## 6. Dry Tortugas National Park, Florida

The remote Dry Tortugas, accessible only by ferry or seaplane from Key West, hold the most important Sooty Tern (Onychoprion fuscatus) breeding colony in North America and regularly produce the rarest spring migrants in the eastern United States — exhausted Neotropical vagrants that overshoot Florida. The park's single island, Fort Jefferson, concentrates migrants so reliably that the American Birding Association runs annual tours.

## 7. Santa Ana National Wildlife Refuge and Bentsen-Rio Grande Valley State Park, Texas

The Lower Rio Grande Valley holds the northernmost extension of the Mexican and Central American bird fauna. Green Jay (Cyanocorax yncas), Altamira Oriole (Icterus gularis), Ringed Kingfisher (Megaceryle torquata), and Plain Chachalaca (Ortalis vetula) are the showpiece species. The Warbler Central, Green Jay Blind, and Kiskadee Blind (documented in the map database) at the Santa Ana complex are among the most productive managed blinds in North America.

## 8. Malheur National Wildlife Refuge, Oregon

Malheur in the high desert of eastern Oregon provides a remote interior wetland with Western and Eastern bird species at their range junction. Sandhill Crane, Greater Sage-Grouse (Centrocercus urophasianus, Near Threatened), and the full Great Basin shorebird community use the refuge's extensive marsh system.

## 9. Point Reyes National Seashore, California

Point Reyes on the Marin County coast is the most species-rich birding site in California, combining coastal scrub, forest, estuary, and headland in a protected peninsula that functions as a migrant trap. Its position at the edge of the continent makes it a regular vagrant location; the Drake's Beach and Limantour Estero areas are particularly productive.

## 10. Presque Isle State Park, Pennsylvania

Presque Isle, a recurving sand spit in Lake Erie, is one of the few accessible Great Lakes sites on the southern shore. Its position makes it a migrant trap for spring and autumn passerines; the various hawk watch platforms on the peninsula count migrating raptors. Breeding Piping Plover (Charadrius melodus, Near Threatened) occurs on the protected beach sections.

## Planning a United States birding trip

The United States' continental scale means that birding trips must be planned by region — the continent is too large for a comprehensive approach, and each biogeographic zone has its own endemic and specialist community. The most productive single region for pure species density is southeastern Arizona in late May and June, when the Mexican highland community arrives for the breeding season while the resident resident desert and mountain species are at their most active.

Southeast Arizona's "sky island" mountain ranges — Huachuca, Chiricahua, Santa Rita, and Rincon — rise from the Sonoran Desert floor at 900 metres to pine-fir forest above 2,700 metres. The resulting habitat stacking within a small geographic area produces extraordinary species lists: Elegant Trogon (Trogon elegans), Montezuma Quail (Cyrtonyx montezumae), and Thick-billed Kingbird (Tyrannus crassirostris) all occur within a short drive of Tucson. The Huachuca Mountains' Ramsey Canyon preserve is one of the premier hummingbird watching sites in North America with over 15 species recorded.

The Atlantic coast migration corridor in the eastern United States concentrates shorebirds, raptors, and passerines during fall migration (August to November). Cape May, New Jersey — accessible from Philadelphia — is the most famous North American migration watchpoint: on days of northwest wind in October, thousands of Sharp-shinned Hawks, Cooper's Hawks, and Merlins funnel through the peninsula point. Hawk Mountain Sanctuary in Pennsylvania holds the longest-running raptor migration count in North America.

Texas's Gulf Coast (the High Island preserve system), the Rio Grande Valley (Santa Ana and Bentsen state parks), and the Big Bend region provide three distinct Texas bird communities accessible from Houston or San Antonio. The Rio Grande Valley holds the most Mexican-affinity species in the US: Altamira Oriole (Icterus gularis), Tropical Parula (Setophaga pitiayumi), and Green Jay (Cyanocorax yncas) are near-endemic to this narrow strip.

The Pacific Northwest — the Olympic Peninsula, Malheur National Wildlife Refuge — and Alaska provide the western US bird community most different from the east. Alaska's Pribilof Islands hold the most accessible North Pacific seabird colony in North America, with Least Auklet (Aethia pusilla) and Red-legged Kittiwake accessible from St. Paul Island's limited accommodation.

Egypt sits on one of the planet's great migratory bottlenecks. Twice a year, hundreds of thousands of soaring birds — White Storks, Honey Buzzards, Steppe Eagles and Levant Sparrowhawks — funnel down the eastern Mediterranean, cross the narrow land bridge of Sinai and the Suez region, and stream south along the Red Sea coast and the Nile Valley to avoid the open water. Wedged between the African savanna and the Eurasian steppe, the country offers an unlikely richness: papyrus marshes alive with herons, hyper-arid wadis where Desert Larks and Sandgrouse hold on, and the world's most important staging area for soaring raptors at the Gulf of Suez. More than 480 species have been recorded.

## 1. Suez and the Gulf of Suez

The northern Gulf of Suez is the single most important raptor migration corridor in the country. In autumn, soaring birds reluctant to cross open sea concentrate at the narrowing funnel between the Eastern Desert and the gulf, passing in their tens of thousands. The Ain Sokhna stretch and the ridges behind El Suweis produce huge streams of White Stork (Ciconia ciconia), Honey Buzzard (Pernis apivorus), Steppe Buzzard (Buteo buteo vulpinus) and Steppe Eagle (Aquila nipalensis, Endangered). Spring passage in March and April reverses the flow northward.

## 2. Sharm el-Sheikh and southern Sinai

The mangroves at Nabq Protected Area and the wadis behind Sharm el-Sheikh combine African and Palearctic species. White-eyed Gull (Ichthyaetus leucophthalmus) and Sooty Gull (Ichthyaetus hemprichii) patrol the Red Sea shore, while the desert interior holds Sinai Rosefinch (Carpodacus synoicus), the iconic bird of the high Sinai, along with Hooded Wheatear and Sand Partridge. The reefs and shallow bays attract Western Reef Heron and Greater Sand Plover on passage.

## 3. Wadi El Rayan and Lake Qarun, Faiyum

The Faiyum Depression, southwest of Cairo, holds two of Egypt's most reliable wetlands. Lake Qarun, a brackish endorheic lake, supports large numbers of wintering ducks, Greater Flamingo (Phoenicopterus roseus) and Slender-billed Gull. The freshwater lakes and reedbeds of Wadi El Rayan add Senegal Thick-knee, Painted Snipe and the localized Kittlitz's Plover. The desert margins of the protected area hold Cream-coloured Courser and Bar-tailed Lark.

## 4. Abu Simbel and Lake Nasser

The far south, around the temples of Abu Simbel, offers Egypt's most African birding. Lake Nasser's shoreline and the surrounding desert hold African Pied Wagtail, Pink-backed Pelican, Yellow-billed Stork and the localized African Skimmer. Long-tailed Cormorant and Egyptian Goose are common. The site is the most reliable place in the country for genuinely Afrotropical species reaching their northern limit.

## 5. Aswan and the First Cataract

Around Aswan, the Nile breaks into channels and islands rich in birdlife. The islands below the Old Dam hold breeding African Pied Wagtail and Senegal Thick-knee, while the river produces Striated Heron, Pied Kingfisher in great numbers, and Egyptian Goose. A felucca trip among the islands at dawn is one of the most pleasant ways to bird the upper Nile.

## 6. Luxor and the West Bank

The cultivated fields, canals and palm groves of the Luxor West Bank are excellent for resident and wintering birds. Egyptian Plover, the legendary crocodile bird, is occasionally found on sandbars upriver, while the fields hold Senegal Coucal, Graceful Prinia, and large numbers of Bluethroat (Luscinia svecica) in winter. Black-shouldered Kite hunts over the agriculture, and the desert edge produces wheatears and larks.

## 7. The Nile Delta: Lake Burullus and Lake Manzala

The northern delta lagoons are internationally important Ramsar wetlands. Lake Burullus, a brackish coastal lake, holds vast numbers of wintering waterfowl, including Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened) and large concentrations of terns. Lake Manzala, despite degradation, still supports breeding herons and remains a key staging point. These wetlands are the wintering ground for waterbirds from across the Western Palearctic.

## 8. Wadi Hagul and the Eastern Desert

The wadis of the Eastern Desert behind Suez are the place for true desert specialists. Wadi Hagul holds Desert Lark, Hooded Wheatear, White-crowned Wheatear, Trumpeter Finch (Bucanetes githagineus) and Sand Partridge. Spotted and Crowned Sandgrouse come to drink at any standing water. Lappet-faced Vulture and Sooty Falcon occur in the more remote parts of the desert.

## 9. Hurghada and the Red Sea coast

The Red Sea coast between Hurghada and Safaga combines coastal and offshore birding. The mangrove islands and the shallows hold breeding White-eyed Gull, Sooty Gull, Bridled Tern, White-cheeked Tern and Lesser Crested Tern. Boat trips can produce Brown Booby and, in the right season, Crab-plover (Dromas ardeola) on the exposed reef flats and sandbanks.

## 10. Saint Catherine and the High Sinai

The granite massif around Mount Catherine, Egypt's highest point at 2,629 metres, holds a montane desert community found nowhere else in the country. Sinai Rosefinch is the flagship species, joined by Tristram's Starling, Scrub Warbler, White-crowned Wheatear and the occasional Verreaux's Eagle (Aquila verreauxii). The high wadis and orchards of the Bedouin gardens are the most reliable place to search.

## Planning a birding trip in Egypt

Egypt's birding peaks in the migration seasons. Autumn passage (late August through October) brings the spectacular raptor and stork concentrations at Suez and along the Red Sea coast, with the largest movements on days of light tailwinds. Spring passage (March to May) reverses the flow and adds breeding-plumage waders and a steady stream of passerine migrants through the oases and along the coast. Winter (November to February) is the season for the delta and Faiyum wetlands, when waterfowl, gulls and wintering passerines are at their peak.

Cairo is the practical hub. The Faiyum sites are a comfortable day trip southwest; the Suez raptor watchpoints are within two hours to the east. For the African specialties of Lake Nasser and Abu Simbel, an internal flight to Aswan saves a long drive. Sinai requires its own itinerary, combining the Red Sea coast at Sharm el-Sheikh with the high mountains at Saint Catherine.

A few practical notes: water is the key to desert birding, so dawn visits to any reservoir, garden or seep are productive. Photography near military or border areas, particularly in Sinai and around the Suez Canal, is sensitive, so a local guide is strongly recommended in those regions. The desert heat is severe from late spring onward, making early starts essential. Egypt rewards patience: the same landscapes that hold the antiquities funnel the migration, and a single ridge above the Gulf of Suez in October can deliver more soaring birds in a morning than most birders see in a year.

The Secretary Bird is Africa's most anomalous raptor and one of the most distinctive birds on the continent. Sagittarius serpentarius stands approximately 1.3 metres tall, walks on long, crane-like legs across open grassland, and kills snakes and other prey by stomping them to death with powerful feet — a hunting method unique among diurnal raptors. Its name is commonly attributed to the long, quill-like crest feathers that suggest a Victorian secretary with quill pens tucked behind the ear; the alternative etymology derives the name from the Arabic saqr-et-tair, "hunter bird."

The Secretary Bird is the sole member of the family Sagittariidae and the order Cathartiformes — or, depending on the phylogenetic treatment used, placed in its own order Sagittariiformes as the sister group to the New World vultures. It is an evolutionary puzzle: a raptor that forsook flight as its primary hunting method and reinvented itself as a terrestrial predator on the African savanna, yet retains fully functional large wings and soars readily to thermal height when traveling between territories.

## Anatomy and Locomotion

The Secretary Bird's legs are its most distinctive anatomical feature: long, grey-scaled, crane-like, and powerful enough to deliver strikes of force sufficient to kill or disable large venomous snakes in a fraction of a second. The tarsal bones are elongated and the feet are short and broad — lacking the gripping talons typical of forest raptors — reflecting adaptation for stamping rather than grasping prey. The thick scales on the lower legs provide some protection against venomous strikes from cornered snakes.

The bird's gait is distinctive: it walks steadily through grass, covering large distances each day in search of prey, occasionally pausing to stamp at prey items located by visual detection. The hunting action against a snake involves a rapid foot-stamp, or a series of stamps, at the prey's head, followed by a toss into the air and a swallow head-first. Prey items include grasshoppers, beetles, lizards, small mammals, birds' eggs and chicks, and snakes including large venomous species such as puff adders and cobras.

## Breeding Biology

Secretary Birds nest in flat-topped acacia trees, constructing large stick platforms that are reused over multiple years and grow to considerable size. The nest is typically located 5 to 7 metres above the ground, often in trees along drainage lines or in isolated trees on open plains that provide good visibility. A clutch of two to three eggs is incubated by both parents for approximately 42 to 46 days.

The chicks grow slowly, spending around 65 to 80 days in the nest before fledging. The parents deliver food — both whole prey items and regurgitated matter — throughout the nestling period. Secretary Birds often produce only one or two chicks successfully from a clutch of three due to differential chick growth rates and prey availability. Breeding in East Africa and southern Africa typically occurs in the dry season, when grassland is shorter and prey more visible.

## Distribution and Habitat

The Secretary Bird is a species of open sub-Saharan Africa, occurring from Senegal and Sudan south through East Africa to the Cape. It prefers open grassland, savanna, and open bush — habitats where its walking hunting technique is effective and where prey detection is not obstructed by tall vegetation or closed woodland. It avoids dense forest, dense scrub, and desert.

The highest densities are in the long-grass and mixed savanna of East Africa — the Serengeti-Mara ecosystem in Tanzania and Kenya, the Amboseli-Tsavo corridor, and the grasslands of the Mara Triangle. In southern Africa, the Highveld grasslands of South Africa, the Karoo, and the open grasslands of Botswana and Zimbabwe support the largest populations. The Kruger National Park and the Kgalagadi Transfrontier Park provide reliable viewing.

In West Africa, the species occurs in the Guinea savanna zone but at lower densities than in East and southern Africa.

## Conservation Status and Threats

The IUCN reclassified the Secretary Bird from Least Concern to Vulnerable in 2016, reflecting a significant and continuing population decline across its range. Long-term monitoring data from South Africa, Zimbabwe, and across the range indicate declines of 30 to 60 percent over recent decades. The causes are multiple.

The conversion of open grassland to cultivated land, tree encroachment into grassland due to fire suppression, and the intensification of pastoralism all reduce the Secretary Bird's hunting habitat. Collision with powerlines is a significant mortality source — the species flies at heights that intersect powerline corridors, and installations of bird diverters and insulating covers on lines across key Secretary Bird areas have been implemented as mitigation measures in South Africa.

Pesticide use in grasslands, particularly poisoned grain laid for rodents and locust control, incidentally poisons Secretary Birds that take affected prey. Secondary poisoning from carcasses laced for large predators — a problem affecting a range of African raptors — has also been documented as a mortality source.

The Secretary Bird's situation reflects a wider crisis in African grassland biodiversity: the open landscapes it requires are among the most converted and degraded habitats on the continent. Conservation efforts focused on maintaining large, unfragmented grassland tracts — the Bushveld-Highveld transition of South Africa, the Serengeti ecosystem, and the Kafue Flats of Zambia — directly benefit the Secretary Bird and dozens of other grassland-dependent species.

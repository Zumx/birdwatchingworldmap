Laos remains one of the most thinly birded countries in mainland Southeast Asia, and that is precisely its appeal. The landlocked kingdom is dominated by rugged limestone karst, the great winding artery of the Mekong, and high forested plateaus that have yielded several species described only in recent decades — among them the bare-faced bulbul, found on the limestone of Khammouane and named as recently as 2009, and the limestone leaf warbler. With a national bird list of well over 700 species and large tracts of forest still poorly explored, Laos rewards the patient and self-reliant birder with a sense of genuine discovery that has all but vanished elsewhere in the region.

## The Mekong Sandbars near Si Phan Don

In the far south, where the Mekong braids into the labyrinth of Si Phan Don — the Four Thousand Islands — seasonal sandbars and channels hold some of Southeast Asia's rarest river birds. This is one of the last strongholds of the river tern and the small pratincole, which nest on exposed sand in the dry season, and a place where the critically endangered river lapwing still clings on. The deep pools around Don Khone are the final refuge in Laos for the Irrawaddy dolphin, and the same channels attract great thick-knees and the rare wire-tailed swallow.

## Nam Et-Phou Louey National Park

In the remote northeast, Nam Et-Phou Louey is the largest protected area in Laos and one of its wildest, a vast block of evergreen and mixed deciduous forest rising to over 2,200 metres at Phou Louey itself. The park is best known for its tiger and large mammal conservation, but for birders the forests hold green peafowl, great hornbill, and a rich community of laughingthrushes, broadbills, and pittas. Night safaris along the Nam Nern river offer a chance at frogmouths, owls, and roosting kingfishers.

## The Bolaven Plateau

Rising abruptly from the southern lowlands to around 1,200 metres, the Bolaven Plateau is famous for its coffee, its waterfalls such as Tad Fane and Tad Yuang, and its cooler montane forest. The remaining forest patches around Paksong support montane species at the southern edge of their range, including barbets, minivets, and the occasional silver pheasant. The plateau's mosaic of coffee plantations, grassland, and forest edge is also good for raptors and open-country species.

## Nam Kading National Protected Area

Straddling the Nam Kading river in central Laos, this protected area is a steep, forested gorge famous among birders for its boat-based access into pristine evergreen forest. The dramatic limestone-flanked river is one of the more reliable places in the country for green peafowl calling at dawn, and the surrounding forest holds great hornbill, crested argus, and a wealth of woodpeckers and bulbuls. The remoteness keeps disturbance low.

## Phou Hin Poun (Khammouane Karst)

The vast karst landscape of Khammouane, protected in part as Phou Hin Poun, is a surreal world of jagged limestone towers, caves, and hidden valleys. This is the home of the bare-faced bulbul, the only bald songbird in mainland Asia, which clings to the scrubby vegetation on the karst pinnacles. The limestone leaf warbler and sooty babbler are other specialities, and the famous Konglor Cave, where boats travel seven kilometres through the mountain, lies within this landscape.

## Dong Hua Sao National Protected Area

On the southwestern flank of the Bolaven Plateau, Dong Hua Sao protects a continuous gradient from lowland to montane forest. The lower evergreen forest holds bar-bellied and other pittas, trogons, and the rare germain's peacock-pheasant, while the higher slopes shift toward montane laughingthrushes and warblers. The reserve's waterfalls and streams support forktails and the white-crowned forktail along the shaded gullies.

## Xe Pian National Protected Area

In the far south near the Cambodian border, Xe Pian combines lowland dry dipterocarp forest, wetlands, and seasonal pools known as trapeangs. This habitat mosaic supports a different suite of birds from the wetter forests further north: woolly-necked stork, lesser adjutant, sarus crane in some seasons, white-rumped falcon, and the rare green peafowl. The open dipterocarp woodland is also good for woodpeckers and the white-rumped pygmy falcon.

## The Plain of Jars, Xieng Khouang

The rolling grasslands of the Plain of Jars in Xieng Khouang, famous for their mysterious ancient stone jars, sit on a high plateau around 1,100 metres and offer open-country birding rare in heavily forested Laos. Grassland and scrub species such as pipits, larks, bushchats, and wintering harriers occur here, and the pine-clad ridges hold montane species. The combination of archaeology and open-country birding makes this a distinctive stop.

## Nakai-Nam Theun

One of the most biologically important areas in Indochina, the Nakai-Nam Theun landscape on the Annamite range along the Vietnamese border is where the saola was discovered. Its wet evergreen forests are exceptionally rich, holding crested argus, the secretive Annamite specialities, and a host of babblers and partridges still being documented. Access is genuinely difficult, which is part of why so much remains to be learned here.

## Vientiane Wetlands and That Luang Marsh

Close to the capital, the That Luang Marsh and surrounding wetlands offer accessible birding for visitors with limited time. These remnant marshes attract herons, egrets, jacanas, bitterns, and a range of wintering waterfowl and warblers, with the occasional surprise on passage. They are a reminder that even near Vientiane, the Mekong floodplain still holds a living wetland community worth protecting. At dawn, before the city wakes, the calls of crakes and waterhens mix with the traffic while purple herons lift over the reeds.

## When to go and practical notes

The dry season from November to March is by far the best time to bird Laos. The Mekong drops to expose the sandbars where river terns and pratincoles nest, the roads are passable, and wintering species fill the wetlands. The hot months of April and May are demanding but bring lively song and breeding in the forest, while the rains from June to October leave many remote trails impassable. Distances are long and the roads slow, so allow several days between the main areas and budget time for unexpected delays. Local guides are often essential for finding the newly described karst specialities and for arranging boat transport on the rivers, and an early start is rewarded almost everywhere.

Morocco is the most rewarding birding destination in North Africa, and one of the easiest. Within a single week a visitor can cross from Atlantic lagoons holding the last wild population of Northern Bald Ibis, over the cedar forests and snow-streaked peaks of the High Atlas where Levaillant's Woodpecker and Crimson-winged Finch live, and down into the true Sahara at the desert edge near Merzouga, where sandgrouse come to drink at dawn. The country sits at the meeting point of Europe, Africa and the desert, and its bird list of more than 460 species reflects that. Add reliable infrastructure, good roads and a short flight from Europe, and Morocco becomes the classic introduction to Western Palearctic desert birding.

## 1. Souss-Massa National Park

This Atlantic coastal park south of Agadir protects the world's largest population of the Critically Endangered Northern Bald Ibis (Geronticus eremita). The cliffs and coastal steppe between the Souss and Massa river mouths hold the bulk of the surviving wild birds. The Oued Massa estuary itself is a superb wetland for Marbled Duck (Marmaronetta angustirostris, Vulnerable), Glossy Ibis, Black-crowned Tchagra and Plain Martin. Brown-throated Martin and Black-headed Bush-shrike reach their northern limit here.

## 2. Oued Sous estuary, Agadir

On the southern edge of Agadir, the Oued Sous estuary is one of the most accessible top-class wetlands in Morocco. The mudflats hold large numbers of Greater Flamingo (Phoenicopterus roseus), Eurasian Spoonbill, Audouin's Gull (Ichthyaetus audouinii) and a great variety of waders on passage and in winter. The Royal Palace grounds border the estuary, and the tidal cycle dictates the best viewing times.

## 3. Merzouga and Erg Chebbi

The dunes of Erg Chebbi, on the Saharan edge near the Algerian border, are the destination for true desert species. The seasonal lake of Dayet Srji, when flooded, holds Greater Flamingo and Ruddy Shelduck in the middle of the desert. The stony hammada and dune margins produce Desert Sparrow, Fulvous Babbler, Egyptian Nightjar, Desert Warbler and several lark species. Spotted and Crowned Sandgrouse come to drink at first light.

## 4. Boumalne Dades and the Tagdilt Track

The famous Tagdilt Track, a network of stony desert pistes near Boumalne Dades, is the most reliable site on earth for Thick-billed Lark (Ramphocoris clotbey), alongside Temminck's Lark, Red-rumped Wheatear, Cream-coloured Courser and Trumpeter Finch (Bucanetes githagineus). Pharaoh Eagle-Owl roosts in the nearby gorges. The bleak, stony plains look empty at first but reward patient scanning.

## 5. The High Atlas: Oukaïmeden

At over 2,600 metres, the ski resort of Oukaïmeden in the High Atlas south of Marrakech is the easiest place to find true mountain birds. Crimson-winged Finch (Rhodopechys sanguineus), Horned Lark, Alpine Accentor, Red-billed Chough and Rock Sparrow occur around the plateau, and in summer Seebohm's Wheatear and Atlas Horned Lark are present. The drive up through the valleys adds African Blue Tit and Levaillant's Woodpecker (Picus vaillantii).

## 6. Middle Atlas cedar forests, Ifrane

The cedar forests around Ifrane and Azrou in the Middle Atlas hold a Mediterranean montane community. Levaillant's Woodpecker, Atlas Pied Flycatcher, Tristram's Warbler, Hawfinch and African Blue Tit are characteristic, and Barbary Macaque shares the cedars. The lakes of the Middle Atlas, including Aguelmame Sidi Ali, add wintering wildfowl and Crested Coot.

## 7. Merja Zerga, Moulay Bousselham

This large coastal lagoon on the Atlantic, north of Kenitra, is one of Morocco's most important wetlands and a Ramsar site. It holds the wintering Slender-billed Curlew historically and remains crucial for thousands of waders, Greater Flamingo, terns and Audouin's Gull. The lagoon is famous for African Marsh Owl, which can be seen at dusk over the surrounding marshes by local boat.

## 8. Lac de Sidi Boughaba

A freshwater lake near Mehdya on the Atlantic coast, Sidi Boughaba is a protected reserve and one of the best inland wetlands in the country. Marbled Duck breeds here, alongside Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened), Crested Coot, Black-crowned Night Heron and large numbers of wintering wildfowl. The surrounding woodland holds passerines on passage.

## 9. Tagdilt to Goulmima: the desert steppe

The wider desert steppe of the south, between Boumalne Dades and the oasis town of Goulmima, holds the full suite of arid-country specialities. Hoopoe Lark, Bar-tailed Lark, Desert Wheatear, Maghreb Wheatear and Lanner Falcon (Falco biarmicus) all occur. The palmeries of the oases add Fulvous Babbler and Laughing Dove, and the wadis hold Trumpeter Finch.

## 10. Tan-Tan and the deep south

The far southwest, around Tan-Tan and the Oued Chbika, reaches into Saharan and Atlantic conditions together. The coast holds Cricket Warbler, Sudan Golden Sparrow and African Royal Tern on occasion, while the desert interior produces Dunn's Lark, Cream-coloured Courser and sandgrouse. This region is the frontier of Afrotropical species reaching Morocco from the south.

## Planning a birding trip in Morocco

Morocco's main birding season runs from late February to May. Early spring brings desert breeders into song and the Atlantic wetlands fill with migrant waders, while the High Atlas remains snow-streaked but accessible. April is the classic month: desert specialities are displaying, the cedar forests are alive, and migration along the coast peaks. Autumn (September to November) offers strong wader and seabird passage on the Atlantic coast and a quieter desert. Summer is brutally hot in the south but good for the high mountains.

The classic itinerary is a loop from Marrakech. Drive south over the Tizi n'Tichka pass into the desert at Boumalne Dades and Merzouga, then return via the gorges and oases. A separate trip from Agadir covers Souss-Massa, the Oued Sous estuary and the western desert. The Atlantic wetlands of Merja Zerga and Sidi Boughaba pair naturally with a northern itinerary from Casablanca or Rabat. A four-wheel-drive is useful for the desert tracks, though much can be done with a standard car and care.

Practical points: the Tagdilt Track and other desert pistes change with the weather, so local knowledge helps; a Moroccan bird guide transforms a desert visit and knows the current drinking pools and roost sites. Water is again the magnet in the desert, so any seasonal lake, oasis or seep deserves attention. Bring a scope for the estuaries and the desert plains alike. Morocco's combination of accessibility, infrastructure and genuinely special birds — bald ibis, Thick-billed Lark, Crimson-winged Finch, Desert Sparrow — makes it the standout Maghreb destination.

Mozambique stretches for nearly two thousand kilometres along the East African coast, from Tanzania in the north to South Africa in the south, with its long Indian Ocean frontage backed by diverse interior habitats ranging from coastal dune forest and mangrove to miombo woodland, montane grassland, and the inland wetlands of the Rift Valley. Over 660 bird species have been recorded — a remarkable total for a single country. The country contains endemic and near-endemic species of global significance, including the Critically Endangered Degodi Lark near the Somali border, the East Coast Akalat of coastal thicket, and several species with ranges centred on Mozambique and adjacent Tanzania.

Birding infrastructure in Mozambique is improving but remains limited compared with East African neighbours. Several specialist tour operators run well-established itineraries, and the combination of birds with Mozambique's beaches, marine wildlife, and national parks makes it compelling for combined nature tourism. The dry season — May to November — provides the best access to remote areas and the clearest views through the woodland. The country shares some of its most productive birding areas, including sections of the Rift Valley and the Niassa plateau, with Tanzania and Malawi.

## 1. Gorongosa National Park, Sofala Province

Gorongosa is one of the most extraordinary wildlife recovery stories in Africa. Once devastated by the civil war, the park has been the subject of a major international restoration programme and now holds populations of large mammals and birds comparable to East Africa's premier parks. The park's rift floor supports a mosaic of floodplain grassland, woodland, and Lake Urema — a shallow lake that contracts dramatically in the dry season, concentrating waterbirds. Wattled Crane — globally vulnerable — breeds here in one of Mozambique's most significant populations. African Fish Eagle is conspicuous above the lake. The miombo woodland on the slopes of Mount Gorongosa holds forest endemics and is a distinct birding habitat from the valley floor.

## 2. Niassa Reserve, Niassa Province

Niassa is one of Africa's largest protected areas, covering over 42,000 square kilometres of miombo woodland and river valley in the remote north of Mozambique. The sheer scale and lack of human disturbance create an ecological integrity rare even in Africa. African Wild Dog, Lion, and Elephant are present in significant numbers, and the bird community of the miombo is extraordinarily rich. Miombo Rock Thrush sings from boulders in the woodland. Racket-tailed Roller — a spectacular roller with elongated tail streamers — inhabits mature miombo. Crowned Eagle hunts in the riverine forest. The logistics of visiting Niassa require a dedicated expedition approach — charter flights from Lichinga and camp-based travel — but the rewards are extraordinary.

## 3. Maputo Special Reserve, Maputo Province

The Maputo Special Reserve, just south of the capital, protects coastal forest, grassland, and the Ponto do Ouro Marine Reserve that includes the southernmost coral reefs in Africa. The coastal dune forest holds the East Coast Akalat — a skulking, orange-flanked forest thrush of the coastal thicket belt. Sombre Nicator calls from the dense undergrowth. Southern Banded Snake Eagle perches on exposed branches above the forest canopy. The estuaries and tidal flats attract large concentrations of waders and terns. Crab Plover is irregular but recorded. The reserve is accessible from Maputo on a day trip and provides the best forest birding nearest to the capital.

## 4. Quirimbas Archipelago, Cabo Delgado Province

The Quirimbas Archipelago off the northern Mozambique coast comprises thirty-two islands and a vast area of coral reef and seagrass. The mangrove-fringed islands hold nesting Roseate Tern colonies — Mozambique holds one of the globally significant populations of this scarce tern. Crab Plover is a regular non-breeding visitor to the tidal flats between the islands. Dimorphic Egret — the East African form of Western Reef Heron with both white and dark morphs — is common along the rocky shores. Dugong and whale sharks occur in the marine park. Boat trips between the islands produce Sooty Tern, Brown Noddy, and Lesser Frigatebird offshore.

## 5. Chimanimani Mountains, Manica Province

The Chimanimani Mountains on the Zimbabwe border rise sharply from the coastal plain to over 2,400 metres and hold a relict montane forest and grassland community. The mountains support the Chirinda Apalis — a warbler restricted to the Chirinda forest on the Zimbabwe side and adjacent Mozambique highlands — and several other Afromontane specialities. Swynnerton's Robin is the flagship species of these mountains, a secretive skulker of dense forest undergrowth with a highly restricted distribution in the Chirinda-Chimanimani block. Blue Swallow breeds in the montane grassland during the summer — a globally vulnerable species dependent on high-altitude grassland kept open by fire and grazing. Gurney's Sugarbird is found in the protea belt.

## 6. Zambezi Delta and Marromeu Complex, Sofala Province

The Zambezi Delta where the river enters the Mozambique Channel is one of the most significant wetland complexes in southern Africa. The Marromeu Buffalo Reserve — named for the enormous buffalo herds — holds Wattled Crane, Saddle-billed Stork, and Openbill Stork in large numbers. Pygmy Geese feed in the floating vegetation on the backwater channels. African Skimmer — a globally vulnerable species restricted to major African rivers — nests on sandbanks in the river channels. Pel's Fishing Owl inhabits the larger trees along the riverine forest. Boat access through the delta channels provides intimate views of waterbirds and reptiles in an undisturbed setting.

## 7. Inhambane Coastal Forests, Inhambane Province

The ancient coastal forests of the Inhambane coast — patches of relict dune forest on Pleistocene sand deposits — hold the richest avifauna in coastal Mozambique. Livingstone's Flycatcher inhabits the forest interior. Spotted Ground Thrush, globally threatened, winters in the coastal thicket. Plain-backed Sunbird feeds at the forest edge flowers. The forests between Tofo and Vilanculos are most accessible, and the adjacent beaches and estuaries contribute shorebirds and seabirds. Dugong-watching boat trips from Inhambane coincide with good seabird watching in the bay, and Roseate Tern is regular offshore.

## 8. Nyika Plateau (Mozambique Sector), Niassa Province

The Nyika Plateau extends from Malawi into northeastern Mozambique, where the high rolling grassland — the largest montane grassland in central Africa — holds specialist bird communities found nowhere else in Mozambique. Denham's Bustard is conspicuous on the open plateau. Mountain Marsh Widow breeds in the dambo grasslands in summer, the male in spectacular orange, black, and white breeding plumage. Wailing Cisticola inhabits the plateau grassland. Augur Buzzard hunts the plateau edge. Access from the Mozambique side is more challenging than from Malawi's Nyika National Park, but cross-border birding trips taking in both countries are feasible.

## 9. Lago Niassa (Lake Malawi) Shore, Niassa Province

The Mozambique shore of Lake Malawi (called Lago Niassa on the Mozambique side) provides freshwater birding of a different character from the coastal sites. African Skimmer is present on the lake shore during the dry season. White-breasted Cormorant breeds in colonies on the rocky promontories. Malachite Kingfisher is common in the emergent vegetation around river mouths. Osprey is a regular non-breeding visitor. The lake's cichlid fish diversity supports a high density of piscivorous birds. The miombo woodland back from the shore holds Miombo Pied Barbet, Miombo Tit, and Bennett's Woodpecker.

## 10. Maputo Bay Mudflats

The extensive intertidal mudflats of Maputo Bay provide some of the best shorebird birding on the East African coast. Greater Flamingo feeds in the shallow bay in flocks of several thousand. Lesser Flamingo occurs in smaller numbers alongside them. The mudflat diversity includes Curlew Sandpiper, Little Stint, Grey Plover, Bar-tailed Godwit, and Whimbrel during the October to March non-breeding season. Caspian Tern is resident year-round. The bay is easily accessed from central Maputo and can be combined with the coastal forest birding at the Maputo Special Reserve to the south. Boat trips from the Maputo fishing harbour give access to the more distant mudflats.

Mozambique offers a birding experience that combines genuine wilderness with high species diversity and the opportunity to encounter birds in landscapes largely free of mass tourism. The country's avifauna is a mixture of East African, southern African, and coastal Indian Ocean elements that produces a distinctive species composition.

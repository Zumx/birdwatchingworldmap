Vietnam's narrow geography — over 3,000 kilometres from north to south — encompasses Himalayan foothills, Red River plains, central mountain range (Truong Son), Mekong Delta, and a long tropical coastline. Its bird fauna of over 900 species includes several species described since 1990 from previously unexplored montane areas, reflecting how incompletely surveyed the country remains. The endemic and restricted-range species of the Indochina area — the central lowlands between Laos, Cambodia, and the Vietnamese coast — are the primary targets for international birding visitors. The map database includes the Bidoup Nui Ba NP biodiversity trail (documented as an eBird hotspot) in the central highlands.

## 1. Cat Tien National Park, Lâm Đồng

Cat Tien is the most accessible lowland forest reserve in southern Vietnam and the primary site for the endemic Orange-necked Partridge (Arborophila davidi, Endangered) and the near-endemic Germain's Peacock-Pheasant (Polyplectron germaini, Vulnerable). The park's lowland evergreen and semi-evergreen forest holds Indochinese Green Magpie (Cissa hypoleuca), Siamese Fireback (Lophura diardi), and Banded Broadbill (Eurylaimus javanicus). Gaur, banteng, and primates share the forest.

## 2. Yok Don National Park, Đắk Lắk

Yok Don in the central highlands holds the driest forest in Vietnam — deciduous dipterocarp woodland similar to the dry forest of Cambodia and Laos. The resulting bird community is quite different from the wetter forests elsewhere in Vietnam: White-rumped Falcon (Polihierax insignis, Near Threatened), Spot-bellied Eagle Owl (Bubo nipalensis), and Mekong Wagtail (Motacilla samveasnae) are among the specialties. Wild Elephant and Banteng inhabit the park.

## 3. Tam Dao National Park, Vĩnh Phúc

Tam Dao, rising to 1,500 metres north of Hanoi, holds the northern Vietnamese montane community and is the most accessible highland birding site from the capital. Slaty-backed Forktail (Enicurus schistaceus), Fuligula Tit (Poecile elegans), and the endemic Vietnamese Cutia (Cutia legalleni) and Bar-bellied Pitta (Hydrornis elliotii) are the primary targets.

## 4. Phong Nha-Kẻ Bàng National Park, Quảng Bình

This UNESCO World Heritage karst landscape in central Vietnam holds the limestone forest community with Limestone Leaf Warbler (Phylloscopus calciatilis, described from here in 2010), Vietnamese Crake (Zapornia cinerea), and the endemic Annam Limestone Pitta (Hydrornis soror). The cave system includes the world's largest known cave.

## 5. Bidoup Nui Ba National Park, Lâm Đồng

Bidoup Nui Ba in the Lâm Đồng highlands holds the most complete central highland endemic community in Vietnam. The Bidoup Nui Ba NP Hon Giao Biodiversity Trail (documented in the map database) provides systematic access to the cloud forest. Collared Laughingthrush (Pterorhinus yersini, Endangered), Vietnamese Greenfinch (Chloris monguilloti), and Vietnamese Cutia (Cutia legalleni) are near-endemic species most readily found here.

## 6. Cúc Phương National Park, Ninh Bình

Vietnam's oldest national park, Cúc Phương in the northern limestone hills, holds the best remaining lowland forest in the north. Large-scaled Pit Viper and Giant Forest Scorpion add to the fauna; the birds include Orange-breasted Pitta (Pitta oatesi), Cachar Wedge-billed Babbler (Sphenocichla humei), and Red-vented Barbet (Psilopogon lagrandieri).

## 7. Bach Ma National Park, Thừa Thiên Huế

Bach Ma on the coastal range of central Vietnam receives prodigious rainfall on the eastern slope facing the South China Sea. The resulting cloud forest holds Edwards's Pheasant (Lophura edwardsi, Critically Endangered) — one of Vietnam's most threatened endemic birds, known from fewer than five confirmed recent localities. Crested Argus (Rheinardia ocellata, Vulnerable) also occurs here.

## 8. Mekong Delta and Tram Chim National Park

Tram Chim National Park in Đồng Tháp province protects one of the last large areas of seasonally flooded Mekong plain. Sarus Crane (Antigone antigone, Vulnerable) — one of the most threatened large birds in Southeast Asia — winters here. Bengal Florican (Houbaropsis bengalensis, Critically Endangered) is a rare possibility; Painted Stork and Asian Openbill are common waterbirds.

## 9. Con Dao National Park, Bà Rịa-Vũng Tàu

Con Dao island national park holds the most important Nicobar Pigeon (Caloenas nicobarica, Near Threatened) population in Vietnam, alongside the full complement of South Vietnamese island forest species. Sea Turtle nesting on the beaches adds to the conservation significance.

## 10. Mù Cang Chải, Yên Bái

The terraced rice landscapes of Mù Cang Chải in the northwest highlands provide the backdrop for birding the open agricultural and secondary scrub habitat favored by Spot-breasted Parrotbill (Paradoxornis guttaticollis) and various high-altitude buntings during winter.

## Planning a Vietnam birding trip

Vietnam's birding is concentrated in two distinct seasons aligned with its monsoon climate. The winter dry season (October to April) is the most productive for the central and southern highlands, when the forest roads are passable and the birds are at their most vocal. Cat Tien and Bidoup Nui Ba are most accessible from November through March. The northern sites — Tam Dao, Cúc Phương, Bach Ma — are productive year-round but best approached in the dry season.

The summer wet season (May to September) brings heavy rain to the central highlands and south, limiting access to some sites. However, the northern highland sites — Tam Dao, Fansipan, and the Yên Bái agricultural landscapes — are at their richest in June and July when breeding activity peaks. The rare species of the montane forests, including Collared Laughingthrush and Vietnamese Cutia, are most vocal in late spring and early summer.

A practical Vietnam birding itinerary covers the country's north-to-south axis in two weeks: three nights at Tam Dao (north), three nights at Bach Ma (central), and three nights at Cat Tien (south), with an option to add Bidoup Nui Ba in the south-central highlands. Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City serve as gateways to the northern and southern sites respectively. Local guide networks organized through Viet Nature Conservation Centre provide specialist expertise for all major sites.

The challenge of Vietnamese birding is that several key target species — Edwards's Pheasant, Crested Argus, and the limestone specialists of Phong Nha — require early morning starts and quiet forest access. Building flexibility into the schedule to return to productive locations multiple times produces better results than a strict point-to-point itinerary.

## Practical field logistics

Vietnam's transportation infrastructure has improved dramatically in the past decade, with domestic flights now connecting Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City to Hue, Da Nang, and Dalat (the gateway to Bidoup Nui Ba). The Hanoi to Ho Chi Minh City train provides an alternative to flying for birders who want to watch the changing landscape; the overnight sleeping train from Hanoi to Da Nang takes 16 hours.

Cat Tien National Park is most accessible from Ho Chi Minh City by bus or organized transfer (three to four hours); the park guesthouses and the Cat Tien Tour operator both arrange accommodation. Bidoup Nui Ba National Park near Da Lat requires a rented motorbike or car from Da Lat city (45 minutes). Both parks have English-language trail signage at the main trail heads.

The Viet Nature Conservation Centre in Hanoi arranges specialist birding tours with English-speaking ornithologist guides to all major sites. For birders targeting the rarer endemic species — Edwards's Pheasant at Bach Ma, Collared Laughingthrush at Bidoup Nui Ba — specialist guide arrangements are essential, as these species require local knowledge of current activity locations that changes seasonally.

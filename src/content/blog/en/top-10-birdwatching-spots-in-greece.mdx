Greece holds over 450 bird species and an exceptionally varied habitat range for a Mediterranean country. Its position at the southeastern corner of Europe places it at the junction of European and Middle Eastern faunas; its mix of continental wetlands, island chains, and montane forest produces species from Dalmatian Pelican and Lesser Kestrel to Wallcreeper and White-backed Woodpecker. The birding infrastructure — guided tours, reserve hides, and the Hellenic Ornithological Society's network — has developed substantially in the past two decades.

## 1. Evros Delta, Thrace

The Evros Delta, where the Greek-Turkish border river meets the Aegean, is Greece's most important wetland. Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened) breeds in a large colony at the river delta. Great White Pelican, Glossy Ibis, and Pygmy Cormorant (Microcarbo pygmeus) breed alongside them. The reedbeds hold Aquatic Warbler (Acrocephalus paludicola, Vulnerable). The national park provides viewing infrastructure from the Greek side; much of the delta is cross-border with Turkey.

## 2. Lake Kerkini, Macedonia

Lake Kerkini, an artificial reservoir in northern Greece created in the 1930s, has become one of the most important waterbird sites in the Balkans. Dalmatian Pelican breeds here alongside Great White Pelican in the largest combined pelican colony in Europe. Pygmy Cormorant, Little Egret, and Night Heron occupy the shoreline willows. Water Buffalo introduced for wetland grazing management attract Cattle Egret (Bubulcus ibis) in large numbers. Boat trips from the village of Kerkini provide the most productive pelican views.

## 3. Lesvos (Lesbos), Aegean

The island of Lesvos is Greece's premier island birding destination and one of Europe's top spring migration hotspots. The island's position in the northeastern Aegean makes it a landfall for migrants from the Middle East and Central Asia in April and May. Cinereous Bunting (Emberiza cineracea, Vulnerable), Krüper's Nuthatch (Sitta krueperi), Rüppell's Warbler (Curruca ruppeli), and regular overshoots from the east including Semicollared Flycatcher make Lesvos the Greek island that UK and European tour operators most frequently target.

## 4. Dadia-Lefkimi-Soufli Forest National Park, Thrace

Dadia holds the most important raptor community in the European Union. Black Vulture (Aegypius monachus) breeds — one of only two EU breeding populations — alongside Griffon Vulture (Gyps fulvus), Egyptian Vulture (Neophron percnopterus, Endangered), and Short-toed Eagle. The park's management includes a vulture feeding station accessible from a dedicated hide that provides some of the closest and most sustained vulture-viewing in Europe.

## 5. Prespa Lakes, Macedonia and Albania border

The Prespa Lakes (Megali Prespa and Mikri Prespa) straddle the borders of Greece, North Macedonia, and Albania. Mikri Prespa holds the largest Dalmatian Pelican colony in Greece and significant Pygmy Cormorant breeding. Eurasian Bittern, Purple Heron, and Ferruginous Duck breed in the lake's reed beds. The Society for the Protection of Prespa operates visitor facilities and a research center.

## 6. Porto Lagos Lagoon, Thrace

Porto Lagos on the Aegean coast is a Ramsar site holding internationally important concentrations of breeding and wintering waterbirds. The lagoon complex hosts Greater Flamingo in winter, Slender-billed Gull in summer, and significant migrant wader assemblages. The adjacent Lake Vistonida holds Dalmatian Pelican in winter.

## 7. Axios-Loudias-Aliakmonas Delta, Macedonia

This delta complex near Thessaloniki holds breeding Little Tern (Sternula albifrons), Kentish Plover (Charadrius alexandrinus), Black-winged Stilt, and Slender-billed Gull. The area is managed partly as agricultural land, creating the patchwork of marsh, grassland, and crop field that supports the full complement of lowland Greek breeding birds.

## 8. Meteora, Thessaly

The rock pinnacles of Meteora, famous for their Orthodox monasteries, provide nesting sites for Lesser Kestrel (Falco naumanni, Vulnerable), a colonial breeder declining across Mediterranean Europe. Peregrine Falcon, Alpine Swift (Tachymarptis melba), and Rock Nuthatch (Sitta neumayer) are part of the cliff community. The surrounding steppe holds Stone Curlew (Burhinus oedicnemus), Calandra Lark (Melanocorypha calandra), and Roller (Coracias garrulus).

## 9. Kerkini and Strymon Valley corridor

The Strymon Valley connecting Kerkini with the Bulgarian border provides migration corridor access. Lesser Spotted Eagle, Honey Buzzard, and Black Stork are regular autumn migrants through the valley's forested ridges.

## 10. Olympos National Park

Greece's highest mountain (2,917 metres) holds a mountain bird community including Golden Eagle, Wallcreeper (Tichodroma muraria), and Rock Thrush (Monticola saxatilis). The lower forest holds Middle Spotted Woodpecker and White-backed Woodpecker (Dendrocopos leucotos). The summit zone's serpentinite soils support a distinctive flora that attracts seed-eating birds in late summer.

## Planning a Greece birding trip

Greece rewards birders willing to move between its distinct habitats in a single trip. The best overall season is spring — April to mid-May — when resident breeding birds and returning Palearctic migrants overlap. By late April, European Roller, European Bee-eater, and Black-headed Bunting have arrived in the agricultural lowlands; Little Bittern, Squacco Heron, and Ferruginous Duck are establishing territories on the wetlands; and raptors are passing north through the Dadia corridor.

The Evros Delta and Lake Vistonida in the northeast offer the most concentrated wetland birding accessible from Alexandroupolis, which has an international airport. The national park office at Dadia can arrange guided forest walks to the vulture feeding station; Dadia is under two hours by road from Alexandroupolis. A five-day circuit from Athens can cover Lake Kerkini (two hours north), then Axios Delta (30 minutes from Thessaloniki), followed by a domestic flight east to Alexandroupolis for Evros and Dadia.

The Prespa Lakes in the northwest — the pelican and cormorant colonies — are more remote, requiring a drive from Thessaloniki or Kastoria. The journey takes three hours on mountain roads; Psarades village on Great Prespa Lake provides basic accommodation and boat access to the pelican colony cliffs. Visiting Prespa in combination with Lake Kerkini in a five-day northwest loop is the most efficient approach.

Lesvos Island is the most convenient island destination — regular flights from Athens and short ferry crossings from Turkey. Spring migration at Kalloni Salt Pans peaks in the third week of April when both the breeding herons and the northbound waders coincide. The island's endemic Kruper's Nuthatch and the Eleonora's Falcon breeding colony at Sigri are best targeted in a separate overnight from the main salt-pan birding.

The Greek mainland's timing recommendation: aim for the last two weeks of April, when spring is far enough advanced that all the summer visitors have arrived but before the agricultural landscape dries out and bird activity decreases. Early mornings in the Mediterranean scrub (phrygana) are essential for Rüppell's Warbler and Sardinian Warbler. Rhodes, Crete, and the Dodecanese add resident Mediterranean species and are most productive in spring when eastern migrants add diversity.

## The Aegean and island hopping

The Aegean islands provide a distinct birding experience from the mainland: each island holds a different subset of the Eastern Mediterranean and Middle Eastern species at the edge of their range. Lesvos is the premier destination, but the lesser-known islands of Chios, Samos, and Rhodes provide productive spring migration birding with fewer visitors. Eleonora's Falcon (Falco eleonorae) breeds colonially on sea cliffs throughout the Aegean; the most accessible colonies are on Paros and Naxos. Cyprus — technically not Greece but biogeographically part of the same Eastern Mediterranean system — holds the endemic Cyprus Warbler (Curruca melanothorax) and the Cypriot subspecies of the Wheatear complex.

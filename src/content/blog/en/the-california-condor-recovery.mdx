The California Condor (Gymnogyps californianus) carries the distinction of having been reduced to the smallest population of any bird from which a successful recovery was achieved. In April 1987, the last free-flying individual was captured in the mountains of southern California, bringing the global population to 27 birds held in two facilities — the Los Angeles Zoo and the San Diego Wild Animal Park (now San Diego Zoo Safari Park). The decision to capture every remaining wild bird was agonizing and controversial; critics argued that the last wild condors were being taken to die in captivity. The decision was correct. As of 2025, more than 500 California Condors exist, with over 300 flying as wild or wild-hatched birds in California, Arizona, Utah, Baja California, and Oregon.

## Why the population collapsed

The California Condor was widespread in North America during the Pleistocene, ranging from British Columbia to Florida and feeding on the megafauna — mammoths, giant ground sloths, camels — that went extinct at the end of the last Ice Age. By the twentieth century, the population had contracted to the mountains of southern and central California, where a combination of threats operated simultaneously: lead poisoning from spent rifle and shotgun ammunition in the carcasses of deer, elk, and coyotes; direct shooting; collision with power lines; and habitat loss reducing the availability of large carrion in the open landscape the condor requires for flight and foraging.

By the 1980s, the wild population had fallen to a level where a single adverse event — a disease outbreak, a run of accidental poisonings — could eliminate it entirely. The captive breeding program was the only alternative to extinction.

## The captive breeding operation

Both breeding facilities developed detailed protocols for raising condors without imprinting them on human handlers — a critical requirement for eventual release into the wild. Condor chicks are fed by handlers using condor-head glove puppets, hear recordings of adult condor vocalizations, and are housed with other condors rather than with humans. The goal is a bird that recognizes itself as a condor, learns behavioral cues from adults, and will be able to integrate into wild social hierarchies after release.

Genetic management across the two captive populations ensured maximum retention of the original genetic diversity. The 27 founders produced a managed captive population that, with careful pairings and transfers between facilities, maintained heterozygosity through the first decades of the recovery.

## The reintroduction program

The first condors were released in California in 1992. Early releases suffered high mortality from the same lead poisoning that had driven the original decline; released birds encountered lead-contaminated carcasses and died. The program moved to releasing birds in remote areas with reduced hunter access, and simultaneously began an intensive outreach campaign to hunters, encouraging the voluntary switch from lead to non-lead ammunition.

Arizona and Utah releases followed in 1996, establishing a second free-flying population centered on the Grand Canyon region. The Vermilion Cliffs, north of the Grand Canyon, became the core release site for the Arizona-Utah population. Baja California releases in Mexico, coordinated with Mexican wildlife authorities, established a third population.

## The lead poisoning crisis and its partial resolution

Lead poisoning from rifle and shotgun ammunition remained the leading cause of condor mortality in the wild for two decades after reintroductions began. Blood lead levels in wild condors were monitored by capturing individuals for annual health checks; birds with elevated lead levels were treated and released. Some individuals required treatment repeatedly. The evidence for ammunition as the lead source was unambiguous: lead isotope ratios in condor blood matched the isotope signature of the specific lead alloys used in hunting ammunition.

California passed Assembly Bill 821 in 2008, mandating the use of non-lead ammunition when hunting in the condor's range. The Ridley-Tree Condor Preservation Act, passed in 2019, extended the non-lead requirement to all hunting in California. Population-level blood lead monitoring showed a measurable decline in average lead exposure following these legislative interventions, though lead poisoning remains the most significant ongoing threat.

## Current population and wild breeding

Wild condor pairs have been reproducing with limited human intervention since the early 2000s. Nest sites in California include remote cliff faces in the Pinnacles National Monument, the Big Sur coast, and the southern Sierra Nevada. The Grand Canyon population nests on sandstone ledges throughout the canyon rim. Nest cameras at several California sites allow remote monitoring of breeding attempts.

The milestone of condors nesting in the wild without management supplementation — where wild-hatched chicks are raised to fledging without human intervention in nest success — was crossed in the 2010s. The population now has multiple cohorts of wild-born birds, some of which are themselves breeding, creating generational continuity independent of the captive breeding facilities.

## Where to see the California Condor

The most reliable public viewing is from the Pinnacles National Monument visitor center in central California, where condors from the central California population soar overhead in most seasons. Condor Gulch Trail and the high peaks trail provide elevated vantage points that put observers at the same elevation as soaring condors. The Vermilion Cliffs pullout in northern Arizona is the best single site for the Arizona-Utah population; birds often gather at the release facilities nearby. eBird hotspot records confirm both locations as productive year-round.

## The California Condor today

The California Condor (Gymnogyps californianus, Critically Endangered) recovery program has become the most closely studied large-bird reintroduction in North American history. From a population low of 22 birds in 1987 (all captured for the captive breeding program), the wild population had grown to over 500 birds by 2024, with nearly 350 flying free in California, Arizona, Utah, and Baja California. This trajectory — from the edge of extinction to a growing wild population in under 40 years — represents one of the most ambitious conservation recovery achievements for any large bird.

The condor's ecology creates specific management challenges that persist even as numbers recover. Condors require 5 to 6 years to reach sexual maturity and typically raise only one chick every two years, making population growth inherently slow. The lifespan in the wild can exceed 50 years; a substantial fraction of the current wild population consists of birds originally released in the 1990s. Population modeling suggests a self-sustaining wild population requires at least 150 individuals (the initial goal) and continues to require supplementary releases to offset mortality.

The lead poisoning problem remains the primary mortality cause in the wild population. Despite decades of advocacy for non-lead ammunition, voluntary compliance in the condor range is incomplete and California's mandatory non-lead ammunition law (effective 2019 for all hunting) has not eliminated exposure — condors scavenge gut piles and carcasses from livestock operations as well as hunting, and lead contamination from other sources continues. All condors in the wild are captured annually for blood testing; those with elevated lead levels receive chelation treatment.

The Pinnacles National Park in San Benito and Monterey counties, California, is the most accessible condor-watching location in North America. A resident population of 25 to 30 birds uses the park's sandstone spires as roosting and thermals sites; the park's visitor center provides current sighting information and a condor-tracking board showing the radio-marked birds present in the park.

The Condor Cam operated by Ventana Wildlife Society at the Big Sur cliff nest site provides live streaming video of nesting pairs, accessible globally — one of the most watched wildlife cameras in North America during the breeding season.

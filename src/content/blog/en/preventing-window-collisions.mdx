A bird hits a window because it does not see glass. It sees either a clear flight path through to the sky and trees reflected on the other side, or it sees the habitat reflected back at it on the surface itself. To a flying bird, both look like open air. The collision that follows is one of the largest direct human causes of bird mortality on the continent: in North America alone, peer-reviewed estimates put the toll at roughly 365 million to nearly a billion birds a year, the great majority of them at homes and low-rise buildings rather than skyscrapers. The reassuring part of that statistic is where the deaths happen, because residential windows are exactly the surfaces an individual can fix.

## Why birds collide with glass

Two optical illusions cause almost all strikes. The first is reflection: a window mirrors the sky, a hedge or a feeder, and the bird flies toward what looks like real habitat. The second is the see-through effect, common with corner windows, glass corridors and glass railings, where the bird perceives a clear route to vegetation visible on the far side. Both problems intensify near vegetation and feeders, which is why a bird table placed in the wrong spot can turn a harmless garden into a death trap. Collisions occur year-round but peak during spring and autumn migration, when exhausted, unfamiliar birds move through gardens in large numbers, and again in autumn when juveniles inexperienced with built structures disperse.

## The two-by-four rule

The single most useful principle in collision prevention is the spacing standard often called the two-by-four rule. Birds will attempt to fly through any gap they judge passable. Research at the American Bird Conservancy and Canada's FLAP organisation shows that most birds will not try to pass through openings smaller than roughly five centimetres in height or ten centimetres in width. Therefore any visual deterrent — dots, lines, decals — must be applied across the whole pane in a pattern no more than about 5 cm apart vertically or 10 cm apart horizontally. A single hawk silhouette in the middle of a large window, the most common and most useless intervention, leaves the rest of the glass wide open and saves almost nothing.

## Markers that work on the outside

The deterrent must be on the exterior surface of the glass. Anything applied inside is largely hidden by the very reflection you are trying to break up. The most durable solution is a product such as Feather Friendly, a tape applied as a grid of small white dots that lasts for years and is barely noticeable from indoors. Acopian BirdSavers — lengths of paracord hung vertically about ten centimetres apart, sometimes called zen curtains — are inexpensive, easy to make at home and highly effective. Tempera paint, soap or a simple grid drawn with a marker also works and is ideal for testing a problem window before committing to a permanent fix. Specialised products including ABC BirdTape and patterned films give a tidier finish for those who want one.

## Screens, netting and external shading

Physical barriers are the most reliable measure of all because they remove the hard surface entirely or cushion the impact. Insect screens fitted on the outside of a window break up the reflection and provide a soft surface; where a window already has a fly screen, mounting it externally rather than internally roughly halves strike rates. Lightweight bird netting hung a few centimetres in front of the glass, taut enough that a bird bounces off rather than becoming entangled, is the standard solution for a known problem window. External shutters, awnings and even strategically grown climbing plants reduce reflection by shading the glass and softening the line between building and sky.

## Move the feeders, not the birds

Feeder placement is the most overlooked fix and costs nothing. Counterintuitively, the safest positions are very close to the glass or very far from it. A feeder placed within about half a metre of a window means birds cannot build up enough speed to be injured if they do fly into it; a feeder placed more than ten metres away keeps the activity well clear of reflective surfaces. The dangerous zone is the middle distance — roughly one to ten metres — where startled birds leaving the feeder have room to accelerate straight into the pane. The same logic applies to birdbaths, nest boxes and any other feature that draws birds toward a building.

## New glass and night lighting

For anyone building or renovating, fritted and patterned glass designed for bird safety incorporates the deterrent into the pane itself, with ceramic dots or etched lines fired into the surface. Ultraviolet-reflective coatings, marketed for residential use, exploit birds' ability to see UV while remaining nearly invisible to humans; their field performance is more variable, so they are best combined with other measures. A separate but linked problem is night lighting. Migrating songbirds navigate partly by the stars and are drawn off course and trapped by illuminated buildings, circling until exhausted or colliding at first light. Turning off unnecessary external and internal lights during the spring and autumn migration peaks, or fitting downward-shielded fixtures and motion sensors, is the simplest contribution any household can make to the broader Lights Out movement now running in cities from Toronto to Chicago to New York.

## What to do when a bird strikes

Even with good prevention, the occasional collision happens. A bird that is stunned but uninjured may simply need quiet time to recover. Place it gently in a ventilated cardboard box lined with a soft cloth, keep it somewhere dark, warm and silent away from pets, and do not offer food or water, which can do more harm than good. After an hour or two, take the box outside and open it; a recovered bird will fly off. A bird that cannot stand, holds a wing abnormally or shows bleeding needs a licensed wildlife rehabilitator. Recording strikes — even informally, even just the date and species — helps you identify which windows are the worst offenders and where your prevention effort will do the most good.

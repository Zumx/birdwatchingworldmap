Venezuela's bird fauna of nearly 1,400 species — around 4 percent of all bird species in the world — reflects a biogeographic position that combines Amazonian lowlands, the northern Andes, the Orinoco wetlands, and Caribbean islands. Despite Venezuela's political and economic difficulties, which have affected tourism infrastructure and guide availability, the country's birds remain one of the most extraordinary assemblages in the Americas. Birding Venezuela currently requires careful logistical preparation and is best approached with established local operators, but the rewards — multiple hummingbird species, near-endemic and endemic birds of the Pantepui, and the enormous waterbird spectacle of the Llanos — remain exceptional.

## 1. Henri Pittier National Park, Aragua

Henri Pittier is Venezuela's oldest national park and one of its most consistently productive birding sites. The park covers the coastal mountain range (Cordillera de la Costa) from the Caribbean coast to the Aragua valley, producing an elevational gradient with distinct communities. More than 570 species have been recorded, including several Venezuelan endemics and the spectacular Harpy Eagle (Harpia harpyja, Vulnerable). The Rancho Grande Biological Station provides lodging and serves as a base for cloud forest birding.

## 2. Hato Piñero, Cojedes

Hato Piñero is a cattle ranch in the western Llanos operated partly as a nature reserve and ecotourism destination. The ranch's combination of seasonal flooding and savanna habitat produces an extraordinary list of over 350 species. Jabiru Stork (Jabiru mycteria), Scarlet Ibis (Eudocimus ruber), Anhinga (Anhinga anhinga), and Hoatzin (Opisthocomus hoazin) — one of the most phylogenetically isolated birds in the world — are regular. Anaconda, Capybara, and Spectacled Caiman share the wetland.

## 3. Los Llanos, Barinas and Apure

The Llanos wetland plain, stretching from the Colombian border across Barinas and Apure states, is one of the world's great seasonal wetland spectacles. In the dry season (December to April), receding water concentrates the waterbird community into remaining pools and channels. Scarlet Ibis, breeding Orinoco Goose (Neochen jubata), Giant Anteater, and Anaconda occur throughout the accessible southern Llanos. Hato El Cedral and Hato La Fe are alternative private reserve options in the southern Llanos.

## 4. Tepuis: Canaima and Auyan-Tepui

The tepuis — flat-topped sandstone mountains rising above the Guiana Highlands — hold a community of endemic species found nowhere else. Tepui Vireo (Vireo sclateri), Tepui Swift (Streptoprocne phelpsi), and Roraiman Barbtail (Roraimia adusta) represent the tepui endemic community accessible from Angel Falls (Salto Ángel) at Canaima National Park. The park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site; access is by light aircraft from Ciudad Bolívar or Puerto Ordaz.

## 5. Rancho Grande and Pico Codazzi

The Henri Pittier coastal mountains around Pico Codazzi hold the endemic Venezuelan Troupial (Icterus icterus), Venezuelan Flycatcher (Myiarchus venezuelensis), and the high-altitude community of the Coastal Cordillera. The Hacienda Agua Fría road is particularly productive at dawn.

## 6. Orinoco Delta, Delta Amacuro

The Orinoco Delta holds the Amazonian riverine community at its most intact in Venezuela. Trinidad Piping Guan (Aburria pipile, Critically Endangered, also known as the Trinidad Piping Guan and rare in Venezuela), and the full Amazonian waterbird assemblage occur in the lower delta. Warao indigenous tourism operations from Tucupita provide boat access through the delta channels.

## 7. Mérida and the Venezuelan Andes

The Mérida Andes hold the Venezuelan expression of the Andean cloud forest community. White-throated Foliage-gleaner (Automolus roraimae), Merida Sunangel (Heliangelus spencei), and the Merida Tapaculo (Scytalopus meridanus) are among the Venezuelan endemic and near-endemic cloud forest species accessible from the Mérida cable car area.

## 8. Henri Pittier Coast: Ocumare de la Costa

The Caribbean coast of Henri Pittier provides access to the coastal scrub and mangrove species: Blue-tailed Emerald (Chlorostilbon mellisugus), White-necked Jacobin (Florisuga mellivora), and the full Caribbean coastal assemblage. Brown Pelican (Pelecanus occidentalis), Magnificent Frigatebird (Fregata magnificens), and various terns are visible offshore.

## 9. San Isidro Road and Maracay surrounds

The road south from Maracay into the Henri Pittier forest has been a productive birding route since the first systematic surveys of Venezuelan birds in the 1930s. Spectacled Parrotlet (Forpus conspicillatus), Glittering-throated Emerald (Chionomesa fimbriata), and a comprehensive secondary-forest species list is accessible from the roadside stops.

## 10. Cumaná and Mochima National Park

The Sucre coast around Cumaná and Mochima holds the most accessible Venezuelan seabird sites. Brown Booby (Sula leucogaster), Red-footed Booby (Sula sula), and Magnificent Frigatebird breed on the offshore islands of Mochima; boat trips from Mochima village provide access.

## Planning a Venezuela birding trip

Venezuela holds one of the most diverse bird faunas in South America — over 1,400 species — but birding access is complicated by the country's political and security situation, which requires advance planning with a specialist operator. The most productive approach for international visitors is to book a dedicated Venezuela birding tour with an established operator based in Caracas or Mérida who provides vehicle, logistics, and local guide coverage.

The two most accessible productive regions from a logistics standpoint are the Henri Pittier cloud forest corridor (accessible from Maracay, two hours from Caracas) and the Los Llanos eastern savannas (accessible from Caracas via Maturín or from the Llanos lodges). The Henri Pittier road transect from Maracay to the Caribbean coast at Ocumare provides one of the most concentrated species accumulations in the Neotropics — over 500 species recorded on the eBird hotspot for the park.

The Llanos lodges — Hato El Cedral, Hato La Doñana — provide organized birding stays with guides who specialize in the open-country raptor and waterbird community. Scarlet Macaw roosts, Jabiru Stork colonies, and anaconda watching combine with the birds in an experience unique to the Venezuelan Llanos. The lodge circuit from Caracas requires a six-hour drive; domestic flights to Barinas or San Fernando de Apure reduce the journey.

The Gran Sabana and the tepui tablelands of Canaima National Park — for which Angel Falls is the primary tourist draw — are accessible by domestic flight from Caracas to Ciudad Bolívar, then a light aircraft transfer to the Canaima camp. The tepui endemic community (Tepui Swift, various tepui-endemic finches and tanagers) requires overnight stays at Canaima or in the Gran Sabana highland camps.

Birding timing: the dry season (December to April) is the most productive for Los Llanos, when wildlife concentrates around diminishing water holes and the long grass dries down. The Henri Pittier cloud forest is productive year-round, with April and May offering the best combination of breeding activity and migrant passage.

## The Henri Pittier corridor

Henri Pittier National Park's Portachuelo Pass is one of the most famous single migration watchpoints in the Americas. Each October, billions of passerines funnel through this narrow mountain pass at night; the mornings after cold fronts produce extraordinary concentrations of migrant warblers, tanagers, and flycatchers resting in the forest near the pass. The Rancho Grande Biological Station provides accommodation and a long-term record of migration activity; the station's bird banding program has operated for decades and the cumulative data are cited in migration ecology research internationally.

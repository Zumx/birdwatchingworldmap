Poland holds some of the most important birding habitats remaining in Europe. Białowieża is the last significant fragment of the primeval temperate forest that once covered most of northern Europe — a UNESCO World Heritage Site holding species eliminated from the rest of the continent by millennia of woodland clearance. The Biebrza Marshes are the largest river valley mire in Central Europe and the most important site for wading birds in Poland. The Baltic coast, the Mazurian Lake District, and the Tatra Mountains add further diversity. Poland's bird list stands at around 450 species.

## 1. Białowieża Forest (Białowieża National Park), Podlaskie

Białowieża is one of the most important ecological sites in Europe — the last large remnant of the primeval lowland forest that covered most of Europe after the last Ice Age. The forest's old-growth character, with large areas of standing and fallen deadwood, supports species eliminated from managed forests: White-backed Woodpecker (Dendrocopos leucotos), Three-toed Woodpecker (Picoides tridactylus), Middle Spotted Woodpecker, and all six European woodpecker species breed here. European Bison (Bison bonasus) is the iconic mammal; the wolf and lynx presence adds the forest's full predator guild.

## 2. Biebrza Marshes National Park, Podlaskie

Biebrza is the largest national park in Poland and the most important site for wetland-breeding birds in Central Europe. Aquatic Warbler (Acrocephalus paludicola, Vulnerable) breeds at one of the most important European colonies; Corn Crake (Crex crex) calls from the wet meadows; Spotted Crake (Porzana porzana), Little Crake (Zapornia parva), and Ruff (Calidris pugnax) breed on the marshy margins. The spring flooding of the Biebrza valley, best experienced from March to May, creates a spectacle of thousands of wildfowl on the flooded meadows.

## 3. Słowiński National Park, Pomerania

Słowiński on the Baltic coast holds the most important breeding tern colony in Poland — Arctic Tern (Sterna paradisaea), Sandwich Tern, and Little Tern — on the barrier spit beaches. The park's moving sand dunes and coastal lake system hold breeding Common Eider (Somateria mollissima) and Velvet Scoter (Melanitta fusca) offshore. Whooper Swan and Bewick's Swan winter on the coastal lakes.

## 4. Narew National Park, Podlaskie

The Narew River's unique braided channel system — a "polesian" river mosaic unlike any other in Europe — provides breeding habitat for White-winged Tern (Chlidonias leucopterus), Whiskered Tern (Chlidonias hybrida), and Black Tern (Chlidonias niger) on the oxbow lakes.

## 5. Tatra National Park and Babia Góra, Małopolska

The Tatra Mountains form Poland's southern border with Slovakia. Alpine Accentor, Snowfinch, and Nutcracker breed at altitude; Wallcreeper (Tichodroma muraria) occurs on the rock faces. The national park's trail network is well-developed and the high-altitude habitats are accessible from Zakopane.

## 6. Milicz Fish Ponds, Lower Silesia

The Milicz Ponds complex in Silesia holds one of the largest heron colonies in Central Europe. Grey Heron (Ardea cinerea), Night Heron, Little Egret, and Great White Egret breed in the heronry; Spoonbill (Platalea leucorodia) is a regular visitor. The fish pond system provides managed shallow water habitats for breeding Great Reed Warbler, Little Bittern, and Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened).

## 7. Kampinos National Park, Masovia

Kampinos immediately west of Warsaw is one of the largest national parks in lowland Central Europe. The park's combination of wet alder swamp, dry pine heath, and wet meadow provides breeding habitat for Corncrake, Curlew, Lapwing, and Red Kite (Milvus milvus). Black Stork breeds in the old forest sections.

## 8. Wigry National Park, Podlaskie

Wigry in the Suwałki lake district holds the northeastern Poland bird community: Crane (Grus grus), White Stork (Ciconia ciconia), and Red-backed Shrike (Lanius collurio) in the agricultural margins; Osprey (Pandion haliaetus) on the larger lakes.

## 9. Rybnik reservoirs, Silesia

The warm-water reservoirs near the Rybnik power station hold wintering gulls and waders in above-average concentrations due to the non-freezing water. Caspian Gull (Larus cachinnans) and Yellow-legged Gull (Larus michahellis) are regular; Smew (Mergellus albellus) uses the open water.

## 10. Gdańsk Bay and Hel Peninsula

The Hel Peninsula jutting into the Baltic provides a migration trap for autumn passerine migrants and seabirds. Sea Duck concentrations — Velvet Scoter, Common Scoter (Melanitta nigra), Long-tailed Duck — are visible from the peninsula shores; the Hel Bird Ringing Station operates a long-term autumn trapping program.

## Planning a Poland birding trip

Poland rewards birders with a distinctive combination of pristine wilderness and accessible infrastructure. The Białowieża Forest is best visited in spring (April to May) when the dawn chorus reaches its peak and woodpeckers are actively excavating nest holes, or in winter when the leafless canopy makes detecting Three-toed and White-backed Woodpeckers easier. Winter is also the season for European Bison watching along the forest roads, with the animals concentrated near supplementary feeding stations.

The Biebrza Marshes are at their most spectacular during the breeding season (April to June) when Aquatic Warblers are singing and the flooded meadows produce extraordinary concentrations of breeding waders. The flooding peaks in late March to early April; midsummer visits find the meadows dry and less productive for the rarest species but still rewarding for Corn Crake, Spotted Crake, and the full breeding community. Early morning visits to the marshes in May — when Bitterns are booming and Cranes calling — are among the most memorable experiences in European birding.

Logistics: Warsaw provides the best hub, with car rental giving access to both Biebrza (three hours northeast) and Białowieża (three hours east) in the same week-long trip. The Białowieża village has several guesthouses and a network of approved local guides — a guide is required to access the strictly protected core zone. Biebrza's road network is well-signed from the town of Łomża; the Tsar's Road along the Red Marsh provides the best access to the marsh interior. Polish birding groups organized through the Polish Society for the Protection of Birds (PTOP) provide additional local contact networks.

## Field logistics for Poland's major sites

Białowieża requires advance booking for the strictly protected core zone, which can only be entered with a licensed guide. The Białowieża Tourist Service Centre (Centrum Turystyczne) in the village provides the guide booking service and maintains a current list of English-speaking guides. Core zone permits sell out in peak season (May, early June) — booking two to three months in advance is recommended.

The Biebrza Marshes are best explored using the marked driving routes from the national park office in Osowiec-Twierdza on the Łomża road. The Tsar's Road (Carskie Drogi) along the Red Marsh basin is a gravel track accessible to standard vehicles; the marsh hides along this road provide the best Aquatic Warbler and Spotted Crake access. Early May, when the flooding is receding and birds are establishing territories, is the optimal time.

Warsaw's Chopin Airport serves as the main entry hub, with a strong domestic rail network. A one-week trip with car rental can cover both Białowieża and Biebrza in a single Podlaskie region loop, with optional extensions to the Mazurian Lakes or the Tatra Mountains.

A garden planned for birds is never empty. In spring a blackbird hauls worms from a damp lawn while blue tits ferry caterpillars to a box in the apple tree; in autumn a holly drips with berries and a flock of redwings strips it bare in an afternoon; in the dead of winter a heated bird bath draws goldfinches to drink while a wren works the leaf litter under the hedge. None of this happens by accident. The difference between a sterile lawn and a living, singing garden is a handful of deliberate choices about plants, water and cover — and they work in any space, from a balcony to an acre.

## Start with native plants

The foundation of a bird garden is native vegetation, because native plants feed the insects that feed the birds. The overwhelming majority of garden birds raise their chicks not on seeds but on caterpillars and other invertebrates, and those insects evolved alongside local plants. A native oak, hawthorn or willow supports hundreds of insect species; an ornamental exotic from another continent often supports almost none. Planting for birds therefore begins with planting for bugs. Choose trees, shrubs and flowers that belong to your region, and the food chain assembles itself.

Aim for a long season of fruit and seed as well. Native berrying shrubs such as hawthorn, elder, rowan, blackthorn and holly carry birds through autumn and winter, each fruiting at a slightly different time. Seed-heads from teasel, sunflower, knapweed and grasses feed finches and buntings well into the cold months — which is the single best reason not to tidy the garden in autumn. Leave the seed-heads standing, leave a patch of nettles for butterfly larvae, and you keep the larder stocked when birds need it most.

## Build in layers and structure

Birds read a garden in three dimensions, and the more layers it has, the more species it holds. A varied garden offers canopy trees, an understorey of shrubs, a herb and flower layer beneath, and ground cover and leaf litter at the bottom — each layer feeding and sheltering different birds. A dunnock skulks at ground level, a robin works the middle, a chaffinch sings from the canopy. A flat lawn with a few isolated specimens supports a fraction of what a layered, structured garden does.

A native hedge is the single most valuable structure you can add. A mixed hedge of hawthorn, blackthorn, hazel and field maple provides nesting cover, safe corridors, insect food and autumn fruit all at once, and it shelters far more life than any fence. Climbers such as ivy and honeysuckle add further value: ivy in particular flowers late and fruits in winter, feeding insects in autumn and birds in the lean months, and its dense evergreen tangle is prized nesting and roosting cover. Let some corners grow wild, and resist the urge to cut everything back.

## Provide water year-round

Water draws birds as reliably as food, and many species that ignore feeders will come to drink and bathe. A simple bird bath — shallow, with a rough surface and sloping sides so birds can wade — is enough, kept topped up and cleaned regularly to prevent disease. Bathing matters as much as drinking, because clean, well-maintained feathers are essential for insulation and flight. Place the bath near cover but not so close that a cat can ambush from it, and refresh the water often.

In winter, water can be scarcer and more important than food, because natural sources freeze. Keeping a bird bath ice-free through frost — by floating a small ball on the surface, refreshing it with warm water each morning, or using a low-wattage heater — can be a lifeline. A small garden pond multiplies the benefit many times over: it supplies drinking and bathing water, breeds the insects that feed birds, and draws in dragonflies, frogs and a wider web of life. Even a half-barrel sunk in the ground, with a gently sloping exit, becomes a hub of activity.

## Offer food and feeders wisely

Supplementary feeding helps birds, especially in winter and during the demanding breeding season, but it works best as a complement to natural food, not a replacement. Offer a range to suit different beaks: sunflower hearts and seed mixes for finches and tits, peanuts in mesh for woodpeckers and tits, nyjer for goldfinches and siskins, fat balls and suet for energy in the cold, and windfall apples on the ground for blackbirds, fieldfares and redwings. Different foods at different heights bring in the widest variety.

Hygiene is non-negotiable. Dirty feeders spread diseases such as trichomonosis, which has driven serious declines in greenfinches and chaffinches across Europe, so clean and disinfect feeders regularly, move them around to avoid fouling the ground beneath, and take them down if you see sick birds. Site feeders within a couple of metres of cover so birds can dart to safety, but not so close that predators can lurk unseen. And remember that feeders are a supplement: the deepest help you give is the habitat itself.

## Make it safe from predators and hazards

A bird garden must also be a survivable one. Domestic cats kill enormous numbers of birds, so place feeders and baths where a cat cannot lie in ambush, fit a bell or bright collar on your own cat, and keep cats indoors at dawn and dusk and through the fledging season when young birds are most vulnerable. Dense, thorny native shrubs give birds escape cover that open lawns never can.

Windows are the other great hazard, as reflections of sky and greenery cause fatal collisions; breaking up large reflective panes with external markers, screens or closely spaced decals reduces strikes dramatically. Avoid pesticides and slug pellets, which poison the very insects and invertebrates the garden is meant to produce and can pass up the food chain to the birds themselves. A chemical-free garden is a fundamentally healthier one. Leave a wild, undisturbed corner where birds can nest and forage without interruption.

## Manage for nesting and the long term

Finally, give birds somewhere to raise their young. Dense hedges, climbers and mature shrubs are the natural nest sites, and nest boxes of the right dimensions add capacity where holes are scarce — a 25 mm hole for blue tits, 32 mm for sparrows, open-fronted boxes for robins. Put boxes up in autumn or winter, face them away from the hot afternoon sun and prevailing wet wind, and leave nesting birds strictly undisturbed.

Think across the seasons and the years. A bird-friendly garden is not a single project but an ongoing relationship: plant for a long succession of food, mow less and let some areas go wild, build a compost heap that breeds invertebrates, and accept a little untidiness as the price of abundance. Over time, as hedges thicken and trees mature, the garden becomes richer, holding more species and more individuals each year — a small but real piece of habitat in a landscape that has lost so much of it.

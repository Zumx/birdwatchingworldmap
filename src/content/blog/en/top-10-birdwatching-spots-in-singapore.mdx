Few people expect one of Asia's most rewarding compact birding destinations to be a city-state of barely 730 square kilometres, but Singapore packs an extraordinary range of habitats into a small, accessible island. More than 400 species have been recorded, a mix of resident tropical forest birds, migratory waders and raptors on the East Asian-Australasian Flyway, and a striking community that has adapted to parks and gardens. Crucially, almost every site is reachable by efficient public transport, the trails are well maintained, and the birding can be done comfortably in a long weekend. This combination of biodiversity and convenience makes Singapore an ideal introduction to Southeast Asian birds.

## Sungei Buloh Wetland Reserve

The crown jewel of Singapore birding lies on the northwestern coast facing the Johor Strait. Sungei Buloh Wetland Reserve protects 130 hectares of mangrove, mudflat, and freshwater pond, and from September to March it hosts thousands of migratory shorebirds — common redshank, marsh and curlew sandpipers, Pacific golden plover, and the occasional rare visitor. Smooth-coated otters work the channels, estuarine crocodiles bask on the banks, and from the boardwalks and hides you can watch collared kingfishers, herons, and the resident copper-throated sunbird. It is an ASEAN Heritage Park and the heart of any visit.

## Bukit Timah Nature Reserve

At 163 metres, Bukit Timah is the highest natural point in Singapore and protects one of the last fragments of primary lowland rainforest on the island. The reserve, dominated by towering dipterocarps, holds forest specialities that are hard to find elsewhere: greater racket-tailed drongo, banded woodpecker, red-crowned barbet, and the secretive lesser mousedeer rustling in the undergrowth. The dawn chorus along the summit trail is the closest you will get to true rainforest birding within a major city.

## Central Catchment Nature Reserve

Adjoining Bukit Timah, the much larger Central Catchment Nature Reserve protects the island's reservoirs within secondary and regenerating forest. The MacRitchie Reservoir trails and the famous TreeTop Walk suspension bridge offer canopy-level views where you can find blue-crowned hanging parrot, long-tailed parakeet, and crested goshawk. The forest streams hold the resident grey-headed fish eagle, and the reservoir edges attract kingfishers and the occasional Oriental darter.

## Pasir Ris Park

This coastal park in the northeast combines a mature mangrove boardwalk with parkland and a small forest patch. Pasir Ris is one of the most reliable sites in Singapore for owls, with spotted wood owls and buffy fish owls regularly roosting in the tall trees, much to the delight of photographers. The mangroves hold the ashy tailorbird and copper-throated sunbird, while the open lawns and ponds attract egrets, herons, and migrating raptors in season.

## Hindhede Nature Park and Dairy Farm

Adjacent to Bukit Timah, these connected green spaces around a former quarry offer more open forest-edge birding that is easier on the legs than the main reserve trails. The scrub and secondary growth here are good for the straw-headed bulbul — a globally endangered species for which Singapore is now a key refuge — as well as flowerpeckers, sunbirds, and the laced woodpecker. The quarry pond attracts kingfishers and the occasional grey heron.

## Kranji Marshes

In the rural northwest, Kranji Marshes is the largest freshwater marsh in Singapore, a mosaic of reedbeds, grassland, and woodland managed for wildlife. The marsh is excellent for bitterns, including the cinnamon and yellow bitterns, as well as purple herons, white-breasted waterhens, and the watercock. Raptors such as the black-winged kite and changeable hawk-eagle quarter the open ground, and the grassland attracts munias, prinias, and the baya weaver at its nesting colonies.

## Pulau Ubin

A short bumboat ride from Changi takes you to Pulau Ubin, a rustic island that preserves a slice of old Singapore with abandoned plantations, mangroves, and the restored wetland of Chek Jawa. The quieter habitats here hold the oriental pied hornbill, which has recolonised the island, as well as the red junglefowl, blue-throated bee-eater, and a good selection of woodpeckers. Chek Jawa's intertidal flats are superb for shorebirds and herons at low tide.

## Gardens by the Bay and the Marina area

Even in the heart of the modern city, the landscaped grounds of Gardens by the Bay and the surrounding Marina reclamation attract birds. The flowering trees draw olive-backed and crimson sunbirds, the lawns hold the introduced but now established Javan myna, and the open reclaimed land has produced records of migrant pipits, wagtails, and raptors. It is proof that thoughtful urban greening can support biodiversity.

## Jurong Lake Gardens

In the west, Jurong Lake Gardens combines a freshwater lake, swamp forest, and parkland. The mature trees and water bodies attract the grey-headed fish eagle, white-throated and collared kingfishers, and a variety of herons and egrets. The swamp forest section is a stronghold for the rare grey-headed canary-flycatcher and other forest species on migration, and the open lake draws cormorants and the occasional wintering duck.

## Coney Island Park

Connected to the mainland by bridges in the northeast, Coney Island is a long, narrow island of coastal forest, casuarina groves, and grassland left deliberately rustic. The casuarinas and scrub here are reliable for the rusty-breasted cuckoo, oriental magpie-robin, and a range of migrant flycatchers and warblers in autumn and spring. The mudflats along the northern shore attract shorebirds, and the grassland holds baya weavers and munias. The unpaved tracks keep the visit feeling refreshingly remote despite the skyline rising in the distance.

## When to go and practical notes

Singapore's birding year falls into two rewarding phases. Resident birds sing and breed during the wetter months from March to May, while autumn migration from September to November and the wintering period through to March fill the wetlands with shorebirds and raptors. Dawn, between about seven and nine in the morning, is by far the best window before heat and humidity build. Carry water, insect repellent, and an umbrella for the sudden downpours, and make use of the MRT metro, which reaches almost every reserve. Many of the best sightings come from working the boardwalks slowly rather than from long hikes, and a single unhurried morning at Sungei Buloh or Bukit Timah can produce more than thirty species.

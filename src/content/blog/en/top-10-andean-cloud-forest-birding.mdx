The Andean cloud forest occupies the zone between roughly 1,200 and 3,500 metres on the eastern and western slopes of the Andes, from Venezuela and Colombia in the north to Bolivia in the south. It is persistently wet, draped in mosses and bromeliads, and saturated with bird species that occur nowhere else on earth. The tanager family reaches its evolutionary peak here — dozens of brilliantly colored species in mixed foraging flocks. The hummingbirds diversify into every conceivable beak shape for every conceivable flower. The antpittas, notoriously difficult to observe on the ground, are lured to feeding sites by dedicated lodges. Of all the world's bird habitats, Andean cloud forest provides the highest density of unfamiliar species per unit time for any birder arriving from outside the region.

## 1. Mindo Valley, Ecuador

Mindo, in the western Andes of Ecuador two hours from Quito, is consistently ranked among the most species-rich birding destinations in the world. The valley and its surrounding cloud forest within the Mindo-Nambillo Cloud Forest Protected Area hold over 500 species in a day's accessible range. eBird hotspots at Sachatamia Lodge and the main road through Mindo regularly record 200 species in a weekend. Plate-billed Mountain Toucan, Lanceolated Monklet, Toucan Barbet, and hundreds of hummingbird species occur in the mix.

## 2. Tandayapa Bird Lodge, Ecuador

Tandayapa, above Mindo at slightly higher elevation, provides access to the upper cloud forest zone and a slightly different community from the Mindo floor. The lodge feeders attract up to thirty hummingbird species; the surrounding forest holds Velvet-purple Coronet, Booted Racket-tail, and the elusive Giant Antpitta (Grallaria gigantea) at specialized feeding stations where these secretive birds have been habituated to take worms.

## 3. San Isidro Lodge, Ecuador

San Isidro on the eastern Andes slope, northeast of Quito, provides access to the cloud forest and montane forest of the eastern Andean flank and the beginning of the Amazon watershed. Napo Sabrewing, Ochre-breasted Antpitta, and the full eastern-slope tanager assemblage occur. The lodge's antpitta feeding program, developed independently of similar programs at other Andean lodges, has habituated multiple species to close-range observation.

## 4. Wayqecha Cloud Forest Station, Peru

Wayqecha, operated by the Amazon Conservation Association on the border of Manu Biosphere Reserve in Cusco department, provides research-station accommodation within the transition between cloud forest and high-altitude puna. The elevational range covered by day trips from Wayqecha encompasses cock-of-the-rock (Rupicola peruvianus) leks, Andean Cock-of-the-rock being the most spectacular cloud forest display species.

## 5. Machu Picchu, Peru

The Machu Picchu sanctuary combines the world's most visited archaeological site with a cloud forest that holds over 420 bird species. Torrent Duck (Merganetta armata), White-capped Dipper (Cinclus leucocephalus), and Versicolored Barbet occur along the Urubamba River canyon. The road between Aguas Calientes and the sanctuary entrance, birded at dawn before the tourist buses depart, produces the most productive sessions.

## 6. Cerro de la Muerte, Costa Rica

The Cerro de la Muerte section of the Panamerican Highway south of San José traverses the páramo and highland cloud forest of the Talamanca range. Resplendent Quetzal (Pharomachrus mocinno) is reliable along this road from February through May. Volcano Junco (Junco vulcani), Sooty-capped Chlorospingus (Chlorospingus pileatus), and highland hummingbirds including Fiery-throated Hummingbird (Panterpe insignis) are characteristic.

## 7. San Gerardo de Dota, Costa Rica

Savegre Valley (San Gerardo de Dota) is the most reliably productive single cloud forest site in Central America and the reference site for Resplendent Quetzal. The valley holds the full Talamancan endemic assemblage year-round, accessible from lodges at 2,000 metres with excellent guide networks.

## 8. Bellavista Cloud Forest Reserve, Ecuador

Bellavista, in the Mindo-adjacent western Andes at 2,000 metres elevation, focuses specifically on the cloud forest community at its most accessible. The lodge's hummingbird feeders attract species from multiple altitudinal zones simultaneously, producing comparison opportunities not available in the wild.

## 9. Yungas of Bolivia: Caranavi and Coroico

The Bolivian Yungas — the humid eastern slope of the Bolivian Andes — hold significant Bolivian endemic and near-endemic species including Bolivian Recurvebill (Simoxenops striatus), Rufous-faced Antpitta (Grallaria rufula), and the recently described Yungas Manakin (Chiroxiphia boliviana). The Caranavi-Coroico road between La Paz and the lowland Amazon is the primary access.

## 10. Vilcabamba Mountains, Peru

The remote Vilcabamba range in Cusco and Apurímac departments is one of the least-birded sections of the Peruvian Andes and holds a range of species with restricted ranges within this mountain massif. Yellow-scarfed Tanager (Iridosornis reinhardti), Vilcabamba Thistletail, and Masked Fruiteater represent the endemic and near-endemic complement. Access requires 4WD vehicle and a multi-day expedition.

## Planning an Andean cloud forest birding trip

The Andean cloud forest — the ceja de selva or "eyebrow of the jungle" — runs in a nearly continuous band from Colombia south through Ecuador and Peru to Bolivia, with the most accessible and best-studied sections concentrated in Ecuador and Peru. Birding the cloud forest requires patience with the weather (cloud and mist are the defining features, and visibility can be very limited), patience with dense vegetation (most birds are heard rather than seen), and time to wait for productive clearing moments.

Ecuador provides the most developed cloud forest birding infrastructure, with a network of lodges on both the eastern (Amazonian) and western (Pacific) slopes of the Andes accessible from Quito. The Mindo cloud forest on the western slope — three hours from Quito — has become one of the most visited birding destinations in South America for its extraordinary hummingbird diversity and the Cock-of-the-rock leks. The Paz de las Aves reserve near Mindo provides one of the most organized antpitta-at-feeders experiences on the western slope.

Peru's Manu Road from Cusco to the Amazon lowlands passes through all the Andean altitudinal zones — Puna at the top, Polylepis woodland in the mid-altitude, cloud forest, and lowland Amazonia at the bottom — in a single spectacular drive. The Wayqecha research station and the Villa Carmen field station (both managed by the Amazon Conservation Association) provide accommodation and guide services on this transect.

Bolivia's Yungas Road (La Paz to Coroico) provides the steepest altitudinal gradient in the shortest distance of any Andean cloud forest transect. The road's notoriety as a dangerous mountain drive means it is now used primarily by downhill cyclists and birding vehicles, reducing traffic and improving birding conditions.

The optimal cloud forest birding conditions are dry-season mornings — the period from 6:00 to 10:00 AM before cloud moves up the slope — combined with the breeding season (October to March on the southern slopes, April to August on the northern slopes) when territorial activity maximizes response to playback.

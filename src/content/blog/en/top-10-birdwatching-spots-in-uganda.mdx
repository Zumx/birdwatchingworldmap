Uganda is often called the birding capital of Africa, and the claim is hard to dispute. A country roughly the size of Britain holds more than 1,000 recorded species, around half the total for the entire continent, concentrated where the forests of the Congo basin meet the savannas of East Africa and the high cloud forest of the Albertine Rift. The crown jewel is the shoebill, a prehistoric, slate-grey giant that stalks the papyrus swamps with a bill like a clog, and that single bird draws travellers from across the world to the wetlands of the Nile and Lake Victoria. Around it gathers a cast that includes Albertine Rift endemics, turacos in impossible greens and reds, and a wealth of forest specials.

The birding is also remarkably concentrated. Many of Uganda's best sites cluster along the western arm of the Rift Valley and the road south from Entebbe, so a two-week loop can sample swamp, savanna, montane forest and lakeshore without enormous distances. What follows is a route through ten of the country's finest sites.

## Bwindi Impenetrable National Park

Bwindi, a tract of ancient montane forest in the southwest, is the single richest birding site in Uganda and the best place on earth for Albertine Rift endemics. The famous Mubwindi Swamp trail in the Ruhija sector is the place to seek the Grauer's broadbill, the shy African green broadbill, alongside the handsome francolin, regal sunbird, and dwarf honeyguide. Around 350 species occur, including 23 of the 24 Albertine Rift endemics. The forest is also home to the mountain gorillas, making this a site of double pilgrimage.

## Mabamba Swamp

A vast papyrus wetland on the shore of Lake Victoria, less than an hour from Entebbe, Mabamba is the most reliable place near the capital to see the shoebill. Local guides pole dugout canoes through the channels at dawn, and the swamp also yields blue-breasted bee-eater, papyrus gonolek, lesser jacana, and African pygmy goose. Designated a Ramsar wetland, it is the classic first or last stop for visitors flying in and out of Entebbe.

## Queen Elizabeth National Park

Straddling the equator between Lakes Edward and George, Queen Elizabeth has recorded over 600 species, one of the highest park totals in Africa. The Kazinga Channel boat trip puts you among African skimmer, pink-backed pelican and great flocks of waders, while the Ishasha sector and Maramagambo forest add forest and savanna birds. The Kasenyi plains hold larks, pipits and raptors, and the crater lakes attract lesser flamingos in good years.

## Murchison Falls National Park

At Murchison the entire Nile is forced through a gap barely seven metres wide before thundering into the gorge below. The launch trip to the base of the falls is one of the best birding boat rides in Africa, with red-throated bee-eaters nesting in the riverbanks, Goliath heron, and a good chance of shoebill on the Nile delta where the river enters Lake Albert. The northern savanna holds Abyssinian ground-hornbill, black-billed barbet and Denham's bustard.

## Kibale Forest National Park

Kibale, famous for its chimpanzees, is also a superb mid-altitude forest birding site. The forest holds green-breasted pitta, one of the most sought-after birds in Africa, best found at dawn during its display, as well as black-eared ground-thrush, blue-headed sunbird and great blue turaco. The adjacent Bigodi Swamp, run by the local community, adds papyrus species and is excellent for the great blue and Ross's turacos.

## Semuliki National Park

Tucked in the far west against the Congo border, Semuliki is an extension of the great Ituri forest and holds a Congo-basin avifauna found nowhere else in East Africa. Specials include the Nkulengu rail, lyre-tailed honeyguide, yellow-throated nicator, and an array of hornbills including the white-crested and black dwarf hornbills. The Sempaya hot springs area is the focus, and the bird list reads like that of central, not eastern, Africa.

## Lake Mburo National Park

The smallest of Uganda's savanna parks, Lake Mburo sits in an acacia landscape midway along the road to the southwest. It is the most reliable site for the red-faced barbet, a localised special, and holds African finfoot on the lake, white-winged warbler in the papyrus, and a good range of acacia birds. Its small size and walking safaris make it an easy and pleasant stop.

## Budongo Forest

The Budongo Forest, near Murchison, is the largest mahogany forest in East Africa and a key site for forest birding. The Royal Mile, a straight forest track, is legendary among birders for species such as the chocolate-backed kingfisher, Ituri batis, yellow-crested woodpecker and forest robins. Early mornings here can produce a dense, fast-moving mixed feeding flock that demands sharp eyes and patience.

## Mgahinga Gorilla National Park

Part of the Virunga volcanoes in the far southwest, Mgahinga adds high-altitude bamboo and Hagenia forest to the Ugandan list. The slopes of the volcanoes hold Rwenzori turaco, dusky crimsonwing, and the localised Shelley's crimsonwing, one of Africa's least-known birds. The Afromontane habitat here complements Bwindi for the full set of Albertine Rift specials.

## Entebbe Botanical Gardens and the Lake Shore

The botanical gardens at Entebbe, beside Lake Victoria, are a gentle introduction to Ugandan birds and an excellent first morning. The mature trees and lakeshore hold great blue turaco, orange weaver, red-chested sunbird and a parade of kingfishers, herons and weavers. For visitors with only a few hours before a flight, the gardens reliably deliver a colourful taste of what the country offers.

## Explore on the map

A classic Uganda circuit runs from Entebbe through Mabamba and the savanna parks to the forests of the southwest, returning via Kibale and Queen Elizabeth. The equatorial climate means birding is good year-round, though the drier months of June to August and December to February ease forest access.

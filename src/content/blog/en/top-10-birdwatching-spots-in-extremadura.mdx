Extremadura in western Spain is widely regarded as the finest birdwatching region in all of continental Europe. The combination of vast dehesa — the ancient agroforestry landscape of grazed cork and holm oak woodland — open cereal steppe, major river systems backed by extensive reservoirs, and rocky sierras supporting cliff-nesting raptors creates an extraordinary mosaic of habitat. The region holds the largest populations of Great Bustard in the world, significant breeding numbers of Black Stork, Spanish Imperial Eagle, Black Vulture, and both Lesser and Great Spotted Eagles. Temperatures in summer can be extreme, but spring — particularly March through May — combines resident breeding birds in full activity with arriving summer migrants in pristine fresh plumage.

The relative lack of intensive agriculture compared with the rest of Spain has preserved the open landscapes that specialist steppe and savannah species require. The dehesa system, shaped by centuries of cork harvesting and free-ranging pig and cattle grazing, creates a park-like woodland structure with high insect diversity that sustains a food chain all the way up to eagles. Local landowners and tour operators have built a well-developed birding tourism infrastructure, and dedicated observatories and hides are maintained across the region.

## 1. Trujillo Steppe, Caceres Province

The rolling cereal plains around Trujillo are among the best places in Europe to observe Great Bustard, Little Bustard, Montagu's Harrier, and Roller. Male Great Bustards display in early spring, gathering in leks on open ground where they can be observed from the roadsides. Little Bustards are more secretive but can be flushed from tall cereal stubble and short grass. Pin-tailed Sandgrouse and Black-bellied Sandgrouse visit drinking pools in the afternoons. Stone Curlew calls from the dry scrub at dusk. The castle of Trujillo itself is used as a nesting site by Lesser Kestrel, which can be seen hunting over the plaza in numbers during summer.

## 2. Monfrague National Park, Caceres Province

Monfrague is the iconic Extremadura raptor site. The narrow gorge where the Tagus River cuts through quartzite ridges concentrates vultures and eagles on thermals rising from the sun-heated rock. Black Vulture and Griffon Vulture number in the hundreds. Spanish Imperial Eagle — one of the world's rarest raptors, with a global population of only a few hundred breeding pairs — nests within the park and is regularly seen from the Penalcon viewpoint. Black Stork breeds on the cliffs. Bonelli's Eagle and Short-toed Eagle hunt over the lower slopes. The Tagus reservoir margins hold Purple Gallinule and Kingfisher. A single morning at the main viewpoints above Monfrague village is usually enough to see the headline species.

## 3. Caceres Steppe (Llanos de Caceres), Caceres Province

The protected steppes immediately east and north of Caceres city constitute one of the key Great Bustard strongholds in Spain. The site is a Special Protection Area encompassing over 67,000 hectares of dryland cereal and fallow. Lesser Kestrel nests in the farm buildings on the plain's edge and forages over the fields. Little Owl is common. Calandra Lark — the largest European lark, identified by its broad wings and dark underwing — is the most abundant lark species here, singing from fence posts and fence lines. Spring ploughing disturbs large numbers of insects and creates excellent feeding conditions for both migrant and resident species.

## 4. Arrocampo Reservoir, Caceres Province

Arrocampo is a shallow reservoir used for cooling water from the Almaraz nuclear power station, and its artificially maintained warm water temperatures create extensive reedbeds that hold an exceptional community of wetland birds. White-headed Duck breeds in the reedbeds — a globally threatened species whose European stronghold is in Spain. Purple Swamphen is easily observed at the water's edge. Great Reed Warbler and Purple Heron breed. Little Bittern is resident. Marbled Duck has been recorded during migration. The warmth-extended growing season of the reedbeds means the site remains productive year-round. A public hide managed by the local birding association gives direct views over the open water.

## 5. Zorita Reservoir and Rio Almonte, Caceres Province

The Zorita area, where the Rio Almonte meets the Tagus reservoir system, offers riverside birding through cork oak woodland and along exposed rocky shores. Black Stork fishes along the shallower sections. Bee-eater colonies nest in the sandy banks of the Almonte. Kingfisher is common. The reservoir margins attract waders during autumn drawdown. Rollers — intensely blue and orange insectivores that are easy to find in Extremadura but have declined across much of Europe — are here in summer. The drive along minor roads through the cork oak woodland is also productive, with Golden Oriole, Hoopoe, and Woodchat Shrike in the roadside trees.

## 6. Embalse de Cornalvo, Badajoz Province

Cornalvo is a Natural Park centred on an ancient Roman reservoir that has been used for water storage since the first century AD. The area encompasses dehesa woodland, Mediterranean scrub, and the reservoir itself. Raptors breeding in or near the park include Black Vulture, Booted Eagle, and Short-toed Eagle. The Roman dam wall and surrounding stone structures provide nest sites for Lesser Kestrel and Barn Owl. The forested areas hold resident Black Woodpecker — scarce in Extremadura's south but present here — as well as both Common and Iberian Green Woodpecker. Walking the park's circular trails in April produces excellent views of raptors soaring on thermals.

## 7. Badajoz Rice Fields (Vegas Bajas del Guadiana), Badajoz Province

The intensively cultivated rice fields in the Guadiana valley south of Merida have been transformed by irrigation into a year-round wetland complex. Black-winged Stilt and Collared Pratincole breed on the bare banks between the paddies. White Stork gathers in post-breeding flocks of hundreds before autumn migration. Glossy Ibis has become a regular non-breeding visitor in increasing numbers. Night Heron and Squacco Heron frequent the flooded margins at dusk. Garganey and Pintail use the paddies during migration. The flat terrain makes scanning straightforward, and the raised road between the fields provides elevated views.

## 8. Sierra de San Pedro, Caceres Province

The remote dehesa and rocky hills of the Sierra de San Pedro form a large, lightly visited wilderness along the Portuguese border. Spanish Imperial Eagle holds territory here, as does Black Vulture in a smaller cliff-nesting colony. Cinereous (Black) Vulture numbers during autumn roost flights can be remarkable. Wildcat and Iberian Lynx — though rarely seen — are present, and the overall sense of ecological completeness makes this one of the more atmospheric corners of Extremadura. Birding is best done slowly, on foot or by vehicle along forest tracks, listening for Iberian Chiffchaff, Firecrest, and the distant calls of soaring raptors.

## 9. La Serena Steppe, Badajoz Province

La Serena in eastern Badajoz Province is the largest area of uninterrupted steppe in Western Europe, covering over 150,000 hectares. It is the most important site in the world for Demoiselle Crane during autumn migration, with thousands stopping here. Great Bustard is present year-round in significant numbers. Sociable Lapwing — an extremely rare Asian vagrant with a globally declining population — has been recorded here during migration. Calandra Lark, Thekla Lark, and Lesser Short-toed Lark all breed on the plain. The scale of the open landscape makes this a profoundly different birding experience from anywhere else in Europe.

## 10. Embalse de Garcia de Sola (Puerto Peña), Badajoz Province

This large reservoir on the Guadiana near the Portuguese border is surrounded by steep, thickly vegetated hillsides of granite and holm oak. Black Stork and Grey Heron fish along the rocky shores. Osprey is a regular visitor during autumn migration. The cliffs above the waterline hold breeding Peregrine and Lesser Kestrel. In the woodland surrounding the reservoir, Azure-winged Magpie is conspicuous and vocal. Cetti's Warbler calls from every area of dense lakeside scrub. Boat trips on the reservoir are available locally and provide unusual angles for watching cliff-nesting species at eye level.

---

Extremadura rewards birders who take time to combine the major site types — a morning at Monfrague for raptors, an afternoon on the steppe for bustards and larks, and evenings in the dehesa listening for owls and nightjars.

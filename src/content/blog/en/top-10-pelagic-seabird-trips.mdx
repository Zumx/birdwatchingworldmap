Pelagic seabirds spend most of their lives at sea, coming to land only to breed. Albatrosses, petrels, storm petrels, and shearwaters may travel thousands of kilometres between feeding and breeding, crossing ocean basins in patterns shaped by wind, ocean current, and prey distribution. Seeing them requires going offshore — on pelagic birding trips that range from day boats leaving harbor before dawn to multi-day expedition voyages to remote island breeding sites. The rewards are encounters with some of the most extraordinary birds on earth: a Wandering Albatross (Diomedea exulans) with a 3.5-metre wingspan gliding alongside the boat; a flock of thousands of shearwaters in a tuna school; a storm petrel dancing on the surface in a feeding flutter.

## 1. Monterey Bay, California

Monterey Bay's upwelling system produces the most consistently productive pelagic birding in the continental United States. The Monterey Submarine Canyon brings cold nutrient-rich water to the surface, driving extraordinary seabird and cetacean concentrations. Ashy Storm Petrel (Hydrobates homochroa, Endangered), Black-footed Albatross (Phoebastria nigripes, Near Threatened), and Long-tailed Jaeger (Stercorarius longicaudus) are regular summer and autumn features. Pelagic trips depart from Monterey harbor; Debi Shearwater's trips are the longest-established.

## 2. Scilly, Cornwall, and Outer Hebrides, UK

The Southwest Approaches and the waters west of the Outer Hebrides produce large concentrations of Atlantic shearwaters and petrels during August and September. Cory's Shearwater (Calonectris borealis), Sooty Shearwater (Ardenna grisea), and Balearic Shearwater (Puffinus mauretanicus, Critically Endangered) are regular; Wilson's Storm Petrel (Oceanites oceanicus) occurs offshore. Pelagic trips from Scilly and from Newquay, Cornwall, or from Tiree in the Hebrides cover the most productive waters.

## 3. Kaikoura, South Island, New Zealand

Kaikoura's deep submarine canyon, where the continental shelf plunges to 1,200 metres within a few kilometres of the coast, produces extraordinary seabird diversity throughout the year. Wandering Albatross (Diomedea exulans, Vulnerable), Royal Albatross (Diomedea epomophora, Vulnerable), and nine other albatross species have been recorded on regular half-day trips from the harbor. Hutton's Shearwater (Puffinus huttoni, Endangered), endemic to the Kaikoura Mountains, also occurs. This is the most species-rich pelagic trip relative to distance traveled of any site in the Southern Hemisphere.

## 4. Cape Hatteras, North Carolina

Cape Hatteras is where the Gulf Stream and the Labrador Current converge, producing a productivity hotspot that draws Gulf Stream seabirds within accessible distance of land. Audubon's Shearwater (Puffinus lherminieri), Band-rumped Storm Petrel (Hydrobates castro), and Bermuda Petrel (Pterodroma cahow, Endangered) are among the specialties of Gulf Stream pelagics. Brian Patteson's pelagic trips from Hatteras are the primary operator.

## 5. Sagres, Portugal

The Cape St. Vincent headland at Sagres is the most productive European seawatch point for autumn Atlantic pelagics visible from land. Cory's Shearwater, European Storm Petrel (Hydrobates pelagicus), and Sabine's Gull (Xema sabini) are regular; Great Shearwater (Ardenna gravis) and Pomarine Skua are autumn features. Trips offshore from the cape in autumn produce Wilson's Storm Petrel and Leach's Storm Petrel (Hydrobates leucorhous).

## 6. Manu Tuku, New Zealand — Kaikōura to Bounty Islands

Expedition voyages to the Bounty and Antipodes Islands south of New Zealand reach the core breeding areas for several of the rarest albatrosses on earth. Antipodean Albatross (Diomedea antipodensis, Vulnerable), Gibson's Albatross (Diomedea gibsoni, Endangered), and Bounty Island Shag (Leucocarbo ranfurlyi) breed only in this island group.

## 7. South Georgia and the Falklands

Expedition cruises to South Georgia pass through some of the most productive seabird waters in the world. The combination of the Antarctic Convergence with South Georgia's immense breeding colonies — millions of Macaroni Penguin (Eudyptes chrysolophus), thousands of Wandering Albatross, and 22 other breeding seabird species — produces unparalleled density of seabird encounters.

## 8. Azores pelagics, September to October

Autumn pelagic trips from Faial and Terceira in the Azores target the annual concentration of Cory's Shearwater gathering before migration, alongside passage Wilson's Storm Petrel, Leach's Storm Petrel, and occasional Cape Verde Shearwater (Calonectris atlanticus). Rare petrels including Fea's Petrel (Pterodroma feae, Near Threatened) are regular.

## 9. Wollongong, New South Wales

Pelagic trips from Wollongong south of Sydney access the East Australian Current seabirds. In winter and spring, Wandering Albatross, Black-browed Albatross (Thalassarche melanophris, Least Concern after recovery), and White-chinned Petrel (Procellaria aequinoctialis, Vulnerable) are regular. Australian provider Seabird Pelagics runs monthly trips from the Wollongong harbor.

## 10. Canary Islands pelagics

The Fuerteventura and Lanzarote waters hold the breeding habitat of Cape Verde Shearwater and the primary waters for wintering Barolo Shearwater (Puffinus baroli). Bulwer's Petrel (Bulweria bulwerii) breeds on the islands; Madeiran Storm Petrel (Hydrobates castro) breeds in small numbers. Day trips from the Gran Tarajal harbor cover the most productive offshore zone.

## Planning a pelagic seabird trip

Pelagic birding has a learning curve specific to the open ocean environment — recognizing tube-noses in high-speed flight at distance, distinguishing shearwater species by wing pattern and flight style, and reading the sea conditions that concentrate feeding activity at predictable locations. First-time pelagic visitors benefit substantially from pre-reading shearwater identification guides and familiarizing with the species expected on the specific route.

Seasickness prevention is the most commonly underestimated logistical consideration. Calm-weather pelagic trips to close continental shelf waters can be rough; deep-ocean swells in the Southern Ocean or the North Pacific are consistently challenging even for experienced sailors. Cinnarizine, scopolamine patches, or similar anti-nausea medications taken the evening before departure are more effective than over-the-counter remedies. Standing on deck in fresh air and focusing on the horizon reduces onset; remaining below decks dramatically increases it.

The most productive pelagic routes globally share several characteristics: departing from ports close to submarine canyon edges (where nutrient upwelling brings food to the surface), timing trips to coincide with offshore wind events that push pelagic birds closer to shore, and using chum (fish oil and offal mixture deployed in the ship's wake) to attract petrels and shearwaters within viewing range. The chum bucket technique was pioneered by Brian Patteson on the Outer Banks of North Carolina and is now standard on high-quality pelagic operations worldwide.

Booking a pelagic trip requires researching operator quality carefully. The best operators maintain systematic species lists, employ experienced ornithologists as guides, and provide post-trip checklists useful for eBird submission. The Tern and Petrel Conservation online directory, Surfbirds, and the Birdguides forum all maintain lists of reputable pelagic operators by region.

For Atlantic ocean birders, the Azores (São Miguel and Faial) provides the most concentrated pelagic diversity in the North Atlantic accessible from Europe — a short-haul flight rather than an ocean voyage — with multiple shearwater species feeding in the warm-cold water convergence around the island banks.

## Pacific pelagic birding

The Pacific Ocean's pelagic routes require different preparation from Atlantic trips because the diversity is higher (more alcid species, more shearwater species, several albatross species) and the distances from shore are greater. The California Current off Monterey Bay is the most productive accessible Pacific pelagic route in North America: Monterey Bay's submarine canyon brings deep-water upwelling close to the coast, concentrating seabirds in numbers visible from the boat trips operating from Moss Landing and Santa Cruz. Black-footed Albatross (Phoebastria nigripes, Near Threatened), Buller's Shearwater (Ardenna bulleri, Near Threatened), and Sabine's Gull (Xema sabini) are regularly encountered on Monterey Bay pelagic trips during the autumn season.

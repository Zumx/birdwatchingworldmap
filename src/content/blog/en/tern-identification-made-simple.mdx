A line of terns rests on a sandbar at the river mouth, all of them grey above, white below, with black caps and forked tails. To the beginner they form a single indistinguishable mass. To the experienced observer the same line resolves into three or four species, sorted by bill color, leg length, head shape and the smoky wedge on the outer wing. Terns have a deserved reputation as a difficult group, but most of that difficulty evaporates once you stop looking for color and start reading a short, consistent set of structural and pattern features that hold across the family.

## A family built on subtle differences

Terns are slender, elegant relatives of the gulls, generally smaller and lighter, with pointed wings, forked tails and a buoyant, dipping flight. Most species share the same basic palette — pale grey upperparts, white underparts, a black cap in breeding season fading to a white forehead in winter. Because the plumage is so uniform across species, the differences that matter are in the bill, the legs, the head and the wingtips rather than in any bold splash of color. Learning terns is therefore largely a matter of learning to notice small, reliable distinctions.

## Start with size and the bill

Bill color and size are the first and often the decisive feature. The Sandwich Tern shows a long, slender black bill with a distinctive yellow tip, as if dipped in mustard. The Common Tern in breeding plumage shows an orange-red bill with a black tip; the very similar Arctic Tern shows a blood-red bill with no black tip and a shorter, more delicate look. The Gull-billed Tern shows a short, thick, all-black bill more like a gull's. The Caspian Tern, the largest tern in the world, shows a massive carrot-orange bill visible at great distance, while the Royal Tern shows a slimmer orange bill.

Size sorts the extremes immediately: the Caspian is nearly gull-sized and heavy, the Little Tern and Least Tern are tiny with a fast, flickering flight. Fix the bill and the size in mind before anything else.

## Legs, head shape and tail length

After the bill, read the legs and the head. The Arctic Tern has notably short legs — it looks almost legless when standing next to a longer-legged Common Tern. Head shape and the extent of the black cap help in winter, when many terns show a white forehead and a black rear crown; the pattern of that black mask differs between species and is diagnostic with practice.

Tail length is the Arctic Tern's signature: at rest its long tail streamers project beyond the wingtips, while on the Common Tern the wingtips and tail tip are roughly level. The Roseate Tern takes this further, with extremely long tail streamers and a pale, almost ghostly look, a black bill with a red base, and a pinkish flush on the breast in fresh breeding plumage. Note how the folded wings and tail relate to each other; this single comparison separates several species.

## The outer primaries hold the answer

The most reliable feature for separating Common and Arctic Terns in flight is the pattern of the outer primaries seen from below. On the Common Tern the underside of the outer primaries shows a diffuse dark wedge and a broad dark trailing edge across several feathers. On the Arctic Tern the flight feathers are translucent, with a narrow, neat black trailing edge confined to the very tips, giving a clean, almost transparent wing in good light.

From above, the Common Tern shows a darker wedge on the outer primaries that contrasts with the paler inner wing as the season wears the feathers, while the Arctic Tern keeps a more uniform grey upperwing. This wingtip pattern, more than any plumage tone, is what experienced observers use to call distant Commic Terns — the deliberate shorthand for a Common-or-Arctic that cannot be safely separated.

## The marsh terns are a separate puzzle

The marsh terns of the genus Chlidonias — Black Tern, White-winged Tern and Whiskered Tern — are smaller, darker and behave differently, dipping to pick food from the water rather than plunge-diving. In breeding plumage the Black Tern shows a sooty-black body, the White-winged Tern a striking black body with white wings and tail, and the Whiskered Tern a grey body with a white cheek stripe below the black cap.

In autumn these three become confusing pale grey-and-white birds, separated by the dark shoulder smudge of the Black Tern, the cleaner rump of the White-winged, and the structure and bill of the Whiskered. They favor freshwater marshes and lakes inland rather than the coastal beaches where the sea terns dominate, so habitat already narrows the choice before the field marks come into play.

## Building a workable routine

Approach a tern flock methodically. Scan first for the outliers of size — a Caspian or a Little Tern declares itself instantly. Then work bird by bird through bill color and pattern, the relationship of wingtip to tail at rest, and leg length. When the birds fly, watch the underside of the outer primaries and the trailing edge. Note the call: the Sandwich Tern's grating kirrick, the Arctic Tern's higher, rasping notes and the Common Tern's down-slurred keeyah are learnable and often diagnostic at range.

Accept the honest category of unidentified birds. A distant tern flying away in poor light may genuinely be uncallable, and logging Commic Tern or simply tern sp. is correct practice rather than failure. The reward of terns is that the framework is small and stable: master bill, structure and primaries on the common coastal species, and the whole grey-and-white family steadily comes into focus, season after season, from the breeding colonies of early summer to the post-breeding gatherings of autumn passage.

Peru is one of the top two most species-rich countries on earth for birds, with over 1,870 species recorded. Its vertical geography — from the Pacific coastal desert at sea level to the Andean puna at 5,000 metres to the Amazonian lowlands back at 200 metres — produces more bird habitats per unit area than almost anywhere else. The combination of the cold Humboldt Current coast, the Andean gradient, and the world's largest accessible tropical forest produces a bird list that includes species from virtually every Neotropical family in a single country. Peru's birding infrastructure — lodges, guides, and road access — has developed significantly, though much of the most species-rich lowland remains remote.

## 1. Manu Biosphere Reserve, Madre de Dios and Cusco

Manu is Peru's supreme birding destination and the most species-rich single protected area on earth by most estimates. The biosphere reserve's gradient from Andean highland at 3,500 metres to lowland Amazonia at 300 metres encompasses over 1,000 bird species within a single management unit. The Manu Wildlife Center and associated lodges provide access to the lowland zone where cock-of-the-rock leks, macaw clay licks, and oxbow lake fishing boats produce extraordinary daily lists. The Colpa de Aves Salvadorcillo clay lick (documented in the map database) is near the Madre de Dios sector.

## 2. Tambopata-Candamo Reserved Zone, Madre de Dios

Tambopata is the more accessible complement to Manu, reachable by road and air from Puerto Maldonado. The Tambopata Research Center, five hours by boat from Puerto Maldonado, provides access to the Chuncho macaw clay lick — one of the most spectacular wildlife events in the Americas. Harpy Eagle (Harpia harpyja, Vulnerable) is recorded regularly from the Center's forest.

## 3. Abra Málaga, Cusco

The Abra Málaga mountain pass and road on the way to Manu from Cusco provides access to the high Andean community: Royal Cinclodes (Cinclodes aricomae, Critically Endangered), White-browed Tit-Spinetail, and Tawny Tit-Spinetail on the Polylepis woodland; Condor soaring above the ridges. The road is one of the most productive single drives in highland Peru for Andean endemics.

## 4. Machu Picchu and the Urubamba Valley

The Urubamba (Vilcanota) gorge below Machu Picchu holds Torrent Duck (Merganetta armata), White-capped Dipper (Cinclus leucocephalus), and the cloud forest community at lower elevation than most Andean cloud forest sites. The Machu Picchu Sanctuary holds over 420 species; the cloud forest on the road from Aguas Calientes is productive before the tourist buses begin.

## 5. Pantanos de Villa, Lima

This coastal wetland reserve within Lima's urban area holds over 200 species and is the most accessible birding site for visitors to Peru's capital. The Peruvian Thick-knee (Burhinus superciliaris), Peruvian Pelican (Pelecanus thagus), and the endemic Peruvian Tern (Sternula lorata, Vulnerable) occur along the coastal strip near the reserve.

## 6. Santuario de Colibríes Ensifera Ensifera, San Martín

This hummingbird sanctuary (documented in the map database) in the San Martín region focuses on the Sword-billed Hummingbird (Ensifera ensifera) — the hummingbird with the bill longer than its body. The cloud forest of the eastern Andes in San Martín holds a species-rich community intermediate between Amazonian lowland and highland puna.

## 7. Cordillera Blanca, Ancash

The Huascarán National Park in the White Mountains holds the high-puna community above 4,000 metres: Puna Ibis (Plegadis ridgwayi), Giant Coot (Fulica gigantea), and the Andean flamingos at high-altitude lakes. The dramatic snow-capped peaks above provide Andean Condor views.

## 8. Chaparri Ecological Reserve, Lambayeque

Chaparri in the coastal desert hills of northern Peru holds the largest accessible White-winged Guan (Penelope albipennis, Critically Endangered) population — a species whose captive breeding and reintroduction program at the reserve is one of Peru's most successful single-species conservation programs. Peruvian Screech Owl and Peruvian Pigeon (Patagioenas oenops) are among the dry-forest endemics.

## 9. Sacred Garden, Madre de Dios

The Sacred Garden ecotourism site near Puerto Maldonado (documented in the map database) provides accessible lowland Amazon forest birding near the city. Blue-and-yellow Macaw, Scarlet Macaw, and the full Madre de Dios lowland community are accessible within day-trip range.

## 10. Laquipampa Wildlife Refuge, Lambayeque

Laquipampa in Lambayeque holds the primary population of the White-winged Guan and provides the dry-forest birding that complements the wetter forest of the Andes' eastern slope. Koepcke's Screech Owl (Megascops koepckeae), Marvelous Spatuletail (Loddigesia mirabilis, Endangered), and Longwhiskered Owlet (Xenoglaux loweryi, Endangered) represent Peru's extraordinary endemic specialties.

## Planning a Peru birding trip

Peru is the second most species-rich country in the world after Colombia and regularly produces the highest species totals in organized Big Day events. The country's extraordinary altitudinal range — Pacific coast at sea level to the Puna above 4,500 metres, dropping to Amazonian lowlands below 200 metres — creates the habitat stacking that produces its diversity. Planning a Peru trip requires deciding which altitudinal zone to prioritize.

The most productive approach for international visitors focuses on the southeastern corner: Cusco as the base, with Manu Road connecting the Puna grasslands, cloud forest, and lowland Amazonia in a single four-to-six-day drive. This gradient from Puna to lowland is the most biologically diverse accessible transect on Earth. The Wayqecha Cloud Forest reserve (around 3,000 metres) and the Villa Carmen research station (around 700 metres) in the Manu buffer zone provide research accommodation; advance booking through Centro Manu or the Wayqecha reserve office is essential.

The Manu Biosphere Reserve proper requires a minimum of five days and a registered guide. Colpa de Guacamayo (the macaw clay lick in the Manu national park zone) and the Tapir Clay Lick are the iconic experiences; both require river-boat access from the Puerto Maldonado entry point or the Boca Manu airstrip. The combination of a cloud-forest approach to Manu (via Paucartambo Road from Cusco) and a lowland Manu visit by boat is the most comprehensive approach but requires ten to fourteen days.

For visitors focused on the Atacama coast, the Paracas Peninsula and the Humboldt Current offshore are accessible from Lima with a domestic flight to Pisco or a five-hour drive south. The Colpa de Aves Salvadorcillo clay lick in the Ucayali region is documented in the map database as a specific destination for macaw and parrot activity.

Lima as entry point provides day-trip access to the coastal lomas (fog-oasis vegetation) and the Pantanos de Villa wetland, both productive for Peruvian coastal endemics in the morning hours before driving inland.

## Southern Peru birding circuit

The most efficient Peru circuit for first-time visitors combines three regions in ten days: two nights at Wayqecha Cloud Forest (3,000m, accessible from Cusco), two nights at the Manu lowland zone (700m, accessible by continuing down the Manu Road), and three nights at Tambopata (280m, accessible from Puerto Maldonado airport). This route covers puna-to-lowland Amazonia in a single geographic sweep with minimal backtracking.

The Manu Road is recognized as the most species-rich single road transect in the world. Over 1,000 species have been recorded along its length; a dedicated two-day drive stopping at all productive roadside habitats can yield 400 to 500 species. Local operators in Cusco organize Manu Road day trips and multi-day journeys with expert local guides; advance booking in the May-October dry season is essential as demand exceeds guide availability.

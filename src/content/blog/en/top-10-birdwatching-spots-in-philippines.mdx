The Philippines' position as a large tropical archipelago far from any continental land mass has produced extraordinary levels of bird endemism. More than 200 species breed only in the Philippines; several island groups — Palawan, the Sulu Archipelago, Mindanao — hold their own sets of locally endemic species that overlap minimally with adjacent islands. The combination of Wallacea-region endemism and Philippine-specific speciation produces a bird list unlike that of any adjacent country. The challenges are significant: the Philippines has lost more than 90 percent of its original lowland forest, several species are Critically Endangered with fewer than 100 individuals known, and political instability in parts of Mindanao limits access to some of the most productive areas.

## 1. Puerto Princesa Subterranean River National Park, Palawan

Palawan is the most species-rich birding island in the Philippines for endemic species. The Subterranean River National Park provides access to the Palawan endemic community: Palawan Peacock-Pheasant (Polyplectron napoleonis, Vulnerable), Palawan Hornbill (Anthracoceros marchei, Vulnerable), and Blue-backed Parrot (Tanygnathus sumatranus) are among the 15 Palawan endemics. The UNESCO World Heritage karst forest is the most visited natural site on the island.

## 2. Mt. Kitanglad Range Natural Park, Mindanao

Mindanao holds the highest concentration of Philippine endemic species. Mt. Kitanglad is the most accessible of the Mindanao highland sites, with a trail system through montane forest holding Philippine Eagle-Owl (Bubo philippensis, Vulnerable), Mindanao Racquet-Tail (Prioniturus waterstradti, Endangered), and Apo Myna (Basilornis miranda). Access from Bukidnon requires a local guide and permit from the park office.

## 3. Philippine Eagle Center, Davao

The Philippine Eagle Center near Davao on Mindanao maintains captive Philippine Eagles (Pithecophaga jefferyi, Critically Endangered) as part of the breeding and research program. Wild eagles can be observed in the surrounding forest; the Center's resident pair provides close-range views impossible in the wild. The Philippine Eagle is one of the world's largest and rarest raptors, with fewer than 800 pairs estimated in the wild.

## 4. Rajah Sikatuna Protected Landscape, Bohol

Rajah Sikatuna on the island of Bohol holds the best remaining lowland forest on the Visayan islands. Philippine Tarsier (Carlito syrichta) shares the forest with Philippine Scops Owl (Otus megalotis), Tarictic Hornbill (Penelopides panini, Vulnerable), and the endemic Visayan drongo. The Birding Site documented in the map database at this location provides access to the forest interior.

## 5. Subic Bay Former US Naval Base, Luzon

Subic Bay's forest, surrounding the former US Navy facility, is the most accessible old-growth forest on Luzon. Philippine Cockatoo (Cacatua haematuropygia, Critically Endangered), Philippine Hawk-Eagle (Nisaetus philippensis, Vulnerable), and Luzon Bleeding-heart Pigeon (Gallicolumba luzonica, Vulnerable) breed in the forest. The area is managed as the Subic Bay Freeport Zone, with vehicle access to forest roads.

## 6. Mt. Apo Natural Park, Mindanao

Apo, the Philippines' highest mountain at 2,954 metres, holds the island's highest-altitude community. Philippine Eagle has been documented here at altitude; Apo Sunbird (Aethopyga boltoni), Turquoise Flycatcher (Eumyias panayensis), and Philippine Bush Warbler (Locustella seebohmi) represent the highland endemics.

## 7. Olango Island Wildlife Sanctuary, Cebu

Olango is the most important shorebird site in the Philippines and one of the most significant in the East Asian-Australasian Flyway. Tens of thousands of waders — Red Knot (Calidris canutus), Bar-tailed Godwit, and the full flyway shorebird community — stage here on northward migration in spring. The island is accessible by boat from Mactan.

## 8. Candaba Swamp, Pampanga, Luzon

Candaba is a seasonal freshwater marsh on the Pampanga River plain north of Manila, holding wintering ducks and waders in large concentrations from November to March. Northern Pintail (Anas acuta), Garganey (Spatula querquedula), and migrant waders use the swamp as a staging area.

## 9. Hardin ng Rosas, Quezon City, Luzon

The Hardin ng Rosas Bird Watching Site (documented in the map database) in Quezon City provides an urban birding location within Metro Manila accessible to visitors staying in the capital. Philippine Pied Fantail (Rhipidura nigritorquis) and Lowland White-eye (Zosterops meyeni) are among the endemic species visible in city parks.

## 10. Balabac Island, Palawan

The southernmost Palawan island, closest to Borneo, holds the Bornean endemic community at the edge of its range alongside the Palawan endemics. Borneo Spiderhunter (Arachnothera latirostra) and Dulit Frogmouth (Batrachostomus harterti) represent the Bornean component; access requires boat from Bataraza.

## Planning a Philippines birding trip

The Philippines' island geography requires advance logistical planning. Palawan — the richest single island for endemics — is a separate destination from Mindanao or Luzon and requires a domestic flight from Manila. A productive first Philippines trip typically focuses on either Palawan plus Luzon (including Subic Bay and Mt. Apo via Davao) or on Mindanao intensively (Kitanglad, Apo, the Agusan Marsh). Combining all major islands in a single trip requires two weeks minimum.

The dry season (November to May) is the most practical travel window. The northeast monsoon brings rain to the eastern coasts of Mindanao and Luzon from November to February; the southwest monsoon from June to October affects the western islands including Palawan. Timing a Palawan visit in the February to April window provides the best combination of dry access roads, active bird breeding season, and manageable heat.

Philippine Eagle viewing at the Philippine Eagle Center in Davao is possible year-round. Wild eagle sightings are highly variable and unpredictable; visits to Mt. Apo with guide permits are essential for any hope of a wild encounter. The local guide network in Davao is well-organized and has connections to the most productive forest sites on Mindanao.

Manila-based birding provides day-trip access to the Subic Bay forest and La Mesa Eco Park, both productive for Philippine lowland endemics within an hour of the capital. For international birders with limited time, a Subic Bay day trip combined with a Mt. Makiling forest walk provides a representative Philippine lowland experience without requiring domestic flights.

## Birding around Manila and Luzon

Luzon, the Philippines' largest and most populated island, provides the most accessible birding for visitors who need to maximize time in Manila. The Subic Bay Freeport Zone's old-growth forest — one of the few remaining lowland forest patches in the north — is a two-hour drive from Manila and worth a full day. Philippine Cockatoo (Cacatua haematuropygia, Critically Endangered) can sometimes be seen from the forest road; Philippine Hawk-Eagle and Luzon Bleeding-heart Pigeon require walking the forest trails with a local guide.

La Mesa Eco Park in Quezon City provides urban birding accessible without leaving Metro Manila. The park's secondary forest and lake hold Lowland White-eye (Zosterops meyeni), Philippine Pied Fantail, and a range of visiting waterbirds. Candaba Swamp north of Manila is a two-hour drive; the seasonal marsh is most productive for wintering ducks from November to February.

A practical first Philippines itinerary for international birders: two days at Subic Bay, then a domestic flight to Puerto Princesa (Palawan) for three days targeting the Palawan endemic community, followed by a flight to Davao for the Philippine Eagle Center and Mt. Kitanglad. This circuit covers three of the four main Philippine bird zones in eight to ten days.

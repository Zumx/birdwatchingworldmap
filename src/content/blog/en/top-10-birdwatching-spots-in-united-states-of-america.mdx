The United States is one of the world's great birding destinations by any measure. The continental forty-eight states alone span from Pacific temperate rainforest to Florida swamp, from Arctic tundra in Alaska to tropical dry forest in the Rio Grande Valley. Nearly 1,000 species occur regularly within US borders, and the country's extensive protected lands, well-developed access infrastructure, and long tradition of organized birding make it approachable for birders at every level of experience. The nation's most experienced birders debate endlessly which areas offer the most in sheer diversity — Southeast Arizona, the Rio Grande Valley, and the Gulf Coast migration corridor are the most frequently cited contenders — but remarkable birding exists in every state.

The list below selects ten sites that together represent the full geographic and ecological range of American birding. These are places where the combination of bird diversity, accessibility, and the overall quality of the experience make them stand out from the hundreds of other excellent birding areas across the country.

## 1. Southeast Arizona: Madera Canyon and the Huachuca Mountains

Southeast Arizona is the most species-rich corner of the continental United States for resident birds, driven by the convergence of the Rocky Mountain, Sierra Madre, and Chihuahuan Desert bird communities within the sky island mountain ranges. Madera Canyon in the Santa Rita Mountains is the classic introduction site, with feeders at the Santa Rita Lodge attracting multiple hummingbird species, including Rivoli's, Blue-throated Mountain Gem, and Lucifer Hummingbirds in summer. The Huachuca Mountains add Elegant Trogon at Garden Canyon and Ramsey Canyon. Montezuma Quail, Whiskered Screech-Owl, and an array of Mexican specialties push southeast Arizona species lists to 400 or more with extended effort.

## 2. Cape May, New Jersey

Cape May is a peninsula pointing into Delaware Bay, and the geography creates a funnel effect for southbound migrants in autumn. The hawk migration from mid-September through November is among the most watched in North America: Broad-winged Hawk, Sharp-shinned Hawk, and Cooper's Hawk pass in their thousands, with Peregrine Falcon hunting the migrant warblers at the tip. The New Jersey Audubon's Cape May Bird Observatory coordinates migration counts that have been continuous since 1976. In spring, the Delaware Bay shore is among the best places in the world to observe the spectacle of migratory shorebirds — Red Knot, Sanderling, and Ruddy Turnstone — refueling on horseshoe crab eggs.

## 3. Point Reyes National Seashore, California

Point Reyes on the California coast is a peninsula that juts into the Pacific and functions as a vagrant trap for western vagrants and rare Asian species during autumn migration. The combination of dense coastal scrub, freshwater ponds, and direct Pacific Ocean access produces an extraordinary variety, and the Point Reyes Christmas Bird Count has historically been among the highest-totaling in the country. Resident birds include Black Oystercatcher and Pigeon Guillemot on the coast, and the Tomales Point tule elk reserve borders excellent passerine habitat. Tule Elk were actually important for Point Reyes long before birding was fashionable there.

## 4. Bosque del Apache National Wildlife Refuge, New Mexico

Bosque del Apache in the Rio Grande Valley of New Mexico is the premier site for wintering Sandhill Cranes and Snow Geese in the American Southwest. The refuge manages water levels on its agricultural fields and shallow marshes to support tens of thousands of Lesser Sandhill Cranes from November through February, with the morning flights from the roost ponds particularly spectacular. Ross's Goose winters alongside Snow Goose in the fields. Greater Roadrunner is resident in the surrounding desert scrub. The combination of crane and goose concentrations with excellent desert riparian birding makes Bosque one of the most rewarding single sites in the country.

## 5. Everglades National Park, Florida

The Everglades is the largest subtropical wilderness in North America and holds a bird community with strong Caribbean affinities found nowhere else in the continental United States. The main road through the park, from the Ernest F. Coe Visitor Center to Flamingo, passes through pine rocklands, tropical hardwood hammocks, and open sawgrass prairie that together produce over 350 species. Anhinga, Roseate Spoonbill, and Snail Kite are all accessible from roadside viewing. Short-tailed Hawk is a Florida specialty breeding in the park. In winter, concentrations of wading birds around the Anhinga Trail and Mrazek Pond are among the most photogenic wildlife gatherings in the country. Cape Sable Seaside Sparrow, an endangered subspecies, inhabits the coastal marshes.

## 6. Gulf Coast Migration Corridor: High Island to Sabine Woods, Texas

The upper Texas Gulf Coast is the landing site for neotropical migrants crossing the Gulf of Mexico in spring, and when frontal systems stall birds over the water, they arrive at Gulf Coast woodlands in their thousands in what birders call a fallout. High Island's oak mottes, Sabine Woods, and the Big Thicket region behind the coast together form the most productive migration corridor in North America. On a good April morning at Sabine Woods, a single small grove of trees can hold 30 species of warblers simultaneously, often at arm's length in the vegetation. The Texas Gulf Coast also holds impressive winter concentrations of Whooping Crane at Aransas National Wildlife Refuge.

## 7. Dry Tortugas, Florida

The Dry Tortugas are a small archipelago 110 kilometres west of Key West, accessible only by ferry or seaplane. The historic brick fort on Garden Key, Fort Jefferson, is a natural vagrant trap: any songbird or hawk migrating across the Gulf is drawn to the only vegetation for miles in any direction. The fort's moat walls and the few trees on the island regularly hold exhausted migrants in spring, with species ranging from common warblers to extreme rarities. The sea around the islands supports breeding Magnificent Frigatebird, Masked Booby, and Brown Noddy. Brown Booby is resident on the tower. Sooty Tern nests in its thousands on Bush Key, one of the largest colonies in North America.

## 8. Hawk Mountain Sanctuary, Pennsylvania

Hawk Mountain in the Appalachian ridges of Pennsylvania is the world's first refuge established to protect migrating raptors, founded in 1934. The North Lookout viewpoint above the Kittatinny Ridge channels migrant raptors moving along the ridge on northwest winds in autumn, and on good weather days in September and October the concentration is extraordinary. Golden Eagle passes through from late October through December. Broad-winged Hawk numbers in the tens of thousands pass on peak September days. Sharp-shinned Hawk, Cooper's Hawk, and Red-shouldered Hawk are all numerous. The Sanctuary's continuous monitoring record since 1934 constitutes one of the most important long-term raptor datasets in the world.

## 9. Churchill, Manitoba, Canada

While technically in Canada rather than the United States, Churchill on Hudson Bay is so routinely included in North American birding itineraries that its exclusion would be conspicuous. The accessible subarctic tundra around Churchill is the only place where most birders from the lower 48 states will see breeding species including Smith's Longspur, Willow Ptarmigan, Ross's Gull, and Ivory Gull. The polar bear migration from October through November coincides with late season birding when Snowy Owl and Gyrfalcon become regular. The Arctic coast gives views of Beluga Whale alongside seabirds including King Eider and Long-tailed Duck.

## 10. Olympic Peninsula, Washington

The Olympic Peninsula in Washington State contains some of the wettest temperate rainforest in the world in the Hoh and Quinault valleys, where annual rainfall can exceed 3,500 millimetres. The forest here supports Marbled Murrelet, a seabird that nests not on cliffs but in the mossy branches of old-growth conifers far inland from the sea. Olympic is the primary Murrelet stronghold in the lower 48 states, and the fate of its nesting population is tied directly to the preservation of old-growth trees. Olympic also holds Northern Spotted Owl in the dense conifer forest, and the coast at La Push and Rialto Beach provides excellent seabird watching with regular Common Murre, Pigeon Guillemot, and Tufted Puffin offshore.

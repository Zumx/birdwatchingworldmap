Arizona is one of the richest birdwatching states in the United States, a distinction that owes much to its position at the intersection of the Rocky Mountain, Great Basin, Chihuahuan Desert, Sonoran Desert, and Sierra Madrean biogeographic zones. The sky island mountains of southeastern Arizona — isolated ranges rising from desert grassland to pine-oak forest and coniferous woodland — provide habitat for species found nowhere else north of the Mexican border. The state list exceeds 550 species, and the southeast corner of the state in particular draws visiting birders from across North America and beyond. ## 1. Ramsey Canyon Preserve, Huachuca Mountains

The Nature Conservancy's Ramsey Canyon Preserve in the Huachuca Mountains south of Sierra Vista is one of the most famous hummingbird sites in North America, with 14 or more hummingbird species recorded in a single year. The canyon's running stream, dense riparian vegetation, and elevation range draw an extraordinary concentration of species. Broad-tailed, Magnificent, Blue-throated, and White-eared Hummingbirds are regularly present in summer, alongside Elegant Trogon, Sulphur-bellied Flycatcher, and the Arizona-endemic subspecies of Painted Redstart. The preserve limits daily visitor numbers; advance reservation is strongly recommended for peak season visits from April through September.

## 2. Madera Canyon, Santa Rita Mountains

Madera Canyon in the Santa Rita Mountains south of Tucson is among the most visited birdwatching destinations in the American Southwest, offering reliable access to sky island specialties within a two-hour drive of Tucson's airport. The road through the canyon passes through riparian scrub, oak woodland, and pine-oak forest, with feeders at the Santa Rita Lodge attracting year-round hummingbirds and other nectar visitors. Elegant Trogon breeds in the canyon and is regularly seen from April to August. Five owl species roost or breed in the canyon, and the canyon is an excellent site for Spotted Owl.

## 3. Portal and Cave Creek Canyon, Chiricahua Mountains

Cave Creek Canyon in the Chiricahua Mountains near the town of Portal is the most species-rich single birding location in Arizona. The combination of a perennial stream, magnificent riparian forest, and the full elevation gradient of the Chiricahuas produces a checklist that exceeds 300 species. Mexican Chickadee, Sulphur-bellied Flycatcher, Greater Pewee, Painted Redstart, Olive Warbler, and multiple owl species breed in the canyon. Thick-billed Parrots have been reintroduced to the nearby Chiricahua National Monument area.

## 4. Patagonia-Sonoita Creek Preserve, Santa Cruz County

The Nature Conservancy's preserve along Sonoita Creek at Patagonia protects one of the finest stretches of cottonwood-willow riparian forest in the state. The preserve has a long history of producing exceptional rarities — the first North American records of several Mexican species have been made here — but its regular suite includes Rose-throated Becard, Thick-billed Kingbird, Gray Hawk, Yellow-billed Cuckoo, and Blue Grosbeak. The creek itself can be walked along a maintained trail.

## 5. Buenos Aires National Wildlife Refuge, Altar Valley

Buenos Aires NWR occupies a large area of semi-desert grassland and mesquite bosque in the Altar Valley west of Tucson. The refuge was specifically acquired to protect Masked Bobwhite, a grassland quail subspecies extirpated from the United States and subject to a long-running reintroduction effort. The grassland habitat also supports Pronghorn, Buff-breasted Sandpiper in migration, and, in wet years, breeding Aplomado Falcon.

## 6. Saguaro National Park, Rincon Mountain District

The Rincon Mountain district of Saguaro National Park east of Tucson combines Sonoran Desert with the full elevational sequence of the Rincon Mountains. At lower elevations, Cactus Wren, Curve-billed Thrasher, Gilded Flicker, and Gila Woodpecker are characteristic of the saguaro-palo verde community. Elf Owls breed in saguaro cavities. The Cactus Forest Drive provides excellent birding from a vehicle.

## 7. Sweetwater Wetlands, Tucson

Sweetwater Wetlands is a constructed wastewater treatment facility on the northwest side of Tucson that has become an outstanding birding site for wetland species not otherwise easily found in this desert city. The ponds and emergent marsh attract migrant waders, breeding Black-necked Stilt and American Avocet, abundant dabbling and diving ducks in winter, and regular rarities. The site is free to enter and accessible year-round, making it a consistently productive stop for visiting birders.

## 8. San Pedro Riparian National Conservation Area

The San Pedro River — one of the last undammed rivers in the Southwest — supports the most extensive riparian forest in the region, and the Bureau of Land Management's riparian conservation area along the river corridor between the Mexican border and St. David holds an extraordinary concentration of breeding and migrant landbirds. The site is best known as a spring migration concentration point: on days with appropriate wind conditions, hundreds of warblers, flycatchers, and other migrants stack up in the cottonwood gallery forest. The San Pedro House visitor center provides access and information.

## 9. Willcox Playa, Sulphur Springs Valley

The Willcox Playa in the Sulphur Springs Valley is the preeminent shorebird and crane site in Arizona. The dry playa floods after monsoon rains to create extensive shallow mudflat habitat that attracts large numbers of shorebirds from July through October. In winter, the valley hosts roosting flocks of tens of thousands of Sandhill Cranes and, in most years, a handful of Whooping Cranes. The Whitewater Draw Wildlife Area nearby is the best concentration point for the crane roost.

## 10. South Fork of Cave Creek, Portal

The South Fork Trail from the Portal trailhead in Cave Creek Canyon is the single most productive specific trail for forest interior species in Arizona's sky islands, passing through a remarkable concentration of mixed pine-oak forest and riparian woodland at approximately 1,700 metres elevation. Whiskered Screech-Owl, Northern Pygmy-Owl, Cordilleran Flycatcher, Grace's Warbler, Painted Redstart, and the full array of Chiricahua highland species are regularly recorded on a single morning walk. The 4-mile round trip to the end of the maintained trail is accessible for birders of most fitness levels.

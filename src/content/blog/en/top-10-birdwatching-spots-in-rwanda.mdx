Rwanda is small enough to cross in a day, yet it concentrates an extraordinary slice of African birding into its thousand hills. The country has recorded over 700 species, and its appeal lies in how easily they can be reached. In the southwest, the vast Nyungwe Forest is one of the last great montane rainforests of the Albertine Rift, holding the highest concentration of regional endemics anywhere accessible to travellers. In the east, Akagera National Park spreads savanna, lakes and papyrus swamp along the Tanzanian border, adding a completely different avifauna. Between them lie crater lakes, marshes and the volcanoes of the far northwest, so a single compact loop delivers an astonishing range.

Roads are good, distances are short, and English and French are widely spoken, making Rwanda one of the most comfortable countries in which to chase difficult African birds. What follows is a circuit through ten of its finest sites.

## Nyungwe Forest National Park

Nyungwe, over 1,000 square kilometres of unbroken montane forest in the southwest, is the crown jewel. It holds around 300 species including 29 Albertine Rift endemics, more than anywhere else readily birdable. The Uwinka and Bigugu trails offer red-collared mountain babbler, Rwenzori turaco, handsome francolin, and the much-wanted red-throated alethe. The Kamiranzovu marsh within the forest is the place for Grauer's swamp warbler and the localised swamp species, while the forest canopy walkway gives a rare overhead view of feeding flocks.

## Akagera National Park

In the east along the Tanzanian border, Akagera is Rwanda's only savanna park and the perfect counterpoint to Nyungwe. Its mosaic of acacia woodland, rolling grassland and a chain of lakes fed by the Akagera River supports over 500 species. The papyrus swamps hold the prized shoebill and the papyrus gonolek, while the lakes draw African fish-eagle, pink-backed pelican, and saddle-billed stork. The grasslands add Souza's shrike, lilac-breasted roller, and a strong cast of raptors.

## Volcanoes National Park

Famed for its mountain gorillas, Volcanoes National Park in the far northwest protects the Rwandan slopes of the Virunga massif. Above the farmland, the bamboo and Hagenia forest holds Rwenzori turaco, dusky crimsonwing, strange weaver, and the scarce Shelley's crimsonwing. The high-altitude habitat here complements Nyungwe for the full Albertine set, and a gorilla trek doubles as a chance for montane birds along the way.

## Rugezi Marsh

Rugezi, a high-altitude papyrus and sedge wetland in the north near Ruhengeri, is a Ramsar site and an Important Bird Area of the first rank. It is the key Rwandan site for the Grauer's swamp warbler and Carruthers's cisticola, and holds grey crowned cranes, the national symbol, in significant numbers. The marsh is best worked from its edges, and patient watching can reveal a suite of wetland specials found in few other places.

## Lake Kivu Shore

The shore of Lake Kivu, particularly around Gisenyi, Kibuye and Cyangugu, offers relaxed lakeside birding between the major sites. The lakeside fig trees and gardens hold weavers, sunbirds and barbets, while the open water attracts terns, cormorants and the occasional grey-headed gull. As a place to recover between Nyungwe and the volcanoes, the lakeshore is both scenic and surprisingly productive.

## Nyabarongo Wetlands

The Nyabarongo River and its associated marshes in central Rwanda form an important wetland for waterbirds and papyrus specialists. The area holds papyrus gonolek, white-collared oliveback, and seasonal concentrations of herons and storks. Less visited than the headline parks, the Nyabarongo system rewards birders willing to explore the marshes near Kigali and along the river valley.

## Gishwati-Mukura National Park

Rwanda's newest national park, Gishwati-Mukura protects two remnant montane forests in the northwest that are slowly being restored and reconnected. The forests hold a subset of the Albertine endemics, including Rwenzori turaco and regal sunbird, in a less-visited setting. As restoration continues, the park is steadily growing in interest for birders seeking the regional specials closer to the volcanoes.

## Mukura Forest

The Mukura sector, part of the Gishwati-Mukura park, is a small but botanically rich montane forest that holds mountain species and provides a quieter alternative to Nyungwe. Its trails pass through dense undergrowth where the careful observer can find the localised forest birds and a chorus of greenbuls, apalises and forest warblers.

## Kigali City and Environs

Even the capital rewards a morning's birding. The Nyandungu Eco-Park, a restored wetland within Kigali, holds herons, kingfishers, weavers and the occasional crowned crane, and serves as an easy introduction on arrival or departure. The city's hills and gardens add common but attractive species, making Kigali a worthwhile first taste of Rwandan birds.

## Cyamudongo Forest

Cyamudongo, an isolated forest fragment connected to the Nyungwe ecosystem, is famous for its habituated chimpanzees but also holds a distinctive bird community. Its isolation has produced a slightly different mix from the main Nyungwe block, and it is a fine place to combine primate tracking with forest birding in the southwest.

## Explore on the map

A classic Rwanda circuit runs from Kigali southwest to Nyungwe, north along Lake Kivu to the volcanoes and Rugezi, and east to Akagera, sampling forest, wetland and savanna in a compact loop. The drier seasons of June to September and December to February ease forest trails, though birding is good year-round.

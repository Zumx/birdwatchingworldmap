Azerbaijan is one of the least-watched birding countries on the western edge of the Caspian Sea, yet it sits squarely on a major Eurasian flyway. The shallow lagoons and reedbeds of the Kura-Aras lowland funnel staggering numbers of wintering waterfowl down from Siberia, while the wooded ridges of the Greater and Lesser Caucasus hold a suite of montane and forest species found almost nowhere else in Europe. In a single trip you can move from sub-sea-level deserts at Gobustan to alpine meadows above 2,500 metres, and the contrast in birdlife is dramatic. Roughly 400 species have been recorded across the country, and several globally threatened birds winter here in internationally important numbers.

## 1. Gizilaghaj State Nature Reserve

Gizilaghaj (Kyzylagach), a vast Ramsar wetland on the south-western Caspian coast near Lankaran, is the single most important bird site in the country. Its shallow bays and brackish lagoons hold hundreds of thousands of wintering ducks, geese, swans, and coots, plus internationally significant numbers of Greater Flamingo (Phoenicopterus roseus) and Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened). In hard winters the reserve becomes the principal refuge on the entire western Caspian. Marbled Duck and White-headed Duck (Oxyura leucocephala, Endangered) both occur, and the surrounding semi-desert holds wintering raptors.

## 2. Lankaran and the Talysh Lowlands

The humid Talysh corner in the far south-east is the most biologically distinct part of Azerbaijan, with subtropical Hyrcanian forest climbing from the coast into the Talysh Mountains toward the Iranian border. This is the place for Caspian Tit, Green Warbler, and wintering passerines that filter down the coast. The tea plantations and rice paddies around Lankaran are excellent for herons, crakes, and migrant waders, and the warm microclimate keeps the area busy with birds even in midwinter.

## 3. Shirvan National Park

Shirvan, a flat expanse of semi-desert and saline depressions south of Baku on the Caspian shore, is best known as the stronghold of the goitered gazelle, but it is also outstanding for steppe and wetland birds. Look for wintering Common Crane (Grus grus), great flocks of larks and pipits, and raptors including Eastern Imperial Eagle (Aquila heliaca, Vulnerable) and Steppe Eagle. The coastal lagoons here add waders and wildfowl to the open-country list.

## 4. Greater Caucasus near Quba and Xinaliq

The northern mountains around Quba and the high village of Xinaliq, one of the highest continuously inhabited settlements in Europe at around 2,300 metres, give access to genuine Caucasian specialities. Caucasian Snowcock (Tetraogallus caucasicus) and Caucasian Grouse (Lyrurus mlokosiewiczi, Near Threatened) — both near-endemic to the Caucasus — occur on the alpine slopes, along with Güldenstädt's Redstart, Great Rosefinch, and Wallcreeper on the rock faces.

## 5. Shahdag National Park

Azerbaijan's largest national park spans the high Greater Caucasus and protects forests, subalpine meadows, and rugged peaks above Shahdag mountain. Bearded Vulture (Gypaetus barbatus, Near Threatened), Golden Eagle, and Lammergeier-style soaring birds patrol the ridges, while the conifer and birch zones hold Krüper's-type tits, Red-fronted Serin, and breeding Caucasian montane species. The park is the best single area for combining endemic gamebirds with high-mountain raptors.

## 6. Gobustan

Gobustan, famous worldwide for its prehistoric rock carvings and mud volcanoes, is a stark semi-desert south of Baku that drops below sea level. Its dry gullies and scrub hold Finsch's Wheatear, Pied Wheatear, Eastern Rock Nuthatch, and Chukar, with wintering Pale Rock Sparrow and the occasional desert wanderer. The combination of UNESCO archaeology and arid-zone birds makes it a rewarding day from the capital.

## 7. Absheron Peninsula and Baku Bay

The peninsula around Baku, despite its oil infrastructure, is a migration trap and a fine seawatching base. Absheron National Park at the tip holds wintering grebes, divers, and sea ducks, while the bays and salt pans attract gulls, terns, and waders. Caspian Gull is abundant, and seawatching in passage periods can produce skuas and shearwater-type movement along the coast.

## 8. Lake Aggol (Ağgöl) National Park

Aggol, a large freshwater lake in the Kura lowland, is one of the most important inland wetlands of the South Caucasus and a Ramsar site. Its reedbeds and open water support breeding Marbled Duck, Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened), White-tailed Plover, and large colonies of herons, ibises, and terns. In winter the lake fills with wildfowl, and the surrounding steppe holds harriers and eagles.

## 9. Lake Sarisu and the Kura River Wetlands

The Kura and Aras river floodplains hold a chain of marshes and reservoirs — Sarisu among them — that act as stepping stones for migrating waterbirds. Pygmy Cormorant (Microcarbo pygmeus), Glossy Ibis (Plegadis falcinellus), Squacco Heron, and breeding terns are characteristic, and the riverine tugay woodland holds Menetries's Warbler and Black Francolin (Francolinus francolinus), a localised speciality of the region.

## 10. Hirkan National Park

Hirkan, in the Talysh Mountains south of Lankaran, protects the ancient Hyrcanian forest — relict broadleaf woodland of ironwood, oak, and hornbeam that survived the Ice Ages. This UNESCO-recognised forest holds Caspian Tit, Semi-collared Flycatcher, and a southern-Caspian assemblage of woodpeckers and warblers unlike anything elsewhere in the country. Walking the forest tracks in spring delivers a dense dawn chorus rarely heard this far north.

## When to visit

Azerbaijan birding peaks in two seasons. Winter, from November to February, is the headline period: Gizilaghaj, Aggol, and Shirvan hold their full complement of wildfowl, flamingos, pelicans, and cranes, and the mild Talysh coast stays productive throughout. Numbers at Gizilaghaj alone can exceed a million waterbirds in a severe winter, making it one of the great waterbird spectacles of western Asia.

Spring, from late March to May, brings northbound migration through the Caspian lowlands and the start of the breeding season in the mountains. This is the window for Caucasian Snowcock and Caucasian Grouse, which display on the high slopes around Xinaliq and Shahdag when snow still lies in the gullies. The forests of Hirkan and Talysh fill with singing warblers, and the steppe reserves see passage raptors funnel north along the Caspian.

Autumn passage, from September to October, is less concentrated than the Black Sea bottlenecks further west but still moves good numbers of raptors and waterbirds south. For mountain birding the snow-free months of June to September are best, while the lowland reserves are uncomfortably hot in midsummer and quieter for birds.

## Practical notes

Baku is the entry point, with a modern airport and good road connections. A hired car or local driver is essential, as the key reserves are widely scattered: Gizilaghaj and the Talysh sites lie a long four to five hours south of the capital, while Quba and the northern Caucasus are a two-to-three-hour drive north. Permits are required to enter several state reserves, including Gizilaghaj and Shirvan, and are most easily arranged through a local guide or operator. A handful of small Azerbaijani birding services — operating under names such as Mənzərə — can handle reserve permits, mountain logistics, and access to the more remote Caucasus villages.

English signage is limited outside Baku, and Russian remains useful in rural areas. Roads to the high Caucasus villages can be rough and are sometimes closed by snow into late spring, so flexibility helps. Bring a scope for the Caspian wetlands, where distances are large, and warm layers for the mountains even in summer.

The Wandering Albatross carries the largest wingspan of any living bird: the maximum reliably recorded is 3.63 metres, achieved by a male shot on Possession Island in the sub-Antarctic Crozet archipelago in 1965. Most adults have wingspans between 2.5 and 3.5 metres. These are not dimensions for flapping flight — a bird this large could not sustain flapping flight for the distances it travels. The Wandering Albatross is an engineering solution to oceanic travel, a machine for extracting energy from the wind over the Southern Ocean through a technique called dynamic soaring.

Diomedea exulans breeds on sub-Antarctic islands — South Georgia, the Crozet Islands, Kerguelen, Macquarie Island, and the Prince Edward Islands — and spends the rest of its life at sea, covering extraordinary distances on feeding trips and on its migrations between breeding seasons. Satellite-tagging studies have documented individual birds traveling circumglobal routes in a matter of weeks, crossing the Southern Ocean repeatedly in a year without any terrestrial stopover. A bird may travel 10,000 kilometres on a single foraging trip from the breeding island.

## Dynamic Soaring

The aerodynamic basis of albatross flight has been an object of scientific study since Lord Rayleigh first described the mechanics in 1883. Dynamic soaring exploits the differential wind speeds at different heights above the ocean surface: near the surface, friction with the water slows the wind; at height, the wind blows faster. An albatross descends from height into the low-wind zone near the surface, banking and turning, then climbs steeply into the higher-speed zone, where the stronger wind provides lift. By continuously cycling between descent and ascent, the bird extracts kinetic energy from the wind gradient rather than from its own muscles.

The wings are highly specialized for this technique: long, narrow, and held stiffly, with a high aspect ratio similar to a sailplane. The shoulder joint locks in the extended position, allowing the wings to be held open with minimal muscular effort. In laboratory conditions, instrumented birds have been shown to maintain flight for hours without any recorded increase in heart rate above resting level. The physiological cost of cruising flight in a Wandering Albatross is extraordinarily low.

## Life History and Breeding

Wandering Albatrosses are among the longest-lived birds: ringed individuals at South Georgia have been monitored for over 50 years, and the maximum documented age exceeds 70 years. Breeding begins at 10 to 11 years of age following several years of courtship dances at the breeding colony with potential partners. Pairs are monogamous and long-term — the same pair returns to the same nest site across multiple decades.

A single egg is incubated by both parents alternately for approximately 78 days, the longest incubation period of any bird. After hatching, the chick is brooded initially and then left alone at the nest for increasingly long periods as both parents forage at sea. The chick fledges after approximately 278 days — more than nine months. As a consequence, successful pairs can only breed in alternate years; a failed breeding attempt occasionally allows a pair to attempt to breed in consecutive years.

The prolonged chick-rearing period means that any disturbance to the breeding site during the nesting season — research activity, tourism, or predator pressure — can have immediate and serious consequences for that year's breeding success.

## South Georgia and Key Breeding Sites

South Georgia in the South Atlantic holds the largest Wandering Albatross breeding population, with approximately 1,500 breeding pairs. Access to the island is regulated, and most visitors arrive by expedition cruise vessels that circle the island from the Falkland Islands or from Ushuaia in Argentina. The breeding colony at Prion Island — a small island off South Georgia's north coast — is accessible to visitors via a boardwalk and represents one of the world's great wildlife experiences: adults performing elaborate wing-spreading and bill-touching courtship dances, downy chicks waiting on their large nest mounds, and birds in all stages of the eight-year breeding cycle.

The Crozet Islands (French territory) and Kerguelen Islands hold the other major populations. Access to both is limited to scientific personnel, making South Georgia the primary destination for birders and expedition travelers.

## The Longline Fishing Crisis

The Wandering Albatross is classified as Vulnerable by the IUCN, with a declining population trend directly attributable to mortality from longline fishing. Pelagic and demersal longline fishing vessels deploy lines tens of kilometres long carrying thousands of baited hooks. Albatrosses attracted to the bait dive for the hooks as the line is paid out, become hooked, and drown as the line sinks. Estimates from the early 2000s placed annual longline bycatch mortality across all albatross species at more than 100,000 birds per year.

Mitigation measures are known and effective: bird-scaring lines (tori lines) attached to the vessel and streaming behind it create a visual barrier that frightens birds away from the baited section of line; setting lines at night reduces encounters; weighted lines sink more quickly and are unavailable to surface-diving birds for a shorter time. The Agreement on the Conservation of Albatrosses and Petrels (ACAP), signed by 13 range states and major fishing nations, provides the framework for coordinating mitigation uptake.

Progress has been made: South Georgia's fishing fleet operates under stringent bird-bycatch requirements and has achieved near-zero albatross bycatch in recent audited seasons. However, high-seas fishing fleets operating under flags with weaker enforcement remain a source of mortality that is difficult to quantify and control.

The Wandering Albatross's position at the intersection of the world's most remote ocean and the world's largest industrial fisheries makes its conservation a test of international governance as much as a biological problem.

The Pantanal spreads across roughly 150,000 to 195,000 square kilometres of tropical floodplain in western Brazil, with extensions into Bolivia and Paraguay. It is the world's largest tropical wetland and one of the most productive wildlife viewing areas on Earth. Unlike the Amazon, where dense canopy makes most wildlife invisible, the Pantanal's open savanna, grassland, and seasonally flooded fields keep animals accessible. The Jabiru Stork, standing nearly 1.5 metres tall, is the most visible symbol of the region, but the Pantanal also holds the world's largest population of Hyacinth Macaw, the world's heaviest flying parrot. Over 650 bird species have been recorded in the Pantanal, including spectacular concentrations of herons, ibises, kingfishers, and raptors.

The Pantanal's seasonal rhythm is fundamental to planning a visit. The wet season from November through March floods the lowlands and makes most roads impassable but concentrates birds on elevated patches of dry land. The dry season from April through October (with July and August the driest months) draws birds to shrinking water bodies, creating spectacular concentrations. The following ten sites span the Brazilian Pantanal from north to south.

## 1. Transpantaneira Road, North Pantanal, Mato Grosso

The Transpantaneira is the most famous birding road in South America, a raised earthen causeway stretching roughly 150 kilometres south from Poconé into the northern Pantanal. The road crosses approximately 120 wooden bridges, and the pools beneath each bridge hold a dense gallery of wildlife. Yacare Caiman, Giant River Otter, and Capybara are present at almost every pool, and the birds are extraordinary: Rufescent Tiger Heron, Striated Heron, Whistling Heron, and Capped Heron all fish the same stretches. Hyacinth Macaw is reliable in the palms along the road. Jabiru Storks stand in the open fields, and the large nest mounds in tall trees are visible from some distance.

## 2. Poconé and the Northern Gateway

The town of Poconé is the most common entry point to the Pantanal, and the agricultural margins around the town are productive for open-country species before entering the main Transpantaneira. Seriema — a bizarre large terrestrial bird that looks like a long-legged crane but is most closely related to falcons — stalks the cattle pastures. Chestnut-bellied Guan is visible in the small gallery forest fragments around farm buildings. The wetlands immediately south of Poconé hold Roseate Spoonbill in impressive numbers during the dry season, and the flooded fields are a reliable site for the curious Sunbittern, which spreads enormous patterned wings in a threat display when approached.

## 3. Hotel Porto Jofre Area

Porto Jofre is the end of the Transpantaneira where the road terminates at the Cuiabá River, and the boat-based wildlife watching from Porto Jofre is exceptional. This is the best-known area for Jaguar sightings, and the riverside forest also holds Giant River Otter regularly. The water-level areas around Porto Jofre concentrate large numbers of herons, kingfishers, and waterbirds. The Black-collared Hawk, a specialist fisher of freshwater fish, is common along the Cuiabá. Boat trips down the Três Irmãos tributary give access to forest interior where Amazonian Umbrellabird has been recorded. Green-and-rufous Kingfisher inhabits the shaded backwater channels at this end of the road.

## 4. SESC Pantanal and Barão de Melgaço

The SESC Pantanal private reserve near Barão de Melgaço in the eastern northern Pantanal protects a large area of relatively undisturbed habitat and operates as a research and ecotourism facility. The reserve's trails and boat launches give access to river-edge forest where the Sungrebe and Sunbittern both occur. The reserve is particularly known for Giant Anteater sightings in the grassland sections, and the ground-foraging behavior of the anteaters often disturbs insect populations that attract Curve-billed Scythebill and various ground-foraging birds. The reserve has well-maintained infrastructure for overnight stays.

## 5. Miranda and the Southern Gateway, Mato Grosso do Sul

The town of Miranda near the BR-262 highway is the main access point for the southern Pantanal, and the road through the Buraco das Piranhas area between Miranda and Corumbá passes through classic southern Pantanal habitat. The roadside ponds are regularly used by Jabiru, Maguari Stork, and Wood Stork simultaneously. Hyacinth Macaw occurs here as well, in the manduvira palms of the campo cerrado. Black-and-white Monjita and Strange-tailed Tyrant inhabit the open grassland. Crab-eating Fox is conspicuous, often active in daylight, and its behavior sometimes flushes ground-nesting birds including South American Painted-Snipe.

## 6. Fazenda San Francisco, Mato Grosso do Sul

Fazenda San Francisco near Miranda is one of the most visited private lodges in the southern Pantanal, offering multi-day stays with guided horse-riding, boat, and vehicle tours. The lodge grounds include a large colony of Monk Parakeet in the ranch buildings, and the surrounding grassland regularly produces the bare-legged owl species unique to the Pantanal: the endangered Burrowing Owl has a stable population on the fazenda's raised embankments. The dense gallery forest strips along the Rio Miranda hold Plum-throated Cotinga and Red-capped Cardinal alongside more typical forest species. The horse-back tours reach areas inaccessible to vehicles, including floodplain margins productive for marsh birds.

## 7. Corumbá and the Paraguay River

Corumbá is the largest city in the Pantanal, sitting on a bluff above the Paraguay River near the Bolivian border. The waterfront at Corumbá provides views over the river where Giant River Otter, Brazilian River Otter, and the occasional Giant Anteater swimming between islands can be seen. The harbour at Porto Geral is a productive early morning spot for herons and kingfishers before boat traffic intensifies. The semi-urban scrub around the outskirts of Corumbá is good for Pale-breasted Thrush, Burrowing Owl, and Glittering-throated Emerald hummingbird. Boat trips from Corumbá into the Paraguay river channels reach areas of flooded forest holding species rarely accessible by road.

## 8. Nhecolândia, South-Central Pantanal

Nhecolândia is an ancient river meander system in the south-central Pantanal that has evolved into a landscape of hundreds of shallow, lens-shaped lagoons separated by strips of elevated campo cerrado. The lagoons range from fresh to highly saline, and the salinity gradient supports an unusual variety of waterbirds not typical elsewhere in the Pantanal. South American Flamingo uses the saline lagoons during the dry season in varying numbers. Chestnut Jacamar inhabits the cerrado strips, and the Stripe-backed Antbird is found in the thicker scrub patches. The area is most easily explored from the fazendas that operate tourist accommodation in the Nhecolândia corridor.

## 9. Fazenda Rio Negro, Aquidauana Area

Fazenda Rio Negro is a National Geographic Society conservation project operating as an ecotourism lodge near Aquidauana. The fazenda grounds include a famous resident Giant Anteater population and a wide variety of habitats from flooded campo to semi-deciduous forest. The forest patches here hold Toco Toucan in its most accessible Pantanal setting, with the species nesting in hollow trees near the lodge buildings. Buff-necked Ibis forages in the pastures around the fazenda infrastructure. The boat trips on the Rio Negro target Jabiru nesting colonies in the tall trees visible from the river, and the Hyacinth Macaw is reliably seen at the palm groves visible from the vehicle tracks.

## 10. Caiman Ecological Refuge, Miranda

The Caiman Ecological Refuge is a large private reserve near Miranda that has been operating conservation-focused ecotourism for several decades. The refuge combines working cattle ranch with conservation management and is particularly known for its successful Hyacinth Macaw monitoring program, which has reversed local declines through nest box provision. The number of macaws visible at the refuge makes it arguably the single best place in the world to observe this spectacular species at close range. The nesting boxes installed throughout the property are occupied each year, and the activity around them during the breeding season from August through November is remarkable. The birding beyond the macaws is also excellent, with over 400 species recorded on the property.

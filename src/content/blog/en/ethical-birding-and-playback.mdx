Every birder causes some disturbance to some birds some of the time. The question is not whether to be perfect — you cannot be — but how to make decisions that keep harm proportionate and concentrated in the species and contexts that can absorb it. The American Birding Association's Code of Birding Ethics is the clearest written statement of those principles, and it is worth understanding rather than just agreeing with in the abstract.

## The ABA Code of Birding Ethics

The ABA Code, first adopted in 1996 and most recently revised in 2024, organises its principles around a single overriding rule: the welfare of birds comes before the interests of the observer. Everything else follows from that.

The code asks observers to promote the welfare of birds and their environments; to avoid stressing birds or exposing them to danger; to keep a respectful distance from nests, roosts, display areas, and feeding sites; to stay on trails and roads where possible; and to respect the rights of landowners. It also asks birders to respect fellow birders, which includes not harassing people at a site who are making different ethical judgements, and to follow all laws and regulations governing access and species protection.

The code is not enforceable law. It is a community standard. Adherence to it is voluntary, which means it only works if experienced birders model it and explain it openly rather than assuming others know it.

## Playback: the rules and the reasoning

Playing a recorded bird call or song through a speaker to attract a species is the most contested technique in field birding. Used carefully, it is a tool that helps observers connect with secretive species that would otherwise go undetected. Used carelessly or repeatedly, it wastes the energy of territorial birds defending against phantom rivals, can distract breeding birds from nest or chick duties, and at popular sites can subject individual birds to dozens of playback events per day.

The general guidance is: use playback sparingly and briefly. One or two short sequences, then stop and wait. If the bird does not respond, move on rather than escalating volume or frequency. Playback used to confirm a bird's presence for a large group — twenty birders getting a clear view from one brief interaction — is less damaging than the same technique used twenty times by twenty individuals on twenty separate visits.

The prohibitions are clear. Playback should never be used for species listed as endangered or threatened. A single breeding pair of a range-restricted endemic does not have spare energy to waste on phantom rivals. The Boreal Owl (Aegolius funereus) in its North American range and various Strigiformes on oceanic islands with small populations are examples where ornithological consensus holds that playback during the breeding season is not justified. For most common species in non-breeding contexts, the calculus is different.

A secondary harm is social. When playback works at a well-publicised site, it establishes an expectation that the species should be producible on demand. The next fifty visitors try the same technique. The bird spends the breeding season in a continuous simulated territorial dispute. Several studies on Marsh Wren populations in the United States have documented reduced nesting success at heavily birded sites where playback was routine; Bicknell's Thrush breeding plots in the northeastern Appalachians have been used to model the same effect.

## Nest disturbance

Nest photography is the area where the stated values of birding and wildlife photography most frequently diverge in practice. Clearing vegetation to improve sight lines to a nest, repeatedly visiting a nest to photograph chick development, and approaching active nests closely enough to cause parents to flush all cause real harm. Parent absence from the nest, even briefly, exposes eggs and chicks to predation and thermal stress.

In most jurisdictions, actively disturbing the nest of any wild bird carries a legal penalty. In the UK, the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 protects all wild birds at the nest. In the US, the Migratory Bird Treaty Act covers most species. Photographing a bird at its nest without appropriate licensing is illegal in most European countries regardless of how the image was obtained.

The practical rule is simple: if you have to modify the environment to get the photograph, you are doing harm. If the parent flushes when you approach, you are too close.

## Flushing birds for better views

Deliberately flushing a roosting owl to observe its flight, walking towards a shorebird flock to photograph birds in flight, or repeatedly disturbing a feeding bird to observe its alarm response are all forms of disturbance that serve the observer's interest at a cost to the bird. Shorebirds on staging areas during southbound migration are particularly vulnerable: they are building fuel reserves for ocean crossings, and each flush forces an energy expenditure they cannot easily replace.

The standard advice is to watch from the point at which the bird is aware of you but not alarmed. Birds signal awareness — they look toward you, posture stiffens, feeding stops — before they flush. Finding that distance and staying behind it consistently is better practice than approaching to the flush point and retreating.

## Baiting raptors

Using live or dead prey to attract raptors into photographic range is practiced at some tour sites and widely condemned by professional ornithologists. Live mice and rats used as lures for great grey owls in Finland and Snowy Owls in North America are the most commonly cited examples. Beyond animal welfare issues with the lure itself, habituating large raptors to human presence as a food source changes their behaviour in ways that increase their long-term mortality risk from collision, human interaction, and reduced wariness around roads.

Several major photography competitions now disqualify images obtained via baiting. The argument is not complex: a wild animal is not wild if its behaviour in the photograph was purchased.

## Social media and rare breeding species

eBird's rare bird alert function, rare bird networks on social media, and WhatsApp birding groups move information about unusual sightings extremely fast. For a lost migrant or winter vagrant on a publicly accessible site, rapid dissemination is largely harmless. For a rare breeding species at a previously undocumented nest site, the same rapid dissemination can cause the site to receive hundreds of visitors in the first weekend.

The ABA Code is explicit: share information about rare birds with care and judgment. Consider the vulnerability of the species, the security of the site, and the likely behaviour of the audience before posting. A rare breeding species at a private site where the landowner has not consented to visits should not be posted publicly at all. A rare breeding species on a public reserve with ranger infrastructure and defined viewing points is a different situation.

The difference between these cases is not always obvious, and the social pressure to share is strong. Experienced birders have an obligation to think it through rather than default to sharing.

## What good ethical practice looks like

It looks like staying on the path, using a hide when one is provided, keeping playback brief, leaving a nest alone entirely, retreating when a bird flushes rather than following it, and telling newer birders what you are doing and why. The code is most effective when it is transmitted as practice — modelled in the field — rather than preached abstractly online.

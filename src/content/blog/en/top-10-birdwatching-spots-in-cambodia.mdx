Cambodia holds a concentration of mega-rare birds out of all proportion to its tourist profile. The vast seasonal lake of the Tonle Sap, which swells and shrinks dramatically each year as the Mekong reverses its flow into it during the monsoon, supports breeding colonies of large waterbirds that have vanished from most of Southeast Asia, while the northern dry dipterocarp forests shelter the last viable populations of giant ibis, white-shouldered ibis and three Critically Endangered vulture species. For the dedicated lister, no other Southeast Asian country offers so many globally threatened birds in a single circuit. Community-based ecotourism, much of it run by the Wildlife Conservation Society and the Sam Veasna Conservation Tours network, channels birding income directly into protecting these species and into the villages that guard their nests, making a visit unusually meaningful.

## Prek Toal Core Area, Tonle Sap

Prek Toal, a biosphere core zone at the northwestern end of the Tonle Sap, is the single most important waterbird breeding colony in Southeast Asia. Reached by boat from Siem Reap, it holds large numbers of Spot-billed Pelican, Greater and Lesser Adjutant, Milky Stork, Painted Stork, Black-headed Ibis and Oriental Darter nesting in the flooded forest. The dry season from December to early March concentrates the birds as water levels fall and the flooded forest becomes a maze of fish-rich channels; ranger-guided boats reach observation platforms and stilted towers among the colonies, from which the sheer density of nesting storks and pelicans is overwhelming. Visiting before about nine in the morning gives the best light and activity. This is among the most spectacular waterbird sights in Asia, and the entrance fees underwrite the rangers who protect the colony from egg collectors.

## Tmatboey, Northern Plains

The village of Tmatboey in the dry forests of Preah Vihear province is the flagship of Cambodian community birding and the world's most reliable site for the Giant Ibis, the national bird, and the White-shouldered Ibis. Both are Critically Endangered and number in the low hundreds globally. The open dipterocarp woodland with seasonal pools (trapeangs) also holds Rufous-winged Buzzard, Brown Fish-Owl, White-rumped Falcon and Greater Flameback. Visitor fees fund a conservation contract with the community, who protect ibis nests in return and refrain from clearing the trees the birds depend on. A typical visit involves a night or two in the simple village lodge and pre-dawn drives to known feeding pools, where a guide scopes the open woodland for ibis coming to drink. The model pioneered here has since been copied at other Cambodian sites.

## Florican Grasslands, Tonle Sap floodplain

The seasonally flooded grasslands around the Tonle Sap, particularly the Stoung-Chikraeng area, are the last stronghold of the Bengal Florican, a Critically Endangered bustard whose males perform leaping display flights over the grass in the dry season. The grasslands also hold Manchurian Reed Warbler in winter and large numbers of harriers roosting communally. These grasslands are under intense pressure from dry-season rice expansion, and protected display sites are now the species' main hope; early mornings from February to April are best for displaying males.

## Ang Trapeang Thmor

This former reservoir from the Khmer Rouge era, northwest of Siem Reap, has become a key reserve and the most accessible site in Cambodia for the Sarus Crane, the world's tallest flying bird, which gathers here in the dry season. The wetland and surrounding grassland also support Greater Spotted Eagle, Black-necked Stork, and large numbers of wintering ducks. The eastern wild water buffalo also persists here. A day trip from Siem Reap can combine cranes, raptors and open-country specialties in a relaxed half-day circuit.

## Seima Protection Forest, Mondulkiri

In the eastern hills of Mondulkiri, Seima protects extensive evergreen and semi-evergreen forest that holds a different, more Indochinese-Sundaic avifauna. Germain's Peacock-Pheasant, Bar-bellied Pitta, Banded Kingfisher and several broadbills inhabit the forest, alongside black-shanked douc langurs and yellow-cheeked gibbons whose dawn calls fill the canopy. The area around the village of Andong Kraloeng offers community-based stays. The wildlife-rich forest here represents the wetter, hillier counterpoint to the northern dry plains.

## Kratie and the Mekong River

The stretch of the Mekong around Kratie and Sambor is best known for the Irrawaddy dolphin, but it is also vital bird habitat. The sandbars and braided channels in the dry season host breeding River Tern, River Lapwing, Small Pratincole, and the Critically Endangered White-shouldered Ibis along the banks. The Mekong Flooded Forest holds Mekong Wagtail, a species described only in 2001 and largely confined to this river system. Boat trips at dawn combine dolphins with sandbar-nesting birds.

## Veun Sai-Siem Pang National Park

Straddling Ratanakiri and Stung Treng provinces in the far northeast, this park protects lowland evergreen forest along the Sekong River and is a stronghold for the white-shouldered ibis and the northern yellow-cheeked crested gibbon. The forest holds Pale-capped Pigeon, Green Peafowl and a rich hornbill community. Remote and lightly visited, it rewards birders willing to travel for a genuine frontier-forest experience, and the gibbon-tracking here is among the best in the country.

## Kirirom and the Cardamom foothills

Kirirom National Park, on a pine-clad plateau southwest of Phnom Penh, and the wider Cardamom Mountains hold a southern, more montane and Sundaic bird community. Pin-tailed Parrotfinch, Red-vented Barbet, and various bulbuls inhabit the pine and evergreen mosaic, while the Cardamoms proper shelter Chestnut-headed Partridge and the localised Cambodian endemic taxa. The cool plateau at around 700 metres is a pleasant contrast to the hot lowlands and an easy weekend from the capital.

## Practicalities, timing and the bat exodus

The dry season from November to April is the prime birding window across Cambodia: waterbirds concentrate at Prek Toal, ibises gather at Tmatboey, cranes arrive at Ang Trapeang Thmor, and floodplain access improves. Many itineraries also build in the famous bat's viewing near Battambang, where millions of wrinkle-lipped bats stream from a limestone cave at dusk in a ribbon that lasts nearly an hour — a wildlife spectacle worth pairing with the dawn birding. Sam Veasna-style community tours from Siem Reap remain the most efficient way to reach the key sites and ensure your fees support conservation.

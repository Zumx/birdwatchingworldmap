Taiwan is one of the most rewarding birding destinations in East Asia, combining a remarkable suite of endemic species with excellent infrastructure, active local birding organisations, and a compressed geography that allows productive birding across multiple habitat zones within a single week. The island's central mountain spine — the Zhongyang Mountains — rises to nearly 4,000 metres, creating a full altitudinal gradient from subtropical lowland through temperate broadleaf forest to subalpine conifer forest and alpine meadow. The 15 recognised endemic bird species are concentrated in the montane forest zone and are the primary target for visiting birders.

Taiwan also occupies an important position on East Asian migration routes, with the island serving as a staging and wintering area for species moving between Japan, Korea, and mainland China and their Southeast Asian winter quarters. The coastal wetlands of the western plain, in particular Aogu and Budai, hold globally important concentrations of wintering waterbirds and Palearctic-breeding shorebirds. Black-faced Spoonbill — one of the world's rarest birds with around 6,000 individuals — winters in significant numbers on Taiwan's southwestern coast.

## 1. Yushan (Jade Mountain) National Park, Central Taiwan

Yushan National Park protects the highest peaks of the central range and the associated high-altitude habitats that hold Taiwan's most sought-after endemic species. Mikado Pheasant — arguably the most spectacular of Taiwan's endemics, the male jet black with white bars and a brilliant red orbital wattle — inhabits the dense montane forest between 2,000 and 3,000 metres. Early morning walks on the Tatajia trail consistently produce Mikado Pheasant. Taiwan Bush Warbler sings from the subalpine grassland. Flamecrest — a tiny firecrest relative endemic to Taiwan's higher forests — inhabits the fir and hemlock belt. White-whiskered Laughingthrush calls from the dense undergrowth. Yushan is the centrepiece of Taiwan's endemic birding circuit.

## 2. Taroko National Park, Hualien County

Taroko Gorge on the eastern flank of the central range is one of the world's most dramatic geological landscapes — a marble gorge cut by the Liwu River over millennia. The forest along the gorge walls and the riverside holds Taiwan Barbets, Plumbeous Redstart, and Little Forktail in the turbulent stream. Brown Dipper inhabits the river margins. The mountain roads above the gorge reach subalpine forest that holds Rufous-crowned Laughingthrush — an endemic restricted to the east slope of the central range. Taiwan Rosefinch inhabits the high meadow edges. The accessibility of the gorge from Hualien city and the quality of the mountain road birding make Taroko an efficient site for combining dramatic scenery with endemics.

## 3. Dasyueshan National Forest Recreation Area, Taichung County

Dasyueshan is widely regarded as the best single site in Taiwan for observing montane endemics at close range. The mountain road through the recreation area passes through dense broadleaf forest where Taiwan Macaque and endemic birds share the canopy. Swinhoe's Pheasant — the second species of endemic pheasant — inhabits the dense forest between 1,500 and 2,500 metres. The species is conspicuous on the forest road in early morning. Taiwan Blue Magpie — Taiwan's national bird, brilliant blue with white and black markings and a long tail — is common here and along the road sides. Rufous-faced Warbler inhabits the forest scrub. Taiwan Barbet is easily found at fruiting trees throughout the site.

## 4. Wuling Farm Area, Taichung County

Wuling Farm at 1,740 metres in the upper Dajia River valley is the most celebrated high-altitude birding site in Taiwan. The combination of orchard trees, meadows, and adjacent montane forest creates an ideal edge habitat. Mikado Pheasant is regularly seen on the farm tracks at dawn. Taiwan Blue Magpie is conspicuous. Vinous-throated Parrotbill inhabits the bamboo scrub — an attractive small bird restricted to Taiwan and southern China. Collared Bush Robin is resident in the forest understory. Flamecrest is found in the spruce plantation edges at the upper boundary of the farm. The accommodation available at Wuling makes it possible to be birding at first light.

## 5. Aogu Wetland and Budai Salt Pans, Chiayi County

The coastal wetlands around Aogu and Budai on the southwestern coastal plain are Taiwan's most important site for Black-faced Spoonbill and shorebirds. Black-faced Spoonbill winters here in numbers that can approach a third of the global population, feeding in the shallow lagoons and resting on the salt pan embankments. The site is managed by the Wild Bird Society of Chiayi, which maintains hides and regular counts. Painted Snipe is resident in the marsh edges. Saunders's Gull winters in the shallow lagoons alongside Black-headed Gull and Brown-headed Gull. The salt pan habitats attract Far Eastern Curlew, Nordmann's Greenshank, and Spoon-billed Sandpiper during migration.

## 6. Kenting National Park, Pingtung County

Kenting at the southern tip of Taiwan is a critical migration bottleneck. In autumn, raptors including Grey-faced Buzzard pass through in extraordinary numbers — counts of over 100,000 Grey-faced Buzzard in a single day are documented during the peak October passage. The coastal scrub around Kenting holds Chinese Sparrowhawk and Japanese Sparrowhawk during migration. The reef and beach habitat holds breeding Great Frigatebird offshore. The coral reef diving famously available here gives a sense of the tropical marine fauna, and seabird watching from the southern promontory produces Brown Booby and occasional Roseate Tern. Fairy Pitta passes through during spring and autumn migration.

## 7. Yangmingshan National Park, New Taipei and Taipei City

Yangmingshan above Taipei is the most accessible birding mountain in Taiwan, a volcanic highland immediately behind the capital city with forest, grassland, and wetland habitats within the park boundary. Light-vented Bulbul — the common low-elevation bulbul of Taiwan — inhabits every garden and scrubby patch. Black Bulbul and Taiwan Bulbul inhabit the higher forest. The wetlands at Mianyang hold Common Kingfisher and Little Egret. In April, the Cuckoo migration through the forest produces calls of Oriental Cuckoo, Indian Cuckoo, and Lesser Cuckoo audible simultaneously. The Xiaoyoukeng meadow area in the park holds Stonechat and Zitting Cisticola in open grassland.

## 8. Dongsha Atoll (Pratas Islands), South China Sea

Dongsha Atoll — the Pratas Islands — is a Taiwanese-administered coral atoll in the northern South China Sea, 340 kilometres southwest of Taiwan. The atoll is a seabird colony and migration trap of considerable significance. Brown Noddy and Black Noddy breed on the main island. Red-footed Booby and Brown Booby both nest. The atoll's position in the South China Sea makes it a landing point for exhausted migrants crossing open water, and it has produced an extraordinary list of rare East Asian species for the small area. Access requires military coordination and is typically available only to scientific researchers or under arranged permit, but the atoll is cited in the literature as among the most ornithologically productive islands in the South China Sea.

## 9. Yilan Wetlands and Wuwei Harbor, Yilan County

The northeastern Yilan plain holds one of Taiwan's most diverse coastal bird communities. The Shuanglianpi peat lake in the hills above Yilan is a designated protected area holding Mandarin Duck breeding in the lakeside forest — unusually, a natural breeding population on Taiwan. The coastal wetlands around Wuwei harbour hold Little Tern breeding on the gravel beaches and Black-winged Stilt nesting in the shallow margins. In winter, the lowland rice paddies attract large flocks of Eurasian Wigeon and Teal. The Qingshui Cliff on the northeast coast is a sea-watching point where Pacific seabirds including Streaked Shearwater are visible during autumn.

## 10. Guandu Nature Park, New Taipei City

Guandu is a tidal wetland reserve at the confluence of the Danshui and Jilong rivers at the northern edge of Taipei, managed as a nature park accessible by the Taipei metro system. The reserve is remarkable for its urban accessibility — one of the most productive bird reserves in Asia that can be reached from a major city centre by subway. The reedbeds hold Purple Swamphen and Cinnamon Bittern. Common Kingfisher is visible from the boardwalk over the tidal channel. In winter, waterbirds including Black-faced Spoonbill and Eurasian Spoonbill visit the tidal flats. The reserve's year-round programme of birdwatching education and guided walks makes it the hub of Taipei's birding community.

---

Taiwan is one of the most efficient birding destinations in Asia — compact enough to cover multiple endemic zones in a week yet rich enough in species that repeat visits continue to produce new encounters. The mountain pheasants and endemic forest birds are the primary draw, but the coastal wetlands and migration spectacles are equally compelling.

China's bird list of over 1,400 species reflects the country's extraordinary geographic and climatic range. From the subtropical forests of Yunnan bordering Myanmar to the semi-arid Yellow Sea coast, and from the tropical Hainan Island to the Tibetan Plateau at 5,000 metres, the country encompasses more biomes than any other single nation. The eBird hotspot database for China has grown rapidly in the past decade as the country's domestic birding community has expanded; the Chinese-language birding community now numbers in the millions, and domestic bird photography is a major industry. The map database includes numerous observation towers and birdwatching hides across multiple Chinese provinces.

## 1. Beidaihe, Hebei

Beidaihe on the Bohai Sea coast has been the most visited migration watchpoint in China for over a century. The combination of a jutting coastal headland, adjacent parks, and the funnel effect of the Bohai Gulf produces spectacular raptor and passerine migration in spring (late April to May) and autumn (September to October). Yellow-browed Bunting (Emberiza chrysophrys), Chinese Penduline Tit (Remiz consobrinus), and Chinese Grey Shrike (Lanius sphenocerus) are characteristic autumn species. The site holds the longest continuous birding record in China.

## 2. Poyang Lake, Jiangxi

Poyang is the largest freshwater lake in China and the single most important wintering site for Siberian Crane (Leucogeranus leucogeranus, Critically Endangered) — around 95 percent of the world population winters here. White-naped Crane (Antigone vipio, Vulnerable), Hooded Crane (Grus monacha, Vulnerable), and Common Crane (Grus grus) join the Siberian Cranes in their thousands on the receding lake margins in November to February. The Wucheng National Nature Reserve provides viewing infrastructure.

## 3. Cao Hai (Caohai), Guizhou

Caohai, a high-altitude lake at 2,170 metres in Guizhou, is a critical wintering site for Black-necked Crane (Grus nigricollis, Vulnerable) — several hundred birds winter annually on the lake margins. The lake additionally holds migrant ducks, Ruddy Shelduck (Tadorna ferruginea), and the full highland waterbird community of the Yunnan-Guizhou plateau.

## 4. Napahai, Yunnan

Napahai, near Zhongdian (Shangri-La) in northwest Yunnan, is the most accessible Black-necked Crane wintering site in Yunnan, with birds arriving in October and departing by March. The surrounding plateau holds Upland Buzzard (Buteo hemilasius), Eurasian Crane, and Ruddy Shelduck in large numbers during winter.

## 5. Gaoligong Mountains, Yunnan

The Gaoligong range on the Myanmar border in western Yunnan is China's most species-rich mountain range for birds. The dramatic gradient from tropical foothills to 4,000-metre ridgelines, combined with the biogeographic crossroads between Himalayan and Indochinese fauna, produces a list that includes species not found elsewhere in China. Ward's Trogon (Harpactes wardi), Beautiful Nuthatch (Sitta formosa), and White-browed Nuthatch (Sitta victoriae) are among the Gaoligong endemics and near-endemics.

## 6. Ruili and Xishuangbanna, Yunnan

The tropical lowlands of southern Yunnan — Ruili near the Myanmar border and the Xishuangbanna rubber and tea landscape — hold the tropical component of the Chinese bird list: Green Peafowl (Pavo muticus, Endangered), Great Hornbill (Buceros bicornis, Vulnerable), and Grey Peacock Pheasant (Polyplectron bicalcaratum) are among the most sought species. The Xishuangbanna National Nature Reserve protects a declining area of tropical forest.

## 7. Yancheng, Jiangsu

Yancheng on the Yellow Sea coast is a critical staging site for shorebirds on the East Asian-Australasian Flyway. The intertidal mudflats hold staging Bar-tailed Godwit (Limosa lapponica), Red Knot (Calidris canutus), and Great Knot (Calidris tenuirostris, Endangered) in the tens of thousands during northward migration in April-May. The mudflats were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2019. The observation hides at Yancheng National Nature Reserve (documented in the map database as observation points) provide access to the mudflat edge.

## 8. Tibetan Plateau (Qinghai-Tibet Plateau)

The plateau above 4,000 metres holds a distinctive endemic and high-altitude community: Tibetan Snowcock (Tetraogallus tibetanus), Tibetan Partridge (Perdix hodgsoniae), Grandala (Grandala coelicolor), and several localized bar-headed and brown-headed gulls on the lake systems. Qinghai Lake, the largest lake in China, holds the largest Bar-headed Goose (Anser indicus) breeding colony in the world.

## 9. Lianhuashan, Guangdong

The mountain forests of Guangdong's Lianhuashan — near the map database observation points in the Pearl River Delta area — hold the subtropical forest community of southern China: Crested Goshawk (Accipiter trivirgatus), Scarlet Minivet (Pericrocotus speciosus), and the full complement of southeast Chinese forest species.

## 10. Mai Po Marshes, Hong Kong

The Mai Po Marshes in the northwest New Territories, adjacent to Shenzhen, is one of the most productive birding sites in East Asia. The managed shrimp ponds and intertidal mudflats of the Deep Bay Ramsar site hold wintering ducks, waders, and the most accessible Black-faced Spoonbill (Platalea minor, Endangered) population in the world. The World Wildlife Fund Hong Kong's management includes observation hides maintained to international standards.

## Planning a China birding trip

China's size and the diversity of its landscapes — from the Tibetan Plateau to the subtropical Yunnan forests, from the Loess Plateau to the northeastern taiga — means that productive birding requires a focused regional plan rather than an attempt to cover the country comprehensively. The two most productive regions for international birders relative to accessibility are Yunnan province (accessible from Kunming) and Sichuan province (accessible from Chengdu).

Yunnan holds the highest bird diversity in China — over 900 species recorded in the province alone — in habitats ranging from the tropical Xishuangbanna rainforest at 600 metres to the Tibetan Plateau margins above 4,500 metres. The Gaoligong Mountains in western Yunnan, along the Myanmar border, hold the most significant concentration of Yunnan endemics and near-endemics accessible by road from Tengchong and Baoshan. The Xishuangbanna tropical forest (accessible from Jinghong) provides the Indochinese-affinity tropical species.

Sichuan — the giant panda province — holds a different set of priorities for birders: the Sichuan pheasant community (Chinese Monal, Sichuan Jay, and the full Tibetan Plateau edge community) concentrated in Wolong, Jiuzhaigou, and the Balang Mountain road. Chengdu's Sichuan Basin position allows efficient day trips to the Chengdu Plain wetlands (for wintering cranes and storks) and overnight drives to the highland sites.

The Yangtze River wetlands, particularly the Poyang Lake in Jiangxi province, hold the world's largest concentration of wintering Siberian Crane (Leucogeranus leucogeranus, Critically Endangered) — over 90% of the global population winters at Poyang. Wuhan and Nanchang airports serve the Poyang Lake region; the best viewing is November to March before spring departure.

China's domestic flight network connects all major birding centres efficiently; China Eastern and Air China provide extensive domestic coverage from Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou international hubs.

## Yunnan: the richest single province

Yunnan province's extraordinary bird diversity — over 900 of China's approximately 1,400 species — is concentrated in three distinct biogeographic zones accessible from Kunming. The western border zone (Tengchong, Baoshan, Lijiang) holds the Sino-Himalayan endemic community and provides the highest endemic density. The southern tropical zone (Xishuangbanna, Pu'er) holds the Indochinese community. The northwestern Tibetan Plateau border (Shangri-La, Deqin) holds the high-altitude Tibetan community. Most birding tour operators covering China include a Yunnan circuit as the centrepiece; specialist operators based in Kunming include China Birdwatching Tours and Yunnan Bird Club, both of which maintain updated eBird records.

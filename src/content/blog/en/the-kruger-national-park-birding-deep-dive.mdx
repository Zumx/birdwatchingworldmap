Most first-time visitors come to Kruger National Park for lions and elephants, and they leave astonished by the birds. Spread across nearly twenty thousand square kilometres of northeastern South Africa, between the Crocodile River in the south and the Limpopo on the Zimbabwean border, Kruger holds a recorded list of more than 500 bird species — a total that rivals entire countries. The secret to turning that list into your own is learning to read the park's habitats. A waterhole at dawn, a stand of fever trees, a patch of mopane scrub: each is a different community of birds, and the birder who understands the mosaic will out-find the one who simply drives and hopes.

## Reading the habitat mosaic

Kruger is not one landscape but several, layered north to south and shaped by rainfall, geology, and the great rivers. The granitic west supports broad-leaved woodland; the basalt east runs to open grassland and knobthorn savanna. The far north beyond the Tropic of Capricorn is mopane country, hotter and drier, while the south enjoys higher rainfall and dense riverine forest. Learning these zones tells you where to look: the Sabie and Crocodile valleys for forest specials, the central grasslands for raptors and bustards, the sandveld of the far north for species found almost nowhere else in the country. Match your route to the habitats you most want, and the bird list follows.

## The waterholes and the hides

Kruger's hides are the birder's secret weapon, transforming a chance encounter into patient, comfortable study. Sites such as the Sunset Hide near Skukuza, the Lake Panic Bird Hide on the Sabie, and others scattered along the rivers let you sit quietly while the birds come to you. From a good hide you might watch African Jacanas trotting across lily pads, a Goliath Heron standing motionless, malachite and pied kingfishers diving, and Water Thick-knees stalking the shallows at dusk. The general Bird Hide network and the riverine vantage points reward the patient — settle in for an hour rather than ticking and leaving, and the cast of characters keeps changing as the light shifts.

## Raptors of the open country

Few places on earth offer richer raptor watching. Kruger is a stronghold for vultures, and a kill on the open plains can draw White-backed, Hooded, Lappet-faced, and the rare White-headed Vulture in a wheeling, squabbling mass. Above the grasslands you may pick out Bateleurs rocking on the wind, Martial Eagles surveying from a dead tree, Tawny Eagles, and the long-legged Brown Snake-Eagle. Summer brings migrant Steppe Eagles and Wahlberg's Eagles in numbers. Scan every prominent perch and every soaring speck; the central basalt plains around Satara are classic raptor country, and a single morning drive can yield a dozen species of bird of prey.

## Specials of the mopane north

The far north, around Punda Maria and Pafuri, is where Kruger's bird list grows most exotic. The sandveld and baobab country here holds species at the southern edge of their tropical range: Crested Guineafowl in the thickets, the gaudy Böhm's Spinetail, Tropical Boubou, and the localized Three-banded Courser. The Pafuri region, where the Luvuvhu River meets the Limpopo, is legendary among South African birders for the Pel's Fishing Owl roosting in riverine giants, Racket-tailed Roller, and the chance of Lemon-breasted Canary. This is a long drive from the busier southern camps, but for a serious lister the northern circuit is the heart of a Kruger trip.

## The riverine forests of the south

The southern rivers — the Sabie above all — thread ribbons of tall riverine forest through the savanna, and these green corridors hold birds absent from the surrounding bush. Listen for the African Fish Eagle's ringing call, watch for Purple-crested Turacos bounding through the canopy, and search the understorey for Narina Trogon, a jewel that betrays its presence only by a soft hooting. Brown-hooded Kingfishers, Crested Barbets, and a host of bulbuls and robin-chats fill the dawn chorus. Camps such as Skukuza and Lower Sabie sit within this rich forest belt, so even the rest camps themselves are productive birding.

## Camps as birding bases

In Kruger the rest camps themselves are productive birding, and choosing where to sleep shapes what you see. Skukuza, the largest camp, sits on the Sabie among tall riverine trees and is excellent for forest species and for the African Scops Owl that calls after dark; nearby Lake Panic is one of the finest hides in the park. Lower Sabie offers a superb river frontage where elephants and waterbirds share the same view. Satara, out on the central basalt plains, is the camp for raptors and grassland birds, while Letaba and Olifants overlook broad river valleys rich in waders and herons. In the far north, Punda Maria and the Pafuri picnic site put you within reach of the tropical specials. Birding the camp gardens at dawn, before the gates open, regularly adds species you would never see from the road.

## Seasons and timing

The summer wet season, roughly November through March, is the birding peak. Intra-African and Palearctic migrants pour in — cuckoos calling, Woodland Kingfishers arriving on cue in late November, European Bee-eaters and rollers everywhere, and weavers in full breeding dress. The bush is green and thick, which can hide mammals but fills the air with song. The dry winter, May through August, concentrates birds at shrinking water and opens the sightlines, making it easier to see both birds and big game. For the longest list, come in summer; for comfort and visibility, come in winter.

## Practical birding in Kruger

Kruger is a self-drive park, which suits birders perfectly: you set your own pace, stop where you like, and use the car as a hide. Stay within the speed limits, keep windows down and engines off at sightings, and observe the gate and curfew times that bracket each day. The official camps offer guided early-morning drives and bush walks that reach areas closed to private vehicles and put a knowledgeable guide at your side. Carry plenty of water, a good field guide to southern African birds, and bins you trust; with patience and an eye for habitat, breaking 200 species in a week-long visit is entirely realistic.

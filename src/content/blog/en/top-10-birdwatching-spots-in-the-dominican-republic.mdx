The Dominican Republic occupies the eastern two-thirds of Hispaniola, an island that ranks among the most endemic-rich in the Caribbean with 31 species found nowhere else. The national bird, the Palmchat, is the only member of its entire family and builds huge communal stick nests in royal palms across the lowlands. Hispaniola also holds two todies — the Broad-billed and the Narrow-billed — small green sprites that perch motionless on twigs in the forest. Add the country's extraordinary altitude range, from Pico Duarte at 3,098 metres, the highest point in the Caribbean, down to Lake Enriquillo at 40 metres below sea level, and the result is one of the richest small countries on earth for a birder.

## 1. Sierra de Bahoruco National Park

Sierra de Bahoruco in the southwest is the single most important birding destination in the country, protecting a gradient from arid foothills to pine and broadleaf cloud forest. Nearly every Hispaniolan endemic can be found here, including the Hispaniolan Trogon, Western Chat-Tanager, La Selle Thrush, and Hispaniolan Crossbill in the pines. The road up to the Aceitillar and Zapotén areas climbs steeply through changing habitat, and the high pine forest near the Haitian border is the classic dawn site.

## 2. Lake Enriquillo and Cabritos Island

Lake Enriquillo is a hypersaline lake set in a desert basin 40 metres below sea level, the lowest point in the Caribbean. Isla Cabritos National Park within it is famous for American Crocodiles and Rhinoceros Iguanas, but the lakeshore and surrounding scrub hold large numbers of waterbirds, including American Flamingo, Black-necked Stilt, and wintering waterfowl. The arid thornscrub around the lake is excellent for the Palmchat and dry-country endemics.

## 3. Los Haitises National Park

Los Haitises on the northeast coast is a dramatic landscape of mangrove channels and limestone mogotes draped in forest. Boat trips from Sabana de la Mar pass roosting Brown Pelicans, frigatebirds, and herons among the karst islands, while the forest holds the Ridgway's Hawk, one of the rarest raptors in the world, which has been the focus of an intensive reintroduction programme here. Caves with pre-Columbian rock art add a cultural dimension to the visit.

## 4. Valle Nuevo National Park

Valle Nuevo, in the central Cordillera Central, protects high-altitude pine forest on a plateau around 2,200 to 2,500 metres. The cool, open pinares are the place for the Hispaniolan Crossbill, Antillean Siskin, Pine Warbler, and the endemic Hispaniolan Pewee. The Madre de las Aguas — the mother of the waters — is the source of several major rivers, and the morning chorus in the misty pines is one of the great birding experiences of the island.

## 5. Ébano Verde Scientific Reserve

Ébano Verde, near Constanza in the central mountains, protects montane broadleaf cloud forest rich in tree ferns and endemic plants. The reserve is one of the more accessible places to find the Hispaniolan Trogon, the Hispaniolan Emerald hummingbird, and the Rufous-throated Solitaire, whose haunting song defines the cloud forest. Well-marked trails make it a good introduction to highland birding before tackling the harder access of Bahoruco.

## 6. Del Este National Park and Saona Island

Del Este in the far southeast combines dry coastal forest with the offshore Saona and Catalina islands. The dry forest holds the White-crowned Pigeon, Hispaniolan Lizard-Cuckoo, and a good range of lowland endemics, while the coast and islands support breeding seabirds and large concentrations of frigatebirds and brown boobies. It is the most convenient endemic-rich site for visitors based in the Punta Cana resort zone.

## 7. Laguna de Oviedo, Jaragua National Park

Jaragua, the largest protected area in the country, covers the arid southern peninsula and includes the brackish Laguna de Oviedo. Boat tours of the lagoon pass islands holding American Flamingo, Roseate Spoonbill, Reddish Egret, and breeding terns. The surrounding cactus scrub and dry forest are prime habitat for the Bay-breasted Cuckoo, Flat-billed Vireo, and other dry-country endemics that are hard to find elsewhere.

## 8. Pico Duarte and the Cordillera Central

The trek to Pico Duarte, at 3,098 metres the highest peak in the Caribbean, passes through tall montane pine forest that holds high-elevation endemics. The multi-day route from La Ciénaga near Jarabacoa climbs through the Armando Bermúdez National Park, with the Antillean Siskin, Hispaniolan Crossbill, and Golden Swallow over the ridges. Even without summiting, the Jarabacoa highlands offer rewarding access to mountain birds.

## 9. Constanza Valley

Constanza, a high agricultural valley around 1,200 metres in the central mountains, is set among pine-clad ridges and is one of the cooler corners of the country. The valley and surrounding slopes are good for the Antillean Siskin, Hispaniolan Spindalis, and the introduced agricultural landscape draws raptors and open-country species. It serves as a comfortable base for visiting Valle Nuevo and Ébano Verde.

## 10. Puerto Escondido and the Bahoruco foothills

The lowland approach to Sierra de Bahoruco around Puerto Escondido covers arid thornscrub and transitional dry forest at the base of the mountains. This zone is the most reliable for the Bay-breasted Cuckoo and Hispaniolan endemics of the dry lowlands, and birders often work it at dawn before driving up into the cloud forest. The contrast within a single morning — from cactus to pine — captures the country's remarkable habitat range.

## Planning a Dominican Republic birding trip

A week to ten days allows a serious attempt at the full Hispaniolan endemic set. The classic route bases in the southwest, working Sierra de Bahoruco, Lake Enriquillo, and Jaragua, then moves to the central highlands for Valle Nuevo, Ébano Verde, and Constanza. The main international gateways are Santo Domingo (Las Américas) and Punta Cana; Santo Domingo is the more practical base for reaching the southwestern parks, while Punta Cana suits a Del Este visit.

The best season runs from late November through April, the dry season, when forest tracks into Bahoruco and the Cordillera Central are passable and resident endemics are in song. North American migrants overwinter at the wetlands and coastal sites during this period, adding warblers, shorebirds, and waterfowl to the lists. Dawn is critical in the highlands, as the cloud forest often closes in with mist by mid-morning and the high-elevation endemics fall quiet.

A four-wheel-drive vehicle and a local guide are close to essential for the southwestern parks; the upper roads in Bahoruco are rough and the endemics are easier found by ear. Grupo Jaragua and other Dominican conservation groups have driven the Ridgway's Hawk recovery and protect key sites. Pack for genuine cold in the pine highlands, where night temperatures can drop near freezing, and for desert heat at Lake Enriquillo on the same trip.

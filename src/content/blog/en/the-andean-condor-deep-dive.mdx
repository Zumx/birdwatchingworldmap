The Andean Condor (Vultur gryphus) is not easy to ignore. A wingspan of up to 3.3 metres and a body mass of up to 15 kilograms make it the largest flying land bird in the world. In flight, it exploits thermal and orographic updrafts above the Andes with an economy of movement that belies its mass: hours of soaring on motionless wings, banking on the edge of a thermal, covering hundreds of kilometres between feeding and roosting sites with minimal energetic expenditure. Watching a condor from a Peruvian or Argentine viewpoint, reducing steadily to a dot against a sky that emphasizes the scale of the mountains, is one of the most memorable wildlife experiences in the Americas.

## Distribution and range

The Andean Condor's range spans the Andean chain from Venezuela and Colombia in the north through Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, Chile, and Argentina to Tierra del Fuego in the south. The Pacific coast of Peru and Chile, where the cold Humboldt Current drives marine productivity and provides large carrion (sea lions, whale carcasses), supports some of the highest condor densities. In Argentina, the Andean region from Mendoza south through Patagonia holds substantial populations; the Quebrada del Condorito National Park in Córdoba Province is one of the most reliable condor-viewing sites on the continent.

Venezuela's Henri Pittier National Park and the páramo habitats of the Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta in Colombia represent the northern periphery of the range; populations at these latitudes are small and receive protection under national legislation. The Peruvian Colca Canyon, formed by the Río Colca cutting through Andean volcanic terrain, provides the updrafts and cliff-face roosting sites that condors depend on, and is consistently the most visited condor-watching site in the world.

## Biology: built for long-distance scavenging

The condor is an obligate scavenger. Its bare head and neck — red to orange in adults — are adaptations for accessing carcass cavities without fouling feathers. The large, powerful bill can tear open the thick hides of cattle, horses, and South American camelids that smaller scavengers cannot access, making condors a key component of the Andean nutrient cycling system. Condors locate carrion primarily by sight rather than smell, soaring high enough to observe an area of many square kilometres simultaneously and watching for the behavior of other scavengers gathering at a carcass.

The adult plumage is striking and age-structured: fully adult birds take six to eight years to acquire their definitive black plumage with white wing patches and the white neck ruff that contrasts with the bare carunculate facial skin. Immature birds progress through brown plumages with variable white wing markings; experienced observers at condor watchpoints can often age individual birds in flight using the pattern of white in the wing coverts.

## IUCN status: Vulnerable

The Andean Condor is assessed as Vulnerable (VU) on the IUCN Red List, primarily due to a slow reproductive rate (one egg every two years; a single chick that takes years to become independent) and ongoing mortality from poisoning. Secondary poisoning from strychnine and other toxins placed in carcasses to kill predators targeting livestock is the most serious documented mortality cause throughout the condor's range. Lead poisoning from ingesting bullets or lead shot in carcasses of hunted animals is an additional documented cause of mortality.

Persecution is locally ongoing in some areas, despite legal protection throughout the range. Condors are sometimes killed by farmers who blame them — incorrectly; condors do not kill live prey — for livestock deaths.

## Viewing the condor in the field

The Colca Canyon viewing platform at Cruz del Cóndor, in Peru's Colca Valley, is the most reliable single site. Condors launch from overnight roosts on the canyon walls and gain altitude on the first thermals of the morning; the watching window of 8 to 10 am typically provides the clearest views at closest range. The eBird hotspot at Cruz del Cóndor documents the species with virtually every checklist submitted.

In Argentina, the Quebrada del Condorito and the access road to its condor-viewing points produces reliable sightings in the morning. The Torres del Paine National Park in Chilean Patagonia and the Argentine Patagonian steppe both support condors over vast open terrain, where the species can be seen soaring over the ridgelines above the valley floors.

Bolivia's Madidi National Park and Amboró National Park represent opportunities to find condors in the context of Andean cloud forest rather than open steppe, where the views of the bird against forested mountains are distinct from the classic canyon encounters.

## Reintroduction and population recovery

Captive breeding programs, led by the San Diego Zoo and Californian facilities, have produced birds for reintroduction in Argentina and other range states. The reintroduction program in Patagonian Argentina has established a small free-flying population descended from captive-bred stock. Results have been mixed: survival of released birds is constrained by the same poisoning and lead exposure threats that affect the wild population, and the success of reintroductions depends substantially on reducing these threats in the release area before birds are placed.

## Andean Condor conservation across range states

The Andean Condor (Vultur gryphus, Vulnerable) occupies a range of nearly 7,000 kilometres along the Andes from Venezuela to Tierra del Fuego. Conservation status varies dramatically by country: the northern populations in Colombia and Venezuela are critically depleted and locally classified as Critically Endangered; the southern populations in Chile and Argentina are more stable. This north-south gradient reflects different histories of persecution and different levels of ranching activity that historically treated condors as threats to livestock.

The most significant conservation programs have addressed the persecution problem through community education and outreach. In Peru and Ecuador, where condors were hunted and poisoned by ranchers who believed they killed calves (an activity that Andean Condors, as obligate scavengers, are incapable of), programs run by local NGOs in partnership with community leaders have shifted perceptions. The condor's role as a cultural symbol — Quechua communities associate the condor with the Andean mountain spirit Apu — provides a cultural basis for changed attitudes that purely scientific conservation arguments cannot.

Ecuador's Cóndor Bioreserve and the Galeras volcanic region in southern Colombia provide the most organized condor viewing in the northern range. The Chingaza National Park near Bogotá and the Puracé National Park in Colombia's Cauca department have documented small but breeding populations — among the most northerly confirmed breeders in the species' range.

Lead poisoning from ingested bullet fragments in carcasses — the same problem that drove the California Condor to near-extinction — affects Andean Condors across the range, particularly where deer, guanaco, and rhea are hunted with lead ammunition. The transition to non-lead (copper) bullets in condor range is a priority advocacy issue for condor conservation organizations in Chile and Argentina.

The captive breeding and reintroduction program run by the Zoological Society of San Diego, in partnership with range-state wildlife agencies, has reintroduced over 100 captive-bred birds into the wild since the 1990s. Colombia, Ecuador, Bolivia, and Argentina have all received reintroduced birds; the success rate — measured by survival to breeding age — has improved substantially as release protocols and post-release monitoring have been refined.

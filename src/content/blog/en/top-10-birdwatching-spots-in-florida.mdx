Florida occupies a unique ecological position within North America, stretching south into the subtropics and surrounded on three sides by warm coastal waters. The result is a birdwatching destination unlike anywhere else in the continental United States. Year-round residents include Snail Kite, Florida Scrub-Jay, and Sandhill Crane. Winter brings enormous influxes of migratory shorebirds and waterfowl from northern breeding grounds. Spring migration funnels Wood Warblers, vireos, and tanagers through the Florida Panhandle. The Everglades — one of the largest subtropical wilderness areas in the world — provides irreplaceable habitat for wading bird colonies that once ranked among the largest in North America.

Florida also serves as a gateway for vagrant species arriving from the Caribbean and Central America. Bahama Mockingbird, La Sagra's Flycatcher, and Antillean Nighthawk have all been recorded in southern Florida. The southern tip of the state, including Key West, regularly turns up Caribbean vagrants after strong weather systems. The diversity of accessible habitats — from beach scrub to cypress swamps to mangrove lagoons — means that even a single week can produce a remarkable total.

## 1. Everglades National Park, Miami-Dade and Monroe Counties

The Everglades is the defining Florida birding experience and one of the great wildlife destinations in the Western Hemisphere. The shallow "River of Grass" flowing south across limestone marl supports enormous wading bird populations. Roseate Spoonbill is the flamboyant flagship species, pink and sociable, foraging in the shallow roadside ditches in winter. Great Blue Heron, Tricolored Heron, Little Blue Heron, and Snowy Egret wade the same ditches in conspicuous proximity to visitors. Snail Kite hunts over the freshwater sloughs, carrying apple snails. Anhinga Trail near the entrance station is one of the most productive short birding walks in the United States, with birds at close range year-round.

## 2. Dry Tortugas National Park, Monroe County

Seventy miles west of Key West, accessible only by ferry or seaplane, the Dry Tortugas is a remote seabird nesting colony and legendary migrant trap. The Sooty Tern colony on Bush Key numbers in the tens of thousands during summer — one of the largest colonies in the Caribbean basin. Magnificent Frigatebird soars above the fort in graceful effortless flight. Masked Booby and Brown Noddy breed on the outer islands. During spring migration, a cold front pushing birds south can deposit extraordinary concentrations of exhausted warblers, tanagers, and orioles on the tiny islands, where the trees can be overwhelmed with roosting migrants. Birders make dedicated overnight trips on the park ferry specifically to exploit these fallout conditions.

## 3. Corkscrew Swamp Sanctuary, Collier County

Corkscrew, managed by the National Audubon Society, protects the largest remaining strand of old-growth bald cypress forest in North America. The towering cypresses, draped in Spanish moss, are the nesting site of Wood Stork — a large, bald-headed wading bird that has long been a Florida conservation symbol and whose breeding success at Corkscrew is used as an indicator of Everglades water quality. The boardwalk through the swamp provides unparalleled access to a subtropical forest interior. Swallow-tailed Kite arrives in spring and can be seen soaring over the cypress canopy. Barred Owl is frequently encountered on the boardwalk. Prothonotary Warbler sings from the willows at the swamp margins in spring.

## 4. Fort De Soto Park, Pinellas County

Fort De Soto at the southern end of the Pinellas Peninsula is consistently rated one of the top migration sites in eastern North America. The park's position at the southern end of a long peninsula, combined with its mix of beach, mangrove, hammock, and open grassland habitats, makes it a classic migrant trap. During spring migration in April and May, the North Beach area can produce extraordinary concentrations of warblers, thrushes, and vireos in the morning following overnight northward flights. Painted Bunting winters in the seed feeders at the park entrance. Mangrove Cuckoo is resident in the dense mangroves and is most detectable by call in spring.

## 5. Merritt Island National Wildlife Refuge, Brevard County

Adjacent to Kennedy Space Center, Merritt Island encompasses diverse coastal habitats including impounded marshes, open lagoon, scrub, and pine flatwoods. The Black Point Wildlife Drive is a seven-mile auto tour that is one of the most consistently productive birding routes in Florida. Greater Scaup and Lesser Scaup winter on the impoundments in large numbers. Mottled Duck is a year-round resident. Short-eared Owl hunts over the marsh in winter evenings. Florida Scrub-Jay is resident in the scrub areas of the refuge. Roseate Spoonbill and Wood Stork forage in the shallow impoundments during drawdown periods. The diversity of habitats within a single refuge makes this an efficient one-day destination.

## 6. Wakulla Springs State Park, Wakulla County

Wakulla Springs, south of Tallahassee, is one of the world's largest freshwater springs. The crystal-clear spring run, lined with ancient cypress trees, is used by Limpkin — a secretive, wailing waterbird found almost exclusively in freshwater wetlands with apple snail populations. Limpkins here are approachable and easily photographed. Anhinga dries its wings on overhanging branches. Barred Owl is resident and active by day. The woodland around the spring holds migrant warblers in spring and autumn. The glass-bottomed boat tours provide views of the spring pool and the remarkable underwater clarity that makes this one of the most unusual birding venues in the state.

## 7. Corkscrew Regional Ecosystem Watershed (CREW), Collier County

Distinct from Corkscrew Swamp Sanctuary nearby, the CREW lands are an extensive network of trails through pine flatwoods, prairie, and cypress wetlands managed for public access. Bachman's Sparrow sings from the open pinewoods in spring — a grassland-associated species that requires specific open pine habitat management. Red-cockaded Woodpecker is present in small numbers in the mature longleaf pine areas. Sandhill Crane is resident and conspicuous on the open prairies. Burrowing Owl is easily found in the shortgrass areas at the property boundary. The extensive trail network rewards all-day walks that sample multiple habitat types.

## 8. Paynes Prairie Preserve State Park, Alachua County

Paynes Prairie is a large flooded grassland basin south of Gainesville that has hosted Florida's only wild bison herd reintroduction and is equally significant for birds. Whooping Crane — one of the rarest birds in North America, recovered from near-extinction through intensive captive breeding — has been reintroduced to this part of Florida and occasionally occurs at Paynes Prairie alongside the resident Sandhill Cranes. Northern Harrier hunts over the wet prairie in winter. Sora and Virginia Rail call from the emergent marsh. The La Chua Trail gives excellent views across the basin to the large alligator and bird concentrations around the central marsh.

## 9. Big Pine Key and Lower Florida Keys, Monroe County

The Lower Keys are home to the extremely restricted Key West Quail-Dove, a Caribbean species that maintains a tiny population in the dense tropical hammock. More consistently, the Keys are used by migrant Yellow-billed Cuckoo, Gray Kingbird — a Caribbean species that breeds here in conspicuous numbers — and Antillean species during vagrant events. Mangrove Cuckoo is resident but secretive. The National Key Deer Refuge on Big Pine Key encompasses both hardwood hammock and saltmarsh habitats. Evening visits to the hammock produce Eastern Screech-Owl. The shallow Florida Bay side of the Keys supports large numbers of Reddish Egret and American Oystercatcher.

## 10. St. Marks National Wildlife Refuge, Wakulla County

St. Marks, where the Apalachee Bay meets the Gulf of Mexico, is a premier waterfowl and shorebird site in the Florida Panhandle. In winter, the impoundments hold Tundra Swan — uncommon in Florida — alongside large numbers of Pintail, Teal, and Ring-necked Duck. Peregrine Falcon hunts over the shorebird flocks on the coastal flats. The lighthouse at the end of the main road is a hawk watch point during autumn migration. Buff-breasted Sandpiper occurs on the open grass areas during September. The pine flatwoods hold Red-cockaded Woodpecker and Bachman's Sparrow. The combination of open water, marsh, and flatwoods creates a site that rewards multiple visits across different seasons.

---

Florida is genuinely a year-round birding destination, with the emphasis shifting from breeding wading birds and Caribbean vagrants in summer to spectacular concentrations of wintering waterbirds from October to March. The state's accessibility, infrastructure, and sheer variety make it the entry point into serious birding for many North American naturalists.

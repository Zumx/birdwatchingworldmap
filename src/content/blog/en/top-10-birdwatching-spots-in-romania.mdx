Romania holds one of the most varied ornithological landscapes in Europe. The Danube Delta, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Ramsar wetland, is the largest river delta in the European Union and one of the richest waterbird habitats in the Palearctic. The Dobrogea region between the delta and the Black Sea coast holds the most continental steppe in Romania. The Carpathian Mountains form a horseshoe around Transylvania, protecting significant areas of old-growth forest. Romania's bird list stands at around 400 species, with several globally threatened species breeding.

## 1. Danube Delta, Tulcea

The Danube Delta is Romania's supreme birding destination and one of Europe's most spectacular natural sites. The delta's 580,000 hectares of reed bed, channel, and lagoon hold the largest breeding colony of Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened) in the world, with around 1,500 to 2,000 pairs. Great White Pelican (Pelecanus onocrotalus), Pygmy Cormorant (Microcarbo pygmeus), White-tailed Eagle (Haliaeetus albicilla), and Squacco Heron (Ardeola ralloides) breed in large numbers. The delta is accessed from Tulcea by boat; the village of Sfântu Gheorghe and the Crișan area provide the most productive boat routes through the interior reed channels.

## 2. Dobrogea and Macin Mountains

The Macin Mountains are the most ancient in Romania, holding the continent's oldest geological exposures. The xerophytic scrub and steppe of the Macin range hold Isabelline Wheatear (Oenanthe isabellina), Eastern Rock Nuthatch (Sitta tephronota), and the full eastern steppe community including Saker Falcon (Falco cherrug, Endangered) breeding on the cliffs. Montagu's Harrier and Long-legged Buzzard (Buteo rufinus) hunt the steppe margins.

## 3. Razim-Sinoie Lagoon Complex, Tulcea

The coastal lagoon complex south of the Danube Delta holds the Black Sea coast waterbird community. Greater Flamingo winters in hundreds; Audouin's Gull (Ichthyaetus audouinii), Slender-billed Gull, and Sandwich Tern use the shallow lagoons. The Babadag forest adjacent to the lagoon holds Short-toed Eagle, Roller (Coracias garrulus), and the full Dobrogea woodland community.

## 4. Poiana Brasov and Bucegi Mountains, Transylvania

The mountain tourism area of Poiana Brasov provides access to the Carpathian montane community. Ural Owl (Strix uralensis), Tengmalm's Owl (Aegolius funereus), and all five Carpathian woodpecker species including White-backed Woodpecker breed in the mountain forest. The Bucegi plateau above 2,000 metres holds Alpine Accentor, Snowfinch, and Water Pipit.

## 5. Retezat National Park, Hunedoara

Retezat is the most wilderness-like of Romania's national parks, with large areas of old-growth mountain forest and Alpine meadow. Breeding Golden Eagle, Rock Ptarmigan (Lagopus muta), Dotterel, and the full Carpathian montane community occur in the park's elevational gradient from forest to glacial cirques.

## 6. Comana Natural Park, Giurgiu

The Comana lake and forest complex south of Bucharest holds one of Romania's few accessible White Stork nesting concentrations and the lowland oak forest community. White-backed Woodpecker, Middle Spotted Woodpecker, and Roller breed within reasonable drive of the capital.

## 7. Văcărești Nature Park, Bucharest

Romania's unique urban national park, within Bucharest itself, has developed spontaneously on the site of an abandoned building project. The wetland and scrub hold over 190 species including breeding Night Heron and visiting Spoonbill — a remarkable urban birding resource.

## 8. Parcul Natural Lunca Mureşului

The Mureș River floodplain in western Romania holds breeding Black Stork (Ciconia nigra), Kingfisher (Alcedo atthis), and Common Sandpiper in the river-margin woodland. White Stork breeds in villages along the floodplain.

## 9. Black Sea coast between Constanța and Vama Veche

The southern Black Sea coast of Romania produces Black-throated Diver (Gavia arctica), Yelkouan Shearwater (Puffinus yelkouan, Vulnerable), and Yelkouan Shearwater wintering offshore. Mediterranean Gull and Caspian Tern are regular at the beach access points.

## 10. Cheile Nerei-Beușnița National Park, Caraș-Severin

This limestone gorge national park in the Banat mountains holds the southernmost Romanian breeding population of Eurasian Griffon Vulture (Gyps fulvus) and a Mediterranean-influenced bird community with Roller, Eurasian Scops Owl (Otus scops), and the full Balkan-margin woodland species.

## Planning a Romania birding trip

Romania's birding calendar divides cleanly by season. April and May are the most productive months overall: the Danube Delta is at peak breeding activity, migrant raptors pass through the Dobrogea and Macin range in large numbers, and the full complement of summer visitors — rollers, bee-eaters, lesser kestrels — has arrived across the lowlands. The delta is also navigable by the small wooden boats (pontoane) that access the interior channels, and the reed beds are alive with singing Great Reed Warblers, Marsh Harriers, and the booming bass calls of Eurasian Bittern. The peak pelican congregation at Rosca-Buhaiova in April and May, when thousands of Dalmatian Pelicans gather before dispersing to nest, is one of the most impressive wildlife spectacles in Europe.

August to October brings southward migration. The Via Pontica runs along the Black Sea coast and through the Dobrogea plateau, channeling millions of White Storks, Honey Buzzards (Pernis apivorus), and lesser raptors through Romania's eastern corridor. The Batova Valley near Varna (just across the Bulgarian border at the southern end of the Dobrogea) concentrates soaring raptors in September and October in numbers comparable to the better-known Bosphorus counts. The Razim-Sinoie lagoon complex produces concentrations of Greater Flamingo, ducks, and waders in autumn that dwarf the quieter summer period.

Winter offers a different experience. The Danube Delta's reed beds are brown and silent, but the open water of the Black Sea coast attracts wintering divers, grebes, and sea ducks. Yelkouan Shearwater (Puffinus yelkouan, Vulnerable) gathers offshore in large numbers; Long-tailed Duck (Clangula hyemalis), Velvet Scoter (Melanitta fusca), and Black-throated Diver (Gavia arctica) concentrate near the river mouths. The Carpathian forests hold their full resident woodpecker suite year-round; winter is often the most productive season for woodpecker detection because the absence of foliage makes calling and drumming birds visible from greater distances.

Logistics for the delta require advance booking of boat guides and accommodation at Sfântu Gheorghe or Crișan, as capacity is limited in the peak season. The Macin Mountains are best reached by car from Tulcea. The Bucharest urban sites — Văcărești and Comana — are accessible by public transport and day trip. The eBird hotspot network for Romania is well-developed, with regular checklists from all major sites providing real-time species data throughout the year.

## Getting to the Danube Delta

The Danube Delta is accessible from Tulcea, the last road-connected town before the delta begins. Tulcea is eight hours by road from Bucharest or accessible via a domestic flight (operated seasonally by Blue Air). From Tulcea, public ferries and private boat operators depart for Sulina, Sfântu Gheorghe, and the interior waterway network. Sulina, at the delta's northernmost channel, holds the largest concentrations of Dalmatian Pelican and the most consistently productive colony access from the public ferry. Advance accommodation booking at the Sulina guesthouses is essential in the May-June peak season, as the limited beds fill early with birding tour groups.

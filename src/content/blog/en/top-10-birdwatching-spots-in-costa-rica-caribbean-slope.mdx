Costa Rica's Caribbean slope is a different birding world from the better-known Pacific side. The Caribbean lowlands receive rainfall year-round, support dense, tall rainforest that is among the most productive in Central America, and hold numerous species absent or rare on the Pacific side: Great Green Macaw, Keel-billed Toucan, Bare-necked Umbrellabird, Snowy Cotinga, and a distinct suite of hummingbirds, flycatchers, and forest interior specialists. The slope extends from the lowland canal systems and coastal forests of Tortuguero in the north through the Sarapiquí region and the foothills of the Turrialba volcano to the Talamanca highlands in the south — a continuous habitat gradient covering hundreds of metres of elevation. ## 1. La Selva Biological Station, Sarapiquí

The OTS (Organization for Tropical Studies) La Selva Biological Station near Puerto Viejo de Sarapiquí is one of the most intensively studied tropical forest sites in the world and one of the best birdwatching sites in Central America. The 1,600-hectare reserve of primary and secondary lowland rainforest holds over 450 species including Great Green Macaw, Bare-necked Umbrellabird, Snowy Cotinga, Spectacled Antpitta, and the full suite of Caribbean lowland specialists. The trail system is extensive and well-maintained; guided tours are available daily for day visitors.

## 2. Tortuguero National Park

Tortuguero, accessible only by boat or small plane along the Caribbean coast, is best known for its Green Turtle nesting beaches, but the canal system and surrounding rainforest provide exceptional birding. Great Green Macaws feed in the nut palms along the canals; Boat-billed Herons roost in the mangroves; Sungrebe, Sunbittern, and Agami Heron are found along quiet backwater channels. The forests hold Snowy Cotinga and a productive complement of interior forest species. Boat tours on the canal system are the primary access mode.

## 3. Selva Verde Lodge, Sarapiquí

Selva Verde Lodge on the Sarapiquí River protects a substantial tract of private lowland rainforest and provides well-maintained trail access to excellent habitat. The lodge has built a strong reputation for birding over several decades, and resident guides provide knowledgeable assistance. Key species include Great Green Macaw, Pale-billed Woodpecker, Lanceolated Monklet, and Rufous-tailed Hummingbird.

## 4. Rancho Naturalista, Turrialba Foothills

Rancho Naturalista is a small birding-focused lodge in the foothills above Turrialba that has become one of the most respected birding lodges in Costa Rica. The elevation — around 900 metres — places it at the lower montane transition zone where Caribbean lowland species mix with foothill and lower montane specialties. Over 400 species have been recorded in the lodge grounds and adjacent forest, including Great Potoo, Black-and-yellow Tanager, and the spectacular Keel-billed Toucan at feeders.

## 5. Quebrada González Sector, Braulio Carrillo National Park

The lower Caribbean slope of Braulio Carrillo National Park, accessible via a trail from the Quebrada González ranger station on the Guapiles Highway, provides access to primary rainforest descending from the continental divide. The site is particularly good for forest interior species including army-ant followers — Bicolored Antbird, Spotted Antbird, Ocellated Antbird — and holds Lattice-tailed Trogon, a foothill specialty rarely found elsewhere.

## 6. CATIE Agricultural Research Center, Turrialba

The extensive botanical gardens and arboretum of the CATIE agricultural research center near Turrialba hold an outstanding concentration of fruiting and flowering trees that attract hummingbirds, tanagers, and parrots to accessible viewpoints. The grounds are walkable and pleasant, and the diversity of introduced and native trees creates more foraging opportunities than a corresponding area of uniform forest. Green Hermit, Violet Sabrewing, and Rufous-tailed Hummingbird are regular visitors to flowering shrubs.

## 7. Maquenque National Wildlife Refuge, Northern Zone

Maquenque Wildlife Refuge in the northern lowlands near the Nicaraguan border was established partly to protect critical nesting habitat for the Great Green Macaw, whose breeding population in Costa Rica depends on the mountain almond (Dipteryx panamensis) trees that grow in this region. Visiting the Laguna del Lagarto Lodge and adjacent forest at the right season — January to March — offers the best chance in Costa Rica of seeing large groups of these spectacular, endangered parrots.

## 8. Hitoy-Cerere Biological Reserve, Talamanca Foothills

Hitoy-Cerere in the foothills above the Talamanca indigenous reserves is one of the wettest and most lush forest reserves in Costa Rica, with annual rainfall exceeding 5,000 millimetres. The reserve is not heavily visited, making it attractive for birders seeking less crowded conditions. The foothill forest holds a complement of Caribbean slope species including Green-and-rufous Kingfisher, Tiny Hawk, and numerous forest-interior tanagers and warblers.

## 9. Cahuita National Park, Caribbean Coast

Cahuita protects a stretch of Caribbean beach, coral reef, and coastal rainforest near the town of Cahuita in the southern Caribbean. The coastal forest is excellent for mangrove and coastal species, and the coral forest interior holds many Caribbean lowland species in an accessible setting. Great Curassow, Bare-necked Umbrellabird, and Green Kingfisher have all been recorded in the park.

## 10. Aviarios del Caribe, Estrella River Delta

The Aviarios del Caribe sloth sanctuary and river delta near Cahuita offers guided boat tours on the Estrella River through mangrove and gallery forest that provide excellent access to waterway and wetland species. Black-collared Hawk, Muscovy Duck, and various herons use the river margins, and the forest alongside holds Caribbean specialties including Black-throated Trogon and Streak-chested Antpitta.

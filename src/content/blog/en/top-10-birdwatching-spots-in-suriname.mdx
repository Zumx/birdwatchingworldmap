Suriname is one of the least-birded countries in South America, yet it holds more than 700 species in a territory more than 90 percent forested. The smallest sovereign state on the continent sits on the Guiana Shield between Guyana and French Guiana, and its appeal lies in two contrasting worlds. Along the Atlantic, broad mudflats and mangrove fringes host some of the largest shorebird concentrations in the Neotropics. Inland, beyond the narrow coastal plain where most of the population lives, lies near-continuous primary rainforest reachable mainly by small aircraft and river. The sites below span both, from the Scarlet Ibis roosts of the coast to the cock-of-the-rock of the deep interior.

## 1. Bigi Pan Multiple Use Management Area

Bigi Pan, in the far northwest near Nieuw-Nickerie, is a vast complex of shallow brackish lagoons, mudflats, and mangrove that ranks among the most important shorebird sites on the Atlantic coast of South America. Tens of thousands of Semipalmated Sandpipers, Black-bellied Plovers, and other migrants stage here. The mangroves hold dense roosts of Scarlet Ibis, which return at dusk in flame-coloured ribbons, alongside Roseate Spoonbill and numerous herons.

## 2. Weg naar Zee

Weg naar Zee, just outside Paramaribo, is the most accessible coastal birding site in the country, a stretch of mudflat and mangrove on the city's doorstep. At low tide the flats fill with feeding shorebirds, including Hudsonian Godwit and Whimbrel, and the mangroves shelter the Rufous Crab Hawk and Bicolored Conebill. It is the easiest place to sample the coastal birdlife without a long journey, and the Hindu cremation site nearby adds cultural interest.

## 3. Brownsberg Nature Park

Brownsberg is a forested plateau rising to around 500 metres above the Brokopondo reservoir, roughly two hours south of Paramaribo, and the most accessible interior forest site. Trails through the upland rainforest hold the Guianan Red Cotinga, White-fronted Manakin, Capuchinbird, and a rich antbird community. Viewpoints over the vast Brokopondo lake and the surrounding canopy make it a comfortable introduction to Guiana Shield forest birding.

## 4. Raleighvallen and Voltzberg

Raleighvallen, on the Coppename River within the Central Suriname Nature Reserve, is the classic deep-interior destination. The reserve is a UNESCO World Heritage Site of more than 1.6 million hectares of pristine forest. The Voltzberg, a granite dome rising from the forest, hosts a famous lek of the Guianan Cock-of-the-rock on its lower slopes, and the surrounding forest holds the Harpy Eagle, Crimson Fruitcrow, and Capuchinbird.

## 5. Central Suriname Nature Reserve

The Central Suriname Nature Reserve protects an enormous block of untouched Guiana Shield rainforest in the heart of the country, encompassing Raleighvallen, the Tafelberg, and Eilerts de Haan mountains. Access is by light aircraft and river, and the reward is forest so intact that the full predator guild is present. The reserve is a stronghold for the Harpy Eagle and a host of canopy cotingas and toucans rarely seen elsewhere.

## 6. Brokopondo and the Afobaka area

The Brokopondo reservoir, a large artificial lake created by the Afobaka dam on the Suriname River, is surrounded by forest and gallery habitat. The lakeshore and drowned-forest snags hold Sunbittern along the streams, Spangled Cotinga, and large numbers of fishing raptors and herons. The area combines easily with Brownsberg above it for a varied weekend of forest and water birding south of the capital.

## 7. Peperpot Nature Park

Peperpot, a former coffee and cocoa plantation just east of Paramaribo across the river, has been turned into a nature park with easy trails through secondary forest and old plantation woodland. It is a relaxed, accessible site that produces toucans, trogons, woodpeckers, and a good range of flycatchers and tanagers, ideal for a first morning before heading further afield. The Blood-coloured Woodpecker, a Guianan near-endemic, occurs here.

## 8. Coppename Rivermouth

The mouth of the Coppename River, a protected wetland on the central coast, is one of the most important intertidal sites for migratory shorebirds in Suriname. Vast mudflats exposed at low tide draw Semipalmated Sandpiper, Short-billed Dowitcher, and Willet in large numbers, while the mangroves hold Scarlet Ibis and Tricolored Heron. It is a Ramsar-listed site reflecting the global significance of Suriname's coast on the shorebird flyway.

## 9. Kabalebo Nature Resort

Kabalebo, in the remote far west of the interior, is a private reserve and lodge set in pristine rainforest reached by light aircraft. With its own airstrip and an extensive trail network along the Kabalebo River, it offers some of the best deep-forest birding in the country away from the better-known reserves. Crimson Topaz, Guianan Toucanet, and a full suite of understorey antbirds are among the highlights.

## 10. Sipaliwini Savanna

The Sipaliwini Savanna, in the far south on the Brazilian border, is a patch of open grassland and gallery forest cut off from the Amazonian and Rupununi savannas. This unusual habitat holds open-country birds absent from the forested rest of the country, including the Burrowing Owl, Bearded Tachuri, and savanna seedeaters. Its isolation makes it a fascinating biogeographic outlier and a rarely visited target for committed birders.

## Planning a Suriname birding trip

A productive trip pairs the accessible coast and near-Paramaribo sites with at least one fly-in interior reserve. A typical itinerary works Weg naar Zee, Peperpot, and Bigi Pan on the coast, then takes a light aircraft to Raleighvallen or Kabalebo for several days of deep-forest birding. Paramaribo, the Dutch-colonial capital, is the hub for all logistics, and the country's small size keeps the coastal driving manageable on the single main road west to Nieuw-Nickerie.

The dry seasons — roughly February to April and August to November — offer the best forest access and the most comfortable conditions, though the coast is rewarding year-round. The northbound shorebird passage peaks in spring, when the mudflats at Bigi Pan and the Coppename mouth fill with migrants, while the cock-of-the-rock lek at Voltzberg is active throughout the year. Interior travel depends on charter flights, so building in weather buffer days is wise.

Suriname remains genuinely off the beaten track, with limited birding infrastructure and few resident guides, which is part of its appeal for those seeking quiet, intact wilderness. Dutch is the official language, a legacy that shapes the lodges and reserves. A specialist operator is the practical way to arrange interior charters and reserve permits, and the rewards — empty trails, full predator communities, and globally significant shorebird flats — are considerable.

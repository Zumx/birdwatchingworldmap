South Korea holds a strategically important position on the East Asian-Australasian Flyway, one of the world's most threatened migration corridors. The Yellow Sea coast of Korea, with its extraordinary tidal range — among the largest in the world — creates vast intertidal mudflats that serve as critical staging and fuelling sites for hundreds of millions of migratory shorebirds. Many of these birds are species with declining global populations, including Spoon-billed Sandpiper, Nordmann's Greenshank, and Far Eastern Curlew. South Korea is also the most important wintering ground outside China for Black-faced Spoonbill, a globally endangered species with a total population of fewer than 6,000 individuals.

The country's east coast, facing the Sea of Japan, provides a different birding character — rocky shores, pine-forested headlands, and offshore islands with seabird colonies. Korea's temperate forest holds breeding and wintering passerines of the East Asian boreal and temperate zone, and the rice-farming plains of the south are key wintering habitat for cranes, geese, and raptors.

## 1. Ganghwa Island Tidal Flats, Incheon

Ganghwa Island at the mouth of the Han River estuary is the most accessible major tidal flat site in South Korea, within public transport reach of Seoul. The island's western coast faces the broad tidal flats of the Han estuary, and the combination of intertidal mud and reclaimed rice paddies supports one of Korea's most diverse shorebird communities. Black-faced Spoonbill winters on the flats in numbers that represent a significant proportion of the global population. Great Knot, Dunlin, Sanderling, and Curlew Sandpiper are present in thousands during migration. The adjacent pine forest holds Mandarin Duck in the wooded streams. Shore birding along the seawall road is productive at all states of tide.

## 2. Junam Reservoir, Gyeongnam Province

Junam near Changwon is one of the most important wintering sites for large waterbirds in Korea. The reservoir is surrounded by rice paddies that provide foraging habitat for Hooded Crane — one of the world's rarest cranes, wintering primarily at a handful of Korean sites — and White-naped Crane. Daily counts of over 10,000 cranes roosting on the reservoir and feeding in the surrounding paddies are possible during January. Swan Geese — a globally vulnerable goose species — winter alongside the cranes. The Junam Reservoir Environmental Ecology Park manages hides and viewing areas specifically for waterbird observation. Peregrine Falcon and Hen Harrier hunt over the roosting flocks.

## 3. Geum Estuary, Chungnam Province

The Geum River estuary on the Yellow Sea coast is a globally significant site for Black-faced Spoonbill, with one of the world's three largest wintering concentrations gathering here from October through March. The spoonbills roost on the tidal islands and forage in the shallow lagoons behind the seawall. The estuary also holds Saunders's Gull — a globally vulnerable gull breeding in limited numbers on Chinese salt marshes and wintering on Korean tidal flats — which requires specific attention to distinguish from the common Black-headed Gull. Far Eastern Oystercatcher winters on the rocky shore. The mudflat diversity during spring migration in late April is exceptional.

## 4. Soiido and Gomso Bay, Buan County, Jeollabuk Province

The Saemangeum reclamation project — one of the world's largest land reclamation schemes, creating a new sea wall enclosing 40,000 hectares of tidal flat — has dramatically altered the Yellow Sea coast. The remaining tidal flat areas at Gomso Bay and the islands such as Soiido now hold disproportionately high shorebird concentrations as remaining habitat shrinks. Spoon-billed Sandpiper stages here during spring migration in May — one of the most reliable sites in Korea for this Critically Endangered species. The reclamation has inadvertently created study sites for monitoring how shorebird populations respond to habitat loss. Sharp-tailed Sandpiper and Red-necked Stint are present in thousands during peak migration.

## 5. Eulsukdo Island, Nakdong Estuary, Busan

The Nakdong Estuary at the mouth of Korea's longest river forms a series of islands and tidal flats that are one of the most productive shorebird sites on the east coast. Eulsukdo, accessible by bus from Busan, holds large wintering populations of Dunlin, Knot, and Grey Plover. Black-faced Spoonbill winters here in smaller numbers than the Geum Estuary. Black-tailed Gull breeds on the offshore rocks and is conspicuous year-round. Chinese Sparrowhawk migrates through the estuary in large numbers in autumn — counts of hundreds per day during peak September migration are documented. The Nakdong Estuary Eco Centre maintains records of daily bird counts and recent sightings.

## 6. Cheollipo Arboretum Coast, Chungnam Province

The coast around Cheollipo Arboretum near Taean is a significant migrant trap on the west coast. The arboretum's pine woodland and the adjacent sandy coast attract East Asian rarities during spring and autumn migration. Yellow-browed Warbler, Radde's Warbler, and Dusky Warbler are regular autumn migrants. Fairy Pitta — a jewel-coloured pitta now breeding in diminishing numbers in Korea — passes through during spring. Chinese Goshawk is a common autumn migrant, hundreds passing in September and October. The coastal woodland of Cheollipo is productive for passerines in a way that few other Korean sites can match. Black Paradise Flycatcher breeds in the arboretum's older trees.

## 7. Suncheon Bay Wetland Reserve, Jeollanam Province

Suncheon Bay is one of the most important wetlands in South Korea and is the core of a UNESCO Global Geopark designation. The bay is the most significant wintering site for Hooded Crane in Korea alongside Junam. The vast reed marsh — Korea's largest — holds wintering Marsh Harrier and Hen Harrier. The open mud and sand of the bay holds Black-faced Spoonbill, Eurasian Spoonbill, and various herons. The Suncheon Bay Ecological Museum provides an elevated boardwalk through the reedbed. The city of Suncheon has invested heavily in ecotourism infrastructure, making this one of the most visitor-friendly wetland sites in the country.

## 8. Jeju Island (Jeju-do)

Jeju Island, Korea's largest island and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, lies south of the Korean Peninsula and serves as a resting point for migrants crossing the Yellow Sea and East China Sea. Halla-san volcano, rising to 1,950 metres, holds breeding Eurasian Jay and Mandarin Duck at the mountain lakes. The coast holds breeding Black-tailed Gull and wintering scoters. Japanese White-eye, an East Asian honeyeater species at its northern breeding range limit, inhabits the subtropical woodland of the southern slopes. The Korean passerine migration through Jeju includes many species uncommon or rare on the mainland — Mugimaki Flycatcher, Narcissus Flycatcher, and Yellow-throated Bunting are all regular in season.

## 9. Eocheong Island, North Jeolla Province

Eocheong, a small island west of the Korean Peninsula in the Yellow Sea, is Korea's premier migration hotspot, frequently producing East Asian rarities and species uncommon on the mainland. The island's position as a stepping stone between China and Korea means that during spring and autumn migration, the small area of woodland and scrub can be overwhelmed with migrant passerines, particularly following weather events that produce fall conditions. Siberian Blue Robin, Eyebrowed Thrush, and various East Asian flycatchers occur regularly. The island requires a ferry crossing from the mainland port of Gunsan, and birders typically stay overnight at the small guesthouse to maximise the brief morning activity windows.

## 10. Demilitarized Zone (DMZ), Gyeonggi Province

The DMZ between North and South Korea is one of the most remarkable de facto nature reserves in the world. The 4-kilometre-wide no-man's-land running the width of the Korean Peninsula has been largely undisturbed by human activity for over 70 years, and the fields, wetlands, and woodland within it support wildlife populations of a density and diversity now rarely found elsewhere in the densely populated Korean Peninsula. Red-crowned Crane and White-naped Crane winter in the Cheorwon Basin near the DMZ in significant numbers. Steller's Sea Eagle and White-tailed Eagle are winter visitors. Observation of the cranes from the legal public areas near the civilian control zone fence is possible and is one of the most atmospheric birding experiences in Asia.

---

South Korea rewards birders who plan around both the migration season — April-May and September-October for shorebirds and passerines — and the winter months for cranes, spoonbills, and raptors. The country's infrastructure is excellent and the birding community active and welcoming to visitors.

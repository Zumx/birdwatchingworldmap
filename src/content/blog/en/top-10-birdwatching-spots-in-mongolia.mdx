Mongolia's 1.56 million square kilometres hold fewer than 3.5 million people — one of the lowest population densities on earth. Its bird fauna, around 550 species, includes the entire Central Asian steppe community and several species that are globally threatened or restricted in range: Cinereous Vulture (Aegypius monachus), Pallas's Fish Eagle (Haliaeetus leucoryphus, Vulnerable), Black-necked Crane (Grus nigricollis, Vulnerable), and Siberian White Crane (Leucogeranus leucogeranus, Critically Endangered) on migration. The logistics of birding Mongolia require preparation — much of the country lacks paved roads, camping is the primary accommodation in the interior, and a local guide with off-road vehicle is essential for most productive sites.

## 1. Khövsgöl National Park, Khövsgöl Aimag

Lake Khövsgöl, Mongolia's deepest lake at 262 metres, is surrounded by taiga forest that holds the northern Mongolian forest community: Azure Tit (Cyanistes cyanus), Siberian Jay (Perisoreus infaustus), and Siberian Rubythroat (Calliope calliope). Whooper Swan (Cygnus cygnus) breeds on the lake margins; Black Stork (Ciconia nigra) occurs in the river valleys. The park's forests transition to open steppe at lower elevations, providing the full gradient in a single area.

## 2. Eastern Mongolian Steppe, Dornod Aimag

The Dornod steppe, protected in the Mongolian Steppe National Park complex, is one of the largest remaining intact temperate grassland ecosystems on earth. Demoiselle Crane (Grus virgo) breeds in large concentrations; Mongolian Lark (Melanocorypha mongolica), Horned Lark (Eremophila alpestris), and White-naped Crane (Antigone vipio, Vulnerable) are characteristic steppe species. The steppe also holds one of the largest remaining Mongolian Gazelle populations, providing the large-mammal context for open steppe birding.

## 3. Hustai National Park, Töv Aimag

Hustai, 100 kilometres southwest of Ulaanbaatar, is best known for the reintroduced Takhi (Przewalski's Horse) population, but the steppe and forest-steppe holds Common Crane, Black Kite (Milvus migrans), Eurasian Hobby (Falco subbuteo), and Upland Buzzard (Buteo hemilasius). The park's accessibility from the capital makes it the first birding option for visitors with limited time.

## 4. Gobi Gurvan Saikhan National Park

The southern Gobi Desert's most productive accessible section, centering on the saxaul forest oases and sand dunes near Dalanzadgad. Mongolian Ground Jay (Podoces hendersoni), Cinereous Vulture, and Pallas's Sandgrouse (Syrrhaptes paradoxus) are characteristic. The Yol Canyon (Eagle Valley) produces cliff-nesting Bearded Vulture (Gypaetus barbatus) and Golden Eagle in an extraordinarily narrow gorge.

## 5. Khar Us Nuur National Park, Khovd Aimag

The wetland complex of Khar Us Nuur and associated lakes in western Mongolia holds the most important breeding Great Black-headed Gull (Ichthyaetus ichthyaetus) colony in the world, with several thousand pairs nesting on the lake islands. Relict Gull (Ichthyaetus relictus, Vulnerable) has been recorded at this complex; the saline lakes additionally hold breeding Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened) and Bar-headed Goose (Anser indicus).

## 6. Dalai Nuur Lake, Dornod Aimag

The Hulun Buir plain and Dalai Nuur (Hulun Lake) on the Chinese border hold one of the most important breeding Demoiselle Crane populations in the eastern steppe. Relict Gull has been confirmed as a rare breeder. The surrounding grassland supports the east Mongolian steppe bird community at its most intact.

## 7. Orkhon Valley and Khangai Mountains

The Orkhon River valley in the Khangai Mountains combines the cultural heritage of Karakorum with a river valley holding White-throated Dipper (Cinclus cinclus), Eurasian Kingfisher (Alcedo atthis), and the full complement of Mongolian river forest species. The surrounding Khangai forest-steppe holds Lesser Spotted Eagle (Clanga pomarina) and Black Kite.

## 8. Bogd Khan Uul, Ulaanbaatar

The Bogd Khan mountain forest immediately south of Ulaanbaatar provides accessible day birding from the capital. Siberian Tit (Poecile cinctus), Three-toed Woodpecker (Picoides tridactylus), and Eurasian Nutcracker (Nucifraga caryocatactes) represent the boreal forest element; the forest margins hold Altai Snowcock (Tetraogallus altaicus) in rocky outcrops.

## 9. Tövkhön Monastery area

The forest-steppe zone around Tövkhön and the Khögnö Khan Uul reserve in Övörkhangai holds a steppe grassland community including Mongolian Finch (Bucanetes mongolicus) and Pallas's Bunting (Emberiza pallasi). The ruins of the Karakorum city site lie in the valley below.

## 10. Bayan-Ölgii and Altai Mountains

The Altai Mountains in far western Mongolia hold the highest eagle-hunting tradition in the world — Kazakh horsemen fly trained Golden Eagles for the fox hunt. The avifauna includes Altai Snowcock, Lammergeier (Gypaetus barbatus), and the breeding Central Asian raptors at the junction of the Mongolian and Kazakh steppe.

## Planning a Mongolia birding trip

Mongolia's birding requires serious logistical preparation. The productive birding season runs from late May through September; outside this window, extreme cold and snow make many sites inaccessible. The eastern steppe breeding season peaks in June and July — Demoiselle Cranes displaying, White-naped Cranes at nest, and Mongolian Larks and Horned Larks singing from the grassland — but the distances require a 4WD vehicle, camping equipment, and a local guide who speaks Mongolian and knows the unmarked tracks.

The most practical approach for first-time visitors is a tour with an established Mongolia operator who provides vehicle, driver, and guide. Self-driving is possible but requires navigation skills, satellite communication, and sufficient supplies for multi-day remote travel. The main road network (Ulaanbaatar to Ölgii, Ulaanbaatar to Dalanzadgad) is paved; most birding destinations require off-road travel on tracks that are challenging after rain.

A two-week Mongolia birding trip can reasonably cover: Hustai National Park (accessible day trip from the capital), the Orkhon Valley, the central Khangai Mountains, and either the eastern steppe (Dornod) or the southern Gobi (Dalanzadgad) depending on specific targets. The eastern steppe Demoiselle Crane and White-naped Crane combination requires a full day's drive east of Ulaanbaatar on rough roads. Gobi targets — Mongolian Ground Jay, Pallas's Sandgrouse — are concentrated around the saxaul oases of the Gobi Gurvan Saikhan and are accessible from the Dalanzadgad airport.

Ulaanbaatar's international airport serves direct flights from Beijing, Seoul, and Moscow, providing reasonable connection from major European and Asian hubs. The city's guesthouses and tour operators provide the necessary logistics infrastructure.

## Guide and transport requirements

Mongolia's birding infrastructure is less developed than Central Asian alternatives, making organized tours or independently arranged local guides the most practical approach for all but the most experienced expedition birders. The Mongolian Ornithological Society (based in Ulaanbaatar) maintains a network of English-speaking guides with off-road vehicles, and can arrange multi-day birding circuits to the eastern steppe, the Gobi, and the western Altai on request.

The primary vehicle requirement is a 4WD capable of wilderness camping — preferably a Soviet-era UAZ452 (the "loaf") or a Toyota Land Cruiser with roof tent. GPS navigation is essential; Mongolian steppe tracks are unmarked on standard map products. Mongolian SIM cards (MobiCom or Unitel) provide coverage around Ulaanbaatar and along the main paved routes; remote areas lack cellular connectivity.

Ulaanbaatar's Chinggis Khaan International Airport serves direct connections from Beijing (Air China), Seoul (Korean Air), and Moscow (Aeroflot), with budget connections from Irkutsk available in summer. The principal tour operators based in Ulaanbaatar with birding-specific programs include Mongolian Nature Tours and Birding Mongolia, both with English-language websites and documented tour records on eBird.

Mountain habitats offer some of the most rewarding and demanding birding in the world. The combination of dramatic landscapes, high endemism, and the challenge of physical access makes montane birding a distinct discipline with its own strategies, tools, and rewards. From the cloud forests of the Andes to the moorlands of East African volcanoes, the alpine zones of the Himalayas to the coniferous forests of the Rockies, elevation gradients compress enormous avian diversity into relatively small geographic areas and produce some of the highest concentrations of endemic and near-endemic species anywhere on Earth.

The fundamental principle of montane birding is that bird communities change with altitude, and understanding which species occupy which elevation band allows efficient targeting of specific assemblages. A birder who arrives at a mountain destination without prior knowledge of the elevation zones and their characteristic species will spend significant time and energy in the wrong place.

## Working Elevation Gradients

Most montane regions show distinct transitions in bird communities corresponding to vegetation zones: lowland tropical forest giving way to lower montane forest, then upper montane cloud forest, then elfin woodland, then treeline scrub and paramo or moorland, and finally alpine zone above the treeline. Each transition brings new species and the departure of others.

In the Andes, the treeline is typically around 3,800 to 4,200 metres above sea level depending on latitude. The puna grassland above it holds species like the Giant Coot, Puna Ibis, and numerous finches adapted to high-altitude conditions. The cloud forest zone between roughly 2,000 and 3,500 metres holds the greatest diversity of endemic species, including spectacular hummingbirds, tapaculos, and ant-pittas found nowhere else. Arriving at altitude in early morning, before cloud and mist build up in the cloud forest zone, is essential — birds are most active and most visible in the first two hours of light.

## Key Sites: The Andes

The Andes represent the world's greatest concentration of montane bird diversity. Ecuador's Mindo valley on the Pacific slope of the western Andes is among the most accessible and species-rich birding destinations in the world, with a concentration of hummingbird feeders at lodges like Paz de las Aves and Bellavista drawing species including the Velvet-purple Coronet, Empress Brilliant, and Booted Racket-tail. The Via Nono-Mindo road is a classic route for working the elevation gradient from 1,500 to 2,500 metres, with cloud forest giving way to more open montane scrub.

In Peru, the Manu Road from Cuzco down the eastern Andes into the Amazon basin passes through a complete elevation gradient from puna grassland at 4,000 metres to lowland forest at 300 metres, and is one of the most productive single-road birding routes in the world. Cock-of-the-Rock leks in the cloud forest zone draw birders from around the world.

## Key Sites: Africa

The Bale Mountains of Ethiopia hold the Ethiopian Wolf — Africa's rarest large carnivore — but are equally important for their endemic birds. The Ethiopian Siskin, Rouget's Rail, and Spot-breasted Plover are among the high-altitude specialists of the Sanetti Plateau. The Hagenia-Hypericum woodland belt between 3,000 and 3,800 metres holds the majority of the montane forest endemics, including the exquisite Abyssinian Longclaw.

The Rwenzori Mountains, straddling Uganda and the Democratic Republic of Congo, are among the least accessible but most distinctive African mountain ranges. Their montane forest and afroalpine moorland hold Rwenzori Turaco, Rwenzori Sunbird, and Rwenzori Nightjar, all endemic or near-endemic to this small range.

## Key Sites: The Himalayas

Doi Inthanon in Thailand, while a relatively modest mountain at 2,565 metres, holds a concentration of upper-montane species — Rufous-vented Yuhina, Green-tailed Sunbird, and a remarkable concentration of flycatchers — in its upper forests that makes it one of Southeast Asia's premier birding sites. In the central Himalayas, Langtang Valley in Nepal and Hemis National Park in Ladakh offer access to entirely different altitudinal zones, from subtropical foothills to high alpine desert.

## Practical Altitude Considerations

Altitude affects both the birder and the birds. At elevations above 3,000 metres, acclimatisation is necessary before strenuous walking. Arriving at altitude without a period of acclimatisation produces fatigue, headache, and reduced cognitive and physical performance — all of which compromise birding effectiveness. Building a day of minimal exertion into arrivals above 3,500 metres, and ascending gradually where itinerary permits, both improves health and bird-finding productivity.

Cold is the other practical issue. Mountain mornings can be extremely cold even near the equator, and thermal layers, waterproof outer layers, and gloves are necessary well into the tropics above 3,000 metres. Birds themselves are most active in the first two hours after first light regardless of altitude, but conditions for the observer improve significantly once the sun reaches the forest.

## Cloud Forest Tactics

Cloud forests present a specific challenge: birds are often heard before they are seen, and the tangled vegetation makes tracking a calling bird to a viewpoint extremely difficult. Moving slowly and stopping frequently to listen is more productive than covering ground. Calling birds move toward the observer if you stay still; active movement drives them deeper into cover.

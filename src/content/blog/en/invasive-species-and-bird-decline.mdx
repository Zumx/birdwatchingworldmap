The most significant driver of bird extinctions in recorded history is not habitat loss, climate change, or hunting, though all three have caused enormous damage. It is invasive species, particularly the handful of introduced mammals that have followed human movement around the world for centuries. Domestic and feral cats, rats, mice, stoats, and in specific contexts foxes, mongooses, and pigs have eliminated more bird species from islands than any other cause and continue to suppress populations of vulnerable species globally.

The reason invasive predators are so destructive is that they are new to the systems they enter. Island birds that evolved without mammalian predators have no evolved behavioural responses to them. A petrel that nests in a burrow and has never been predated at the nest site has no instinct to respond to a sniffing rat. Its eggs and chicks are defenseless. The same logic applies to ground-nesting birds more broadly: any species that evolved in a predator-free environment and commits to vulnerable nesting positions is exposed by the introduction of novel predators.

## The Scale of the Problem

The IUCN Red List attributes more than a third of all bird extinctions since 1500 to invasive species, with introduced mammals — particularly rats, cats, and mustelids — responsible for the majority of those. Sixty percent of threatened island birds face pressure from invasive mammals. The Pacific island archipelagos, the Mascarene Islands in the Indian Ocean, the Hawaiian Islands, and the islands of New Zealand have together lost more bird species to invasive predators than any other cause.

New Zealand's avifauna illustrates the scale: Polynesian settlers arrived around 1280 CE and brought the Pacific rat (Kiore); European settlers arrived in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries with ship rats, Norway rats, cats, stoats, ferrets, and weasels. The combination eliminated multiple species of moa, Huia, several species of wren, and numerous seabirds from the main islands, and continues to suppress populations of Kiwi, Kaka, Kakapo, and many seabirds.

## Cats and Birds: A Difficult Conversation

Domestic and feral cats are estimated to kill billions of birds annually in North America alone. A 2013 study published in Nature Communications, based on a meta-analysis of previous research, estimated that free-ranging domestic cats in the United States kill between 1.3 and 4.0 billion birds per year, with un-owned feral cats responsible for the majority of the total. The figures are contested in their specifics but directionally robust.

The harm is not evenly distributed. Common, adaptable species that exist at high population densities are affected far less than species with small populations, slow reproductive rates, and ground or low-shrub nesting habits. Cerulean Warbler, Wood Thrush, and many other Neotropical migrants that breed in North American forests and nest on or near the ground are particularly vulnerable during the nesting season. On islands, where endemic species with no evolved antipredator behaviours exist in small total populations, a single feral cat can drive a breeding colony of a vulnerable species to extinction within a single season.

The response to this evidence from some quarters — particularly defenders of Trap-Neuter-Return programs for feral cat colonies — is that cats are protected by animal welfare considerations that override the impact on wildlife. The scientific consensus among conservation biologists is that TNR programs do not effectively reduce feral cat populations at the scale needed to reduce bird mortality, and that on islands, complete removal of cats is the only conservation measure that reliably prevents extinction.

## Island Rat Eradication Programs

The most successful conservation interventions against invasive predators have been island rat eradications. Removing all rats from an island, through a combination of rodenticide bait station programs, causes rapid recovery of ground-nesting and burrow-nesting birds. The methodology has been refined over decades of work led by the New Zealand Department of Conservation, the Island Conservation organization, the RSPB, and national governments across the Pacific.

South Georgia Island in the South Atlantic was cleared of introduced Norway and ship rats — which had been killing millions of seabirds annually including South Georgia Pipit, the only passerine endemic to Antarctica-adjacent islands — in an eradication program completed in 2015 involving more than 300 tonnes of brodifacoum bait distributed by helicopter across the island's complex terrain. Five years after eradication, South Georgia Pipit populations had increased dramatically and ground-nesting petrels and prions had recolonised areas of the island where they had been absent for over a century.

Anacapa Island in Channel Islands National Park, California, and Rat Island in the Aleutians have undergone similar programs with documented seabird recovery. The New Zealand offshore island network, centred on predator-free islands like Tiritiri Matangi and Zealandia (Karori Wildlife Sanctuary in Wellington), has become a refuge system for species that survive on the main islands only with intensive management.

## Stoats and the New Zealand Crisis

In New Zealand, stoats (Mustela erminea) — introduced in the 1880s to control rabbits — caused more damage to native birds than cats and rats combined. Stoats climb trees, raid nests, and kill adult birds at weights up to a kilogram. The masting cycles of beech forest, which produce massive seed crops every four to five years, trigger eruptions in mouse populations followed by stoat population explosions, resulting in devastating predator pulses that synchronise with breeding seasons.

Kiwi chick survival on the mainland without intensive predator control is estimated at around five percent. The national 1080 (sodium monofluoroacetate) aerial drop program, which targets rats and stoats simultaneously, has proven effective at maintaining populations in otherwise unmanageable terrain, though it remains controversial.

Each autumn a young Eurasian cuckoo that has never met its parents leaves Europe alone, flies to Central Africa, and returns the following spring to the very wood where it hatched. A bar-tailed godwit lifts off from Alaska and flies for eight or nine days without stopping, eating, drinking or sleeping, crossing the entire Pacific to New Zealand in a single feat tracked by satellite tags. An Arctic tern threads a path between the two ends of the Earth and back every year of a life that can span three decades. These journeys demand more than stamina; they demand navigation, the ability to know where you are and which way to go. How birds do it has occupied scientists for more than a century, and the answer is not a single sense but a layered toolkit of cues that birds weigh against one another with remarkable sophistication.

## A compass and a map

It helps to separate two problems the way navigators always have. A compass tells you direction — which way is north — while a map tells you position, where you are relative to your goal. A bird with only a compass can hold a heading but cannot correct if a storm blows it off course; a bird with a map sense can work out that it has drifted east and steer back. Experiments going back to the German researcher Gustav Kramer in the 1950s showed that birds possess both, and that they draw direction and position from several independent sources. The richness of bird navigation lies in this redundancy: when one cue fails, beneath cloud or far out at sea, another takes over.

## Reading the Sun

The most intuitive cue is the Sun, and birds use it as a true compass rather than a mere bright spot. Because the Sun moves across the sky through the day, using it for direction requires a clock to compensate for that movement — a so-called time-compensated sun compass. Kramer demonstrated this by shifting the internal clocks of caged starlings with artificial light cycles, after which the birds oriented in a predictably wrong direction, their compass fooled by a clock running on the wrong time. Birds can also read the pattern of polarised light in the sky, strongest in a band at right angles to the Sun, which lets them calibrate direction around sunrise and sunset even when the Sun itself is hidden.

## The star map

Night-migrating songbirds, which make up the bulk of long-distance migrants, lean heavily on the stars. The classic experiments were done in the 1960s by Stephen Emlen, who raised indigo buntings in a planetarium. Birds shown a normal night sky oriented correctly; birds shown a sky rotating around the wrong star shifted their bearings accordingly. The crucial discovery was that birds do not memorise individual constellations but learn, as nestlings, the centre of celestial rotation — the still point near Polaris around which the northern sky turns. That centre marks true north regardless of season or time of night, a far more reliable beacon than any single star. A young bird that never sees a rotating sky in its first weeks never develops a working star compass.

## The magnetic sense

The most extraordinary of a bird's senses is its ability to detect Earth's magnetic field. Wolfgang and Roswitha Wiltschko showed in 1966 that European robins in a chamber would orient by an artificial magnetic field, and that their compass reads not magnetic polarity but the angle the field lines make with the ground — an inclination compass that distinguishes poleward from equatorward rather than north from south. How birds sense the field is still debated, with two leading ideas. One involves crystals of magnetite in the beak feeding information through the trigeminal nerve. The other, increasingly supported, is a light-dependent chemical reaction in the eye, in proteins called cryptochromes, in which the magnetic field subtly alters the outcome of a reaction triggered by blue light — meaning a bird may, in some sense, see the field. The magnetic field also encodes position: its inclination and intensity vary predictably with latitude, giving birds a coarse magnetic map as well as a compass.

## A nose for home

Smell, long dismissed in birds, turns out to matter more than anyone expected. Decades of work by the Italian researcher Floriano Papi and others on homing pigeons showed that birds deprived of their sense of smell, or prevented from learning the wind-borne odours around their loft, lose much of their homing ability over unfamiliar ground. The proposed mechanism is an olfactory map: pigeons learn to associate particular smells carried on winds from particular directions, building a scent landscape they can use to work out roughly where they are when released far from home. Procellariiform seabirds — shearwaters, petrels and albatrosses — have famously acute noses and appear to navigate the featureless ocean partly by smell, homing in on the dimethyl sulphide released where plankton blooms feed their prey.

## Putting the cues together and learning the route

No single sense rules; birds integrate them and calibrate one against another. Many songbirds treat the magnetic field as a backbone but reset it each evening against the polarised light at sunset, correcting the drift that would otherwise accumulate. The deepest puzzle is true navigation — the ability of an experienced bird, displaced thousands of kilometres to a place it has never been, to detect the displacement and correct for it. A celebrated experiment by Wallraff and colleagues displaced adult and juvenile starlings across Europe: the seasoned adults compensated and reached the normal wintering grounds, while the inexperienced young held their innate compass heading and ended up in the wrong country. The lesson is that birds are born with a compass and an approximate inherited direction and timetable, but the map — the ability to know where you are and to truly navigate — is built through experience, journey after journey.

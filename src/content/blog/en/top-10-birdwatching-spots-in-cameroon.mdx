Cameroon is often called Africa in miniature, and for birders the description is precise: within a single country you can move from steaming Gulf of Guinea lowland forest to montane cloud forest, from the savanna woodland of the Adamawa plateau to the seasonal floodplains and Sahelian scrub of the far north. That habitat range, stacked against the volcanic line of mountains running inland from the coast, makes Cameroon one of the richest bird countries on the continent, with well over 900 recorded species and a remarkable concentration of endemics confined to the Cameroon-Bamenda highlands. A full circuit is logistically demanding and crosses regions of varying accessibility, but the rewards — from Mount Cameroon Speirops to Egyptian Plovers on northern rivers — are among the finest in West-Central Africa.

## Mount Cameroon

Mount Cameroon rises to 4,095 metres from near sea level, the only place in West Africa where forest grades unbroken into montane grassland and volcanic lava fields. The mountain is the stronghold of the Mount Cameroon Speirops and Mount Cameroon Francolin, both found nowhere else, alongside the localised Cameroon Sunbird and Grey-necked Rockfowl in the foothill forest. Birders climb from the town of Buea through the forest belt, where mixed flocks and turacos give way with altitude to montane species. The trek is strenuous and the weather notoriously wet, but no other site delivers the mountain's true endemics so reliably.

## Mount Kupe and the Bakossi Mountains

Mount Kupe, near Nyasoso, is one of the most famous montane forest sites in Africa, celebrated for the Mount Kupe Bushshrike, long thought close to extinction and still one of the continent's hardest birds. The surrounding Bakossi Mountains hold Green-breasted Bushshrike, Bangwa Forest Warbler, White-throated Mountain Babbler, and Cameroon Montane Greenbul. Birders walk the forested trails above the village in search of mixed flocks, while the lower forest produces Grey-necked Rockfowl at known nesting boulders. The dense, mossy forest demands patience, but the density of restricted-range species here is exceptional.

## Bamenda Highlands and Mount Oku

The grassfields of the Northwest, centred on Mount Oku and the Kilum-Ijim forest, protect the largest remaining montane forest in West Africa and a clutch of highland endemics. Lake Oku, a crater lake at around 2,200 metres, is ringed by forest holding Bannerman's Turaco, Banded Wattle-eye, Oku Bushshrike, and the localised Bamenda Apalis. The community forest projects here are among Africa's most celebrated conservation successes. Birding the trails above the lake in the cool highland air is a complete contrast to the lowland forest, and the endemics, though shy, are well worth the altitude.

## Korup National Park

Korup, in the Southwest near the Nigerian border, protects some of the oldest and most species-rich lowland rainforest in Africa, with a tree diversity rivalling any forest on Earth. The bird list is immense: Yellow-casqued Hornbill, Black-casqued Hornbill, Rufous-sided Broadbill, Red-headed Picathartes (Grey-necked Rockfowl), and a wealth of greenbuls, illadopsis, and forest robins. Access is on foot across the famous suspension bridge over the Mana River and along forest transects. The humidity and dense canopy make for challenging birding, but Korup is the classic Cameroonian lowland forest experience.

## Mbam et Djerem National Park

Mbam et Djerem, in the centre of the country, protects a vast mosaic of Guinea-Congo forest, gallery forest, and Adamawa savanna where the two great biomes meet. This transition zone holds an unusual mix of forest and savanna species in close proximity, including White-crested Tiger Heron along the rivers, Pel's Fishing Owl, and a strong range of raptors over the savanna. The park's rivers and grasslands support hippos and large mammals, and boat trips add waterbirds to the forest-edge list. It is one of Cameroon's least-visited but most rewarding parks.

## Ngaoundaba and the Adamawa Plateau

The Adamawa plateau around Ngaoundéré, at roughly 1,100 metres, carries Guinea savanna woodland and crater lakes that hold a distinct avifauna. The lodge at Ngaoundaba Ranch, beside a small crater lake, is a classic birding base for Brown-chested Lapwing, Sun Lark, Stanley Bustard, and the African finfoot on quiet water. The open woodland produces rollers, bee-eaters, and bush shrikes, while the lake edges hold herons and kingfishers. The cooler plateau climate makes for comfortable birding compared with the lowlands.

## Bénoué National Park

Bénoué, in the North along the Bénoué River, is a savanna park of woodland, grassland, and riverine forest that delivers classic Sudanian birds. The river supports Egyptian Plover, African Skimmer, and Rock Pratincole on exposed sandbanks, while the woodland holds Red-throated Bee-eater colonies, Abyssinian Roller, Violet Turaco, and a range of hornbills and bustards. Game drives here combine birding with elephant, hippo, and antelope. Bénoué is an essential northern stop and offers a complete shift from the forest avifauna of the south.

## Waza National Park

Waza, in the far north toward Lake Chad, is Cameroon's most famous savanna park, a flat expanse of acacia woodland and seasonally flooded grassland on the edge of the Sahel. In the dry season the waterholes concentrate huge numbers of birds: Black Crowned Crane, Marabou Stork, Yellow-billed Stork, Greater and Lesser Flamingo, and large raptor numbers including Bateleur and vultures. The open country also holds bustards, coursers, and Sahelian larks. Security in the far north has fluctuated, so current advice should always be checked, but Waza remains a legendary dry-season birding destination.

## Maga and the Logone Floodplain

The Maga dam and the Logone floodplain in the Far North create one of Central Africa's most important wetlands, flooding seasonally to support enormous waterbird concentrations. The reservoir and surrounding waterbodies hold pelicans, herons, ducks, and waders in numbers, with River Prinia and Quailfinch in the reedbeds and grassland. This is also a stronghold for Black Crowned Crane and a passage point for Palearctic migrants. The floodplain at high water is a vast sheet of birds, best worked from the dam road and surrounding tracks.

## Limbe and the Coastal Lowlands

The coast around Limbe and the mouth of the Mungo River offers lowland and mangrove birding close to the sea, an easy add-on for visitors flying into Douala. The estuaries hold waders, terns, and herons, while the remnant coastal forest and plantations produce sunbirds, bulbuls, and the occasional forest species at the edge. Boat trips into the mangroves can find African Finfoot and kingfishers along the channels. The lowland heat and accessibility make this a relaxed contrast to the strenuous mountain sites inland.

## Explore on the map

A Cameroonian birding trip is really several trips in one: the volcanic mountains of the Southwest and Northwest for endemics, the lowland forests of Korup for the great forest list, and the savanna and wetlands of the north for Sudanian and Sahelian species. Distances are long and the country splits naturally into a forested south and a savanna north, so most birders focus on one zone per visit.

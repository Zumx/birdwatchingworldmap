Turkey holds over 470 breeding bird species and more than 480 wintering species — a total bird list exceeding 500 — making it one of the most ornithologically rich countries in the Western Palearctic. Its position bridging Europe, Central Asia, and the Middle East produces a bird community that includes species from all three biogeographic zones simultaneously. Turkey is also the principal site of one of the world's rarest birds: the Northern Bald Ibis (Geronticus eremita, Endangered) colony at Birecik is the only wild breeding population in Asia outside Morocco. The map database includes several Turkish birdwatching infrastructure points including the Kızılırmak Delta observation tower complex and the Yusuf Erdoğan observatory.

## 1. Sultansazlığı National Park, Kayseri

Sultansazlığı, a shallow saline and freshwater wetland on the Anatolian plateau at 1,000 metres, is the most important waterbird breeding site in Turkey. Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened) breeds here alongside Great White Pelican, Pygmy Cormorant, and Glossy Ibis. The wetland's isolation in the Central Anatolian steppe gives it a quality of remoteness unusual for a national park with visitor infrastructure. The surrounding steppe holds Little Bustard (Tetrax tetrax, Near Threatened) and Pallid Harrier (Circus macrourus, Near Threatened).

## 2. Manyas Lake (Kuş Cenneti), Bursa

Manyas Kuş Cenneti National Park ("Bird Paradise National Park") holds one of the largest colonial waterbird breeding colonies in Turkey. Great White Pelican, Great White Egret, Night Heron, Little Egret, Squacco Heron, and Spoonbill breed in the lakeside poplar and willow forest. The Kuş Gözetleme Kulesi (birdwatching tower, documented in the map database) provides an elevated viewpoint over the heronry and open water.

## 3. Birecik, Şanlıurfa

Birecik on the Euphrates River hosts the only wild breeding population of Northern Bald Ibis (Geronticus eremita, Endangered) in Asia. Fewer than 15 pairs breed on the cliffs above the town, supplemented by a semi-wild captive flock. The ibis colony has been protected by the Turkish government since the 1970s; the Bilal Aga Tesisleri (documented in the map database) is the community observation facility. Syrian Woodpecker (Dendrocopos syriacus), Menetries's Warbler (Curruca mystacea), and White-throated Robin (Irania gutturalis) occur in the surrounding habitat.

## 4. Kızılırmak Delta, Samsun

The Kızılırmak Delta on the Black Sea coast is a Ramsar wetland holding the largest breeding colony of Lesser Sand Plover (Charadrius mongolus) in Turkey and the most important Black Sea coastal wetland for breeding terns. The observation tower complex at Kızılırmak Deltası Kuş Cenneti (documented in the map database) provides views over the coastal lagoons; the Observation Tower (also documented) covers the adjacent areas. Dalmatian Pelican is regular in winter.

## 5. Tuz Gölü (Salt Lake), Ankara and Konya

The vast Central Anatolian Salt Lake holds the most important Greater Flamingo (Phoenicopterus roseus) breeding colony in Turkey. In a good water year, several thousand pairs breed on the lake's hypersaline flats. The surrounding steppe holds Great Bustard (Otis tarda, Vulnerable) — Turkey holds one of the largest European Great Bustard populations.

## 6. Göksu Delta, Mersin

The Mediterranean Göksu Delta holds Spur-winged Lapwing (Vanellus spinosus), Slender-billed Gull, Greater Flamingo, and the full Mediterranean coastal bird community. The lagoon freshwater section holds breeding Purple Gallinule.

## 7. Uludağ Mountain, Bursa

Turkey's most accessible mountain exceeding 2,500 metres, Uludağ above Bursa holds the full Pontic montane community: Snowfinch (Montifringilla nivalis), Alpine Accentor, Water Pipit, and Crag Martin. The ski-road provides access to the high ridge zone.

## 8. Yusuf Erdoğan Kompleks, Antalya region

The Yusuf Erdoğan Kompleks observatory (documented in the map database) in the Antalya region provides organized observation access to the Mediterranean coastal and wetland habitats of southwest Turkey. Marsh birds, Little Bittern, and the Mediterranean scrub community are accessible from this facility.

## 9. Akseki and the Taurus Mountains

The Central Taurus above Akseki holds Kruper's Nuthatch (Sitta krueperi) in Anatolian Black Pine forest — one of Turkey's most sought endemic-area specialists. Rufous-tailed Rock Thrush (Monticola saxatilis), Rock Bunting (Emberiza cia), and the high-altitude Snowfinch share the mountain habitats.

## 10. Van Gölü (Lake Van) and eastern Turkey

The enormous Lake Van in eastern Turkey attracts migrant waders and wintering Smew (Mergellus albellus) and diving ducks. The surrounding steppe of Eastern Anatolia holds breeding Long-legged Buzzard, Greater Short-toed Lark (Calandrella brachydactyla), and the Central Asian steppe community at its westernmost range.

## Planning a Turkey birding trip

Turkey's birding seasons divide clearly around migration. Spring (April to May) is the most productive for breeding species and northbound migrants: Little Bustard and Great Bustard are displaying on the Anatolian steppe, the Birecik Northern Bald Ibis colony is at maximum activity, Sultansazlığı Dalmatian Pelicans are establishing their nesting colonies, and the coastal forests are filling with migrant passerines.

The Bosphorus in Istanbul provides one of the world's best urban raptor migration watchpoints. In autumn (September to November), Honey Buzzard, Steppe Eagle (Aquila nipalensis), Lesser Spotted Eagle, and Short-toed Eagle concentrate as they cross between Europe and Asia. The Çamlıca Hills viewpoint on the Asian side and the Sabiha Gökçen Airport surroundings both produce counts of tens of thousands of raptors daily during peak periods. The Bosphorus autumn raptor count, coordinated by local birders, documents the total passage through monthly reports accessible online.

Summer in Turkey is productive for the dry country specialties of the Anatolian plateau: Roller, Bee-eater, and the full steppe lark community are most accessible from June to August. The Kızılırmak Delta observation complex is best during the breeding season (May to July) when terns and waders are nesting. The Yusuf Erdoğan Kompleks in Antalya province provides year-round access to the Mediterranean coastal community.

Istanbul is the most logical entry point. Car rental from Istanbul allows coverage of the Bosphorus, then a circuit through the Anatolian plateau visiting Tuz Gölü, Sultansazlığı, and the Taurus for Kruper's Nuthatch in a week-long circuit. Birecik requires a domestic flight to Gaziantep and is best combined with the Syrian border region sites in a separate southeasterly loop.

## Turkish birding infrastructure

Turkey's birding infrastructure has developed significantly in the past decade, driven by the growing domestic birding community organized around the Kuş Gözlem Derneği (KGD, Turkish Bird Watching Association). The KGD maintains an online bird database, a list of observation facilities, and organizes the annual Kuşbank species documentation program. Most of the country's key sites have documented eBird hotspot records and English-language site guides available through KGD or the national park authorities.

The most organized site infrastructure is at Birecik (the Bilal Aga Tesisleri observation facility for Northern Bald Ibis), the Kızılırmak Delta observation tower complex, and the Sultansazlığı visitor centre. These sites have dedicated public observation facilities and, in the case of Birecik, scheduled release times when the Bald Ibis colony is most active. Guided ornithological tours with English-speaking guides are available through Turkish nature tour operators in Istanbul and Ankara, and through international birding tour companies that include Turkey as a regular destination.

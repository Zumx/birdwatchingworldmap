Estonia is one of Europe's great birding secrets, a small Baltic country where vast tracts of low-lying coast, raised bog, and old-growth forest survive almost intact. Lying directly on the East Atlantic Flyway, its western coast and islands act as a funnel for millions of waterfowl and waders moving between Arctic breeding grounds and wintering areas further south. In spring, the bays and floodplain meadows of the west fill with staging geese, swans, and ducks in numbers that overwhelm the visitor. Inland, the boreal bogs and forests hold a northern community of grouse, owls, and woodpeckers that birders elsewhere in temperate Europe travel far to see. Add a coastline barely above sea level, long northern days, and a tradition of strong nature protection, and Estonia rewards the visiting birder out of all proportion to its size.

## Matsalu National Park

Matsalu, on the western coast at the head of a shallow bay, is Estonia's flagship birding site and one of the most important staging areas in northern Europe. In spring, hundreds of thousands of Barnacle Geese, Bean Geese, and Bewick's and Whooper Swans rest and feed on the coastal meadows and floodplain of the Kasari River before pushing on toward the Arctic. The reedbeds, among the largest in the Baltic, hold Bittern, Bearded Reedling, and Savi's Warbler, while the wet meadows support breeding waders and Corncrake. Observation towers at Haeska, Keemu, and Penijõe give superb views over the flooded grasslands. On a still morning in late April, the combined calling of tens of thousands of geese can merge into an unbroken wall of sound across the water, an experience many visitors describe as the most memorable moment of their Estonian trip.

## Saaremaa Island

Estonia's largest island, Saaremaa, combines wooded meadows, juniper heaths, coastal lagoons, and the dramatic cliffs of Panga. The alvar grasslands and coastal meadows hold breeding waders and the localised Baltic Dunlin, while the bays around Sõrve peninsula at the island's southern tip channel an intense visible migration of seabirds, raptors, and waterfowl in autumn. Sõrve is the place to stand at the lighthouse on a good day and watch divers, scoters, and skuas streaming past offshore.

## Vilsandi National Park

Off the western coast of Saaremaa, Vilsandi National Park protects a scattered archipelago of small islands and skerries that host Estonia's most important seabird colonies. Common Eider, Velvet Scoter, and large numbers of breeding terns and gulls nest on the islets, while the surrounding shallows are a critical moulting and staging area. This was Estonia's first protected area, established in 1910 to safeguard its seabirds, and it remains a stronghold for breeding waterbirds on the Baltic.

## Soomaa National Park

Soomaa, meaning land of bogs, is a wilderness of raised mires, flooded forests, and meandering rivers in southwestern Estonia. The vast Kuresoo and Valgeraba bogs hold breeding Golden Plover, Wood Sandpiper, and Common Crane, and the surrounding forests are home to Black Grouse, Capercaillie, and Hazel Grouse. In spring, the famous fifth season floods the forests entirely, and birders explore by canoe. Owls, including Ural and Tengmalm's, and a full set of woodpeckers including White-backed and Three-toed make this a magnet for northern forest birding.

## Lahemaa National Park

On the northern coast east of Tallinn, Lahemaa is Estonia's oldest national park, a mosaic of bays, peninsulas, bogs, and ancient forest. The coastal meadows and reefs hold breeding and migrating waterbirds, while the extensive old forests of pine and spruce support Capercaillie, owls, and woodpeckers. The bogs at Viru and Laukasoo, crossed by boardwalks, offer accessible encounters with Golden Plover, Crane, and Wood Sandpiper against a classic boreal backdrop.

## Põhja-Kõrvemaa and the Bog Country

Inland from Lahemaa, the bogs and forests of Põhja-Kõrvemaa Nature Reserve form one of the best areas in Estonia for northern forest specialities. This is country where Black Grouse lek at dawn in spring, where Capercaillie display in the pinewoods, and where patient birders may find Tengmalm's Owl and Pygmy Owl. The raised bogs, dotted with pools and stunted pines, hold breeding Golden Plover and Crane.

## Kabli and the Pärnu Bay Coast

The southwestern coast near Pärnu, particularly the bird observatory at Kabli, sits on a major autumn migration route. Established in 1969, Kabli Bird Station runs ringing operations that document the southward passage of passerines, finches, and thrushes along the coast. The shallow Pärnu Bay and its surrounding meadows are important for staging swans, geese, and waders, and the visible migration of raptors and passerines along the coastal pine belt in autumn can be spectacular.

## Alam-Pedja Nature Reserve

In central Estonia, Alam-Pedja protects a huge area of floodplain meadows, mires, and riverine forest along the Emajõgi and Pedja rivers. This roadless wilderness is one of the most important inland wetlands in the country, holding breeding Spotted and Lesser Spotted Eagle, Black Stork, and Corncrake, with displaying Great Snipe at traditional leks in the wet meadows. It is among the largest natural floodplain complexes in Europe.

## Lake Võrtsjärv

Estonia's largest wholly internal lake, Võrtsjärv, lies in the south of the country, a shallow water body fringed by reedbeds and flooded meadows. It is an important staging and breeding area for waterbirds, including grebes, terns, and ducks, and the reedbeds hold breeding Bittern and Marsh Harrier. The flooded meadows at the lake's northern end attract staging waders and waterfowl during migration.

## Endla Nature Reserve

In central Estonia, the Endla Nature Reserve protects an extensive raised-bog and spring-fen system surrounding a chain of lakes. The mires hold breeding Golden Plover, Crane, and Wood Sandpiper, while the surrounding forests support Capercaillie and owls. The Männikjärve bog trail, one of the oldest mire research sites in the Baltic, offers boardwalk access to classic bog habitat and its breeding birds.

## Explore on the map

Estonia rewards a trip planned around the seasons: late April and early May for the staggering spring goose and swan passage at Matsalu, May and June for displaying grouse and breeding bog waders, and September and October for autumn seabird and raptor migration at Sõrve and Kabli. Distances are short and roads quiet, making it possible to combine coast and forest in a single day.

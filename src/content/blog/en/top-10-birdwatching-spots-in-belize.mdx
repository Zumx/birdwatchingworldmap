Belize packs more than 590 bird species into a country smaller than Wales, and its compact geography is part of the appeal: in a single trip you can move from the world's second-largest barrier reef to dense broadleaf forest and broad inland lagoons without ever facing a long drive. English is the official language, distances are short, and the protected-area network is one of the densest in the Americas. The country's flagship birds are spectacular and accessible: the towering Jabiru, the largest flying bird in the Americas, stalks the northern wetlands, while flocks of Scarlet Macaws cross the southern foothills. For first-time tropical birders, Belize is among the gentlest introductions the New World offers.

## Crooked Tree Wildlife Sanctuary

This network of lagoons, marshes, and savanna northwest of Belize City is the country's premier wetland reserve and a Ramsar site of international importance. In the dry season from February to May, falling water levels concentrate astonishing numbers of waterbirds: Jabirus, Wood Storks, Boat-billed Herons, and Black-collared Hawks gather around the shrinking pools. The village sits on a causeway across the lagoon, and dawn boat trips through the channels are the classic way to experience it. Limpkins call from the reeds, Northern Jacanas pick across the lilies, and Snail Kites quarter the open water.

## Chan Chich Lodge and Gallon Jug

Set in private forest reserves in the remote northwest near the Guatemalan border, Chan Chich is built within a Maya plaza and is one of the finest lowland forest birding lodges in Central America. The surrounding broadleaf forest holds Ocellated Turkey, Great Curassow, Crested Guan, and a strong cast of trogons, woodcreepers, and antbirds. Ant-swarm attendants, tinamous, and the occasional King Vulture reward patient mornings on the trails, and the area is also famous for its mammals, including jaguar.

## Red Bank and the Scarlet Macaws

The Maya village of Red Bank in the Stann Creek District is the most reliable place in Belize to see Scarlet Macaws. Between roughly December and March, the birds gather to feed on the fruiting annatto and polewood trees in the surrounding hills, and a community-led conservation programme guides visitors to the feeding sites. Seeing dozens of these vivid red-and-blue parrots wheeling over the forest is one of the great spectacles of the Belizean birding calendar.

## Cockscomb Basin Wildlife Sanctuary

Famous as the world's first jaguar reserve, Cockscomb is also superb broadleaf forest birding in the foothills of the Maya Mountains. The trail network from the headquarters at Maya Centre reaches into forest holding Keel-billed Toucan, Scarlet Macaw, Great Tinamou, and the Stygian Owl, while the rivers add Sunbittern, Agami Heron, and a variety of kingfishers. The backdrop of Victoria Peak, Belize's highest summit at 1,120 metres, makes the setting unforgettable.

## Mountain Pine Ridge

The granite uplands of the Mountain Pine Ridge Forest Reserve, in the Cayo District, offer a completely different avifauna from the lowland forests. The open pine savanna and granite outcrops hold Acorn Woodpecker, Rusty Sparrow, Hepatic Tanager, and the localised Stygian Owl, while the area's waterfalls and rivers, including the famous Thousand Foot Falls, add Black Phoebe and the spectacular Orange-breasted Falcon, one of the rarest raptors in the region.

## The Belize Zoo and Tropical Education Centre

More than a conventional zoo, this celebrated facility on the Western Highway rehabilitates native wildlife and sits in pine savanna and forest that is itself excellent for birding. Night walks here are among the best chances in the country for Yucatan Poorwill, Common Pauraque, and several owl species, while the surrounding habitat holds Vermilion Flycatcher, Yellow-headed Parrot, and Grey-headed Kite. It makes an easy and rewarding first stop after arrival.

## Caracol and the Chiquibul

Deep in the Chiquibul Forest, the vast Maya city of Caracol combines spectacular archaeology with rich forest birding. The road in passes through pine ridge and broadleaf forest, and the ruins themselves are excellent for canopy species: Keel-billed Motmot, Tody Motmot, and a range of trogons and woodpeckers. Ocellated Turkeys often stroll the plazas, and the surrounding Chiquibul holds some of the most intact forest in Central America.

## Ambergris Caye and the Northern Cayes

The largest of Belize's offshore islands offers coastal and mangrove birding to complement the reef. The lagoons and mangroves of the Bacalar Chico National Park at the island's northern tip hold Reddish Egret, Roseate Spoonbill, and a range of herons and shorebirds, while migration brings warblers and raptors funnelling down the coast. Combining snorkelling on the barrier reef with mangrove birding makes the cayes a relaxed counterpoint to the mainland forests.

## Lamanai and the New River

The Maya site of Lamanai, reached by a memorable boat trip up the New River from Orange Walk, combines archaeology with forest and riverine birding. The river journey itself produces Snail Kite, Bare-throated Tiger-Heron, and Northern Jacana, while the forest around the temples holds toucans, oropendolas, and the Yucatan endemic specialities of the northern lowlands. Black Howler Monkeys add a memorable soundtrack.

## Hidden Valley and the Maya Mountains

The forested ridges and waterfalls of the Hidden Valley area, near Mountain Pine Ridge, are a stronghold for the Orange-breasted Falcon and the Solitary Eagle, two of the most sought-after raptors in the region. The mix of pine, broadleaf forest, and cliff habitat supports a distinctive community of birds, and the lookouts over the falls offer the chance to watch raptors soaring at eye level above the canopy.

## When to Go and How to Plan

The dry season from late November to May is the prime birding window, with the best wetland concentrations at Crooked Tree from February to April and the Red Bank macaws gathering from December to March. The wet season, from June to November, brings breeding activity and lush forest but more challenging access on dirt roads, and the southern Toledo District in particular can become difficult to travel. Belize's compact size means a single two-week trip can comfortably combine wetlands, lowland forest, pine ridge, and the cayes. A local guide is invaluable for the skulking forest species and for reading the daily weather, and the well-developed lodge network means comfortable bases are available across every major habitat.

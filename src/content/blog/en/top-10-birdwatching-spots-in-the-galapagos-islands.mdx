The Galapagos archipelago, straddling the equator 1,000 kilometres off the coast of Ecuador, is the most famous wildlife destination on Earth and a place where birding feels almost unreal. Birds that evolved without ground predators show no fear: blue-footed boobies dance within a metre of the trail, mockingbirds investigate your shoelaces, and finches hop onto your boots. The islands hold around two dozen endemic species, including Darwin's celebrated finches, and host vast seabird colonies. Each major island has its own character and its own specialties, and a typical cruise or land-based trip strings several together.

## 1. Española (Hood)

Española, the oldest and southernmost island, is the seabird highlight of the archipelago. It is the only nesting site of the Waved Albatross, with almost the entire world population — around 12,000 pairs — breeding here from April to December. The cliffs at Punta Suárez hold Nazca Booby, Blue-footed Booby, Red-billed Tropicbird, and the endemic Española Mockingbird, while the Española Lava Lizard and marine iguanas crowd the rocks. The albatross courtship and the dramatic blowhole make this one of the great wildlife walks on the planet.

## 2. Genovesa (Tower)

Genovesa, in the remote northeast, is the great seabird island and a sanctuary feel apart from the rest. Darwin Bay and Prince Philip's Steps hold one of the largest Red-footed Booby colonies in the world, alongside Nazca Booby, Great Frigatebird, Swallow-tailed Gull, and Red-billed Tropicbird. The endemic Galapagos Storm-petrel swarms over the cliffs in clouds, hunted by Short-eared Owl that have learned to ambush them on the lava. Genovesa also holds the Large Cactus Finch and Sharp-beaked Ground Finch.

## 3. Santa Cruz and the highlands

Santa Cruz, the most populous island, gives access to the lush highland zone that most lowland-focused visitors miss. The Scalesia forest and the agricultural belt around Los Gemelos hold the Vegetarian Finch, Woodpecker Finch, and the rare Galapagos Crake, while the highland ponds attract White-cheeked Pintail and migrant waders. The Charles Darwin Research Station and the Tortoise Reserve combine giant tortoises with the most accessible finch diversity in the archipelago.

## 4. Isabela (Albemarle)

Isabela, the largest island, sprawls across five volcanoes and holds the most varied habitats. The mangroves and lagoons of Elizabeth Bay and the Flightless Cormorant colony at Punta Moreno and Punta Albemarle hold this remarkable endemic, which lost the power of flight in the absence of predators. Galapagos Penguin — the only penguin found north of the equator — breeds along the cool, productive western shores, and the highlands of Sierra Negra hold the larger finch suite.

## 5. Fernandina (Narborough)

Fernandina, the youngest and most pristine island, is a place of raw black lava and almost no introduced species. Punta Espinoza holds the largest Flightless Cormorant colony, alongside Galapagos Penguin, the densest marine iguana aggregations in the archipelago, and Galapagos Hawk patrolling overhead. The island feels primeval, a window into the Galapagos as it was before human arrival.

## 6. North Seymour

North Seymour, a small flat island near Baltra, holds one of the most accessible and spectacular seabird colonies. Both Magnificent and Great Frigatebirds nest here, the males inflating scarlet throat pouches, and Blue-footed Boobies perform their famous courtship dance right beside the trail. Swallow-tailed Gulls and Galapagos Sea Lions complete the scene on a short, dense circuit.

## 7. San Cristóbal (Chatham)

San Cristóbal, the easternmost island and the provincial capital, holds the endemic Chatham Mockingbird, found nowhere else, and the San Cristóbal race of giant tortoise. The cliffs at Punta Pitt are the one place where all three booby species — Blue-footed, Red-footed, and Nazca — can be seen together. Frigatebird Hill near the town is a reliable spot for both frigatebird species close to port.

## 8. Floreana (Charles)

Floreana, with its rich human history, holds the Medium Tree Finch, found only on this island and critically endangered. Punta Cormorant has a flamingo lagoon — Greater Flamingo feed in the brackish water — and a green-sand beach, while the surrounding scrub holds the Floreana race of mockingbird (now extinct on the main island but surviving on nearby islets). The lagoon also attracts White-cheeked Pintail and migrant waders.

## 9. Santiago (James)

Santiago holds extensive lava fields and productive shorelines. Puerto Egas and the Sullivan Bay lava flows hold Galapagos Hawk, American Oystercatcher, and the Galapagos Dove, while the tide pools attract Lava Heron, Striated Heron, and migrant shorebirds. The island's coast is excellent for the smaller endemic landbirds in the arid-zone vegetation.

## 10. Rábida and the smaller islets

The red-sand island of Rábida and the scattered islets such as Bartolomé and the Plazas hold concentrated wildlife in compact settings. Rábida's lagoon attracts flamingos, Bartolomé offers Galapagos Penguin at the base of its famous pinnacle, and the Plazas hold Swallow-tailed Gull, Red-billed Tropicbird, and the comical Galapagos Land Iguana. These small stops add seabirds and the endemic finches in easy walking circuits.

## Darwin's finches and the story of evolution

No bird group is more closely tied to a scientific idea than the Galapagos finches. The roughly 17 species — the exact count is still debated — descended from a single ancestral colonist and radiated to fill niches normally occupied by warblers, woodpeckers, and grosbeaks. The Large Ground Finch crushes hard seeds with a massive bill; the Woodpecker Finch uses a cactus spine or twig as a tool to extract grubs; the Vampire Finch of Wolf and Darwin islands pecks at booby skin to drink blood. Telling the species apart by bill shape is one of the great challenges and pleasures of Galapagos birding, and the long-term study of finch populations on Daphne Major by Peter and Rosemary Grant has documented natural selection in real time.

The mockingbirds were arguably even more important to Darwin's thinking than the finches. Four species occur, each confined to its own island or group: the Galapagos, Española, San Cristóbal, and the critically endangered Floreana Mockingbird. It was the realisation that each island had its own distinct mockingbird that first set Darwin questioning the fixity of species. Today the Floreana Mockingbird survives only on two small islets off Floreana, the subject of an ambitious reintroduction effort.

The seabirds are the other half of the Galapagos story. The cold, nutrient-rich Cromwell and Humboldt currents that bathe the western islands support the only equatorial penguin and the flightless cormorant, while the warmer eastern and northern waters host the great booby, frigatebird, and tropicbird colonies. The Waved Albatross, the Galapagos Storm-petrel, the Swallow-tailed Gull (the world's only nocturnal gull), and the Lava Gull (among the rarest gulls on Earth) are all endemic or near-endemic to these waters.

## Planning a Galapagos trip

Access is controlled and the islands are a national park; all visits to wildlife sites require a licensed naturalist guide. Most birders choose a cruise of four to eight nights, which reaches the outlying seabird islands of Genovesa, Española, and Fernandina that day-trips cannot. Land-based trips from Santa Cruz, San Cristóbal, or Isabela are cheaper and cover the central islands well but miss the remote colonies. The breeding seasons vary: Waved Albatross are present April to December, while the seabird colonies are active much of the year. The cool, dry garúa season from June to November brings the most active seabirds and the best marine life, while the warmer wet season from December to May is greener and best for the landbirds and finches.

Myanmar is one of the great frontiers of Asian birding. Over 1,000 species have been recorded — a total that reflects the country's position at the junction of the Oriental and Sino-Himalayan biogeographical zones, its enormous range of altitude from sea level to nearly 6,000 metres, and its relatively large areas of intact forest that have survived through decades of restricted access. The country holds significant populations of globally threatened species including Gurney's Pitta, White-browed Nuthatch, and the world's only viable population of Burmese Peacock-Pheasant. Several endemic species are largely known from Myanmar alone, and the avifauna of the northern and western ranges remains incompletely documented.

Access to birding sites in Myanmar has improved considerably since the 2010s, though political and practical conditions require careful advance planning. Several specialist tour operators have detailed knowledge of site logistics. The principal birding areas in Sagaing, Kachin, and the Chin Hills require permissions and are most safely visited with experienced local guides. The dry central zone, accessible from Mandalay, and the extensive coastal wetlands of the Rakhine and Irrawaddy Delta are more straightforward for independent travellers.

## 1. Hukaung Valley Wildlife Sanctuary, Kachin State

The Hukaung Valley is one of the largest protected areas in Asia, covering over 21,000 square kilometres of lowland forest and river plain in northern Myanmar. The valley holds breeding populations of Bengal Florican — a critically endangered bustard of grassland and tall grass meadows with an extremely fragmented global range — and is one of the few places in the world where this species can be reliably found. Hoolock Gibbon and clouded Leopard share the forest with an extraordinarily rich bird community. White-cheeked Hill Partridge and Blyth's Tragopan inhabit the forested lower hills around the valley edge. Access is possible but requires significant logistical preparation and guidance.

## 2. Crocker Range and Natma Taung (Mount Victoria), Chin State

Mount Victoria — called Natma Taung in Myanmar — at 3,053 metres is the highest peak in western Myanmar and the centre of the Chin Hills endemic species complex. White-browed Nuthatch — one of Myanmar's most sought-after endemic birds — is found only on the higher ridges of the Chin Hills, inhabiting temperate oak and rhododendron forest. Grey Peacock-Pheasant inhabits the lower forest zones. Rufous-throated Partridge and Rusty-naped Pitta are present in the dense forest. The summit ridge area in April and May holds an exceptional variety of Blyth's Kingfisher, Beautiful Nuthatch, and numerous warblers. The site requires a day of driving from Bagan and at least two nights at the mountain.

## 3. Hlawga National Park, Yangon Region

Hlawga is the most accessible good birding site from Yangon, approximately 25 kilometres from the city centre. The reservoir and surrounding forest hold a wide variety of lowland forest species including Asian Fairy-bluebird, Scarlet Minivet, and multiple kingfishers. In winter, the site attracts migrants from the north including various thrushes, flycatchers, and warblers. Greater Racket-tailed Drongo and Black-naped Oriole are conspicuous year-round. The park's walking trails along the reservoir shore give good views across the open water to the forest edge. It serves as a practical introduction to Myanmar's bird community for those arriving in Yangon.

## 4. Inle Lake, Shan State

Inle Lake in the Shan Plateau is Myanmar's most visited tourist destination and also a productive birding site. The floating gardens, reed marshes, and open lake water support a diverse waterbird community. Spot-billed Pelican visits the lake during the winter months. Purple Heron breeds in the reedbeds. Ruddy Shelduck is common on the lake margins in winter. Burmese Bushlark — a Myanmar endemic — inhabits the open grasslands on the plateau surrounding the lake and is one of the target species for birders visiting the area. The hill slopes above the lake hold specialities including Blyth's Leaf Warbler and Mountain Tailorbird in the forest patches.

## 5. Bago Yoma Range, Bago Region

The Bago Yoma, a range of low forested hills running north-south through central Myanmar, holds one of the most significant populations of Green Peafowl — a large, declining pheasant species increasingly squeezed by habitat loss across Southeast Asia. The dry dipterocarp forest of the range is the best habitat for Burmese Peacock-Pheasant in accessible terrain. Moustached Hawk-Cuckoo, Indochinese Roller, and Lineated Barbet are all present. The hills are approached from Bago town, and jeep-based access to the interior forest tracks is necessary for reaching the best sites. Local guides with knowledge of specific peacock display grounds are valuable.

## 6. Moyingyi Wetland Wildlife Sanctuary, Bago Region

Moyingyi is a large freshwater lake sanctuary approximately 150 kilometres from Yangon that is one of the most important breeding and wintering waterbird sites in mainland Southeast Asia. Spot-billed Pelican breeds in a significant colony on the lake. Painted Stork nests alongside herons and cormorants in mixed breeding colonies. Lesser Adjutant — a globally vulnerable large stork — is regularly recorded. In winter, the lake holds Bar-headed Goose from Tibetan breeding grounds — the species that undertakes the highest-altitude migration of any bird over the Himalayas. Pheasant-tailed Jacana is present in the floating vegetation year-round.

## 7. Hkakabo Razi Area, Kachin State

The extreme north of Myanmar, dominated by the Hkakabo Razi peak — the highest mountain in Southeast Asia at approximately 5,881 metres — is botanically and ornithologically almost unexplored. The few ornithological expeditions that have penetrated the area have found a rich community of Himalayan and Sino-Himalayan species at their southern distribution limits. Temminck's Tragopan, Monal Pheasant, and Grandala are among the high-altitude species known from the area. Access is extremely challenging and requires expedition-level planning. The area represents a genuine frontier of ornithological discovery, with new records for Myanmar continuing to be generated by each visiting group.

## 8. Tanintharyi Biosphere Reserve, Tanintharyi Region

The Tanintharyi region in the long southern peninsula of Myanmar holds the largest remaining block of lowland evergreen forest in mainland Southeast Asia outside of protected areas in Thailand. The forest holds Gurney's Pitta — a critically endangered pitta with a total global population of possibly fewer than a thousand individuals and a range now largely reduced to southern Tanintharyi and adjacent Thailand. The Pitta inhabits flat, wet, lowland forest and requires specific fruiting fig conditions to sustain populations. Other forest specialities include Banded Broadbill, Lesser Necklaced Laughingthrush, and a full community of hornbills including Great Hornbill.

## 9. Ayeyarwady (Irrawaddy) Delta, Ayeyarwady Region

The delta of the Irrawaddy River where it enters the Andaman Sea is one of Southeast Asia's great waterbird wetlands, though access restrictions and infrastructure limitations mean it is less birded than equivalent sites in neighbouring countries. Lesser Adjutant and Greater Adjutant both occur in the delta. Milky Stork — a globally threatened species with Southeast Asian distribution — is recorded in the coastal mangroves. Mangrove Pitta inhabits the mangrove interior and is best found at dawn. Shorebird diversity on the tidal mudflats is high. Spoon-billed Sandpiper — possibly the world's most critically endangered shorebird — has been recorded on the tidal flats here during migration.

## 10. Pegu (Bago) Wetlands, Bago Region

The network of small wetlands, rice paddies, and seasonally flooded plains around Bago town provide accessible lowland birding accessible from Yangon on a day trip. Bronze-winged Jacana and Purple Swamphen are conspicuous in the lotus-filled tanks. Painted Snipe inhabits the paddy margins at dusk. Oriental Darter dries its wings on dead trees beside the deeper water bodies. The roadside trees between Yangon and Bago are productive for kingfishers, rollers, and bee-eaters. Black-winged Kite hunts over the rice fields. The area also provides good views of domestic Asian elephant working timber at forest edge sawmills near Bago town.

---

Myanmar's ornithological riches are still being documented, and the combination of Himalayan foothills, tropical lowland forest, and extensive coastal wetlands makes it a destination where serious bird listers and general naturalists alike find exceptional rewards. The best months for most sites are November through April during the dry season.

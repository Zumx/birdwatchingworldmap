Drive out the long, fog-prone road to the lighthouse at the very end of Point Reyes on a still October morning, and you arrive at one of the strangest and most thrilling places in North American birding. The peninsula juts more than sixteen kilometres into the Pacific, a wedge of cypress windbreaks, coastal scrub, and treeless headland surrounded on three sides by cold ocean. In the isolated stands of Monterey cypress around the lighthouse and the old ranches, an eastern warbler that should be in the forests of the Appalachians may be feeding alone, hundreds of kilometres off course. Point Reyes is California's most celebrated vagrant trap, and its bird list, well over 490 species, is among the longest of any single site on the continent.

Point Reyes National Seashore lies in Marin County, less than an hour north of San Francisco across the Golden Gate. Geologically it is a fragment of crust riding the Pacific Plate, separated from the mainland by the San Andreas Fault that runs up the length of Tomales Bay. That isolation, and the peninsula's reach into the ocean, are exactly what make it so extraordinary for birds.

## Why Point Reyes Catches Vagrants

A vagrant is a bird that has wandered far outside its normal range, and Point Reyes catches them better than almost anywhere on the West Coast. In autumn, young songbirds making their first migration sometimes set off in the wrong direction, a phenomenon known as reverse migration or mirror-image misorientation. A Blackpoll Warbler or a Black-and-white Warbler that should be heading southeast toward the tropics instead flies southwest and ends up over the Pacific.

When such a bird, exhausted and disoriented, sees the green wedge of Point Reyes surrounded by ocean, it drops into the first cover it can find. Because the peninsula is mostly open headland and grazed ranchland, the few isolated tree groves act as magnets. The cypress windbreaks at the historic ranches, named alphabetically from A Ranch out to the lighthouse, concentrate these lost migrants into searchable patches, which is why birders walk them grove by grove each autumn.

## The Outer Point and the Famous Groves

The classic vagrant-hunting circuit runs along the outer peninsula. The trees at the lighthouse, the cypress at Mendoza Ranch and Nunes Ranch, the willows at the fish docks, and the Monterey cypress at the historic ranches all have reputations built over decades. Drakes Beach, the Chimney Rock area, and the cypress tunnel on the road to the old radio station are part of the same lore. On a good day in late September or October, a single grove can hold several eastern warblers, an out-of-range flycatcher, and a vireo or two that have no business being in California.

The list of rarities found here over the years is staggering: numerous first state records, Asian strays such as Dusky Warbler and Red-throated Pipit, and a near-annual scattering of eastern wood-warblers. The autumn rarity season at Point Reyes is a social event as much as a birding one, with observers comparing notes at the lighthouse parking lot and the Bear Valley Visitor Center.

## Seabirds and the Cold Pacific

The peninsula is as important for seabirds as for vagrants. The cold, nutrient-rich waters offshore, fed by seasonal upwelling, support enormous numbers of seabirds. From the lighthouse and from Chimney Rock, sea-watchers scan for shearwaters, including huge rafts of Sooty Shearwaters in late summer, along with Pink-footed and Black-vented Shearwaters, Northern Fulmars, and three species of jaeger. Black-footed Albatross range over these waters offshore, and pelagic boat trips from nearby Bodega Bay and Half Moon Bay reach them.

The rocky offshore stacks and the cliffs hold breeding seabird colonies: Common Murres in their thousands, Pigeon Guillemots, Brandt's and Pelagic Cormorants, and Black Oystercatchers along the rocky shore. Point Reyes is also a famed spot for watching the spring and autumn movements of loons, scoters, and grebes streaming past the outer headlands.

## Wetlands, Estuaries and Shorebirds

Beyond the dramatic outer point, Point Reyes embraces a rich variety of wetland habitats. Tomales Bay, the long narrow inlet along the San Andreas Fault, and the estuaries of Drakes Estero and Limantour Estero hold large numbers of wintering waterfowl, loons, and grebes. The mudflats and marshes attract shorebirds in great variety, from Marbled Godwits and Long-billed Curlews to Western and Least Sandpipers, and the area is a designated site of hemispheric importance for shorebirds.

Bolinas Lagoon, at the southern end of the peninsula, and the surrounding Audubon Canyon Ranch are known for nesting herons and egrets and for concentrations of wintering waterbirds. The freshwater of Five Brooks and the riparian corridors add forest and woodland species, so that a single day at Point Reyes can span open ocean, estuary, marsh, grassland, and coastal forest.

## Tule Elk, Raptors and the Wider Reserve

Point Reyes is more than a bird list. The Tule Elk Reserve on Tomales Point holds a free-ranging herd of this once nearly extinct California subspecies, and the open grasslands support wintering raptors including Northern Harriers, White-tailed Kites, and large numbers of Red-tailed Hawks. The peninsula's mosaic of habitats, protected as a national seashore since 1962, covers more than 285 square kilometres of headland, beach, forest, and pasture.

The reserve is also a place to appreciate how working landscapes and conservation coexist: historic dairy ranches still operate within the seashore, their windbreaks the very groves that catch the vagrants. Hiking trails such as the Tomales Point Trail, the Estero Trail, and the trails out of Bear Valley let visitors cover the range of habitats on foot, while the Palomarin Field Station of Point Blue Conservation Science, at the southern end, has run continuous bird banding since the mid-1960s.

## Explore on the map

Point Reyes anchors the central California coast as both a seabird hotspot and the West Coast's premier autumn vagrant trap, where the cold Pacific, the fog, and the isolated geography combine to produce birding found nowhere else. Timing a visit means choosing between seabird movements, wintering wetlands, and the thrill of the autumn rarity season.

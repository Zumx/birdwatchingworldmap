Ghana sits at the heart of the Upper Guinea forest block, a band of rainforest that once stretched unbroken from Guinea to Cameroon and now survives in fragments hemmed in by farmland and logging concessions. For birders, this makes Ghana one of the most rewarding destinations in West Africa: a single circuit can deliver the legendary White-necked Rockfowl, a wealth of forest hornbills, glittering sunbirds, and a long list of Upper Guinea endemics found nowhere outside this corner of the continent. The country is compact, English-speaking, and laced with reasonable roads, so a two-week visit can sample lowland rainforest, the moist Volta hills, the dry northern savanna, and a string of productive coastal wetlands. The birding here rewards patience and an early start, because the forest gives up its specialities slowly.

## Kakum National Park

Kakum, a short drive inland from Cape Coast, is Ghana's flagship rainforest reserve and the easiest place in West Africa to bird the canopy. Its celebrated rope-and-plank walkway hangs around 30 metres above the forest floor, level with the crowns where most of the activity happens. At dawn from the platforms you can scan for Yellow-billed and Black Dwarf Hornbills, Black Bee-eaters, Rosy Bee-eaters in passage season, Blue Cuckooshrike, and a roll-call of greenbuls. The forest understorey holds the secretive Forest Robin and trogons, while flowering trees draw in Fraser's and Buff-throated Sunbirds. Kakum protects roughly 360 square kilometres of moist semi-deciduous forest and is the most reliable single stop for canopy birding in the country.

## Antwikwaa and the Aboabo Track

The Aboabo section near Kakum, reached along forest tracks around the village of Antwikwaa, is where Ghana's most wanted skulkers are found. This is prime habitat for the Yellow-headed Picathartes — properly the White-necked Rockfowl — whose mud nests are built under granite overhangs deep in the forest. Birders walk in during the late afternoon and wait quietly near the rocks for the birds to return to roost. The surrounding forest holds Nkulengu Rail, the bizarre Long-tailed Hawk, and a difficult cast of forest babblers and illadopsis. Local guides manage access to the Rockfowl sites carefully, and visits must be arranged so as not to disturb the colonies.

## Ankasa Conservation Area

In the far southwest, hard against the border with Côte d'Ivoire, Ankasa protects the only true wet evergreen forest in Ghana and holds species absent elsewhere in the country. This is the place to search for the Hartlaub's Duck on forest streams, the White-breasted Guineafowl, Rufous-sided Broadbill displaying over the trails, and the diminutive African Piculet. Ankasa's higher rainfall supports a lusher, taller forest, and its bird list includes Yellow-throated Cuckoo, Long-tailed Hawk, and a strong selection of Upper Guinea endemics such as the Western Wattled Cuckooshrike. Facilities are basic and the tracks can be muddy, but the rewards for forest birders are exceptional.

## Atewa Range Forest Reserve

The Atewa hills near Kibi rise to around 800 metres and carry a unique upland evergreen forest perched on bauxite-rich ridges. The reserve is famous among ornithologists for the Blue-moustached Bee-eater and a community of montane-influenced species found at no other Ghanaian site. Birders walk the access road and ridge trails for Many-coloured Bushshrike, Yellow-bearded Greenbul, Tit-hylia, and the much-wanted Akun Eagle-Owl after dark. Atewa is also a vital watershed and the focus of long-running conservation campaigns, since proposed mining threatens its forests. An early start from the gate gives the best chance at the canopy mixed flocks.

## Bobiri Forest Reserve

Bobiri, east of Kumasi, combines a working forest reserve with a famous butterfly sanctuary and a comfortable forest guesthouse, making it an easy and productive base. The trails through this moist semi-deciduous forest produce Yellow-billed Turaco, Brown-cheeked Hornbill, African Pygmy Kingfisher, and a good range of woodpeckers and greenbuls. The relatively open structure of the forest makes viewing easier here than in the denser blocks further south. Mornings are best for mixed-species feeding flocks, and the night around the guesthouse can turn up roosting nightjars and the occasional owl.

## Shai Hills Resource Reserve

Within easy reach of Accra, Shai Hills is a savanna reserve of grassland, rocky inselbergs, and patches of dry forest that offers a complete change of birds from the rainforest sites. The reserve is good for Stone Partridge, Violet Turaco, Senegal Parrot, Double-toothed Barbet, and Blackcap Babbler, while the cliffs hold roosting fruit bats and the occasional raptor. Shai Hills is an excellent place to add open-country species early or late in a trip, and the short drives and clear sightlines make it relaxed birding. Olive baboons and antelope share the grasslands.

## Mole National Park

Mole, in the northern savanna, is Ghana's largest protected area, covering nearly 4,800 square kilometres of Guinea savanna woodland and grassland. From the escarpment-edge motel you can scan a waterhole frequented by elephants, while the surrounding woodland holds a rich savanna avifauna: Red-throated and Northern Carmine Bee-eaters, Abyssinian Roller, White-crowned and Black-billed Wood Hoopoes, Oriole Warbler, and numerous bush shrikes and hornbills. Walking safaris with armed rangers allow close birding on foot. Mole offers an entirely different bird community from the forests of the south and is essential for a full Ghanaian list.

## Tono and Tongo Hills, Upper East

The far northeast around Bolgatanga and the Tono irrigation dam carries Sahel-influenced species at the edge of their range. The Tono reservoir attracts waterbirds and waders, while the surrounding scrub and rocky Tongo Hills hold Fox Kestrel, White-billed Buffalo Weaver, Sun Lark, and Bush Petronia. This dry corner of the country gives birders a taste of the Sudan and Sahel zones, with species such as Speckle-fronted Weaver and Chestnut-bellied Starling. Birding here is best in the cool early mornings before the heat builds.

## Sakumono and Densu Delta Lagoons

The coastal lagoons fringing Accra are internationally important for migratory waterbirds, particularly between September and April when Palearctic waders pour south. Sakumono Lagoon, hemmed by the city, and the Densu Delta Ramsar site near Bortianor hold Curlew Sandpiper, Black-tailed Godwit, Greater Flamingo, terns, and large numbers of roosting gulls. These wetlands are under intense urban pressure but remain the most accessible wader-watching in the country, easily worked on a half-day before or after a flight. Bring a scope for the distant flocks across the open mud.

## Amansuri Wetland and Nzulezo

In the southwest, the Amansuri lagoon and its surrounding swamp forest form one of the most intact freshwater wetland systems in Ghana, reached by canoe from Beyin toward the stilt village of Nzulezo. The waterways hold African Finfoot, White-throated Blue Swallow, Hartlaub's Duck, and a range of herons, kingfishers, and bee-eaters along the channels. The quiet paddle through flooded forest is a memorable way to bird, with the chance of forest species along the banks and waterbirds in the open lagoon. The community-managed reserve combines birding with one of the country's most striking cultural sites.

## Explore on the map

Planning a Ghanaian birding circuit means linking the southern rainforests, the savanna of Mole, the dry northeast, and the coastal lagoons into a logical route, since the country's habitats run roughly south to north. The forest sites demand early starts and patient, quiet walking, while the savanna and wetland sites reward open-country scanning and a good scope.

Bangladesh is built almost entirely of water and silt, the delta where the Ganges, Brahmaputra and Meghna braid into the Bay of Bengal, and its birdlife reflects that watery geography. The country list passes 700 species, and a handful of them are among the rarest on earth: the spoon-billed sandpiper winters on the coastal mudflats of the south, the masked finfoot haunts the tidal creeks of the Sundarbans, and the vast freshwater haor basin of the northeast holds globally important winter waterfowl. Despite being one of the most densely populated countries on the planet, it retains pockets of habitat that punch far above their weight, and a determined birder can compile a remarkable winter list in two weeks. For those willing to travel by country boat, rickshaw and the odd long road transfer, it offers experiences impossible elsewhere in South Asia.

## The Sundarbans

The Sundarbans is the world's largest contiguous mangrove forest, shared with India, and its Bangladeshi portion covers some 6,000 square kilometres of tidal creeks and salt-tolerant forest. It is the global stronghold of the masked finfoot, a secretive, rapidly declining bird of quiet waterways, and holds eight kingfisher species including the Brown-winged and Ruddy Kingfishers, Mangrove Pitta, Mangrove Whistler, and Lesser Adjutant. Birding is done from boats over multi-day cruises out of Mongla, working the narrower channels in small dinghies at dawn when the finfoot is most likely to be seen swimming across; the same forest shelters the famous Bengal tiger, spotted deer, rhesus macaque and estuarine crocodile, so a cruise here is as much a wilderness experience as a birding one. Permits and an armed forest guard are required, and most visitors travel with an organised operator.

## Tanguar Haor, Sunamganj

Tanguar Haor in the northeast is a Ramsar wetland of around 100 square kilometres that floods into a freshwater sea in the monsoon and shrinks to scattered beels (lakes) in winter. From November to February it holds tens of thousands of ducks — Northern Pintail, Ferruginous Duck, Tufted Duck — plus Pallas's Fish-Eagle, Greater Spotted Eagle and the occasional Baikal Teal. The reedbeds support wintering buntings and warblers, and the surrounding hills add raptors quartering the open ground. Country boats from Tahirpur are the way to explore the open water and floating villages, and an overnight on a boat lets you reach the most productive open beels at first light, when the duck rafts lift in their thousands against the morning haze.

## Hakaluki Haor, Sylhet

Hakaluki is one of the largest haors in the country, a mosaic of beels, reed swamp and seasonally flooded grassland in the Sylhet lowlands. In winter it is a major site for waterfowl and raptors, including Steppe Eagle, Eastern Imperial Eagle and Greater Spotted Eagle, with large duck rafts on the open beels. Resident specialties include Black-necked Stork and various bitterns in the reeds. The haor's value rises and falls with the depth of the winter floods, so conditions vary year to year.

## Sonadia Island and the coastal mudflats

The mudflats and sand spits around Sonadia Island and the wider Cox's Bazar coast are the most important wintering and staging area in Bangladesh for the Critically Endangered spoon-billed sandpiper, of which only a few hundred remain worldwide. Dedicated surveys here each winter monitor the species alongside Nordmann's Greenshank, Great Knot, and Indian Skimmer. The flats hold thousands of waders at high-tide roosts; the spoon-billed sandpiper requires patient scoping among Red-necked Stints, and local conservation guides know the current flocks.

## Lawachara National Park, Moulvibazar

Lawachara protects a fragment of semi-evergreen forest in the tea-garden country of Sylhet and is the most accessible forest-birding site in Bangladesh. It is famous for the hoolock gibbon, the country's only ape, but the birds are excellent: Kalij Pheasant, Red-headed Trogon, Oriental Pied Hornbill, several woodpeckers and the elusive Blue-bearded Bee-eater. The well-marked trails near Srimangal town make it an easy introduction to South Asian forest birds, and the surrounding tea estates add open-country species such as drongos, bee-eaters and the occasional raptor perched over the manicured slopes. Srimangal, often called the tea capital of Bangladesh, is the natural base for the whole northeastern circuit, with comfortable accommodation and easy access to the haors and forest reserves nearby.

## Satchari and Rema-Kalenga, Habiganj

These two forest reserves near Habiganj protect some of the best remaining lowland mixed-evergreen forest in the country. Rema-Kalenga, a wildlife sanctuary, is particularly rich, holding Oriental Pied Hornbill, Greater Racket-tailed Drongo, Asian Fairy-bluebird and a good range of babblers and woodpeckers. Satchari adds Phayre's leaf monkey and is reliable for Red Junglefowl. Together they round out a forest-birding circuit in the northeast that complements the haor wetlands only a short drive away.

## Baikka Beel, Hail Haor

Baikka Beel is a permanent sanctuary within Hail Haor near Srimangal, set aside as a no-fishing zone, and the recovery of its bird and fish life has been a conservation success story. In winter the open water and lotus beds hold Cotton Pygmy-goose, Pheasant-tailed Jacana, Bronze-winged Jacana, and large numbers of ducks and herons, watched from a dedicated observation tower. It is the easiest place in the country to enjoy a haor without an overnight boat trip, and an early start gives the best activity.

## Char Kukri-Mukri and the southern chars

The shifting silt islands (chars) of the southern delta, such as Char Kukri-Mukri off Bhola, are dynamic habitats of mudflat, mangrove plantation and grassland. They host wintering waders, gulls and terns, and breeding herons and egrets, plus raptors hunting the open ground. These chars are remote and reached only by local boats, but they give a sense of the delta's restless geography and hold concentrations of waterbirds far from any disturbance.

## Practicalities and timing

Winter, from November to February, is overwhelmingly the best birding season: the haors fill with waterfowl, the coastal flats host their wader spectacle, and forest activity is high in the cooler, drier air. The monsoon from June to September floods the country and makes travel hard, though it transforms the haors into inland seas. Birding in Bangladesh is logistically demanding — much of it by boat — so a local guide is invaluable; outfitters in Dhaka and Srimangal, and shops such as the BD Birds Shop, can arrange trips, optics and the boats needed for the Sundarbans and the haors.

The Cape Floristic Region occupies the southwestern tip of Africa, broadly coinciding with the Western Cape province of South Africa. It is one of the world's six floral kingdoms, despite being the smallest, and contains more plant species per unit area than any comparable terrestrial region on Earth. This extraordinary botanical diversity drives high levels of bird endemism, because many bird species have evolved in tight association with the fynbos shrublands, protea heathlands, and renosterveld habitats found nowhere else in the world. The Cape Sugarbird, the Protea Canary, the Victorin's Warbler, and the fynbos-associated cisticolas are among the species entirely confined to this region.

Birding the Cape Floristic Region requires a different mindset from much of African birding. Densities of large, conspicuous species are lower here than in the savanna biomes of the Kruger region, but the endemics are highly distinctive and the scenery is spectacular. The combination of mountain fynbos, coastal lagoons, wheat-belt agricultural land, and residual lowland forest creates a mosaic that supports a wide variety of bird communities. The following ten sites give comprehensive access to the region's avifauna.

## 1. Cape Point and Table Mountain National Park

Cape Point is the tip of the Cape Peninsula, enclosed within Table Mountain National Park, and it provides spectacular birding amid dramatic coastal scenery. African Penguin colonies are visible at Boulder's Beach near Simon's Town and at Stony Point near Betty's Bay. The fynbos of the peninsula supports Cape Sugarbird, Orange-breasted Sunbird, and Cape Siskin. African Black Oystercatcher breeds on the rocky shores. The sea cliffs at Cape Point offer views of White-chinned Petrel, Cape Gannet, and various albatrosses on pelagic trips departing from Hout Bay. Table Mountain itself, accessible by cable car, has Cape Rock Thrush and Ground Woodpecker on the rocky plateau.

## 2. Kirstenbosch National Botanical Garden, Cape Town

The world-famous botanical garden on the eastern slopes of Table Mountain provides manicured access to fynbos habitats that would otherwise require hiking. Cape Sugarbirds are present throughout the year wherever proteas are flowering, and their long tail feathers and loud chattering song make them unmistakable. Sunbirds, including Orange-breasted, Southern Double-collared, and Malachite, follow the flowering sequences through the seasons. Cape Francolin walks brazenly through the picnic areas. The forest patches at the lower edge of the garden hold African Emerald Cuckoo and Olive Thrush. The garden functions as a convenient half-day introduction to Cape fynbos birds before more remote excursions.

## 3. Elgin Valley and Hottentots Holland Nature Reserve

The Hottentots Holland range separates the Cape Peninsula from the interior valleys and holds some of the most intact mountain fynbos in the region. The area around the Houw Hoek Pass and the Elgin Valley is productive for Protea Canary, which feeds specifically on protea seeds and is most easily found where proteas are seeding. Victorin's Warbler inhabits the tall restio sedge beds along mountain streams and is one of the more secretive fynbos endemics. Grey-winged Francolin, Striped Flufftail, and Neddicky are all present. The reserve's higher areas hold Verreaux's Eagle hunting dassies on the rocky faces.

## 4. De Hoop Nature Reserve, Overberg

De Hoop on the southern coast is South Africa's most important marine protected area and also one of the best birding sites in the Cape. The Vlei, a large coastal lagoon separated from the sea by a dune bar, attracts exceptional numbers of waders, flamingos, and terns. African Spoonbill and Greater Flamingo are present year-round. The fynbos behind the vlei supports Bontebok antelope alongside the bird community. The breeding colony of Cape Vulture in the De Hoop mountains is one of the southernmost in South Africa. The whale-watching from the coastal cliffs between July and November combines well with birding the vlei and fynbos.

## 5. Strandfontein Sewage Works, Cape Flats

The Strandfontein treatment works on the Cape Flats east of Cape Town may not sound like a premier birding destination, but the series of settling ponds has established itself as one of the most productive wetland sites in the southwestern Cape. Greater and Lesser Flamingos are often present in the thousands. South African Shelduck, Cape Shoveler, and Red-knobbed Coot breed on the ponds. Migrant waders including Curlew Sandpiper and Little Stint arrive from August through October. Southern Pochard and Maccoa Duck are regular. The site requires advance permission from the City of Cape Town, but access is routinely granted for birding groups.

## 6. Harold Porter Botanical Garden and Betty's Bay Fynbos

Betty's Bay on the Overberg coast is accessible on a day trip from Cape Town and offers the combination of African Penguin at Stony Point with excellent fynbos birding in the Harold Porter garden. The garden's stream gorge holds Verreaux's Eagle-Owl and African Wood-Owl. The fynbos outside the garden fence extends into the Kogelberg Biosphere Reserve, the most biodiverse fynbos remnant in the Cape, where Cape Sugarbird, Protea Canary, and Cape Rockjumper can all be found on the mountain slopes above 600 metres.

## 7. Agulhas National Park and Cape Agulhas

Cape Agulhas is the southernmost point of Africa, where the Indian and Atlantic Oceans meet, and the surrounding national park protects a landscape of coastal fynbos, limestone renosterveld, and rocky shoreline. The coastal lagoons near Struisbaai support breeding Damara Tern in summer. African Black Oystercatcher is common along the rocky shore. The inland fynbos of the park holds Banded Martin colonies in the breeding season and a good variety of warblers and cisticolas year-round. The sea itself is visible from high ground and passing White-chinned Petrel and Sooty Shearwater are regular during autumn.

## 8. Swartberg Pass and the Klein Karoo

The Swartberg mountain range forms the northern boundary of the Cape Floristic Region, and the dramatic pass road between Oudtshoorn and Prince Albert traverses habitats from semi-arid karoo scrub at the base to mountain fynbos at the summit. Rock Kestrel and Jackal Buzzard are both common at the higher elevations. Cape Rockjumper inhabits the boulder fields near the summit and can be located by its loud whistling call. Layard's Tit-Babbler, Namaqua Warbler, and Black-headed Canary occur in the scrub on the drier northern slopes. The pass itself gives access to otherwise inaccessible mountain habitat within a few hours' drive of Cape Town.

## 9. Rondevlei Nature Reserve, Cape Flats

Rondevlei is a small but productive urban nature reserve on the Cape Flats, managed by the City of Cape Town. The freshwater vlei and surrounding reedbeds support a breeding colony of Goliath Heron alongside Great White Pelican, African Darter, and various herons and egrets. African Rail calls persistently from the reedbed margins. Cape Weaver colonies are conspicuous in the trees around the headquarters. The reserve is small enough to survey thoroughly in a half-day and is an excellent introduction to Cape wetland birds for visitors staying in the city.

## 10. West Coast National Park and Langebaan Lagoon

The Langebaan Lagoon north of Cape Town is a large tidal lagoon protected within West Coast National Park and holds internationally important concentrations of Palearctic migrant waders from August through April. Curlew Sandpiper, Dunlin, Bar-tailed Godwit, and Grey Plover all occur in thousands. African Black Oystercatcher has one of its most accessible populations on the park's shoreline. The postflower season in August sees the surrounding West Coast wildflowers and Cape Cormorant colonies active simultaneously. The park's Langebaan village shore is the best public access point for viewing the wader concentrations at low tide.

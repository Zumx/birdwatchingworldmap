Israel is one of the world's premier migration watchpoints. Its position at the junction of Europe, Asia, and Africa, and its narrow land bridge role in the Palearctic-African flyway, means that an estimated 500 million birds pass through twice a year. The geography is dramatic: the Rift Valley running through the country, from the Hula Valley in the north to the Arava and Eilat in the south, creates a migration corridor that concentrates birds to a remarkable degree. In autumn, hundreds of thousands of raptors pass over the small country in a matter of weeks; in spring, pelicans, storks, and passerines cover the agricultural fields of the Jezreel and Hula valleys.

## 1. Hula Valley and Agamon Hahula

Agamon Hahula, a restored wetland in the northern Hula Valley, is Israel's most celebrated waterbird site. Up to 50,000 Common Crane (Grus grus) winter on the valley's agricultural fields and roost in the lake; the "Operation Crane" program provides supplementary food to cranes, reducing crop depredation and creating a wildlife spectacle. Common Crane and Greater White-fronted Goose (Anser albifrons) are the primary winter attractions; spring migration brings Eurasian Spoonbill, Great White Pelican, and Marsh Harrier across the valley in large numbers.

## 2. Eilat International Birding and Research Center (IBRCE)

Eilat at the tip of the Red Sea is one of the most important spring migration concentration points in the world. In April, hundreds of thousands of birds — raptors, waders, passerines, storks, herons, and flamingos — compress into the small coastal plain between the mountains and the sea. The Bird Watching Center at North Beach manages eBird data and provides an observation point; the IBRCE (formerly the International Birding Center) coordinates the long-running raptor migration count. In a single April day, it is possible to record more species of raptor than anywhere else in the Northern Hemisphere.

## 3. Yotvata Hai-Bar Reserve, Arava

The Hai-Bar reserve at Yotvata in the Arava desert reintroduces desert species and holds the captive breeding program for Asiatic Wild Ass (Equus hemionus). The bird interest is the Arava floor desert community: Lappet-faced Vulture (Torgos tracheliotos, Vulnerable), Houbara Bustard (Chlamydotis undulata, Vulnerable), Egyptian Nightjar (Caprimulgus aegyptius), and Yellow-throated Sandgrouse (Pterocles gutturalis) occur in the vicinity.

## 4. Negev Desert: Sde Boker and Mitzpe Ramon

The Central Negev plateau holds the richest desert bird community in Israel. Macqueens' Bustard (Chlamydotis macqueenii), Cream-coloured Courser (Cursorius cursor), and Hoopoe Lark (Alaemon alaudipes) are regular in the Negev scrubland. The Ramon Crater (Makhtesh Ramon) adds dramatic scenery; the rock faces hold Rock Hyrax and breeding Lanner Falcon.

## 5. Beit She'an Valley and Kibbutz fishponds

The fish pond complexes of the Jordan and Beit She'an valleys concentrate migrant waders and herons. Squacco Heron, Little Bittern, and Night Heron breed in the fishpond margins; on passage, the ponds attract Collared Pratincole (Glareola pratincola), Temminck's Stint (Calidris temminckii), and the full wader assemblage of the African-Eurasian flyway. The מרכז טיבוע ציפורים (bird ringing center) documented in the map database operates in the north of Israel.

## 6. Mt. Hermon, Upper Galilee

The Hermon massif at the Lebanese and Syrian border holds the most diverse mountain bird community in Israel. Sombre Tit (Poecile lugubris), Middle Spotted Woodpecker, and Syrian Woodpecker (Dendrocopos syriacus) are breeding residents; Alpine Swift and Crag Martin breed on the cliff faces. The ski area road provides access to the high-altitude zone.

## 7. Bet Guvrin National Park

The chalk grasslands and ancient cave systems of Bet Guvrin in the Judean foothills provide breeding Lesser Kestrel (Falco naumanni, Vulnerable), European Roller (Coracias garrulus), and European Bee-eater (Merops apiaster) in the scrub edges. The almond orchards on the approach hold Spectacled Warbler (Curruca conspicillata) and Sardinian Warbler (Curruca melanocephala).

## 8. Jerusalem Forest

The Jerusalem mixed forest provides accessible city-edge birding. Syrian Woodpecker, Short-toed Treecreeper (Certhia brachydactyla), and Eurasian Jay (Garrulus glandarius) are regular in the forest sections; the viewpoints over the Judean desert provide occasional passing raptors.

## 9. Nizzana area, Western Negev

The Nizzana scrubland on the Egyptian border holds the most reliable Israeli breeding records of Cream-coloured Courser, Bar-tailed Lark (Ammomanes cinctura), and Desert Lark (Ammomanes deserti) in classic Negev scrub desert.

## 10. Haifa coast and fishponds, Carmel

The Mediterranean coast north of Haifa and the seasonal fishponds at Maagan Michael hold Mediterranean seabirds including Audouin's Gull (Ichthyaetus audouinii, Least Concern after recovery), Sandwich Tern, and regular passage Scopoli's Shearwater (Calonectris diomedea). The Carmel ridge above Haifa provides spring raptor migration views.

## Planning an Israel birding trip

Israel's position at the junction of three continents and the compressed geography of its land bridge makes it one of the world's most extraordinary migration watchpoints. Over 500 million birds pass through Israel on migration each year — raptors, waders, storks, and passerines funnelling through the narrow land corridor between the Mediterranean and the Arabian Desert. The country's small size (just 22,000 square kilometres) means that a single week can cover all the major sites by car.

The International Birding and Research Centre in Eilat (IBRCE) coordinates migration counts and provides visitor facilities at the Eilat Bird Sanctuary — a crucial stop for birds following the Red Sea and Jordan Valley corridor. The spring migration season (mid-March to early May) is the world's largest land-bird migration event outside Africa, with counts of over 1.5 million raptors in a single spring including Honey Buzzard, Steppe Buzzard (Buteo buteo vulpinus), Levant Sparrowhawk (Accipiter brevipes), and Black Kite passing over Eilat and the Negev.

The Hula Valley in the north holds the most famous crane gathering in the Western Palearctic. The Israel Nature and Parks Authority manages crane-watching platforms at Agmon HaHula lake, where up to 50,000 Common Cranes roost from November to March. The Hula Valley flamingo and pelican colonies are active from spring through autumn.

Tel Aviv provides the most practical entry point. Car rental is the most efficient transport option given the small country size; all major sites are within a four-hour drive of Tel Aviv. Jerusalem's Judean Hills and the Dead Sea cliffs add the desert raptor community — Long-legged Buzzard (Buteo rufinus), Bonelli's Eagle, and Egyptian Vulture (Neophron percnopterus, Endangered) — within day-trip range of the capital.

The birding infrastructure is well-organized: SPNI (Society for the Protection of Nature in Israel) maintains the principal nature reserves and can provide guide contacts for the specialist sites.

## Raptor migration at the Bosphorus and beyond

The spring raptor migration over Israel is part of the broader African-Eurasian migration system that concentrates raptors at the Bosphorus in Istanbul (autumn), the Eilat corridor (spring), and the Suez Canal crossing (autumn). The International Birding and Research Centre Eilat (IBRCE) coordinates the spring count, publishing daily totals and species breakdowns online during the peak period of mid-March to late April. The most extraordinary single-day counts at Eilat have exceeded 30,000 Steppe Buzzard in a single morning, with Honey Buzzard, Black Kite, and Short-toed Eagle adding to the raptor diversity.

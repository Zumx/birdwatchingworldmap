Skomer Island sits roughly a kilometre off the Marloes Peninsula in Pembrokeshire, southwest Wales, separated from the mainland by the fierce tidal race of Jack Sound. From late April to late July it becomes one of the most concentrated seabird spectacles in the northern hemisphere. The boat from Martin's Haven takes only fifteen minutes, but it deposits you onto an island that holds a globally significant fraction of the world's Manx shearwaters, tens of thousands of puffins, and dense cliff-ledge colonies of razorbills and guillemots. The noise, the smell of guano and bruised bluebells, and the sheer density of birds make a first landing genuinely overwhelming.

## Getting There and the Landing System

Skomer is managed by the Wildlife Trust of South and West Wales, and access is tightly controlled to protect the breeding colonies. Day boats run from Martin's Haven, typically from April through to the end of September, weather and the notorious swell of Jack Sound permitting. Numbers are capped, and in peak season day tickets are pre-booked online and sell out quickly. The island has a small number of self-catering hostel beds in the old farm buildings, and an overnight stay is the only way to witness the Manx shearwater arrival after dark, which is the island's defining experience. Visitors land at North Haven, climb the steps past the puffin burrows, and follow a fixed network of paths; straying off them risks collapsing burrows underfoot.

## Puffins at Arm's Length

The Atlantic puffins of North Haven and the Wick are Skomer's most beloved residents, and roughly twenty-five thousand pairs breed across the island. Because they have nested here undisturbed for generations and the island has no ground predators such as rats or foxes, the puffins are extraordinarily tame. They land within a metre or two of the path, beaks crammed with sandeels, pausing before diving into their burrows. The best light is early morning and late evening, when the birds gather in greatest numbers and the low sun catches the orange of their bills and feet. Puffins are present from roughly early April to late July; by early August the adults and fledged young have departed for the open Atlantic, and the island falls comparatively quiet.

## The Manx Shearwater Phenomenon

Skomer's true international importance lies underground and after dark. The island, together with neighbouring Skokholm, holds the largest Manx shearwater colony in the world — somewhere in the region of three hundred and fifty thousand pairs. These slender, stiff-winged tubenoses spend the day far out at sea, returning only under cover of darkness to avoid the great black-backed gulls that patrol the cliffs. On a moonless night the air fills with their eerie, cackling, almost demonic calls as birds crash-land near their burrows and answer mates calling from underground. To experience it you must stay overnight. The shearwaters that breed here undertake one of the longest migrations of any British bird, wintering off the coast of Argentina and Brazil and returning across the Atlantic each spring.

## Razorbills, Guillemots and the Cliff Ledges

The northern and western cliffs, particularly around the Wick, hold tightly packed colonies of guillemots and razorbills. Guillemots line the open ledges in dense ranks, while razorbills favour crevices and boulder fields, their heavier bills and blacker plumage distinguishing them at close range. The Wick is a natural amphitheatre where thousands of birds wheel and call, and it is one of the easiest places in Britain to study the two auks side by side. Kittiwakes nest on the sheerest faces, and fulmars — gliding stiff-winged along the updraughts — occupy the grassier slopes above. Below, shags dry their wings on the rocks and the occasional seal hauls out in the coves.

## Beyond the Seabirds

Skomer is far more than its auks. It holds a healthy population of the endemic Skomer vole, a distinct subspecies of the bank vole found nowhere else, which in turn supports breeding short-eared owls that hunt over the bracken by day — an unusual chance to watch this species in daylight. Choughs, the red-billed, red-legged crows of the western coasts, breed on the cliffs and forage on the cropped turf. In spring the plateau erupts into a carpet of bluebells, red campion and sea campion, a display that rivals the birds for sheer impact. Peregrines hunt the island, and Manx shearwater carcasses scattered near the burrows reveal the toll taken by the predatory gulls each night.

## Best Time to Visit and What to Expect

The window for the full experience is narrow. May and June are the peak, with puffins carrying fish, auks crammed onto the ledges, and the bluebells at their height. By mid-July the puffins begin to thin out, and after the end of July the cliffs empty rapidly as the auks take their flightless young to sea. The crossing from Martin's Haven is at the mercy of the weather, and sailings are frequently cancelled by swell even on calm-looking days, so flexibility is essential. Bring sturdy footwear for the uneven paths, sun protection for the exposed plateau, and a camera with a moderate telephoto — the puffins are so close that enormous lenses are unnecessary. Binoculars in the 8x42 range are ideal for scanning the cliff colonies.

## Conservation and Responsible Visiting

The island's value rests on the strict protection of its burrows and ground-nesting birds. Visitors are asked to keep to the marked paths at all times, to keep noise down near the colonies, and never to attempt to handle birds. The shearwater burrows in particular are fragile and easily collapsed. The Wildlife Trust's wardens conduct long-term monitoring of the colonies, and the island's data on shearwater and puffin breeding success feeds into wider understanding of how North Atlantic seabirds are faring against pressures from changing fish stocks, warming seas and plastic pollution. A visit here is a privilege earned through that careful stewardship.

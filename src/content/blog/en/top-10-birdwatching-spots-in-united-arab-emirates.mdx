The United Arab Emirates sits at the southeastern corner of the Arabian Peninsula, bordered by the Arabian Gulf to the west and the Gulf of Oman to the east, with the Hajar Mountains rising on the Oman border. This compact country of approximately 83,000 square kilometres occupies a position of extraordinary biogeographical importance. It sits at the intersection of three major migratory flyways: the Eastern African-Western Eurasian, the Central Asian, and the East Asian. During spring and autumn migration, species from Siberia, Central Asia, East Africa, and the Indian subcontinent all pass through the UAE, creating a diversity of migrant species that belies the country's small size.

Resident bird diversity reflects the country's position at the edge of the Arabian desert. Desert-adapted larks, wheatears, and coursers inhabit the dune systems. The mangroves along the Abu Dhabi coast hold White-collared Kingfisher and nesting Western Reef Heron. Socotra Cormorant, one of the world's most numerous seabirds, breeds on offshore islands. Over 500 species have been recorded in the UAE, a number that continues to grow as more observers cover the country's varied habitats. The following ten sites represent the best birding locations across the Emirates.

## 1. Al Qudra Lakes, Dubai

Al Qudra is a series of artificial lakes created within the desert south of Dubai, and it has become one of the most productive inland birding sites in the UAE. The combination of freshwater and the surrounding desert scrub attracts migrant species that would otherwise pass over the empty desert. Greater Flamingo and Eurasian Spoonbill are regular. Ferruginous Duck, Garganey, and various surface-feeding ducks use the lakes during migration. The scrub surrounding the lakes is consistently good for migrant warblers including Booted Warbler, Sykes's Warbler, and Eastern Olivaceous Warbler. Desert Owl inhabits the larger dune areas beyond the lakes.

## 2. Khor Kalba, Sharjah

Khor Kalba on the Gulf of Oman coast in Sharjah holds the last remaining mangrove stand on the eastern coast of the UAE and the most northerly mangroves in all of Asia. The White-collared Kingfisher breeding here is the most important population of this species in the UAE. The mangrove interior is accessible by kayak and on foot along the boardwalk maintained by the Environment and Protected Areas Authority. Reef Heron hunts the channel edges. The tidal mudflats outside the mangrove attract large numbers of migrant waders including Greater Sand Plover, Curlew Sandpiper, and Little Stint from August through October. The mountain backdrop of the Hajar range makes the site visually striking.

## 3. Sabkhat Madinat Zayed, Abu Dhabi

The salt flat complex at Madinat Zayed in the Liwa region of Abu Dhabi is one of the best desert birding areas in the country. The sabkha habitat, with its sparse vegetation on salt-crusted flats, supports breeding Cream-coloured Courser, Stone Curlew, and Bar-tailed Lark. The Bar-tailed Lark, with its range centred on the Arabian Peninsula, is most easily found at sites like this where the salt flat grades into sandy desert. Houbara Bustard inhabits the open desert margins and while numbers have declined significantly, the species persists in the wilder parts of the Liwa crescent. Desert Wheatear and Isabelline Wheatear are winter visitors to the open salt flat.

## 4. Ras Al Khor Wildlife Sanctuary, Dubai

The Ras Al Khor intertidal creek at the head of Dubai Creek is a protected wetland in the middle of one of the world's most densely developed cities. The sanctuary holds a resident flock of Greater Flamingo visible throughout the year, standing in the shallow creek behind the skyscrapers of central Dubai in a scene of genuine incongruity. Western Reef Heron and Grey Heron are resident. The tidal mudflats attract migrant waders, and the creek is a reliable site for both Saunders's Tern and Lesser Crested Tern. Three observation hides are managed by the Dubai Municipality and provide viewing without disturbing the flamingos.

## 5. Al Wathba Wetland Reserve, Abu Dhabi

Al Wathba is an artificial lake complex east of Abu Dhabi city that was initially a wastewater treatment area but has evolved into a significant waterbird reserve. Greater Flamingo breeds here in one of the few UAE colonies, with several hundred pairs nesting on the island within the main lake. Little Grebe, Black-winged Stilt, and Kentish Plover all nest. The reserve's list of migrant species is exceptional, with records of vagrant marsh birds including Pied Wheatear, Brown Shrike, and numerous Palearctic warblers. The reserve is managed by the Environment Agency Abu Dhabi with a visitor centre and boardwalk access.

## 6. Jebel Hafeet, Al Ain

Jebel Hafeet is the highest peak in the UAE at 1,240 metres, rising steeply from the flat gravel plains near Al Ain. The mountain's rocky slopes are the most accessible example of Hajar mountain habitat in the UAE and support breeding Hume's Wheatear, which nests in rock crevices on the bare upper slopes. Eastern Cinereous Bunting is a winter visitor to the mountain. The road to the summit passes through a zone of acacia scrub that attracts Arabia-specific species including Arabian Babbler and Persian Wheatear in winter. The escarpment faces receive migrant raptors during spring and autumn, and Short-toed Snake Eagle is a regular overhead.

## 7. Sir Bani Yas Island, Abu Dhabi

Sir Bani Yas is a large island in the Arabian Gulf managed as a conservation and ecotourism resort by the Abu Dhabi government. The island has been extensively restored with native vegetation and holds a population of reintroduced Arabian Oryx alongside impressive numbers of birds. Socotra Cormorant breeds on the rocky offshore stacks. The island's wetland area holds breeding Purple Swamphen and Black-winged Stilt. Various migrant warblers and flycatchers use the island's vegetation as a landfall point after crossing the gulf, and the island has produced numerous rarities given its position. Desert Lark and Pale Rockfinch are resident in the rocky areas.

## 8. Flamingo City (Khor Dubai and Al Hamriya)

The northern Dubai intertidal areas around Al Hamriya and the connection to Khor Dubai support large concentrations of Greater Flamingo particularly in winter and spring. The mudflats here attract migrant waders in impressive diversity, and the combination of flamingos at close range with resting Dunlin, Ringed Plover, and Bar-tailed Godwit in the foreground makes the area good for both photography and identification practice. Lesser Sand Plover is numerically dominant among the plovers. Red-necked Phalarope occurs in small numbers during spring migration, often very close to shore in the sheltered creek water.

## 9. Al Ain Zoo and Palm Plantation Oases

The oasis palm plantations around Al Ain are traditional staging posts for migrants in the desert interior, and the combination of dense date palm canopy with irrigated vegetation creates a microhabitat quite unlike anything else in the UAE. Common Redstart, European Robin, and various Phylloscopus warblers use the oasis trees in autumn. Indian Roller is resident in the larger plantations. The Al Ain Zoo gardens are open to the public and effectively function as a botanical garden with excellent migrant passerine diversity during spring and autumn passage. Wryneck, Common Rock Thrush, and Spotted Flycatcher are regular autumn migrants.

## 10. Wadi Wurayah National Park, Fujairah

Wadi Wurayah in Fujairah Emirate is the UAE's first national park and protects the most important freshwater habitat in the country, a perennial mountain stream with pools and cascades flowing from the Hajar Mountains. The Wadi is the only reliable site in the UAE for breeding Kingfisher — the Common Kingfisher nests in the stream banks here, its range barely reaching the UAE at the southern limit. Hume's Wheatear and Pale Crag Martin both breed on the mountain walls. The wadi vegetation, lush compared to the surrounding desert, attracts migrant species seeking water and shade. Great Grey Shrike and Lesser Whitethroat are regular winter visitors.

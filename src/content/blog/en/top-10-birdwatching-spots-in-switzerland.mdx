Switzerland is a small country but a remarkably productive one for birding, with habitats ranging from Mediterranean-influenced Ticino in the south through the Central Plateau's wetlands and farmland to the high alpine zone of the Valais and Graubünden, where unique communities of specialist montane species breed in summer and shelter from winter. Over 400 species have been recorded, including a suite of alpine and subalpine specialities that draw visitors specifically seeking Lammergeier, Alpine Chough, Wallcreeper, and Rock Ptarmigan.

Switzerland's conservation record is mixed but improving. The reintroduction of Bearded Vulture — the Lammergeier — to the Alps, coordinated internationally since 1986, is one of Europe's greatest conservation successes. Over 270 birds have been released across the Swiss, Austrian, Italian, and French Alps, and the population has now established naturally breeding pairs throughout the range. The Rhine wetlands, particularly around Lake Constance and the Klingnau Reservoir, hold internationally important waterbird concentrations in winter.

## 1. Bernese Oberland Alps, Canton of Bern

The Bernese Oberland — the mountain country south of Bern between the Thun and Brienz lakes and the Bernese high Alps — is the most accessible alpine birding area in Switzerland for visitors arriving from the west. The Kleine Scheidegg area above Grindelwald gives easy chairlift access to alpine meadows where Alpine Accentor hops at the feet of hikers near summit cafes. Snow Finch breeds on the rocky slopes above the treeline. Wallcreeper inhabits the vertical rock faces of the Jungfrau massif and is best sought at dawn on the walls near Kleine Scheidegg. Lammergeier is increasingly regular, seen soaring above the high ridge. Ring Ouzel inhabits the rocky alpine slope margins. Golden Eagle nests on the Eigerwand.

## 2. Lake Constance (Bodensee), Cantons of Thurgau and St. Gallen

Lake Constance on the Swiss-German-Austrian border holds internationally important concentrations of wintering diving ducks. The western end of the lake — particularly around Ermatingen and Konstanz — holds tens of thousands of Tufted Duck, Greater Scaup, and Ferruginous Duck in midwinter, with the latter species present in numbers that represent a significant proportion of the European population. Smew is regular in small numbers. Great Crested Grebe and Black-necked Grebe breed in the marginal vegetation in summer. The Rhine mouth delta in the eastern lake (Rheindelta) is a major nature reserve with excellent viewing from the Austrian side. Red-crested Pochard is visible year-round.

## 3. Klingnau Reservoir, Canton of Aargau

Klingnau is one of the most important migration and wintering sites in Switzerland, a wide, shallow reservoir on the Rhine north of Baden created by a hydroelectric dam. The site is renowned for its exceptional wader and waterbird diversity during spring and autumn migration. Temminck's Stint, Broad-billed Sandpiper, and Pectoral Sandpiper are among the regular migrants at Klingnau's mud edges. White Stork gathers in post-breeding flocks in August. Osprey is regular during migration. In winter, the Klingnau area holds large numbers of Eurasian Teal, Pintail, and Goldeneye. The excellent network of footpaths and viewing points alongside the reservoir makes this one of Switzerland's most visitor-friendly birding sites.

## 4. Col de Bretolet, Valais

Col de Bretolet is a high Alpine pass in the Valais used by billions of migrating birds as a crossing point each autumn, and the Swiss Ornithological Institute has operated a migration monitoring station here since 1958. The pass is remarkable for the scale of visible migration that can be observed at close range — on peak nights in September, the station nets thousands of migrants including Pied Flycatcher, Garden Warbler, Blackcap, and various thrushes. By day, raptors cross the pass in significant numbers, and the surrounding alpine terrain holds breeding Wallcreeper, Snow Finch, and Ptarmigan. The station is open to visitors during the autumn migration period in September and October.

## 5. Valais Alps and the Derborence Forest Reserve

The Valais is the driest canton in Switzerland, sheltered from rainfall by the surrounding peaks, and holds the largest population of Eagle Owl in the country. The limestone cliffs of the Valais hold breeding Peregrine Falcon and Alpine Swift. The ancient Derborence Forest Reserve contains some of the few remaining areas of old-growth forest in the Swiss Alps, with resident Black Woodpecker and Tengmalm's Owl. Rock Bunting inhabits the rocky scrubland at lower elevations. Cirl Bunting reaches its Swiss breeding limit in the lower Rhône Valley. The combination of dry Mediterranean-influenced lowlands and high alpine terrain creates unusual species juxtapositions unique in Switzerland.

## 6. Aletsch Glacier Area and Riederalp, Canton of Valais

The Aletsch Glacier — the largest glacier in the Alps and a UNESCO World Heritage Site — is surrounded by conservation areas that include the Aletschwald arolla pine and larch forest, one of the finest remaining stands of this habitat in Switzerland. The forest is a stronghold for Citril Finch — a small, greenish finch confined to coniferous mountain forest. Three-toed Woodpecker inhabits the old larches. Hazel Grouse is resident in the younger mixed woodland. Above the forest, the glacier moraines hold breeding Snow Finch and Water Pipit. Lammergeier is regularly observed above the glacier. The UNESCO site's restricted vehicle access preserves the quiet that allows wildlife encounters at close range.

## 7. Fanel and the Neuchâtel Lakeside, Canton of Bern/Neuchâtel

Fanel at the northeastern end of the Lake de Neuchâtel is one of Switzerland's most important wetland reserves, with extensive reedbeds and open water protected as part of the Grosses Moos landscape. The reedbed holds breeding Bittern, Little Bittern, and Purple Heron alongside Great Reed Warbler. In winter, the reed margins attract Bearded Reedling — the characteristic long-tailed reedbed bird — in small parties. Smew and Goosander winter on the lake. The autumn migration through Fanel is productive for herons, rails, and warblers. The three large lakes of the Jura foothills — Biel, Murten, and Neuchâtel — form a combined wintering and migration staging area for significant waterbird numbers.

## 8. Graubünden Alps and Engadin Valley

The Engadin high valley in Graubünden, the largest and most alpine of Swiss cantons, provides a complete mountain birding experience across an extended altitudinal gradient. Nutcracker — the bold, spotted corvid of subalpine conifer forest — is conspicuous in the arolla pine forest above St. Moritz. The upper Engadin lakes hold breeding Goldeneye in the lakeside forest. Alpine Swift hunts over the high valley. Lammergeier is regularly seen from the valley floor, soaring on the warm afternoon thermals above the valley walls. In winter, the deep snow in the Engadin concentrates Siberian Jay and Crested Tit around the valley's habitation.

## 9. Neeracherried Wetland Reserve, Canton of Zürich

Neeracherried near Zürich Airport is one of the most productive small wetlands in Switzerland, a restored former mire that despite its position adjacent to a major international airport holds breeding Bittern, Water Rail, and Bluethroat. The Bluethroat — the White-spotted form of central Europe — breeds in the willow scrub here at the westernmost part of its Swiss range. Little Bittern is regular in the reedbeds. The reserve is most visited during spring migration in April and May when a wide variety of warblers, flycatchers, and waders pass through. The proximity to Zürich makes this a practical half-day destination for birders passing through the city.

## 10. Mont Vully and the Canton Fribourg Farmland

The gentle hill of Mont Vully overlooking the Lake de Morat holds one of the best populations of Red-backed Shrike in the Swiss Mittelland, nesting in hawthorn scrub on the vineyard edges. The surrounding farmland supports Corn Bunting — a declining species of open agricultural land that maintains populations in the relatively extensive cereal fields of Fribourg Canton. Wryneck breeds in old orchards on the lower slopes. The wetland at Chevrille east of Morat holds breeding Black-tailed Godwit — among the Swiss breeding population's westernmost sites. Lapwing nests in the moist agricultural fields. The combination of vineyards, scrub, and farmland creates a productive agricultural landscape mosaic uncommon in intensively farmed Switzerland.

Switzerland's birding calendar runs from the first alpine species becoming accessible with snowmelt in May through the autumn migration of September and October, and the important winter duck counts on the lakes. The country's rail and cable car network gives unusually good access to alpine habitats.

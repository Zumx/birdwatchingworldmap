Botswana is a country defined by water in a land of thirst, and that contradiction shapes its birding. The Okavango Delta, where a river dies in the sand of the Kalahari rather than reaching the sea, is the headline act, but the country offers far more: the seasonal flooding of the Makgadikgadi pans, the floodplains of the Chobe and Linyanti, and the dry Kalahari where bustards and sandgrouse define the open horizon. Over 590 species have been recorded, and many of the best sightings come from a quiet hide overlooking a waterhole at dawn. This is safari birding at its most rewarding, where birds and big game share the same lens.

## 1. Okavango Delta

The Okavango is the jewel: a vast inland delta where the Okavango River fans out across the Kalahari sands, creating a shifting mosaic of channels, lagoons and palm-studded islands. Mokoro (dugout canoe) trips bring you close to African pygmy goose, malachite kingfisher, African jacana and the slaty egret, a delta near-endemic. The prize is Pel's fishing owl, a huge ginger owl that hunts the quiet waterways at night and roosts in riverine forest by day.

## 2. Chobe National Park

Along the Chobe River in the north, the floodplains and riverine woodland support one of the densest concentrations of birds and big game in Africa. The river attracts African skimmer, which nests on the sandbars, along with carmine bee-eaters in their riverbank colonies, fish eagles and rosy-throated longclaw. A boat cruise at dusk brings elephants to drink while pratincoles wheel overhead, and the riverside hides give close views of waders and herons.

## 3. Makgadikgadi and Nxai Pan

The Makgadikgadi salt pans, among the largest in the world, transform after the rains into shallow lakes that draw greater and lesser flamingos in their thousands. Sua Pan and the Nata Bird Sanctuary on its eastern edge are the focus, with chestnut-banded plover, pelicans and a wealth of waterbirds. In the dry season the pans become a stark white expanse where the wet-season spectacle seems impossible, and Nxai Pan to the north adds grassland and Kalahari species.

## 4. Moremi Game Reserve

Moremi, on the eastern side of the Okavango Delta, combines permanent water, floodplain and dry woodland, giving an exceptional range of habitats in one reserve. The mopane and riverine forest hold woodpeckers, hornbills and the localised wattled crane on the floodplains. Xakanaxa Lagoon is famous for its heronries, where yellow-billed storks, egrets and herons nest in dense breeding colonies, best watched from the water.

## 5. Linyanti and Selinda

The Linyanti Swamp and the Selinda Spillway in the far north form a quieter, wilder counterpart to the Okavango, with reed-fringed channels and floodplain woodland. The area is excellent for African skimmer, rosy-throated longclaw and the rare slaty egret, and the open floodplains hold large numbers of waterbirds. Game densities are high, and the remoteness keeps the birding uncrowded and intimate.

## 6. Central Kalahari Game Reserve

One of the largest protected areas in Africa, the Central Kalahari is dry savanna and fossil river valleys far from any permanent water. This is the country for arid specialists: kori bustard, the world's heaviest flying bird, secretarybird striding the grass, and Burchell's and Namaqua sandgrouse flying to drink. After the rains the Deception Valley grasslands fill with larks, coursers and raptors drawn to the temporary abundance.

## 7. Savuti

The Savuti Channel and marsh in the Chobe ecosystem are famously unpredictable, drying and flooding over the decades as the channel shifts. When water is present, the marsh teems with waterbirds and raptors, and the surrounding savanna holds ostrich, kori bustard and red-crested korhaan. A hide overlooking a Savuti waterhole at midday in the dry season delivers a constant procession of sandgrouse, doves and the predators that follow them.

## 8. Tuli Block

In the far east, where Botswana meets Zimbabwe and South Africa at the Limpopo, the Tuli Block offers riverine forest, rocky koppies and mopane woodland quite different from the rest of the country. The area is good for raptors, including Verreaux's eagle on the cliffs, and riverine specials such as Meyer's parrot and the three-banded courser. The dramatic rock landscape makes it one of the most scenic birding areas in Botswana.

## 9. Lake Ngami

When it floods, Lake Ngami southwest of the Okavango becomes one of the greatest waterbird spectacles in southern Africa, holding hundreds of thousands of birds. Pelicans, flamingos, ducks and a vast array of waders cover the shallows, and the lake has produced some of the largest waterbird counts ever recorded in the region. Its level varies enormously year to year, so a flooded Ngami is a sight worth chasing.

## 10. Gaborone and Mokolodi

Even around the capital there is rewarding birding. The Gaborone Dam, the Mokolodi Nature Reserve and the surrounding bushveld hold a solid list of bushveld species, raptors and waterbirds within easy reach of the city. A well-placed hide at a waterhole here lets a traveller log a strong selection of southern African birds on an arrival or departure day, without venturing into the deep wilderness.

## Planning a Botswana birding trip

Botswana's seasons drive its birding. The wet summer from November to March brings breeding plumage, migrant species, the flooding of the pans and the green flush of the Kalahari, though some tracks become impassable. The dry winter from May to September concentrates birds and game at permanent water, making hides and river cruises superb, with cool, clear days ideal for travel. The Okavango is paradoxically at its highest flood in the dry season, when the distant rains finally arrive.

Maun is the gateway to the Okavango and Makgadikgadi, with light aircraft serving the remote delta camps, while Kasane in the north is the base for Chobe and the Linyanti. Self-drive is possible but demanding, requiring a fully equipped 4WD, recovery gear and experience with sand and water crossings; many visitors combine fly-in safari camps with guided activities. BirdLife Botswana is the national partner and the best source for current site access and conservation news, particularly for wattled crane and slaty egret.

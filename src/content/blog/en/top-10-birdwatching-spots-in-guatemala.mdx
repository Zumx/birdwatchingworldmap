Guatemala compresses an astonishing range of habitats into a country the size of Tennessee: volcanic highlands above 3,000 metres, dripping cloud forest, Caribbean lowland rainforest, and the dry interior of the Motagua Valley. The national list exceeds 750 species, and several of the region's most coveted birds — the Resplendent Quetzal, the Horned Guan, the Pink-headed Warbler and the Azure-rumped Tanager — are far easier to find here than anywhere else in their range. Add the spectacle of toucans flying over Tikal's Maya temples and you have one of Central America's most rewarding and underrated birding destinations.

## Tikal National Park

Tikal protects more than 575 square kilometres of Petén lowland rainforest wrapped around one of the great Maya cities, and the combination of accessible temple-top vantage points and rich forest makes it one of the easiest places in the Neotropics to see canopy birds at eye level. Dawn from atop Temple IV, with the canopy stretching unbroken to the horizon and howler monkeys roaring in the half-light, is a famous birding moment: Keel-billed Toucan, Collared Aracari, Montezuma Oropendola and parrots cross the treetops while Ocellated Turkeys strut around the ruins below. The forest trails hold Royal Flycatcher, Black-faced Antthrush, Crested Guan and, with luck, Orange-breasted Falcon nesting on the temples. Staying inside the park lets you reach the towers before the crowds.

## Biotopo del Quetzal, Baja Verapaz

The Mario Dary Biotope near Purulhá was created specifically to protect the Resplendent Quetzal and its cloud-forest habitat at around 1,500 to 2,300 metres. Wild avocado relatives in the genus Ocotea are the quetzal's key food, and the breeding season from March to June is the most reliable window to see the long-tailed males displaying. The reserve's trails also produce Highland Guan, Slate-colored Solitaire, Emerald Toucanet and Black-throated Jay. Nearby lodges along the road frame productive feeders for hummingbirds and tanagers.

## Lake Atitlán and the western highlands

Ringed by volcanoes, Lake Atitlán sits at 1,560 metres and is one of the most scenic birding bases in the Americas. The lakeshore and reed beds hold migrant and resident waterbirds, while the surrounding pine-oak slopes give Rufous-collared Robin, Bushtit, and Blue-and-white Mockingbird. The lake is also linked to the now-extinct Atitlán Grebe, a flightless species lost in the 1980s after the introduction of predatory bass and the loss of reed habitat, a sobering reminder of how quickly a localised endemic can vanish. From the towns of San Marcos, Panajachel and Santiago Atitlán, boat crossings and day trips climb into prime highland pine-oak and cloud forest, and the lake itself is worth scanning at dawn for grebes, cormorants and migrant ducks.

## Fuentes Georginas and Volcán Santa María

The hot springs of Fuentes Georginas above Zunil sit in humid highland forest on the flanks of Volcán Santa María. This is excellent terrain for Pink-headed Warbler, a Guatemalan-Mexican endemic of the high pine-evergreen zone, along with Rufous-browed Wren, Black-capped Swallow and Wine-throated Hummingbird. The steaming pools at the end of a birding morning are a welcome reward at around 2,500 metres, where mornings can be genuinely cold and the forest drips with epiphytes.

## Volcán San Pedro and the Horned Guan

The Horned Guan, a bizarre turkey-sized bird with a scarlet horn, is one of the most sought-after birds in the Americas and survives only in a narrow band of high cloud forest in Guatemala and Chiapas. Volcán San Pedro above Lake Atitlán and the slopes of Atitlán and Tolimán volcanoes are classic search sites. Finding it demands a pre-dawn climb above 2,500 metres with a local guide, but the payoff — a heavy, croaking guan clambering through mossy trees — is unforgettable. The same forest holds Highland Guan and Mountain Trogon.

## Cerro San Gil and the Caribbean slope

Near Puerto Barrios, the Cerro San Gil reserve protects very wet Caribbean-slope forest rising from sea level. The lowland and foothill rainforest holds White Hawk, Keel-billed Motmot, Lovely Cotinga and a rich mixed-flock community. The humid conditions support exceptional epiphyte loads and a different set of species from the highlands, making it a natural complement to a quetzal-and-volcano itinerary. The nearby Río Dulce wetlands add herons, jacanas and the chance of Agami Heron in quiet backwaters.

## The Motagua Valley dry forest

The arid Motagua Valley is the driest place in Central America, with cactus thornscrub that hosts birds found nowhere else in Guatemala. This is the home of the threatened Guatemalan endemic subspecies of various dry-country birds, plus White-bellied Chachalaca, Russet-crowned Motmot, and the localised Beautiful Hummingbird. The valley also shelters the endemic and critically endangered Guatemalan Spiny-tailed Iguana. Birding here is hot and best done early; the contrast with the cloud forests just an hour away is one of the country's pleasures.

## Los Tarrales and the Pacific slope

The Los Tarrales reserve, a working coffee and ornamental-plant farm on the south slope of Volcán Atitlán, is one of the country's best-organised birding lodges and the prime accessible site for the Azure-rumped Tanager, a Pacific-slope endemic of southern Mexico and Guatemala. Trails climb from shaded coffee through foothill forest, where the famous Hummingbird Observation Pavilion concentrates many species at close range. Long-tailed Manakin, Tody Motmot and Blue-tailed Hummingbird are regular, and the reserve is also a launch point for guided Horned Guan climbs.

## Practicalities and timing

Guatemala's dry season from November to April offers the most comfortable conditions, clearer volcano views and easier trails; the quetzal breeding peak in March to May overlaps the end of it nicely. The green season brings heavy afternoon rain but lush forest and active birds, and the boreal-winter months add North American migrant warblers and flycatchers to the highland flocks. Most birders base in Antigua or Guatemala City and radiate outward, combining the highlands (Atitlán, Biotopo del Quetzal), the Petén (Tikal) and the Caribbean slope across roughly two weeks. A typical first visit might spend three or four days in the western highlands for quetzal, Pink-headed Warbler and Horned Guan, then fly or drive north to Flores for Tikal, with the Motagua and Caribbean slope as add-ons. Antigua makes a comfortable, walkable base with strong guiding infrastructure. Local guiding through operators in Antigua and reserves like Los Tarrales is well developed, eBird coverage is growing fast, and the road network is far better than its reputation.

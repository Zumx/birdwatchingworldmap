Iran is one of the most underrated birdwatching destinations in the world. A country of enormous geographical and ecological diversity, it encompasses the Caspian coastal forests, the Zagros mountain chain, the central salt deserts, the Persian Gulf coast, and extensive inland wetlands fed by snowmelt from the Alborz and Zagros ranges. Over 530 species have been recorded within its borders, including several species with highly restricted global distributions and important breeding or wintering populations of globally threatened birds. The Siberian Crane, formerly wintering at Fereydunkenar in the Caspian coastal strip, is now likely extinct as a western population, but other globally significant waterbird concentrations remain, particularly at the great inland wetlands.

Birding logistics in Iran require advance planning — foreign visitors need visas that are obtainable but require patience, and local guides are strongly recommended for reaching remote sites and navigating regional formalities. Several specialist tour operators run well-established itineraries that combine the major sites in two to three weeks. The rewards are considerable: flamingos on desert salt lakes at sunset, Pied Kingfisher over the Caspian, Caucasian and Persian specialities in the Alborz forests, and raptors over the Zagros crags.

## 1. Lake Urmia, West Azerbaijan Province

Lake Urmia was once the largest salt lake in the Middle East and the sixth largest in the world. Although the lake has shrunk dramatically due to water extraction and climate change, the remaining saline waters still attract enormous flocks of Greater Flamingo — hundreds of thousands of birds during peak periods. Lesser Flamingo also occurs. The shallow brine shrimp-rich shallows support breeding flamingo colonies on isolated islands. Ruddy Shelduck, White-headed Duck, and numerous waders use the lake margins and freshwater inflows. Conservation efforts to restore water levels have had partial success, and the flamingo population remains the headline attraction for visiting birders approaching from Tabriz.

## 2. Fereydunkenar and Miankaleh Peninsula, Mazandaran Province

The southern Caspian shore in Mazandaran Province is one of the most important wetland areas in the Middle East and Central Asia for migratory waterbirds. The Miankaleh Peninsula is a narrow spit enclosing a large shallow lagoon, and the combination of fresh, brackish, and saline water creates extraordinary waterbird diversity. Dalmatian Pelican — one of the world's largest flying birds — winters here in significant numbers. Pygmy Cormorant uses the reedbeds. Greater Spotted Eagle and Imperial Eagle hunt over the wetland margins. Smew, Ferruginous Duck, and Marbled Duck are among the wildfowl recorded. The Caspian Sea itself provides sea-watching for Armenian Gull and Caspian Tern.

## 3. Khar Turan National Park, Semnan Province

Khar Turan is a vast protected area in the northern part of the central Iranian plateau, covering semi-desert steppe and saxaul (Haloxylon) scrub. The park protects Asiatic Cheetah and Persian Wild Ass, and the open desert habitat holds specialist bird communities. Houbara Bustard — a target species for many visiting birders — inhabits the flat plains and semi-desert. Black-bellied Sandgrouse drinks at desert pools at dawn. See-see Partridge is heard calling from the rocky gullies. Greater Short-toed Lark and Asian Short-toed Lark occur on the open plains. Steppe Eagle and Steppe Buzzard are common autumn migrants through this region. Visits require a permit from the Department of Environment.

## 4. Hormoz Island and Qeshm Island, Hormozgan Province

The Persian Gulf islands off the southern Iranian coast hold breeding seabirds and a distinctive bird community influenced by proximity to the Arabian Peninsula. Socotra Cormorant breeds on offshore islets in enormous colonies. Crab Plover is a common non-breeding visitor to the tidal flats of Qeshm — one of the most accessible sites globally for this distinctive species. Striated Heron and Western Reef Heron are resident along the rocky shores. In winter, the mangroves of Qeshm's Hara Forest hold Collared Kingfisher — at its Iranian range limit here. Osprey is a common resident. The Mangrove forests of Hara are a Ramsar Wetland of International Importance.

## 5. Shadegan Marshes, Khuzestan Province

Shadegan is a large freshwater and brackish marsh in southwestern Iran, fed by the rivers draining the Zagros Mountains. It is a Ramsar Wetland and one of the most important breeding sites in the Middle East for Marbled Duck and Basra Reed Warbler — the latter a globally vulnerable species with a restricted breeding range in Iraq and southwestern Iran. In winter, Shadegan holds large numbers of geese, ducks, and waders that breed in Siberia and Central Asia. Greater Flamingo and Dalmatian Pelican are regular. The marshes are reached from Ahvaz and are best visited during spring or autumn when both breeding and migratory species are present simultaneously.

## 6. Arasbaran Forests, East Azerbaijan Province

The Arasbaran Biosphere Reserve in the extreme northwest of Iran, bordering Armenia and Azerbaijan, contains dense temperate forests that are biogeographically part of the Caucasus. The bird community reflects this, with species more typical of the Caucasus region than of the Iranian plateau. Caucasian Grouse is present in the subalpine zone at the upper forest edge. Caspian Tit — a species closely related to Marsh Tit but restricted to the Caucasus and northern Iran — inhabits the deciduous forest. Middle Spotted Woodpecker and Grey-headed Woodpecker are both present. Roller, Bee-eater, and Hoopoe are conspicuous in the open areas adjacent to the forest.

## 7. Hamoun Lake, Sistan and Baluchestan Province

The Hamoun wetland complex on the Iranian-Afghan border is a system of shallow endorheic lakes fed by the Helmand River. When filled, it is one of the most important waterbird sites in the Middle East and Central Asia. Asian Houbara Bustard and MacQueen's Bustard are present in the surrounding desert. The wetland itself attracts Sarus Crane — the world's tallest flying bird — during migration; the Afghan population of this species passes through regularly. Great White Pelican, Glossy Ibis, and Ferruginous Duck occur in large numbers when water levels are high. The site's water levels fluctuate enormously with upstream conditions, and its accessibility from the Zabol area is complex.

## 8. Kavir National Park, Tehran and Semnan Provinces

Kavir is Iran's largest national park, protecting a vast area of saline desert, kavir (salt flat), and rocky steppe accessible from Tehran. The barren landscape holds a sparse but specialised bird community. Asian Desert Sparrow — a compact, pale desert sparrow with a restricted distribution — occurs in the saxaul scrub. Isabelline Wheatear and Rufous-tailed Rock Thrush are regular migrants through the desert in spring. Greater Sand Plover breeds on the salt flats. Cream-coloured Courser inhabits the sandy areas. The park protects Asiatic Cheetah and Persian Onager as well, and combined wildlife tours from Tehran visit both mammals and birds. Night drives on the desert tracks produce Persian Horned Owl.

## 9. Hoor Al Azim Wetland, Khuzestan Province

Hoor Al Azim is a transboundary wetland shared between Iran and Iraq, a vast shallow lake and marsh fed by the Tigris-Euphrates system through Iraqi channels. The Iranian sector was severely damaged by drainage during the Saddam Hussein era, but partial restoration has occurred. Breeding Basra Reed Warbler is the key species justifying conservation attention. The site also holds breeding African Darter — at its northern distribution limit — and large concentrations of herons, egrets, and waders during migration. Pied Kingfisher is conspicuous, reaching its eastern range limit in this region. Access is from Ahvaz.

## 10. Alborz Mountains above Tehran

The Alborz Mountains north of Tehran rise to over 4,000 metres and provide accessible alpine birding within two hours of the capital. The ski resorts above the Chalus and Karaj roads give access to alpine meadows in summer that hold breeding Radde's Accentor — a high-altitude accentor endemic to the Alborz and Zagros ranges. Alpine Chough and Red-billed Chough both occur above the treeline. In the forest zone below, Caspian Tit and Persian Nuthatch are resident specialities. In spring, migrant raptors pass through the mountain valleys in significant numbers, including Steppe Eagle and Levant Sparrowhawk.

Iran rewards birders who invest in the logistics of visiting — a private vehicle with a local guide is the most practical approach for covering the dispersed major sites across such a large country. Spring, from March to May, is the optimal season combining resident breeding activity with departing winter visitors and arriving summer migrants.

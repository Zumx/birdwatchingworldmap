Since 1900, the world has lost more than half of its wetlands. Drained for agriculture, filled for urban development, diverted for water supply, and degraded by pollution, wetlands have disappeared faster than any other major habitat type on Earth. In the 20th century alone, the loss rate accelerated as engineering technology allowed the draining of areas previously considered inaccessible. Inland marshes, coastal mangroves, floodplain lakes, and tidal mudflats — all the habitat types that waterbirds depend on for breeding, feeding, and migration stopovers — have contracted across every continent. The consequences for waterbird populations have been severe and are worsening.

The State of the World's Birds report, published by BirdLife International in 2022, identified waterbirds as among the most threatened avian groups globally, with shorebirds — the waders that use tidal mudflats and wetland margins — showing the steepest declines of any major bird group. In the Yellow Sea tidal flat system of China and Korea, where the combined reclamation and development since 1980 has eliminated more than 65 percent of intertidal habitat, wader populations dependent on this flyway bottleneck have crashed. Bar-tailed Godwit, Red Knot, Great Knot, and Spoon-billed Sandpiper populations using the East Asian-Australasian Flyway have all declined precipitously, with the Spoon-billed Sandpiper reaching the threshold of global extinction.

## The Yellow Sea Crisis

The Yellow Sea, enclosed between China and the Korean Peninsula, is one of the world's most important migration bottlenecks for shorebirds. It is the primary refueling point for millions of shorebirds migrating between their Siberian breeding grounds and their southern hemisphere wintering areas in Australia and New Zealand. The birds stop for several weeks in spring to accumulate fat reserves, feeding on the dense populations of bivalves, polychaete worms, and other invertebrates in the intertidal sediments. Without this refueling stop, the non-stop flight from the Yellow Sea to Australia would be impossible for most species.

The tidal flats of the Yellow Sea have been systematically reclaimed for agriculture and urban development since the 1960s in both China and South Korea. The total area lost exceeds 35 percent of the original intertidal habitat across the region, with the losses continuing into the 2010s. At particular sites the losses are more severe: Saemangeum in South Korea, a tidal flat of over 400 square kilometres that was the most important shorebird site in the country, was reclaimed by a seawall completed in 2006. Populations of shorebirds counted at that site declined by 80 percent within five years of the reclamation. The correlation between habitat loss and population decline at the Yellow Sea is among the best-documented cases of wetland destruction driving bird population collapse anywhere in the world.

## Migratory Shorebirds: The Flyway Problem

Migratory shorebird conservation is inherently a flyway-scale problem. A shorebird species that breeds in Siberia, migrates through the Yellow Sea, and winters on the coasts of Australia depends on the quality of habitat at every point along its route. Protecting the breeding ground without protecting the stopover sites does not protect the bird. This seems obvious, but the political reality is that conservation action in breeding and wintering areas is often managed by different national governments with different priorities, and the coordination needed to protect a flyway as an integrated system is enormously difficult to achieve.

The East Asian-Australasian Flyway Partnership is the institutional mechanism for coordinating waterbird conservation across the 22 countries of the flyway, from Russia and Alaska in the north to Australia and New Zealand in the south. The partnership has been instrumental in identifying critical stopover sites and advocating for their protection, but lacks binding authority to compel national governments to act.

The situation in the Americas follows a similar logic. The Atlantic Americas Flyway carries hundreds of millions of shorebirds between the Canadian and Alaskan Arctic and the coasts of South America. The Delaware Bay, where horseshoe crabs spawn in enormous numbers and provide the egg resource that Red Knots and other species depend on for spring refueling, is a known bottleneck. The collapse of horseshoe crab populations due to overharvesting in the 1990s triggered a major decline in Red Knot numbers that prompted successful regulatory action in several US states — a rare example of conservation intervention successfully protecting a migration stopover resource.

## Waterbird Breeding Habitat Losses

Beyond migration, the direct loss of breeding habitat is a major driver of waterbird decline. Colonial waterbird species — herons, egrets, ibises, pelicans — require specific vegetation or topographic features for nesting that have been degraded or eliminated across large parts of their former range. The Purple Heron requires extensive mature reedbed for nesting and has been eliminated from many European rivers and wetlands where reedbeds have been drained or cut. The Dalmatian Pelican, which requires undisturbed islands or floating reed rafts for nesting, has been restricted to a handful of sites in Eastern Europe and Central Asia where such features still exist in suitable condition.

Freshwater wetlands in sub-Saharan Africa have been particularly severely affected by agricultural expansion over the past three decades. The inner Niger Delta in Mali is one of the most important waterbird areas in Africa, supporting millions of waterbirds in the flood season, but agricultural water extraction upstream from the delta has reduced flood volumes significantly, shrinking the productive wetland area available. The Hadejia-Nguru wetlands of northeastern Nigeria, another critical site for waterbirds including the Black-crowned Crane, have contracted severely due to irrigation development upstream of the floodplains.

## Wetland Conservation: What Works

Despite the scale of losses, successful wetland conservation does occur. The Ramsar Convention on Wetlands, established in 1971, has designated over 2,400 Wetlands of International Importance covering more than 250 million hectares, providing a framework for national protection that has shielded some critical sites from development. The convention's effectiveness depends entirely on the willingness of signatory governments to implement protection, which is uneven, but the Ramsar designation has been a useful tool in conservation advocacy for many threatened sites.

Wetland restoration is increasingly recognised as a viable conservation strategy, not only for ecological reasons but because restored wetlands deliver flood attenuation, water purification, and carbon storage services that have measurable economic value to adjacent communities. The restoration of 1,500 hectares of tidal flat in South Korea at Suncheon Bay, completed in 2011, demonstrated that reclaimed coastal land could be successfully restored to functional tidal flat habitat that supports shorebirds within years of restoration.

In Europe, the Common Agricultural Policy reform process has created financial mechanisms for rewetting agricultural land adjacent to rivers and coasts. The Netherlands' Room for the River program, which deliberately restored floodplain functionality to the Rhine-Meuse delta, has produced measurable benefits for waterbird populations using the restored floodplain. The UK's Environmental Land Management scheme includes payments for wetland creation and management, providing an economic incentive structure for farmers to restore wetlands voluntarily.

## What Birders Can Do

Birding and waterbird conservation are connected in practical ways. The collection and submission of waterbird count data through programs like the Wetland Bird Survey in the UK, the International Waterbird Census coordinated by Wetlands International, and the eBird database contribute directly to the population monitoring that guides conservation decisions. Waterbird count data are the basis for the assessments of population change that determine conservation priority. Accurately recorded counts from individual sites add to a global dataset that would be impossible to collect without the voluntary effort of birders.

The loss of wetlands is reversible in ways that some other habitat losses are not. Peat can re-accumulate. Reed can recolonise drained land if water levels are restored. Tidal flat invertebrates can recolonise within years if sediment quality is adequate. The waterbirds are waiting to return if the habitat is there for them. That reversibility makes wetland conservation one of the more tractable challenges in the global biodiversity crisis — if the political and economic conditions for protection and restoration can be established in time.

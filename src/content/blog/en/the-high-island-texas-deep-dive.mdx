There are mornings in late April on the upper Texas coast when the small woods of High Island seem almost too full of birds to be real. A north wind has been fighting a band of rain offshore all night, and the migrants that launched from the Yucatán the previous evening, after crossing nearly a thousand kilometres of open Gulf of Mexico, arrive over the coast exhausted and into a headwind. They drop from the sky into the first trees they see, and those trees are the live oaks of High Island. Scarlet Tanagers, Rose-breasted Grosbeaks, Baltimore Orioles, and a dozen species of warbler crowd the branches at eye level, too tired to be wary. Birders call this a fallout, and there is nothing else quite like it.

High Island is not actually an island but a low rise on the coastal plain of Galveston County, southeast of Houston, lifted just a few metres above the surrounding marsh by an underground salt dome. That slight elevation lets trees grow where the flat coast is otherwise grassland and wetland, and those scattered groves are the only cover for migrants for many kilometres along the shore.

## The Science of the Trans-Gulf Crossing

To understand High Island you have to understand the journey that delivers the birds. Each spring, hundreds of millions of songbirds wintering in Central and South America funnel north toward their breeding grounds in North America. Many take the most direct route: a non-stop flight across the Gulf of Mexico, leaving the Yucatán Peninsula at dusk and flying through the night and into the next day, a crossing of around eighteen hours over open water with no place to land.

In fair weather with following winds, these migrants make landfall with energy to spare and fly straight on inland, barely pausing at the coast. But when a late cold front pushes south and they meet north winds and rain over the water, the crossing becomes a battle for survival. Birds that complete it arrive depleted and desperate for cover and food, and they concentrate in the first woodland they reach. This is the meteorological recipe for a fallout, and the upper Texas coast is one of the few places on Earth where it can be witnessed at close range.

## The Woods of High Island

The Houston Audubon Society manages several sanctuaries at High Island, and they are the heart of the spectacle. Boy Scout Woods, with its grandstand benches around a small clearing and water drip, is the classic spot, a place where birders sit and let the migrants come to them. Smith Oaks, the larger sanctuary, holds taller trees, trails, and ponds, and is famous for both migrants and its breeding waterbird rookery.

These sanctuaries are tiny by the standards of great birding sites, just a few hectares each, yet on a fallout day they can hold extraordinary numbers and diversity. The list of regularly occurring spring migrants includes Tennessee, Nashville, Magnolia, Blackburnian, Chestnut-sided, and Black-throated Green Warblers, American Redstart, Northern Parula, Indigo and Painted Buntings, Summer Tanager, and many more. Vagrants and rarities turn up most springs, drawing birders from across the continent.

## The Smith Oaks Rookery

While migrants are weather-dependent and unpredictable, Smith Oaks offers a reliable spectacle of its own. On islands in a pond known as Claybottom Pond, a large colony of wading birds nests each spring within easy view of a boardwalk and observation platform. Roseate Spoonbills, Great and Snowy Egrets, Neotropic Cormorants, Tricolored Herons, and others build their nests in the willows, and the islands keep them safe from the alligators that patrol the water below.

The rookery is a photographer's dream, with breeding-plumage spoonbills glowing pink against the green, courtship displays, and the constant traffic of birds carrying nest material and food. Unlike the fallout, which may or may not happen on any given day, the rookery is active throughout the spring, making Smith Oaks worthwhile even when the migration is quiet.

## Bolivar Flats and the Wider Coast

High Island sits within a much richer birding landscape. A short drive west, the Bolivar Flats Shorebird Sanctuary on the Bolivar Peninsula holds one of the great shorebird concentrations in North America. At the right tide, the flats teem with American Avocets, Black-necked Stilts, Marbled Godwits, Willets, plovers, and sandpipers, along with Reddish Egrets and large numbers of terns and gulls. It is a designated site of hemispheric importance for shorebirds.

The Anahuac National Wildlife Refuge, on the mainland marshes nearby, adds rails, bitterns, Seaside Sparrows, wintering waterfowl, and a chance at the elusive Yellow Rail. Together with the prairies and rice fields inland, the upper Texas coast in spring offers one of the densest and most varied birding experiences anywhere, with High Island as its famous centrepiece.

## Timing and Etiquette

The fallout season runs roughly from late March through early May, with the peak diversity usually in the third and fourth weeks of April. There is no guarantee of a fallout on any given day; the best ones depend on the unlucky combination of weather that pushes the birds down, and many fine spring days at High Island are pleasant but quiet. Patience and repeated visits are part of the experience, and seasoned birders watch the Gulf weather forecasts as intently as anywhere.

Because the sanctuaries are small and the birds tired, etiquette matters here more than at most sites. Houston Audubon charges a modest patch fee that funds the sanctuaries, and visitors are asked to keep to trails, avoid crowding exhausted birds, and refrain from playback. The reward for this restraint is the chance to stand quietly while a Cerulean Warbler or a Cape May Warbler forages an arm's length away, a bird that has just crossed an ocean.

## Explore on the map

High Island anchors the upper Texas coast, the most celebrated stage for trans-Gulf spring migration in North America, where geography, salt domes, and weather combine to bring the migrants to ground. Planning a visit means matching the calendar to the peak and watching the Gulf forecasts for the fronts that make fallouts.

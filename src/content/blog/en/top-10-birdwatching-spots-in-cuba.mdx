Cuba is the Caribbean's birdwatching jewel, an island with 29 endemic species, an additional range of near-endemics shared only with nearby islands, and the extraordinary distinction of being home to the Bee Hummingbird — the smallest bird on Earth. The island's relatively intact forests, wetlands, and scrublands, preserved partly by the economic circumstances of recent decades, support bird populations that would have been decimated by the development pressures affecting more economically active Caribbean islands. For visiting birders from North America and Europe, Cuba offers a combination of accessible endemics, spectacular wetland birding, and the distinctive cultural landscape of the island. ## 1. Zapata Swamp (Ciénaga de Zapata), Matanzas Province

The Zapata Swamp is the most important birdwatching destination in Cuba and one of the most significant wetland habitats in the Caribbean. The swamp covers approximately 4,500 square kilometres of freshwater and brackish marsh, mangrove, and flooded forest on the south coast of western Cuba, designated as a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and Ramsar Wetland. Three species are essentially restricted to this swamp: the Zapata Rail (Cyanolimnas cerverai), one of the rarest birds in the Caribbean; the Zapata Wren (Ferminia cerverai); and the Zapata Sparrow (Torreornis inexpectata). The latter also occurs at a few other sites, but the swamp subspecies is the most accessible population.

## 2. Las Terrazas Biosphere Reserve, Pinar del Río

Las Terrazas is a reforestation and ecological reserve in the Sierra del Rosario mountains of Pinar del Río province, providing access to relatively undisturbed forest in a pleasant landscape of hills, coffee plantations, and a central lake. The reserve is a reliable site for Cuban Trogon — the national bird — Cuban Tody, Cuban Vireo, Cuban Gnatcatcher, and several other endemics in an accessible, day-visitable setting within easy reach of Havana.

## 3. Guanahacabibes Peninsula National Park, Pinar del Río

The Guanahacabibes Peninsula at the western tip of Cuba protects the most extensive dry deciduous forest in Cuba, a habitat that holds several specialties including the Bee Hummingbird — here in one of its more reliable sites. The national park also holds Cuban Parakeet, Cuban Crow, and a good selection of resident forest species. The remoteness of the peninsula relative to Havana means visitor numbers are lower than at more central sites.

## 4. Viñales Valley and Sierra de los Órganos, Pinar del Río

The Viñales Valley in the Órganos mountain range of Pinar del Río, famous for its dramatic mogote limestone formations and tobacco farming, provides access to both the karst vegetation on the limestone hills and the tobacco valley forest edges. The endemic Cuban Bullfinch and Cuban Grassquit are common in agricultural edges; the limestone hills hold cave-roosting Barn Owls and the entire Cuban Trogon community.

## 5. Peninsula de Zapata Coastal Road, Matanzas Province

In addition to the deep swamp interior, the coastal road along the Bay of Pigs coast south of the Zapata swamp provides access to mangrove and coastal scrub with several additional endemics. Cuban Black Hawk nests in the mangroves; the beach vegetation holds Cuban Nightjar, and the dry coastal scrub supports Cuban Grassquite and Cuban Bullfinch. The town of Playa Girón (Bay of Pigs) provides a convenient base.

## 6. El Nicho, Cienfuegos Province

El Nicho is a scenic waterfall and forest reserve in the Escambray Mountains of central Cuba, providing excellent access to humid montane forest at 600 to 1,000 metres elevation. The forest at El Nicho holds Cuban Trogon, Cuban Tody, Cuban Solitaire, Cuban Vireo, and a productive community of tanagers and warblers. The site is one of the most aesthetically pleasing birding destinations on the island.

## 7. Caguanes National Park, Sancti Spíritus Province

Caguanes National Park on the north coast of Sancti Spíritus province encompasses a mosaic of mangrove, coastal lagoon, and dry forest on a peninsula jutting into the Bahía de Buena Vista. The wetland and coastal habitats support Flamingo, Roseate Spoonbill, and White Ibis alongside the mangrove birds. The dry forest interior holds Cuban Parakeet and Cuban Crow, and the site is less visited than the western Cuba endemics spots.

## 8. Playa Ancón and Trinidad, Sancti Spíritus Province

The forests around the colonial city of Trinidad, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and the Peninsula Ancón coastline provide good lowland forest and coastal birding in a historically rich setting. The Topes de Collantes protected area in the Escambray Mountains above Trinidad extends the birding habitat to humid montane forest holding Cuban Solitaire and other highland endemics.

## 9. Birama Swamp, Granma Province

The Birama Swamp at the mouth of the Cauto River in eastern Cuba is a large, relatively undisturbed mangrove and freshwater wetland supporting large colonies of herons and egrets and providing habitat for Cuban Black Hawk and Sandhill Crane — the Cuban subspecies of the Sandhill is resident in this wetland. The site requires more logistical planning to reach than western Cuba sites but rewards the effort with near-pristine wetland habitat and lower visitor pressure.

## 10. Gran Parque Nacional Sierra Maestra, Santiago Province

The Sierra Maestra mountains in eastern Cuba hold the highest peaks on the island and correspondingly distinct montane bird communities. The forests around Pico Turquino — Cuba's highest summit at 1,974 metres — hold Cuban Solitaire in its most accessible eastern population and the Oriente Warbler, a species restricted to the eastern Cuban mountains. The park is reached from Santiago de Cuba and requires a day's hiking to reach the best forest zones.

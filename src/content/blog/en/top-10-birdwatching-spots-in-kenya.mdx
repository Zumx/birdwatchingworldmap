Kenya sits at the intersection of East African, West African, Palearctic, and Southern African bird distributions, and the result is a country list that exceeds 1,100 species despite being smaller than Texas. The Rift Valley lakes provide some of the most dramatic waterbird concentrations on earth; the western forests share species with equatorial West Africa; the coast holds Indian Ocean island relatives. A two-week birding circuit can cover three or four entirely different bird communities. All sites are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Kakamega Forest, Western Kenya

Kakamega is the easternmost remnant of the equatorial West African forest that once extended continuously from the Atlantic coast. Its bird community is consequently a mixture of East African and West African species found together nowhere else in Kenya. Specialties include the Great Blue Turaco, Blue-headed Bee-eater, Cassin's Spinetail, Turner's Eremomela, and Chapin's Flycatcher — the last two rare and poorly known. The forest is accessible from the town of Kakamega; Forest Edge hotel and guided walks into the core forest area are the standard approach. Early mornings on the Isecheno trail network are most productive.

## 2. Lake Nakuru National Park, Rift Valley

Lake Nakuru is a shallow alkaline lake whose high pH encourages blue-green algae blooms that support enormous flamingo concentrations — Lesser and Greater Flamingos in the hundreds of thousands when conditions are right. Water level fluctuations have affected flamingo numbers in recent years, but the lake additionally holds the largest population of White Rhino in Kenya, large numbers of Pelicans, and a falcon migration including Amur Falcons in October. The surrounding Acacia and Euphorbia woodland adds Augur Buzzard, Verreaux's Eagle-Owl, and the Jackson's Widowbird display.

## 3. Lake Naivasha, Rift Valley

Naivasha is a freshwater lake in the Rift Valley accessible from Nairobi by two hours of road. The papyrus swamp edge holds African Fish Eagle, Grey-crowned Crane, and good numbers of herons; hippopotamus rafts punctuate the open water. Crater Lake Game Sanctuary on the rim above the lake has distinctive alkaline grassland with Kori Bustard and Jackson's Francolins. The Hell's Gate National Park adjacent to Naivasha is walkable and bikeable without vehicles, making it the most physically accessible large wildlife area near Nairobi; Lammergeier (Bearded Vulture) has been recorded from the gorge.

## 4. Arabuko-Sokoke Forest, Coast

Arabuko-Sokoke is the largest single block of coastal forest remaining in East Africa and holds three globally threatened species found nowhere else: the Sokoke Scops Owl (Otus ireneae), Clarke's Weaver, and Sokoke Pipit. The forest is divided into zones: Cynometra woodland holds the scops owl; the Brachystegia woodland holds Amani Sunbird; mixed forest along the trails gives access to African Crowned Eagle and African Broadbill. Dawn visits with a skilled local guide are essential; both owl species are audible regularly but finding them visually requires knowledge of current territory positions.

## 5. Aberdares National Park, Central Province

The Aberdares ridge above Nyeri runs from moorland at 3,000-plus metres down through bamboo and montane forest. Jackson's Francolins are tame and close on the moorland; the bamboo belt holds Mountain Bamboo Warbler, Sharpe's Longclaw, and African Stonechat. The forest at middle elevations adds Mountain Buzzard and African Green Ibis. The park's Tree Hotels — Treetops and The Ark — provide elevated viewing platforms over waterholes that are visited at night by elephants and buffaloes, and at which several difficult forest species are sometimes seen at close range.

## 6. Mount Kenya National Park

Mount Kenya's forest and alpine zone at 2,000 to 5,000 metres produces a distinct high-altitude bird community. Jackson's Widowbird displays over the moorland; Moorland Chat is common on rocky outcrops. The forest at 2,000 to 3,000 metres adds Hartlaub's Turaco, Narina Trogon, and Bar-tailed Trogon. Simba Col and Lake Ellis are the most productive upper-zone locations. The forest zone around the Sirimon Gate gives the most accessible general birding, with Kenyan endemic (or near-endemic) Montane White-eye and Abyssinian Crimsonwing in the understorey.

## 7. Tsavo East and West National Parks, Coast and Rift

Tsavo covers 21,000 square kilometres of semi-arid thornbush and is Kenya's largest national park. The Tsavo River in Tsavo West is the most bird-rich area: Rosy-patched Bushshrike, Peter's Finfoot on the river, and Von der Decken's Hornbill in the Acacia are park specialties. Tsavo East's open terrain gives the best views of Somali Ostrich, Vulturine Guineafowl, and the localised Somali Long-billed Crombec. Combined, the two parks represent the most extensive semi-arid birding habitat accessible from a road in East Africa.

## 8. Maasai Mara National Reserve, Rift Valley

The Mara is best known for the wildebeest migration, but the birdlife is consistently exceptional. African Hawk-Eagle, Martial Eagle, Bateleur, and Tawny Eagle are regularly seen; the river banks hold Giant Kingfisher, Woodland Kingfisher, and Wire-tailed Swallow. The open grassland holds Secretarybird, Kori Bustard, and, during the rains, large flocks of waders on flooded sections. The short grass plains adjacent to the Mara River are the most reliable location in Kenya for Denham's Bustard and the migrant Montagu's and Pallid Harriers.

## 9. Saiwa Swamp National Park, Western Kenya

Saiwa is Kenya's smallest national park, protecting a papyrus swamp inland from Kitale. It is the only reliable site in Kenya for Sitatunga antelope, which is a bonus; the birds are the draw. De Brazza's Monkey is also resident. More relevantly, Sitatunga Flycatcher (Grey-chested Illadopsis in some taxonomies), Grey Crowned Crane displaying on the swamp edge, and a recently regularised population of Shoebill make this a worthwhile half-day stop. Shoebill records are irregular but not as rare as at most African sites.

## 10. Mida Creek, Coast

Mida Creek is a tidal mangrove and mudflat system within the Watamu Marine National Reserve, 100 kilometres north of Mombasa. The tidal flats attract the full range of Palearctic waders in winter: Bar-tailed Godwit, Sanderling, Greater and Lesser Sand Plover, Terek Sandpiper, Crab Plover (uncommon but regular). The mangrove forest holds Mangrove Kingfisher and Lemon-bellied Crombec. Tidal timing is critical: low tide concentrates waders on exposed mud; high tide pushes them to the mangrove fringe. The Mida Creek boardwalk gives access without vehicle.

## Planning a Kenya birding trip

Kenya's birding divides into four circuits that can be combined: Nairobi and the Rift Valley lakes (Nakuru, Naivasha, Bogoria), the western highlands and Kakamega, the coast (Arabuko-Sokoke, Mida Creek, Shimba Hills), and the northern Rift and Turkana specialties. A two-week trip in the Nairobi-Rift-Coast triangle is the most efficient first visit.

October to March is the best general period: Palearctic migrants are present on the coast and at inland wetlands, the Rift Valley lakes are most active, and temperatures at altitude are moderate. April and May bring the long rains; July and August bring cooler and drier conditions in the highlands. Kakamega is productive year-round; the best bird activity in the forest is November to January.

Kenyan guide networks are well-developed; Birding Kenya and the local chapter of Nature Kenya provide organised tours and guide contacts. eBird Kenya is growing rapidly with contributions from both resident expatriates and visiting birders.

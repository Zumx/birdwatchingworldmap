Hokkaido is Japan's northernmost main island, separated from Honshu by the Tsugaru Strait and stretching northward toward the Kuril Islands and the Kamchatka Peninsula. Its climate is decisively continental, with cold winters that bring deep snow and extensive coastal sea ice — conditions that create a unique winter wildlife spectacle unlike anywhere else in Japan. The island is home to the Japanese Crane, or Red-crowned Crane, one of the rarest and most charismatic birds in Asia. In winter, Steller's and White-tailed Eagles patrol the ice-fringed rivers and coastal areas in numbers that far exceed what can be seen elsewhere. Hokkaido in January and February is genuinely one of the world's great winter wildlife experiences.

The island's summer is equally rewarding, though less visited by overseas birders. The volcanic highlands of Daisetsuzan host breeding Eurasian Dotterel and Siberian Rubythroat. The boreal forests hold Blakiston's Fish Owl — the world's largest owl — along the forested river valleys. Migration brings Narcissus Flycatcher, Yellow-rumped Flycatcher, and dozens of East Asian specialities through Hokkaido. Over 350 species have been recorded on the island. Tourist infrastructure in the birding areas around Kushiro is well developed, with photography hides, specialist tour operators, and detailed local birding guides available.

## 1. Kushiro Shitsugen National Park, Eastern Hokkaido

Kushiro Marshland is the largest wetland in Japan, covering approximately 183 square kilometres of fen, reed marsh, and bog in eastern Hokkaido. It is the headquarters of the Japanese Crane recovery programme and the place where this extraordinary bird is most reliably and closely observed. In winter, hundreds of cranes gather at the Akan International Crane Centre feeding station, where management has supported the population's recovery from fewer than 30 birds in the 1950s to over 1,000 today. The cranes are joined by Whooper Swans on the partially frozen river channels. White-tailed Eagle and Steller's Eagle hunt over the marsh edge. Dawn and dusk are the most active periods, when cranes fly between roost sites on the river and feeding areas on the marsh.

## 2. Rausu, Shiretoko Peninsula, Eastern Hokkaido

The Shiretoko Peninsula is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, a largely roadless wilderness of volcanic mountains meeting the sea. In winter, the eastern coast near Rausu is the best single site in Japan — and arguably in the world — for observing Steller's Eagle at close range. The eagles congregate along the coast where drift ice from the Sea of Okhotsk concentrates fish. Eagle boat trips from Rausu harbour run from January through March and provide extraordinary close views of both Steller's and White-tailed Eagles on the ice and in the air. Spectacled Guillemot, Pelagic Cormorant, and Harlequin Duck are regular from the boats. In summer, Brown Bear is conspicuous from the coast road.

## 3. Lake Furen, Eastern Hokkaido

Lake Furen is a large coastal lagoon connected to the Pacific Ocean by a narrow channel, surrounded by mixed forest and reedbeds. The combination of brackish lagoon, open sea access, and forested shores creates an extremely diverse bird list. In winter, Steller's Eagle and White-tailed Eagle roost in the trees around the lake in large numbers — counts of over 200 eagles of both species combined are possible on a single winter morning. Red-crowned Cranes forage in the nearby fields. In summer, the forest holds Blakiston's Fish Owl — Furen is one of the most accessible sites for this species — and the lagoon supports breeding Osprey and nesting colonies of Black-headed Gull.

## 4. Tsurui-Ito Tancho Sanctuary, Eastern Hokkaido

The Tsurui-Ito crane sanctuary, run by the Wild Bird Society of Japan, operates feeding stations specifically managed for Red-crowned Cranes through the winter months. The proximity of the hides to the cranes makes this the premier site for close photography of this species. In deep winter, cranes perform courtship dances in the snow, leaping, calling, and bowing to each other — a behaviour that is one of Japan's most celebrated wildlife spectacles. The surrounding fields attract Whooper Swans and Bean Geese. The village of Tsurui has accommodation and facilities specifically catering to wildlife visitors, making it a logical base for multi-day crane watching trips.

## 5. Lake Utonai, Southern Hokkaido

Lake Utonai, near New Chitose Airport outside Sapporo, is a Ramsar-designated wetland and the first sanctuary established by the Wild Bird Society of Japan. Its proximity to the airport makes it an excellent first stop for birders arriving in Hokkaido before travelling further north. In spring, Bean Goose, Greater White-fronted Goose, and Tundra Bean Goose pass through in large numbers. In winter, the lake holds Smew and Goosander. Eurasian Bittern has been recorded in the reedbeds. The woodland fringe around the lake contains Japanese Pygmy Woodpecker and Long-tailed Tit. The sanctuary visitor centre provides species lists and recent sightings records.

## 6. Kiritappu Marsh, Eastern Hokkaido

Kiritappu Marsh near the Nemuro Peninsula is a large coastal wetland of fen, marsh, and tidal creek that attracts waders, cranes, and raptors. In winter, Short-eared Owl hunts over the open marsh in daylight. Rough-legged Buzzard quarters the meadow edges. Common Crane — the Eurasian species rather than the Japanese endemic — occurs alongside Red-crowned Cranes. In summer, the marsh holds breeding Whimbrel, Common Snipe, and Marsh Grassbird — a reed warbler relative that is a Hokkaido speciality. The Kiritappu headland itself is a productive sea-watching point for alcids and sea ducks offshore in winter.

## 7. Cape Nosappu, Nemuro Peninsula, Eastern Hokkaido

Cape Nosappu is the easternmost point of Hokkaido and is separated from the Russian-administered Kunashiri Island by a narrow strait clearly visible on a fine day. The cape is the best sea-watching point in Hokkaido for observing seabird migration. Ancient Murrelet is a regular winter visitor. Spectacled Guillemot, Rhinoceros Auklet, and Tufted Puffin pass offshore. The rocky shore holds wintering Harlequin Duck, Pelagic Cormorant, and Black Scoter. The town of Nemuro itself, approached through flat agricultural land on the peninsula, is good for Brambling and Hawfinch in winter. Japanese Crane occurs in the fields between the port town and the cape.

## 8. Daisetsuzan National Park, Central Hokkaido

Japan's largest national park covers over 2,000 square kilometres of volcanic highland and boreal forest in central Hokkaido. In summer, the Asahidake and Tokachidake volcanic areas above the treeline hold breeding Siberian Rubythroat — red-throated and melodious — perched on low willow scrub. Eurasian Dotterel is found on the highest stone fields. Northern Fulmar nests on the cliff faces. Hazel Grouse and Eurasian Jay inhabit the lower forest. The cable car from Asahidake Onsen reaches the alpine zone efficiently, allowing a full morning above the treeline before descending through the forest zone in the afternoon for a combined alpine and forest species list.

## 9. Notsuke Peninsula, Eastern Hokkaido

The Notsuke Peninsula is a long, narrow spit of land extending into the Notsuke Bay, covered in stunted forest of Sakhalin fir. In winter, the peninsula is renowned for its White-tailed Eagle population, which roosts in the windswept firs and hunts over the bay ice. The Notsuke Peninsula also holds Steller's Sea Lion on the offshore rocks — the sight of eagles and sea lions sharing the same sea ice is remarkable. The summer season brings Eurasian Curlew, Common Sandpiper, and Dunlin to the bay shore. The peninsula is best reached from the town of Betsukai, and the road runs the entire length to the tip.

## 10. Habomai Islands Sea-Watch, Near Cape Nosappu

The narrow Nemuro Strait between Hokkaido and the Habomai Islands provides one of the most concentrated seabird channels in the North Pacific. In winter, the passage of Brunnich's Guillemot, Common Guillemot, Ancient Murrelet, and Long-tailed Duck through the strait can be counted in thousands on cold clear days. Short-tailed Albatross — a recovering Pacific albatross species once hunted almost to extinction — passes through the strait during autumn and winter and is seen occasionally from fixed points near Cape Nosappu. The Habomai area is also the primary wintering site in Japan for Whooper Swan, with flocks of several hundred birds on the semi-frozen lagoons.

---

Hokkaido's winter — from November through March — is the internationally famous season for cranes and eagles, but the island's summer and migration periods hold equal rewards for those pursuing boreal and East Asian endemics. The infrastructure for wildlife tourism, particularly around Kushiro and the Nemuro Peninsula, is some of the best in Asia.

Austria is a country of dramatic contrasts for the birdwatcher. In the west and south, the Alps rise above 3,000 metres, holding high-altitude specialists such as Wallcreeper, Snowfinch, and the reintroduced Bearded Vulture. In the east, the land flattens abruptly into the Pannonian plain, where the shallow steppe lake of Neusiedl and its surrounding reedbeds host a Mediterranean-flavoured assemblage of herons, spoonbills, and warblers found almost nowhere else north of the Adriatic. Between these extremes lie the Danube floodplains, beech forests of the Vienna Woods, and the marshes of the alpine foothills. For a country smaller than Scotland, Austria's habitat diversity translates into a bird list that rewards anyone willing to travel from the lowland reeds to the snowline in a single trip.

## Lake Neusiedl and Seewinkel

The Neusiedler See, straddling the border with Hungary in Burgenland, is Austria's single greatest birding destination and a UNESCO World Heritage site. The lake is enormous but shallow, fringed by the largest reedbed in Central Europe, and the Seewinkel area to its east is dotted with shallow soda pans known locally as Lacken. Great White Egret, Purple Heron, Little Bittern, Bluethroat, and Bearded Reedling breed in the reeds, while the pans hold Avocet, Black-winged Stilt, Kentish Plover, and breeding Great Bustard nearby on the surrounding steppe. The Nationalpark Neusiedler See–Seewinkel visitor centre at Illmitz is the best starting point, with marked trails and hides overlooking the most productive pans.

## Hohe Tauern National Park

Austria's largest national park covers the highest peaks of the Central Alps, including the Grossglockner at 3,798 metres. This is the place to find true alpine birds: Golden Eagle, the spectacular Bearded Vulture reintroduced here from the 1980s onward, Alpine Chough wheeling around the summits, White-winged Snowfinch on the high screes, and Rock Ptarmigan above the treeline. The Grossglockner High Alpine Road provides road access to habitats that elsewhere would demand a hard day's hike, and the visitor infrastructure around Ferleiten and the Edelweissspitze makes raptor watching straightforward.

## The Danube Floodplains (Donau-Auen)

Between Vienna and the Slovak border, the Nationalpark Donau-Auen protects one of the last free-flowing stretches of the Danube and its riparian forest. This is a stronghold for White-tailed Eagle, which now breeds again along the river, alongside Black Stork, Kingfisher, and a full suite of woodpeckers including Middle Spotted and the elusive Black Woodpecker. The floodplain forest, backwaters, and gravel banks support Common Sandpiper and Little Ringed Plover. Trails from Orth an der Donau and the schloss-based visitor centre give easy access to the heart of the reserve.

## The Vienna Woods (Wienerwald)

The forested hills on the western edge of Vienna are a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and an excellent introduction to Central European woodland birds. Beech and oak forests here hold Hawfinch, Collared and Red-breasted Flycatcher, Wood Warbler, and all the region's woodpeckers. In the clearings and meadows, Red-backed Shrike and Yellowhammer are common, and Honey Buzzard breeds in the canopy. The proximity to the capital makes the Wienerwald an ideal half-day excursion when based in the city.

## Marchegg and the March-Thaya Floodplains

Northeast of Vienna, where the River March marks the border with Slovakia, lies one of Central Europe's largest White Stork colonies, with birds nesting directly in the ancient oaks of the floodplain forest at Schloss Marchegg rather than on rooftops. The wet meadows and oxbows of the March-Thaya-Auen attract Corncrake, Black Stork, and large numbers of migrating waterfowl. In spring, the flooded meadows ring with the calls of displaying snipe and breeding Whinchat.

## Hohe Wand and the Eastern Limestone Alps

The dramatic limestone cliffs of the Hohe Wand, only an hour south of Vienna, are among the most accessible places in Austria to look for Wallcreeper, the almost mythical crimson-winged climber of rock faces. The plateau above holds Rock Bunting, Rock Thrush, and breeding Peregrine and Eagle Owl on the cliffs. The mix of warm, south-facing slopes and montane forest produces a long species list within a small area.

## The Rhine Delta at Lake Constance

In Austria's far west, where the Rhine enters Lake Constance (Bodensee) in Vorarlberg, the Rheindelta nature reserve is the most important wetland on the lake. Vast numbers of wintering and migrating waterfowl gather here, including Red-crested Pochard, Goosander, and rafts of diving ducks, while the reedbeds and wet meadows hold breeding Bluethroat and Water Rail. The reserve at Fussach and Hard offers superb autumn and winter watching from the lakeshore.

## The Lechtal and the Tiroler Lech

The braided gravel river of the Lech in Tyrol is one of the last near-natural wild rivers of the northern Alps, protected as the Naturpark Tiroler Lech. The shifting gravel banks support breeding Little Ringed Plover and Common Sandpiper, while the steep flanking forests hold Three-toed and White-backed Woodpecker, two of the most sought-after alpine forest species. Tengmalm's Owl and Pygmy Owl call from the conifers in early spring.

## The Waldviertel Fishponds

In the granite highlands of northern Lower Austria, the Waldviertel is a landscape of forests, bogs, and historic carp ponds. The larger ponds, such as those around Heidenreichstein and the Schremser Hochmoor, attract migrating waders, Black-necked Grebe, and large numbers of duck, while the surrounding spruce forests hold Black Grouse, Capercaillie, and Boreal owls. The acid bogs are a regional rarity, with breeding Common Crane having recolonised in recent decades.

## The Karwendel and the Inntal

The Karwendel mountains north of Innsbruck form the largest nature park in Austria, a limestone range of high peaks and deep forested valleys. Alpine Accentor, Citril Finch, Ring Ouzel, and Nutcracker are characteristic of the higher zones, and the Inn valley below funnels migrating raptors in autumn. The combination of cable-car access to alpine habitats and well-marked trails makes the Karwendel a rewarding base for combining mountain hiking with birding.

## Explore on the map

Austria's compact size means a determined birder can sample alpine summits, Danube floodplains, and the Pannonian reedbeds of Neusiedl within a week-long trip. Spring, from late April to early June, is the richest season, when breeding song and migration overlap, though autumn raptor passage and winter wildfowl at Lake Constance offer their own rewards.

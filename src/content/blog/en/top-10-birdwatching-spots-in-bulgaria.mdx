Bulgaria punches above its size in European birding. The Via Pontica, one of Europe's most important migration corridors, runs along the Black Sea coast and concentrates raptors, storks, and passerines in spring and autumn. The Black Sea coastal wetlands — Atanasovsko, Poda, and Burgas Lake — hold internationally significant waterbird communities year-round. The Rhodope Mountains provide a stronghold for the largest Eastern Imperial Eagle (Aquila heliaca, Vulnerable) breeding population in the EU. Bulgaria is also the site of one of Europe's most significant colonial waterbird complexes at Srebarna Biosphere Reserve.

## 1. Atanasovsko Lake, Burgas

A large hypersaline lagoon on the Black Sea coast immediately north of Burgas, Atanasovsko is one of the most important waterbird sites in Bulgaria and a Ramsar site. Greater Flamingo (Phoenicopterus roseus) breeds here — one of the few Bulgarian flamingo breeding records. Pied Avocet (Recurvirostra avosetta), Black-winged Stilt (Himantopus himantopus), and large concentrations of migrant waders make this a year-round site. The eBird hotspot records over 300 species.

## 2. Poda Conservation Centre, Burgas

Poda, managed by the Bulgarian Society for the Protection of Birds (BSPB), is a small freshwater lagoon immediately south of Burgas that serves as the Via Pontica bottleneck point. In autumn, hundreds of thousands of raptors, storks, and bee-eaters concentrate and pass overhead. The conservation center provides hides, guided walks, and educational programs. Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus, Near Threatened) is regular in autumn and winter.

## 3. Lake Burgas (Vaya), Burgas

The largest natural lake in Bulgaria, with its mix of freshwater and brackish zones, holds Pygmy Cormorant (Microcarbo pygmeus), Ferruginous Duck (Aythya nyroca, Near Threatened), and the breeding Glossy Ibis (Plegadis falcinellus) population. The lake's three arms cover different habitats; the reed-fringed bays hold Great and Little Bittern, Aquatic Warbler (Acrocephalus paludicola, Vulnerable), and Penduline Tit.

## 4. Srebarna Biosphere Reserve, Silistra

Srebarna, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve on the Danube floodplain, contains one of Eastern Europe's most important Dalmatian Pelican (Pelecanus crispus) colonies. The lake complex additionally supports Great White Pelican (Pelecanus onocrotalus), Ferruginous Duck, and Whiskered Tern (Chlidonias hybrida). The reserve's floating reed islands, which rise and fall with Danube water levels, are among the most photographically productive waterbird colonies in the region.

## 5. Rhodope Mountains

The western Rhodopes form a stronghold for two Balkan vulture species: Griffon Vulture (Gyps fulvus) and the recovering Egyptian Vulture (Neophron percnopterus, Endangered) population. The Eastern Rhodopes additionally hold the largest Eastern Imperial Eagle population in the EU and the Black Vulture (Aegypius monachus) — the only Bulgarian Black Vulture colony. The BSPB-operated vulture feeding station at Madzharovo provides reliable close-range views of Griffon Vulture and occasional Egyptian Vulture from a dedicated hide.

## 6. Ovcharitsa Reservoir and Straldzha

The reservoir complex in the Stara Zagora region provides open water for migrant raptors, ducks, and waders during spring and autumn. White Pelican and Dalmatian Pelican are regular. The surrounding farmland holds Red-footed Falcon (Falco vespertinus) in spring passage and Montagu's Harrier (Circus pygargus) on passage.

## 7. Skalenski Lakes

This small wetland system near Nova Zagora represents the type of inland freshwater habitat that holds breeding Little Bittern (Ixobrychus minutus), Whiskered Tern, and Red-necked Grebe alongside common reed-bed passerines including Great Reed Warbler (Acrocephalus arundinaceus) and Marsh Warbler.

## 8. Rila National Park

Bulgaria's highest national park, containing the highest peak in the Balkans (Musala, 2,925 metres), Rila holds an Alpine bird community of Snowfinch (Montifringilla nivalis), Alpine Accentor (Prunella collaris), and Horned Lark. The lower forests produce Three-toed Woodpecker (Picoides tridactylus), Nutcracker (Nucifraga caryocatactes), and the full suite of mountain conifer birds.

## 9. Strandja Nature Park

Strandja, near the Turkish border in the far southeast, holds some of the largest areas of ancient mixed oak and hornbeam forest in Bulgaria. Semi-collared Flycatcher (Ficedula semitorquata), Olive-tree Warbler (Hippolais olivetorum), and Short-toed Snake Eagle (Circaetus gallicus) are characteristic species. The park's coastal cliffs hold breeding Middle Spotted Woodpecker (Leiopicus medius) and Eurasian Roller (Coracias garrulus).

## 10. Belene Islands Complex

The Belene Islands, in the Danube floodplain of northern Bulgaria, support Pygmy Cormorant and colonial waterbird breeding. The complex forms part of the longer Danube wetland corridor connecting Srebarna with Romanian wetlands across the river.

## Planning a Bulgaria birding trip

Bulgaria's birding year peaks twice: in spring (April to May) for breeding species and northward migration, and in autumn (September to October) for the southbound raptor migration along the Via Pontica. The Via Pontica corridor is particularly dramatic in October, when Honey Buzzard, Short-toed Eagle, Lesser Spotted Eagle (Clanga pomarina), and White Stork concentrate against the Black Sea coast and pass through the Burgas wetland complex. Counts at Poda Conservation Centre in October can reach several thousand raptors per hour in optimal conditions.

April is the month for the Rhodope vulture stations. Egyptian Vulture (Neophron percnopterus, Endangered) returns from Africa in late March and April; the Madzharovo feeding station near Kardzhali, operated by the Bulgarian Society for the Protection of Birds (BSPB), provides the most reliable close-range Egyptian Vulture viewing in Europe. Griffon Vulture is resident; Black Vulture is present throughout the year on the breeding cliffs of the western Rhodopes, though breeding activity peaks in March to May.

The Burgas wetlands — Atanasovsko, Poda, and Burgas Lake — are productive year-round. Greater Flamingo at Atanasovsko is present in all seasons; summer breeding activity of Avocet, Black-winged Stilt, and Pied Avocet is at its most visible from May to July; autumn shorebird migration peaks in August to September. The BSPB visitor center at Poda provides an excellent base for half-day excursions covering all three lakes in sequence.

Sofia is the most practical entry point; Burgas airport serves the wetland and Strandja region. The Rhodopes are best explored by car from Plovdiv or Kardzhali. The BSPB's online rare bird reporting system provides current information on what is being seen at each major site, updated daily during the main seasons.

## Bulgaria birding logistics

Sofia airport provides the main international entry point, with car rental necessary for covering Bulgaria's dispersed birding sites. The Burgas wetland complex is 400 kilometres east of Sofia — a four-hour drive or one-hour domestic flight. The Rhodope Mountains and the Arda River valley in the south are accessible within three hours from Sofia.

Bulgaria's birding calendar peaks twice: in spring (April to May) when the waterbird breeding colonies at Lake Atanasovsko and Lake Pomorie are at their most active, and in autumn (September to October) when the Bosphorus raptor migration spills into the Black Sea coast corridor. The raptor passage through the Burgas peninsula in early October is one of the largest in Europe.

Birding infrastructure is less developed than in western Europe but improving: the Green Balkans NGO and the Bulgarian Biodiversity Foundation provide English-language site guides and guide contacts for the main birding areas.

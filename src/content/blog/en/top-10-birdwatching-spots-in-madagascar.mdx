Madagascar is unlike anywhere else on Earth for a birder. Isolated from Africa for around 88 million years, the island has produced five entirely endemic bird families found nowhere else: the mesites, the ground-rollers, the cuckoo-rollers, the asities, and the vangas, a group whose remarkable adaptive radiation echoes Darwin's finches. Of the roughly 290 species recorded, more than half are endemic, and the great challenge and pleasure of birding Madagascar is the variety of habitats you must visit to find them: humid eastern rainforest, the bizarre spiny forest of the south, dry western deciduous woodland, and a scatter of crucial wetlands. A classic circuit links three or four of these zones, and the birds, though often shy and localised, are the most distinctive on any island in the world.

## Andasibe-Mantadia National Park

Andasibe, in the eastern rainforest belt within a few hours of the capital, is the most accessible and most visited birding site in Madagascar. The forest holds three ground-rollers, including the Pitta-like and Scaly Ground-Rollers, plus Velvet Asity, Nuthatch Vanga, and a strong list of greenbuls and couas. The Mantadia section, with taller primary forest, adds the difficult Red-breasted Coua and Short-legged Ground-Roller. Andasibe is equally famous as the home of the Indri, the largest living lemur, whose haunting dawn calls accompany the morning birding. It is the perfect introduction to rainforest endemics.

## Ranomafana National Park

Ranomafana, in the southeastern highlands, protects steep montane rainforest cut by rushing rivers and is one of the richest birding sites on the island. The forest holds Yellow-browed Oxylabes, Brown Mesite, Pollen's Vanga, Crossley's Vanga, and the localised Grey-crowned Tetraka, alongside Henst's Goshawk overhead. The trails are steep and the forest dense, demanding patience, but the diversity of vangas and ground-dwellers is exceptional. Ranomafana is also a celebrated centre for lemur research and a UNESCO World Heritage site within the eastern rainforest corridor.

## Masoala National Park

Masoala, on the remote northeastern peninsula, is the largest protected area in Madagascar and protects vast tracts of lowland rainforest running down to the sea. This is the place for the most difficult eastern endemics: the Helmet Vanga, with its enormous blue bill, the Bernier's Vanga, Scaly Ground-Roller, and the Red-breasted Coua. Access is by boat from Maroantsetra across the Bay of Antongil, and the birding is genuinely wild and demanding. For those willing to make the effort, Masoala offers the fullest experience of intact eastern lowland rainforest on the island.

## Berenty Reserve and the Spiny Forest

Berenty, in the far south near Fort Dauphin, is a private reserve set among gallery forest and the extraordinary spiny forest of octopus trees and baobabs. The spiny forest holds species adapted to this arid thorn-scrub: Running Coua, Subdesert Mesite nearby, Lafresnaye's Vanga, Archbold's Newtonia, and the Sickle-billed Vanga in noisy flocks. Berenty is also famous for its habituated ring-tailed lemurs and Verreaux's sifakas dancing across the open ground. The contrast with the eastern rainforest could not be greater, and the spiny forest endemics are found nowhere else.

## Ifaty and the Subdesert Spiny Forest

The spiny forest north of Toliara around Ifaty is the classic site for two of Madagascar's most sought-after birds, both confined to this narrow strip of southwest thorn-scrub: the Long-tailed Ground-Roller and the Subdesert Mesite. Local guides walk birders through the dense Didierea forest at dawn to find these skulkers, along with Running Coua, Thamnornis, and Archbold's Newtonia. The bizarre vegetation of spiny octopus trees and baobabs makes this one of the strangest landscapes on the island, and the two endemics here are on every visiting birder's list.

## Ankarafantsika National Park

Ankarafantsika, in the northwest on the road between Mahajanga and Antananarivo, protects dry deciduous forest and the lake of Ravelobe, offering a different suite of western endemics. The forest holds Van Dam's Vanga, White-breasted Mesite, Schlegel's Asity, and Coquerel's Coua, while the lake supports Madagascar Fish Eagle, one of the world's rarest raptors, and the localised Madagascar Jacana. The relatively open dry forest makes for easier viewing than the eastern rainforest, and night walks produce nightjars and the tiny mouse lemurs. It is the premier western dry-forest birding site.

## Tsingy de Bemaraha

The Tsingy de Bemaraha, a UNESCO site of razor-sharp limestone pinnacles in the remote west, combines dramatic karst scenery with western dry-forest birds. The forest and gorges hold Coquerel's Coua, Crested Coua, White-breasted Mesite, and a range of vangas, while the river offers waterbirds and the chance of Madagascar Fish Eagle. Access is difficult and seasonal, requiring river crossings and rough roads, but the combination of the surreal tsingy formations and the dry-forest endemics makes it a memorable destination for adventurous birders.

## Lake Alaotra

Lake Alaotra, the largest lake in Madagascar, sits in the eastern highlands and is the most important wetland on the island, though heavily affected by agriculture. The marshes are the only home of the critically endangered Alaotra Grebe relatives and the Madagascar Pochard's former range, and they still hold Madagascar Snipe, Meller's Duck, and the localised Madagascar Rail. The reedbeds support the endemic Alaotra gentle lemur. Conservation work here is intensive, and the lake remains essential for the island's threatened wetland birds despite the pressures on its marshes.

## Anjajavy and the Northwest Dry Forest

The Anjajavy peninsula in the northwest combines dry deciduous forest, mangroves, and limestone tsingy beside the sea, offering relaxed birding around a remote lodge. The forest holds Coquerel's Coua, Crested Coua, White-breasted Mesite, and a range of vangas, while the coast and mangroves add waterbirds and the chance of Madagascar Fish Eagle along the shore. Sifakas and brown lemurs move through the trees. The combination of accessible dry-forest endemics and a coastal setting makes Anjajavy a comfortable and productive western stop.

## Isalo and the Western Highlands

The Isalo massif in the southwest, a sandstone range of canyons, oases, and palm-fringed pools, offers birding in a dramatic landscape on the route between the highlands and the spiny forest. The rocky country holds Benson's Rock-Thrush, a localised endemic, along with Madagascar Partridge, Madagascar Buttonquail, and raptors over the cliffs. The natural pools in the canyons attract waterbirds and the gallery vegetation holds couas and vangas. Isalo is a scenic and worthwhile stop that breaks the long southern journey and adds open-country and rock-dwelling endemics.

## Explore on the map

A Madagascar birding trip is defined by its habitats: the eastern rainforests of Andasibe, Ranomafana, and Masoala for the ground-rollers and asities, the southern and southwestern spiny forest for the mesites and the Long-tailed Ground-Roller, and the western dry forests for the couas and Madagascar Fish Eagle. Internal flights and long road journeys link these zones, and most trips take two to three weeks to do them justice.

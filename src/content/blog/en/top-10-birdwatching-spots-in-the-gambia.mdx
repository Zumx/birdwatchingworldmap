The Gambia is the smallest country in mainland Africa, a thin sliver of land following the Gambia River inland from the Atlantic, and it has become the classic gateway to West African birding. The reasons are practical: a short flight from Europe, English widely spoken, a stable and friendly reputation, and an extraordinary density of birds within easy reach of the coastal hotels. More than 560 species have been recorded in a country smaller than many European provinces. A first-time visitor can comfortably see two hundred species in a week without travelling far, from kingfishers along the mangrove creeks to bee-eaters, rollers and hornbills in the dry savanna woodland upriver. It is the place where many birders meet their first Afrotropical families.

## 1. Kotu Creek and the coastal strip

Kotu Creek, on the edge of the main tourist area, is often the first birding most visitors do, and it is superb. The creek mouth, the sewage ponds and the surrounding scrub hold a remarkable concentration: Blue-breasted, Malachite, Pied and Giant Kingfisher all occur, alongside Hamerkop, Western Reef Heron, Senegal Thick-knee and Black-headed Plover. The famous Kotu cycle track and the nearby golf course add Yellow-crowned Gonolek and Bearded Barbet.

## 2. Abuko Nature Reserve

The Gambia's oldest protected area, Abuko is a small but rich patch of gallery forest just inland from the coast. Its shaded trails are the best place near the resorts to find true forest birds: Western Bluebill, Green Turaco, Grey-headed Bristlebill, African Pygmy Kingfisher and Verreaux's Eagle-Owl. The reserve's pools hold crocodiles and the photogenic Hadada Ibis, and the forest edge produces sunbirds and the localised White-spotted Flufftail.

## 3. Tanji Bird Reserve

This coastal reserve south of the resorts protects beach, lagoon and dune scrub. The offshore Bijol Islands hold the only seabird breeding colonies in the country, with Caspian Tern, Royal Tern and Grey-headed Gull. The beach and lagoon attract huge numbers of terns and waders, and the scrub holds Brown Babbler, Black-crowned Tchagra and Northern Crombec. Tanji is the place to scan the Atlantic for skimmers and pelicans.

## 4. Brufut Woods

A community-managed patch of dry coastal woodland near Brufut, this small site is celebrated for its reliable roosting owls. Long-tailed Nightjar and Northern White-faced Owl are regular, and the woods hold Klaas's Cuckoo, Yellow-billed Shrike, Lavender Waxbill and Grey Woodpecker. The drinking pool set up by local guides draws a steady stream of seedeaters and doves.

## 5. Tanbi Wetland Complex and Lamin Lodge

The mangrove creeks of the Tanbi wetlands, best explored by boat from Lamin Lodge near Banjul, are a Ramsar site of great importance. The channels hold Goliath Heron, African Darter, Mouse-brown Sunbird, Pygmy Kingfisher and large numbers of waders on the exposed mud. A morning boat trip through the mangroves at low tide is one of the great Gambian birding experiences.

## 6. Marakissa River Camp

In the southwest near the Casamance border, Marakissa is a riverside lodge surrounded by woodland and farmland. Its drinking pools and feeders attract a constant flow of birds: Red-billed Firefinch, Bronze Mannikin, Village Indigobird, Splendid Sunbird and Double-spurred Francolin. Violet Turaco and Grey-headed Bushshrike are found in the surrounding scrub.

## 7. Tendaba Camp and the Bao Bolong wetlands

Roughly halfway upriver, Tendaba is the classic base for exploring the north-bank Bao Bolong Wetland Reserve. Boat trips into the mangrove bolongs produce African Finfoot, White-backed Night Heron, Mouse-brown Sunbird and Pel's Fishing Owl, one of Africa's most sought-after birds. The surrounding savanna holds Bateleur, Dark Chanting Goshawk and Abyssinian Roller.

## 8. Kiang West National Park

The largest national park in The Gambia, Kiang West protects extensive dry savanna woodland on the south bank near Tendaba. The Tubabkollon escarpment is a vantage point for raptors, with Bateleur, Brown Snake Eagle, Martial Eagle and the occasional Lappet-faced Vulture. The woodland holds Stone Partridge, White-fronted Black Chat and a strong selection of bush birds.

## 9. Georgetown (Janjanbureh) and the upper river

Around the island town of Janjanbureh, the upper river holds species scarce or absent on the coast. The riverside and surrounding woodland produce Egyptian Plover, the famous crocodile bird, Northern Carmine Bee-eater, Swallow-tailed Bee-eater and Bruce's Green Pigeon. This is the heart of true Sudan-savanna birding in the country.

## 10. Basse Santa Su and the far east

The far eastern end of the country, around Basse, reaches the driest savanna and produces the most Sahelian species. Sahel specialities such as Sudan Golden Sparrow, Chestnut-bellied Starling, Egyptian Plover and Northern Carmine Bee-eater occur, and the river holds African Skimmer on the sandbars. The journey upriver itself, by road or boat, is a gradual transition through the country's habitats.

## Planning a birding trip in The Gambia

The Gambia's birding season is the dry season, from November to April, when European winter visitors are present, the resident birds are breeding or displaying, and travel is easy. November to February is the most comfortable, with cooler nights and excellent activity; March and April are hotter but bring some Palearctic migrants in breeding plumage and the start of the build-up to the rains. The wet season (June to October) is humid and many lodges close, though it brings breeding plumage and intra-African migrants for the dedicated.

The standard pattern is to base on the coast — at Kotu, Bijilo or Brufut — for the first days, covering Abuko, Tanji, Brufut Woods and the mangrove creeks, then travel upriver to Tendaba and, for the most ambitious, Georgetown and Basse. A local guide is almost universal and well worth it: Gambian bird guides are knowledgeable, the guiding tradition is strong, and they know the current roosts and drinking pools that make the difference. Many guides are accredited through the Gambia Birdwatchers Association.

Practical notes: the dirt roads upriver are slow, so allow generous time for the eastern leg; a boat is essential for the mangrove and river specialities such as African Finfoot and Pel's Fishing Owl. Light is harsh by mid-morning, so the early hours are by far the most productive. The Gambia rewards the relaxed birder: with so much accessible within minutes of the hotels, it is possible to combine a gentle holiday with a serious bird list, which is exactly why it remains the classic first step into Afrotropical birding.

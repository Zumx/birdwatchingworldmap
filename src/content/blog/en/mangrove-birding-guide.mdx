Mangrove forests occupy one of the most productive but underbirded habitats on Earth. These intertidal forests, dominated by salt-tolerant trees with complex prop root systems, fringe tropical and subtropical coastlines across Africa, Asia, the Americas, and northern Australia. Their combination of dense aerial root systems flooded at high tide and exposed at low tide, sheltered waterways, and a continuous food supply from marine invertebrates, small fish, and mud-dwelling organisms makes them among the most consistently productive environments for waders, herons, kingfishers, raptors, and — in the Indo-Pacific — a distinctive community of mangrove specialist passerines.

Mangroves are also among the most threatened habitats globally. Conversion for aquaculture, particularly shrimp farming in Southeast Asia, has reduced global mangrove extent by a substantial fraction over the past fifty years. The species adapted to mangroves — many with restricted ranges and small populations — are therefore conservation priorities as well as birding targets. Visiting a productive mangrove system and contributing observation data to eBird adds to the biological documentation on which management decisions rest.

## Mangrove Specialist Species

In the Indo-Malayan region, several birds are almost entirely confined to mangroves. The Mangrove Whistler (Pachycephala cinerea) is a drab olive-brown species with a deceptively loud, penetrating song, found in mangroves from Myanmar to the Philippines. The Brown-capped Kingfisher (Halcyon capensis) is another near-obligate mangrove bird of South and Southeast Asia, using channels and mudflats within the forest for fishing while nesting in arboreal termite mounds. The Mangrove Pitta (Pitta megarhyncha) is among the most sought-after of mangrove specialists, ranging through coastal mangroves from southern Thailand to Sumatra, identifiable by its bold blue, black, and buff plumage and preference for foraging on the mud among the prop roots.

## Herons, Egrets, and Bitterns

Mangroves worldwide support diverse assemblages of herons and egrets. In the Americas, Yellow-crowned Night-Heron and Tricoloured Heron are characteristic of mangrove-fringed bays and channels. The Boat-billed Heron, with its grotesquely broad bill adapted for scooping fish in darkness, roosts in mangrove stands by day and forages at low tide.

The Rufous Night-Heron (Nycticorax caledonicus) is the Indo-Pacific equivalent of the familiar Black-crowned Night-Heron, roosting colonially in mangroves and nesting in mixed heronries in the larger mangrove systems of Indonesia and northern Australia. In Africa, the Goliath Heron — the world's largest heron — favours large mangrove channels and associated estuaries from West Africa through to East Africa.

## Raptors

Mangroves attract raptors both as hunting grounds over tidal mudflats and as nesting cover. The Osprey nests in mangroves across its global range and hunts the shallow, clear waters of mangrove-lined bays with good visibility. White-bellied Sea Eagle is one of the dominant raptors in the mangrove systems of South and Southeast Asia, nesting in the tallest emergent trees and hunting over open water.

In Central and South America, the Black-collared Hawk of larger Amazonian and coastal mangroves specialises in fish, combining the behaviour of a fishing eagle with a relatively small body. The Mangrove Black-Hawk, found in mangrove edges from Mexico to Ecuador, is a nearshore specialist that hunts crabs and other intertidal invertebrates.

## Shorebirds at Low Tide

Mangrove systems at low tide expose extensive mudflats colonised by fiddler crabs, mudskippers, and invertebrates that support dense concentrations of wading birds. The exposed mangrove root systems provide foraging micro-habitats not available on open mudflats. Lesser Adjutants — large storks — use Southeast Asian mangroves at low tide, stalking through the channels and between prop roots for fish. Milky Stork in the same region is almost completely confined to mangroves and is classified as vulnerable due to mangrove loss and hunting.

## Best Mangrove Birding Sites

Sundarban Mangroves in Bangladesh and West Bengal, India, constitute the world's largest single mangrove system and hold species rarely found elsewhere in the region. The Sundarbans Tiger Reserve is renowned for Bengal Tiger but the birds — including Brown-winged Kingfisher, Ruddy Kingfisher, Masked Finfoot, and Mangrove Pitta — make it one of the world's premier mangrove birding destinations.

Mai Po Marshes in Hong Kong, while partly artificial in its current form, incorporates extensive mangrove-lined gei wai (tidal shrimp ponds) that hold large numbers of wintering waders and support a colony of Egret species in the mangrove stands. The site is formally protected as a Ramsar wetland of international importance.

Everglades National Park in Florida encompasses the largest mangrove forest in the continental United States, and its mangrove-lined creeks and coves hold breeding Roseate Spoonbill, Reddish Egret, and large wintering concentrations of shorebirds including impressive numbers of dowitchers and yellowlegs.

Daintree Rainforest and the adjacent mangroves of Cape Tribulation in Queensland, Australia, offer the combination of the world's oldest rainforest abutting rich mangrove systems. Buff-breasted Paradise Kingfisher, a species that migrates from New Guinea to breed in the adjacent rainforest, regularly forages at the mangrove-rainforest interface.

## Technique for Mangrove Birding

Mangroves present specific logistical challenges. Access is often restricted to boat or canoe, as walking through mangrove root systems is impractical at most states of tide. A small kayak or flat-bottomed boat allows approach to channels and root systems without the disturbance caused by a motorised vessel. Timing visits to low tide exposes the maximum area of foraging substrate and brings egrets and waders into open areas visible from a boat channel. Arriving at first light, before boat traffic increases on busy estuaries, dramatically improves sightings of secretive species.

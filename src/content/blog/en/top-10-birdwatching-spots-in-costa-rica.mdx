Costa Rica's national parks and private reserves protect around 28 percent of the country's land area — the highest proportion in Central America — and the result is a bird list of over 900 species that is accessible from excellent infrastructure. Roads, lodges, and guide networks are well-developed by the standards of any tropical birding destination. The sites below cover the main habitat zones: cloud forest, lowland rainforest, Pacific coast, Caribbean lowlands, and montane oak forest. All are on the [map](/map).

## 1. Monteverde Cloud Forest Reserve, Puntarenas

Monteverde is the most visited private reserve in Costa Rica and, despite the tourist pressure, one of the most consistently productive for cloud forest species. Resplendent Quetzal (Pharomachrus mocinno) is the primary target; breeding season (February to April) gives the best views of displaying males with their full tail plumes. Three-wattled Bellbird calls from the top of the cloud forest canopy in season, the call audible from the reserve entrance. The Santa Elena reserve adjacent to Monteverde is less crowded and covers similar habitat; a morning at each covers the full cloud forest complement.

## 2. La Selva Biological Station, Heredia (OTS)

La Selva, operated by the Organisation for Tropical Studies, is a 1,600-hectare lowland tropical rainforest reserve at the base of Braulio Carrillo. The station gives access to the full Caribbean lowland forest bird community: Great Green Macaw (one of the rarest macaws in the world), Sunbittern, Snowcap hummingbird, and a comprehensive selva family list. Access for day visitors is possible but restricted; staying overnight at the station's facilities provides early morning and evening hours that day visitors miss. The combination of research-grade infrastructure and genuine wilderness is unusual.

## 3. Carara National Park, Puntarenas

Carara bridges the Pacific dry forest and the wet Pacific rainforest — the only place in Costa Rica where this transition is accessible. The result is a bird list that combines dry-forest species with humid-forest species and an enormous resident and visiting Scarlet Macaw (Ara macao) population: hundreds of macaws roost along the Tárcoles River at the park entrance and fly in spectacular formation at dawn and dusk. The park trail network is well-maintained and manageable for self-guided visits; crocodiles in the Tárcoles add to the experience.

## 4. Rancho Naturalista, Cartago

Rancho Naturalista is a private lodge and reserve in the Turrialba foothills that has built one of the best bird lists for its elevation range in the country. Over 420 species are recorded on the property, including the spectacular Snowcap, Violet-crowned Woodnymph, and a reliable population of Rufous-tailed Hummingbird at the feeders. The lodge's guides are specialists in the Caribbean slope bird community. The property sits at 1,050 metres, bridging the lowland and montane species zones.

## 5. Savegre Valley, San José

The Savegre Valley in the Talamanca Mountains, accessed via the town of San Gerardo de Dota, is the most reliable site in Central America for Resplendent Quetzal during the non-breeding season. The valley's apple orchards and secondary forest hold large quetzal populations year-round; fruit-bearing trees along the Savegre River road produce predictable morning and evening feeding gatherings. The valley additionally holds Volcano Junco, Black-and-yellow Silky-flycatcher, Large-footed Finch, and Slaty Flowerpiercer — the specialties of Talamancan highland forest.

## 6. Tortuguero National Park, Limón

Tortuguero is accessible only by boat or small aircraft. Its canal system threads through lowland Caribbean rainforest and palm swamp, and the boat-based birding is superb: Anhinga, Boat-billed Heron, Keel-billed Toucan, Montezuma Oropendola, and Green-and-rufous Kingfisher are all observed from the water. The park is better known for nesting sea turtles (leatherback, July to October), but the bird list exceeds 300 species and the canal setting makes extended observation possible without walking through dense forest.

## 7. Corcovado National Park, Puntarenas

Corcovado on the Osa Peninsula is the most pristine lowland rainforest remaining in Central America. The park is famous for mammal diversity — jaguar, tapir, giant anteater — but the bird list reflects the same ecological integrity: Harpy Eagle has been recorded, Scarlet Macaws are abundant, and the full suite of Neotropical lowland families is present. Access is restricted: visitors must be accompanied by certified guides and permits cap daily entry. Stay options at La Leona ranger station or at lodges around Drake Bay; plan the logistics well in advance.

## 8. Manuel Antonio National Park, Puntarenas

Manuel Antonio is Costa Rica's most visited national park and, despite the crowds, holds a genuinely accessible bird community at the forest-beach interface. The White-faced Capuchin troops that move through the park's trails push forest birds ahead of them, effectively flushing species into view. Fiery-billed Aracari, Baird's Trogon, and Cherrie's Tanager are common. The beach approach means you can combine birding with excellent views of Brown Boobies and frigatebirds over the bay. Morning sessions before 8 am are far more productive than midday visits.

## 9. Bosque de Paz, Alajuela

Bosque de Paz is a private biological reserve at 1,800 to 3,000 metres on the northern slope of the Cordillera Central, bordering Juan Castro Blanco National Park. The reserve's elevational range encompasses both highland oak forest and lower cloud forest, giving access to Bare-shanked Screech Owl, Chiriqui Foliage-gleaner, and a full complement of highland tanagers. The feeder station at the lodge produces Coppery-headed Emerald and Purple-throated Mountain-gem hummingbirds at close range. The lodge is small and remote, with excellent food and a genuinely high-quality guiding operation.

## 10. Tapantí National Park, Cartago

Tapantí protects the headwaters of the Reventazón River on the Caribbean slope of the Talamanca range. The park receives some of the highest annual rainfall in Costa Rica, which maintains a permanently moist forest that holds an exceptional range of cloud and montane forest species. Sunbittern is regular along the river; Gartered Trogon, Blue-and-white Swallow, and Streak-breasted Treehunter occur throughout. The park road follows the river for several kilometres before becoming trail; the first two kilometres of road are the most productive and can be walked early before the gate opens.

## Planning a Costa Rica birding trip

Costa Rica's geography divides into three productive axes: the Pacific slope (Carara, Corcovado, Manuel Antonio), the Caribbean lowlands (La Selva, Tortuguero), and the mountain spine and highlands (Monteverde, Savegre, Tapantí, Bosque de Paz). A two-week trip that crosses the country from Pacific to Caribbean and spends three nights in the highlands gives the most efficient species accumulation.

December to April is the dry season on the Pacific slope, making trails and roads most accessible. The Caribbean slope receives rain year-round; the driest period there is February to April. The quetzal viewing at Savegre peaks February to May, coinciding with the Pacific dry season.

Costa Rica's guide network is well-developed and ATEC-certified naturalist guides are available at every major site. Self-driving is feasible and gives flexibility; road quality varies significantly away from main routes. The [map](/map) shows all ten sites with their geographic relationships — the compact size of the country means that most can be connected in a logical loop from San José.

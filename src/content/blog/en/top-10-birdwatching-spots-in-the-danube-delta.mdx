The Danube Delta is where Europe's second-longest river fans into the Black Sea across three primary channels and a labyrinth of secondary waterways, oxbow lakes, reed islands, and willow galleries. At roughly 5,800 square kilometres, it is the largest and best-preserved river delta in Europe, and it was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Biosphere Reserve in 1991. The delta straddles the border between Romania and Ukraine, with the largest portion on the Romanian side centred on Tulcea. The birdlife is genuinely extraordinary: both Great White Pelican and Dalmatian Pelican breed in large numbers, the White-tailed Eagle is common, and the delta is the most important breeding site for the Pygmy Cormorant in Europe.

Spring and early summer are the peak seasons, when breeding colonies of herons, egrets, cormorants, and ibises are at maximum activity and migrant passerines move through the reed beds. Autumn brings an impressive southward migration of raptors and waterfowl. Most visiting birders base themselves in Tulcea or in the small village of Crisan on the Sulina channel, from which boat-based exploration of the interior waterways becomes possible. The following ten sites and areas within the delta represent the most productive locations.

## 1. Lacul Fortuna and the Northern Letea Forest

The Letea Forest is a remnant of ancient forest enclosed within the delta north of the Sulina channel, an anomaly in a landscape dominated by reeds and water. The forest supports Middle Spotted Woodpecker, Black Woodpecker, and White-backed Woodpecker, all absent from the surrounding delta habitat. The adjacent Lacul Fortuna wetland has historically held nesting colonies of Dalmatian Pelican and is an excellent site for Ferruginous Duck. The area requires boat access from Crisan or Letea village and is best visited with a local guide familiar with the waterways. Wild horses roam the Letea sands between the forest blocks, an unexpected spectacle alongside the birds.

## 2. Rosca-Buhaiova Strict Nature Reserve

The Rosca-Buhaiova complex is the most important Great White Pelican breeding colony in Europe, supporting several thousand pairs. The reserve is a strict protection zone accessible only with permits from the Danube Delta Biosphere Reserve Authority, and access is typically arranged through authorized guides. The colony is visible from the perimeter channels, and the sight of thousands of pelicans in the air simultaneously during the morning thermals is among the most spectacular avian events in Europe. White-tailed Eagle, Marsh Harrier, and Pygmy Cormorant are all present around the colony perimeter.

## 3. Sulina Channel and Sulina Town

The Sulina channel runs east from Tulcea to the Black Sea and is the most travelled waterway in the delta. Birding by boat along the channel reveals a succession of habitats: reed beds with Savi's and Great Reed Warblers, willow galleries with Penduline Tit and Golden Oriole, and open water with Little Egret and Black-crowned Night Heron roosting. The town of Sulina at the channel mouth is a productive birding area in its own right, with Common Rosefinch in the scrubby wasteland and terns on the Black Sea beach. The Black Sea coast itself attracts migrant waders and, in autumn, significant numbers of raptors including Steppe Buzzard and Lesser Spotted Eagle.

## 4. Mila 23 Village Area

The village of Mila 23 (kilometre 23 on the Sulina channel) is one of the most commonly used bases for interior delta exploration. The channels and lakes in the surrounding area are productive for all typical delta species including Squacco Heron, Whiskered Tern, and Great White Pelican. Little Bittern is frequently seen from boats in the early morning, clinging to reed stems and freezing in characteristic upright posture when disturbed. The Lipovan fishing community at Mila 23 provides boat hire and accommodation, and the quiet channels around the village are less visited than the main routes, giving a more intimate experience of delta wildlife.

## 5. Lacul Razim and Sinoe Lagoon Complex

South of the main Danube channels lies a series of coastal lagoons connected to the sea by narrow barrier bars. Razim and Sinoe are the largest, and their shallow waters attract large concentrations of migrant and wintering waterbirds including Pygmy Cormorant, Dalmatian Pelican, and various diving ducks. The lagoon shores support nesting Pied Avocet, Black-winged Stilt, and Kentish Plover. In winter, the lagoons hold Smew, Velvet Scoter, and Red-throated Diver. The road along the western shore of Razim provides good viewing points without the need for a boat, making this area accessible to birders without waterway transport.

## 6. Sacalin Island and Black Sea Beach

Sacalin is a sandy island formed at the mouth of the Sfantu Gheorghe channel and is inaccessible except by boat from the village of Sfantu Gheorghe. The island holds one of the only breeding colonies of Mediterranean Gull in Romania and supports nesting Sandwich Tern, Common Tern, and Little Tern. The shallow waters around the island attract feeding pelicans and, during migration, large numbers of waders on the sandbanks. The exposed position at the Black Sea means that seabird movements are sometimes visible from the island, including regular Yelkouan Shearwater in autumn.

## 7. Sfantu Gheorghe Channel

The southernmost of the three main Danube channels, the Sfantu Gheorghe channel is less commercialized than the Sulina and gives the most naturalistic experience of the delta interior. The channel winds through extensive reed beds and passes several large lakes before reaching the sea. Aquatic Warbler, one of Europe's most threatened songbirds, has been recorded in the tall sedge and reed margins along this channel. White-tailed Eagle is regularly seen perched in the willows. The village of Sfantu Gheorghe at the channel mouth has basic accommodation and provides access to the Sacalin Island area.

## 8. Caraorman Area

Caraorman, like Letea, is a sand ridge with forest within the delta, accessible by boat from Crisan. The forest here is less extensive than Letea but the surrounding lakes — particularly Isacul Mare — are productive for waterbirds. The open sandy areas between the forest blocks hold Tawny Pipit and Short-toed Lark in summer. The village of Caraorman itself has experienced significant depopulation, and the overgrown gardens around abandoned buildings create ideal habitat for Nightingale, Common Whitethroat, and Red-backed Shrike. The area also provides reliable access to Roller, which nests in tree cavities in the forest fringe.

## 9. Tulcea Town and Danube Frontage

Tulcea, the largest city in the delta region, is the point of departure for most boat trips and has its own productive birding areas. The Danube frontage at the northern edge of town has consistently attracted rare herons and egrets over the years, and Black Stork passes regularly during migration. The wetland areas northwest of town near the Balta Somova hold Ferruginous Duck and Pygmy Cormorant in the quieter channels. The Information Centre for the Danube Delta Biosphere Reserve in Tulcea provides useful orientation and the permit system for restricted areas is administered here.

## 10. Chilia Branch and the Romanian-Ukrainian Border Area

The northern Chilia channel forms the border between Romania and Ukraine and is the least-visited of the three main branches. The Romanian bank has road access in places, and the wide channel and adjacent floodplain lakes attract large numbers of White-tailed Eagles in winter when fish are concentrated in the shrinking water bodies. The floodplain forests here hold Collared Flycatcher in spring and the wetland margins support Penduline Tit and Bearded Reedling. The border character of the region means access requires some planning, but the low visitor pressure makes it one of the most rewarding areas in the entire delta.

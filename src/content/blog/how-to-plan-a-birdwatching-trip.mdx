A birding trip lives or dies on timing. The same wetland is a world wonder in
May and an empty pond in July. Plan around the birds' calendar, not yours, and
everything else follows.

## Step 1: Decide — list or spectacle

Are you chasing species (a long target list) or spectacle (huge numbers, big
events like crane staging or a migration fallout)? The two need different
routes. Spectacle trips concentrate on a few famous sites at one peak date;
list trips spread across habitats. Decide first.

## Step 2: Sync to migration

Birds move on a schedule. Spring passage, autumn raptor funnels, winter
waterfowl concentrations, and breeding-season song all peak in different
months. Pick the window for your target, then build the trip inside a
two-to-three-week band around it. A week early or late can halve the trip.

## Step 3: Map the hides

Open the [map](/map), filter to your region, and cluster sites by habitat —
wetland, coast, forest, montane. A strong itinerary alternates habitats so the
species list grows instead of repeating. Note which sites have hides versus
open watching; hides extend a productive day.

## Step 4: Book what must be booked

Operator hides (eagles, owls, specialist photography blinds) have limited seats
and seasonal windows — reserve months ahead. Reserves with timed entry or
permit quotas need the same. Free public towers do not, but dawn competition
for the best slot is real; arrive early instead.

## Step 5: Plan the daily rhythm

Birds front-load the day. Be in the hide before sunrise, work hard until
mid-morning, rest and travel in the dead hours, and return for the evening
roost. Build the itinerary around two short, intense windows, not one long
slog.

## Step 6: Pack the right kit

8x42 or 10x42 binoculars, a scope and tripod for open water and coasts, a
regional field guide plus a sound-ID app, a notebook, neutral clothing, and far
more warm layers than the forecast suggests — sitting still gets cold fast.
Power banks for phone-based ID and a small folding seat for hides without
benches.

## Step 7: Get local intelligence

Check recent sightings (eBird, reserve boards, local groups) the week before
and the morning of. A site can switch from quiet to extraordinary overnight
during migration. A half-day local guide at the key site pays for itself in
species and saved time.

## Step 8: Field ethics

No call playback near breeding birds, no flushing for a photo, no approaching
nests, stay on paths, gates closed, and report rare or injured birds rather
than crowding them. Ethical birders keep sites open and birds returning.

## Put it together

Target, then timing, then map, then bookings, then daily rhythm. Open the
[map](/map), pick the cluster, lock the date to the migration window, and let
the birds' calendar write the itinerary.

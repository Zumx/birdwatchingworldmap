Some places are not just good birding — they are phenomena. Geography squeezes
millions of birds through a few corridors, or evolution packs absurd diversity
into one valley. These are the sites that define a birding life.

## Eilat, Israel

A desert bottleneck between three continents. In spring, raptors and storks
stream over in their hundreds of thousands, channelled by the Rift Valley into
one of Earth's great migration spectacles.

## Panama: the Raptor River of Raptors

Over the isthmus each autumn, millions of broad-winged and Swainson's hawks
form rivers of birds visible from a single ridge — the largest raptor count
anywhere.

## Manú and the Andes, Peru

An elevational transect from cloud forest to lowland Amazon holding one of the
densest bird lists on the planet — hundreds of species in a few days.

## The Pantanal, Brazil

A flooded plain where birds are vast, close, and unbothered — jabirus, hyacinth
macaws, and hundreds of waterbirds against open sky.

## Falsterbo, Sweden

A southern Scandinavian peninsula that funnels the autumn migration of an
entire region to its tip, watched from a single famous point.

## Bharatpur (Keoladeo), India

A compact wetland that becomes, in winter, a wall of storks, cranes, and
waterfowl within walking distance of the gate.

## The Wadden Sea, Netherlands–Germany–Denmark

The most important intertidal staging area on the East Atlantic flyway —
millions of shorebirds wheeling over the largest unbroken mudflat system in
the world.

## Cape May, USA

The classic Western Hemisphere migration trap, where geography stacks
songbirds, raptors, and seabirds in a single autumn town.

## Liminka Bay, Finland

Northern Europe's wetland showcase, where 24-hour spring light and dense
hides put cranes and arctic migrants within reach.

## What "impressive" really means

Numbers stagger first, but the lasting impression is concentration — the moment
a sky empties of nothing and fills with everything because a continent is
funnelling through one point you happen to be standing on. That is the
experience these sites exist to give.

## Going there responsibly

These hotspots carry heavy birder traffic. Stick to paths and hides, never
flush a flock for a photo, support the local reserves that protect the
habitat, and travel with operators who reinvest in conservation. The spectacle
persists only while the habitat does.
